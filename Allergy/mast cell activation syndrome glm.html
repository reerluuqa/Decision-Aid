ACUTE EPISODE: Manage as Anaphylaxis immediately.

1. Clinical Symptoms

Required: 2+ Organ Systems

  • Skin: Flushing, Urticaria, Angio-oedema
  • Resp: Wheeze, Stridor, Dyspnoea
  • CV: Hypotension, Tachycardia, Syncope
  • GI: Cramps, Nausea, Diarrhoea
2. Lab Evidence

Required: Transient Rise

Serum Tryptase: Rise > (1.2 x Baseline) + 2 ng/ml

OR Urinary histamine/PGD2/LTC4 metabolites rise.

3. Response to Tx

Required: Improvement

Symptoms must improve with:

  • H1/H2 Antihistamines
  • Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists
  • Sodium Cromoglicate / Ketotifen
  • Omalizumab (Anti-IgE)
üö© Red Flags for Primary MCAS

If present, investigate for Clonal Mast Cell Disorder (Mastocytosis).

  • Baseline Tryptase > 20 ng/ml (Highly suspicious for SM)
  • Urticaria Pigmentosa (Maculopapular skin lesions)
  • Hypotensive Syncope/Pre-syncope (Unprovoked)
  • Recurrent unprovoked anaphylaxis
Investigations (If Suspected Primary)
  • Peripheral Blood: KIT D816V mutation analysis (Highly sensitive PCR).
  • Bone Marrow Biopsy: Indicated if mutation found or tryptase >20.
  • Serum Tryptase: Use baseline >20ng/ml cut-off for systemic mastocytosis screening.
Stepwise Management Approach
1. Trigger Avoidance

Identify and avoid IgE-dependent triggers (food, drugs, venoms) and physical stimuli (heat, pressure, exercise).

2. Pharmacotherapy (Step-up)
  • Baseline: H1 Antagonist + H2 Antagonist.
  • Add-on: Leukotriene Receptor Antagonist (e.g., Montelukast).
  • Add-on: Sodium Cromoglicate (Mast cell stabilizer).
  • Refractory: Omalizumab (Anti-IgE) or Cytoreductive therapy for clonal disease.
Emergency Kit

All patients should carry:

  • Adrenaline Autoinjector (x2)
  • High-dose Non-sedating Antihistamine
  • Corticosteroids (per protocol)
Long-term Specifics
  • Venom Sensitivity: Lifelong immunotherapy recommended (e.g., Wasp/Bee).
  • Mixed MCAS: Treat both the allergy (secondary) and the clonal disease (primary).
Classification (Aetiology)
Type Cause Key Features
Primary Clonal Mast Cell Disease (e.g., Mastocytosis) KIT mutation, CD25+, often unprovoked.
Secondary Normal Mast Cells + Trigger Most common. IgE-dependent allergy.
Idiopathic Unknown cause Meets criteria, no clonality, no trigger found.
Pathophysiology

Mechanism: Multi-organ systemic symptoms due to release of mediators from activated mast cells.

  • Degranulation: Histamine, Tryptase.
  • Lipid Mediators: Leukotrienes, Prostaglandin D2.
  • Cytokines: IL-6, TNF.

Note: Serum Tryptase is the most specific marker (Mast cells contain 500x more than basophils).

Pitfalls & Overdiagnosis

MCAS is rare. Do not confuse with:

Chronic Urticaria Hereditary Alpha-Tryptasemia IBS Fibromyalgia Anxiety

These conditions may have non-specific symptoms but do not meet the strict Vienna Consensus criteria (Recurrent systemic episodes + Lab rise + Treatment response).

Case #1: Mixed MCAS

Patient: 57-year-old man.

Event: Anaphylaxis to Wasp sting (Loss of consciousness, BP 65 mmHg).

Labs: Acute Tryptase 45, Baseline 16. Positive Wasp IgE.

Workup: KIT D816V mutation detected. Bone marrow confirmed Indolent Systemic Mastocytosis.

Diagnosis: Mixed Primary (Mastocytosis) + Secondary (Venom Allergy).
Action: Venom-specific immunotherapy.
Case #2: Idiopathic MCAS

Patient: 33-year-old woman.

History: Recurrent anaphylaxis since age 8 (17 episodes total). 15 involved syncope.

Labs: Baseline Tryptase 2.5-3.5. Acute Episode Tryptase 11-12.

Workup: No IgE allergies found. Bone marrow negative for clonality.

Diagnosis: Idiopathic MCAS.
Action: High-dose Desloratadine (No anaphylaxis in 4 years follow-up).
