Immediate Clinical Attention

  • Anaphylaxis: Watch for urticaria, respiratory distress, or angioedema (rare, triggered by protein allergens like latex).
  • Systemic Reaction: "Auto-eczematization" ‚Äì dermatitis appearing distant from contact site.
  • Infection Signs: Honey-colored crusting, increased pain, or fever (secondary impetiginization).
  • Chemical Burns: Ulceration or necrosis from strong acids/alkalis (requires acute care/irrigation).

üìã Differentials Checklist

Consider these if presentation is atypical.

Atopic Dermatitis +

Key Difference: Symmetric flexural areas (antecubital/popliteal fossa), family history of atopy, less vesicular. Patch test may be positive if co-existent.

Nummular Eczema +

Key Difference: Coin-shaped plaques, pinpoint vesiculation, symmetric, extensor lower extremities. Patch test negative.

Dyshidrotic Eczema +

Key Difference: Exclusively palms and soles. Minimal dorsal hand/foot involvement. Patch test negative.

Tinea Pedis +

Key Difference: Usually between toes, soles, sides of feet. Contact dermatitis is more common on dorsum of foot. Confirm with KOH prep.

Scabies +

Key Difference: Burrows, typical distribution (hands, waist, axilla, groin). Confirm with mineral oil scraping.

üî¨ Key Investigations

Patch Testing

Gold standard. Read at 48h and 72-96h. Use screening series or specific trays.

Skin Biopsy

Use if diagnosis in doubt or chronic. Note: Usually cannot distinguish ICD vs ACD, but rules out lymphoma/psoriasis.

ROAT / PUT

Repeated Open Application Test if patch testing unavailable. Good for leave-on products.
