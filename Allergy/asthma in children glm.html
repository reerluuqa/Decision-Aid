Immediate Action Required

Treat children immediately if they are acutely unwell or highly symptomatic at presentation. Do not delay treatment for diagnostics if unstable.

Diagnostic Approach

Age < 5 Years

Cannot perform reliable lung function tests. Use a probability-based approach + therapeutic trial.

Age ≥ 5 Years

Confirm diagnosis with objective tests of variable expiratory airflow limitation (Spirometry/PEF/FeNO).

Caution: Do not confirm diagnosis without a suggestive clinical history AND a supporting objective test. Record basis of diagnosis clearly.
Key History Features
  • ✓ Episodic symptoms
  • ✓ Wheeze confirmed by HC pro
  • ✓ Worse at night/early AM
  • ✓ History of atopy
  • ✓ Recurrent events over time
  • ⚠️ Isolated cough is rarely asthma

Investigations Reference

Spirometry (Gold Standard)
Age ≥ 5

Preferred method for assessing variability. Requires cooperative child.

Metric Normal/Asthma Indicator
FEV₁/FVC Ratio Normal > 0.90 in children.
Obstructive: < LLN or < 80%
Reversibility Positive: ≥12% improvement from baseline FEV₁
Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF)
Home/Acute

Less reliable than spirometry but useful where services unavailable or for monitoring.

Criteria Threshold
Bronchodilator response ≥15% improvement
Diurnal Variability >13% (twice daily)
UK Specific (BTS/NICE) Avg variability ≥20%
FeNO (Fractional Exhaled NO)
Type 2 Inflammation

Measures airway-specific eosinophilic inflammation. Guidelines vary by region.

Guideline Thresholds (ppb)
UK (BTS/NICE) ≥35 ppb confirms diagnosis
US (NAEPPCC) Low: <20 | High: >35
GINA (Biologics) ≥20 ppb suggests Type 2
*Note: Smoking lowers FeNO. Viral infections can raise or lower it.
Blood Eosinophils
Severe Asthma

Useful for identifying type 2 inflammation and directing biological therapy.

Severe Asthma / Biologics threshold:
≥150/microlitre
Exclude parasitic infection if ≥300.

Strong Associations

  • ✔
    Allergic Sensitization
    Positive skin prick to dust mites/pollen.
  • ✔
    Atopic Disease
    Eczema, allergic rhinitis (Allergic March).
  • ✔
    Early Respiratory Infections
    RSV or Rhinovirus in early life.
  • ✔
    Family History
    Asthma or atopy in 1st degree relatives.
  • ✔
    Environmental Exposure
    Tobacco smoke, air pollution (NO2, PM2.5).

Weaker / Contributory

  • •
    Obesity
    Rapid weight gain increases risk.
  • •
    GORD & OSA
    Weak/moderate association, bidirectional.
  • •
    Mode of Birth
    Cesarean section linked to wheezing.
  • •
    Medications in Pregnancy
    Paracetamol, antibiotics, acid-suppressors.
General Principles
Child-Specific Guidelines

Do not extrapolate adult guidelines to children. Most children have mild intermittent asthma and do not require daily therapy.

Minimum Dose, Max Control

Use minimum doses for maximal control. Therapy must be individualized.

Consider Differentials

Especially in very young children or those requiring high-dose ICS.

Referral Criteria (Preschool)

Refer to consultant respiratory paediatrician if:

  • Hospital admission with wheeze.
  • ≥2 Accident & Emergency admissions with wheeze in 12 months.
Severity Classification (GINA)
Severity Definition
Mild Well controlled on Step 1 or 2 (SABA or Low-dose ICS).
Moderate Well controlled on Step 3 or 4 (ICS-LABA).
Severe Uncontrolled despite high-dose ICS-LABA.
