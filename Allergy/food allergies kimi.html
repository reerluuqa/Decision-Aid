üö® Immediate Anaphylaxis Screening Emergency

  • Symptoms within 2 hours of food ingestion?
  • Airway, breathing, or circulation problems?
  • Skin/mucosal changes (urticaria, angioedema)?
  • Hoarseness, throat tightness, wheezing?
  • Vomiting, cardiovascular collapse?
Action: If anaphylaxis suspected, give IM adrenaline immediately and assess airway/breathing/circulation

üìñ Detailed History for Suspected Food Reaction

  • All foods ingested within 2-4 hours before reaction
  • Time interval between ingestion and symptom onset
  • Quantity of food consumed
  • Prior exposures and reactions to same food
  • Associated factors: exercise, aspirin, alcohol
  • Symptom progression: rapid onset suggests IgE-mediated

üë∂ Age-Specific Considerations

Infants (0-2 years)
  • Moderate to severe eczema? ‚Üí Evaluate for food allergy

  • Profuse vomiting 2h after milk/soya? ‚Üí Consider enterocolitis
  • Blood-streaked stools in thriving infant? ‚Üí Consider proctocolitis
Children (2-12 years)
  • Severe atopic dermatitis? ‚Üí Screen for common allergens
  • Heartburn, dysphagia? ‚Üí Consider eosinophilic oesophagitis
  • Exercise-associated reactions? ‚Üí Food-dependent exercise-induced anaphylaxis
Adults
  • Pollen/latex allergy? ‚Üí Assess for oral allergy syndrome
  • Food impaction, dysphagia? ‚Üí Consider eosinophilic oesophagitis
  • Diarrhea, weight loss, anemia? ‚Üí Consider coeliac disease

üîç Key Physical Examination Findings

  • Acute reaction: Urticaria, angioedema, wheezing, hypotension
  • Allergic rhinitis signs: Allergic shiners, pale nasal mucosa
  • Atopic dermatitis: Rough, red plaques in flexor/extensor surfaces
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis: Papulovesicular rash on extensor surfaces
  • Failure to thrive: Low weight, delayed growth (infants/children)

‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral Urgent

  • Anaphylaxis requiring emergency treatment
  • Food protein-induced enterocolitis with dehydration/shock
  • Severe eczema with suspected multiple food allergies
  • Food impaction or severe dysphagia
  • Failure to thrive with GI symptoms
  • Alpha-gal reaction with cardiovascular symptoms

üéØ Condition-Specific Workup

Anaphylaxis IgE-Mediated

Testing: Skin prick test or specific IgE to suspected foods identified by history

Common culprits: Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish (adults); milk, eggs, soy, wheat (children)

Next step: Confirm with oral food challenge if needed

Oral Allergy Syndrome Pollen-Food Cross-reactivity

Testing: Skin prick test for pollens (birch, ragweed) or latex

Associations: Birch ‚Üí stone fruits, nuts; Latex ‚Üí avocado, kiwi, banana

Next step: Avoid identified cross-reactive foods

Eosinophilic Oesophagitis Mixed IgE/Cell-Mediated

Testing: Upper endoscopy with biopsy (‚â•15 eosinophils/HPF)

Additional: Skin prick test, specific IgE, atopy patch testing

Empirical: Elimination diet (milk, wheat, egg, soy, nuts) or elemental diet

Coeliac Disease Cell-Mediated

First test: IgA-tTG (while on gluten-containing diet)

If IgA deficient: IgG-tTG or IgG-DGP

Confirmatory: Duodenal biopsy (villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia)

Genetic: HLA-DQ2/DQ8 testing if serology-histology discrepancy

Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Cell-Mediated

Diagnosis: Clinical history ONLY (no specific tests)

Triggers: Milk and soy most common; also grains, vegetables, poultry

Confirmation: Resolution after elimination of culprit food

Lactose Intolerance Non-Immune Enzyme Deficiency

First test: Dietary exclusion for 2 weeks with symptom diary

Confirm: Lactose hydrogen breath test (increase >20ppm H2 at 60min)

Definitive: Small intestine biopsy with lactase assay (rarely needed)

Alpha-Gal Allergy Tick-Borne IgE-Mediated

History: Delayed reaction (1-6h) to mammalian meat

Testing: Serum IgE to alpha-gal

Challenge: Consider if testing inconclusive (specialist supervision)

Food Sensitivities (MSG, Tyramine) Controversial/Non-Immune

Confirmation: Double-blind placebo-controlled challenge

MSG: Multiple studies show no consistent reactions

Tyramine: No proven association with migraines in controlled trials

Sulfites: Confirmed bronchospasm in sensitive asthmatics (PFT monitoring)

Management & Treatment
+

üö® Emergency Anaphylaxis Management First Priority

Immediate Treatment
  • Adrenaline IM: 0.5mg (0.5mL of 1:1000) in adults ‚Ä¢ 0.01mg/kg in children
  • Route: Intramuscular into mid-outer thigh
  • Repeat: Every 5 minutes if no improvement
Basic Life Support
  • Airway assessment and support
  • High-flow oxygen
  • IV fluids for hypotension (1-2L rapid)
  • Position: Supine with legs elevated
Adjunctive Therapies
  • Antihistamines (H1 and H2 blockers)
  • Corticosteroids (prednisolone 1-2mg/kg)
  • Bronchodilators for wheeze (salbutamol)
Monitoring & Observation
  • Children: Observe 6-12 hours from symptom onset
  • Biphasic risk: Monitor for recurrence after resolution
  • Discharge: Only if stable with written emergency plan

üìã Long-term Management by Condition

IgE-Mediated Food Allergy

Avoidance: Strict elimination of identified allergens

Education: Label reading, cross-contamination prevention

Emergency preparation: Prescribe adrenaline auto-injectors (minimum 2)

Training: Use of auto-injector, recognition of symptoms

Written plan: Allergy and anaphylaxis emergency plan

Risk reduction: Medical alert jewelry, inform schools/workplaces

Food-Dependent Exercise-Induced Anaphylaxis

Strategy: Avoid trigger food 4-6 hours before exercise

Common triggers: Wheat (most common), shellfish, vegetables, nuts

Precautions: Avoid aspirin/NSAIDs with trigger foods

Emergency: Carry adrenaline auto-injector during exercise

Oral Allergy Syndrome

Management: Avoid raw forms of cross-reactive foods

Tolerance: Often tolerate cooked/heated forms

Caution: Rare progression to systemic symptoms

Monitoring: Annual review of tolerated foods

Atopic Dermatitis with Food Allergy

Approach: Eliminate only confirmed food allergens

Goal: Avoid unnecessary dietary restrictions

Monitoring: Regular eczema assessment and growth monitoring

Re-challenge: Consider every 12-24 months to assess tolerance

Eosinophilic Oesophagitis

Options: Dietary elimination vs. Topical corticosteroids

Empiric elimination: Remove 6 most common allergens

Elemental diet: Amino acid-based formula (severe cases)

Monitoring: Repeat endoscopy after 8-12 weeks

Gradual reintroduction: Systematic food challenges to identify triggers

Coeliac Disease

Primary treatment: Strict lifelong gluten-free diet

Avoid: Wheat, rye, barley, and their derivatives

Monitoring: Annual serology (IgA-tTG) and dietetic review

Complications: Screen for anemia, osteoporosis, vitamin deficiencies

Associated conditions: Type 1 diabetes, autoimmune thyroid disease

Family screening: First-degree relatives (5-10% prevalence)

Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis/Proctocolitis

Acute: IV rehydration for dehydration/shock

Long-term: Elimination of trigger food (usually milk/soy)

Prognosis: Usually resolves by age 3-5 years

Monitoring: Nutrient intake and growth parameters

Reintroduction: Supervised in clinic after 12-24 months

Lactose Intolerance

Management: Lactose-restricted diet

Alternatives: Lactose-free dairy, lactase enzyme supplements

Calcium: Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake

Tolerance: Usually small amounts may be tolerated

Congenital form: Permanent; Adult-onset: often progressive

Alpha-Gal Allergy

Avoidance: All mammalian meat and products

Safe: Poultry, fish, non-mammalian foods

Hidden sources: Gelatin, dairy, some medications

Unique aspect: Delayed reaction (2-6 hours) makes diagnosis challenging

Prognosis: May resolve after 3-5 years if no further tick bites

üéØ Indications for Re-challenge

  • IgE-mediated allergy: Annually for children, 2-3 years for adults
  • Natural history: Many childhood allergies resolve (milk, egg, soy, wheat)
  • Persistent allergies: Peanut, tree nut, fish, shellfish less likely to resolve
  • Test improvement: Declining skin test wheal or specific IgE levels
  • Accidental tolerance: Tolerated accidental ingestion without symptoms
  • Quality of life: Significant dietary restriction impact
Caution: Re-challenges must be performed under medical supervision with emergency equipment available

üìà Long-term Prognosis by Condition

IgE-Mediated Food Allergies

Resolves by adolescence: Milk (~50%), egg (~50%), soy (~45%), wheat (~40%)

Persists into adulthood: Peanut (~80%), tree nuts (~90%), fish/shellfish (~85%)

Monitoring: Annual specific IgE levels, consider supervised challenge when declining

Food-Dependent Exercise-Induced Anaphylaxis

Natural course: Often persists but predictable with avoidance

Strategy: Maintain 4-6 hour gap between trigger food and exercise

Improvement: Some outgrow after several years of avoidance

Eosinophilic Oesophagitis

Chronic relapsing: Requires long-term management

Options: Continued elimination diet or low-dose topical steroids

Monitoring: Symptom diary, repeat endoscopy if symptoms recur

Coeliac Disease

Lifelong condition: Requires permanent gluten-free diet

Complications prevention: Adequate adherence prevents malignancy, osteoporosis

Monitoring: Annual serology, bone density scan, nutritional assessment

Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis

Typical resolution: By 3-5 years of age

Reintroduction: 12-24 months after elimination, supervised in clinic

üéì Prevention Strategies

Primary Prevention (Infants)
  • Early introduction of allergenic foods (4-6 months)
  • Maintain regular consumption once tolerated
  • Exclusively breastfeed for first 4-6 months if possible
  • Maternal diet during pregnancy/lactation: No restriction needed
  • High-risk infants (severe eczema, egg allergy): Consider peanut introduction by 4-6 months
Secondary Prevention
  • Strict avoidance of identified allergens
  • Prevent cross-contamination in food preparation
  • Carry emergency medication at all times
  • Educate family, friends, school/work about allergy
  • Medical alert identification (bracelet/necklace)
Emergency Preparedness
  • Written emergency action plan (AAP template)
  • Minimum 2 adrenaline auto-injectors available
  • Regular practice with trainer devices
  • Check expiration dates quarterly
  • Train caregivers, teachers, coworkers
Risk Factor Management
  • Asthma: Optimize control (major anaphylaxis risk factor)
  • Adolescents: Targeted education on risk-taking behaviors
  • Alcohol: Avoid with allergen exposure (increases risk)
  • Exercise: Avoid after trigger food ingestion (FDEIA)
  • Beta-blockers: May complicate anaphylaxis treatment

  • Support groups: Local and online food allergy communities
  • Apps: Allergy translation cards, ingredient scanners
