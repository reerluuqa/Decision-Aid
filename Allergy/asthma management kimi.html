Asthma Management Algorithm

Based on NICE CKS Guidelines (May 2025) | BTS/NICE/SIGN NG245

Critical: Do not confirm asthma diagnosis without objective testing. Always assess severity and refer urgently when red flags present.

Assessment & Initial Evaluation

Systematic history and examination to suspect asthma and identify red flags

Key History Questions

  • Symptom pattern: Wheeze, cough, breathlessness, chest tightness - variable over time & intensity
  • Triggers: Exercise, allergens (dust, pollen, pets), viral infections, cold air, emotions, drugs (NSAIDs/β-blockers)
  • Risk factors: Personal/family atopy, smoking exposure, prematurity, low birth weight, obesity
  • Occupational history: High-risk jobs (baking, printing, healthcare, animal handling) - symptoms better away from work?
  • Severity indicators: Previous ED visits, hospital admissions, ICU admissions, mechanical ventilation
  • Comorbidities: Allergic rhinitis, sinusitis, GERD, anxiety/depression, obesity

Examination Findings

Normal examination does NOT exclude asthma
  • Vital signs: RR, HR, O₂ saturation, temperature
  • Chest: Expiratory polyphonic wheeze (may be absent), use of accessory muscles
  • Nasal: Signs of allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps
  • Growth: Height/weight (children) - assess for faltering growth

Critical Red Flags

Life-Threatening Features
  • Cyanosis, drowsiness, exhaustion
  • SpO₂ <92% on air
  • PEF <33% predicted
  • Hypotension, bradycardia, arrhythmia
  • 'Silent chest' on auscultation
  • Unable to speak in full sentences

Objective Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis with objective tests based on age group

Age-Specific Diagnostic Pathways

Adults & Young People (>16 years)

Step 1: FeNO & Eosinophils
Diagnose if: FeNO ≥50 ppb OR Blood eosinophils > upper limit of normal
Step 2: Spirometry (if Step 1 negative)
Diagnose if: FEV₁ increase ≥12% AND ≥200ml post-bronchodilator
Step 3: Peak Flow Variability (if spirometry unavailable)
Diagnose if: ≥20% variability over 2 weeks (twice daily readings)
Step 4: Specialist Referral
Bronchial challenge test if still suspected but tests negative

Children (5-16 years)

Step 1: FeNO
Diagnose if: FeNO ≥35 ppb
Step 2: Spirometry (if FeNO <35)
Diagnose if: FEV₁ increase ≥12% post-bronchodilator
Step 3: Peak Flow Variability
Diagnose if: ≥20% variability over 2 weeks
Step 4: Allergy Testing
Skin prick test for house dust mite OR total IgE + eosinophils >0.5 x10⁹/L

Children Under 5 Years

Clinical diagnosis: Trial of low-dose ICS for 8-12 weeks. Response confirms diagnosis. Re-assess at age 5 with objective tests.

Differential Diagnosis

Consider alternatives when:
  • Persistent productive cough with purulent sputum → Bronchiectasis
  • Onset >35 years + smoking history → COPD
  • Prominent systemic features → TB, lung cancer
  • Dry cough + crackles → Interstitial lung disease
  • Paroxysmal cough with vomiting → Pertussis
  • Inspiratory stridor → Foreign body, laryngeal obstruction
  • Orthopnea + edema → Heart failure
  • Dizziness + tingling → Panic attack/breathing pattern disorder

Severity Indicators

Urgent Referral: Life-threatening features, diagnostic uncertainty Caution: Atypical symptoms, poor response to treatment Stable: Classic symptoms + positive objective tests

Treatment Strategy

Age-appropriate pharmacological management & inhaler technique

Initial Treatment by Age Group

Adults & Young People (≥12 years)

First-line: Anti-Inflammatory Reliever (AIR) Therapy
  • Low-dose ICS/formoterol as needed for symptom relief
  • If using ≥3 times/week → Switch to Maintenance & Reliever Therapy (MART)
  • Continue MART with low-dose ICS/formoterol (maintenance + as-needed reliever)
  • If uncontrolled: Moderate-dose MART
  • Still uncontrolled? Add LTRA or LAMA for 8-12 week trial
  • Persistent symptoms → Refer to asthma specialist

Children (5-11 years)

First-line: Paediatric Low-dose ICS
  • Twice-daily paediatric low-dose ICS + SABA as needed
  • If child can manage MART: Consider paediatric low-dose MART (off-label)
  • If uncontrolled: Add LTRA trial (8-12 weeks)
  • Still uncontrolled: ICS/LABA combination + SABA
  • Next step: Moderate-dose ICS/LABA
  • Persistent symptoms → Paediatric specialist referral

Children Under 5 Years

Trial of Treatment Approach
  • 8-12 week trial of paediatric low-dose ICS twice daily
  • Response confirms diagnosis: Continue treatment & review
  • No response: Check technique, adherence, environment, reconsider diagnosis
  • Uncontrolled: Step up to moderate-dose ICS
  • Add LTRA if still uncontrolled
  • Refer to paediatric specialist if no improvement

Inhaler Technique & Devices

Critical Management Points:
  • NEVER prescribe SABA without concomitant ICS
  • Check technique at EVERY consultation
  • Prescribe spacer with pMDI (especially children)
  • Consider environmental impact - prefer DPIs when suitable
  • Ensure dose counters on inhalers
  • Provide written personalised asthma action plan

Personalised Asthma Action Plan (PAAP)

  • Green Zone (Well controlled): Continue maintenance therapy, no symptoms, normal activities
  • Yellow Zone (Worsening): Increase reliever (AIR/MART), consider quadrupling ICS dose (adults), start oral prednisolone if needed
  • Red Zone (Emergency): Call 999 if reliever needed every 1-3 minutes or symptoms not improving after 6-10 puffs

Follow-up & Monitoring

Regular review, stepping therapy, and referral criteria

Review Schedule

  • Minimum: Annual review (more frequent if uncontrolled)
  • Post-exacerbation: Within 2 working days of discharge
  • Pregnancy: Early pregnancy & post-partum review
  • Children: Every 3-6 months, monitor growth (height/weight)

Review Components

1. Inhaler Technique & Adherence
Observe technique, check prescription records, ask about barriers
2. Asthma Control Assessment
Symptoms (day/night), reliever use (>2 inhalers/year = poor control), exacerbations, time off work/school
3. Objective Measures
FeNO monitoring (if available), spirometry/PEF, consider before/after treatment changes
4. Risk Factors & Comorbidities
Smoking cessation, weight management, rhinitis/sinusitis, GERD, anxiety/depression
5. Action Plan & Education
Review/update PAAP, provide educational materials, discuss triggers

Stepping Therapy

Stepping Up (if uncontrolled for 8-12 weeks)
  • Address adherence, technique, triggers first
  • Increase maintenance ICS dose OR add LTRA/LAMA
  • Consider MART regimen if not already on it
  • Refer to specialist if uncontrolled on moderate-dose MART + LTRA/LAMA
Stepping Down (if well controlled for 8-12 weeks)
  • Discuss risks/benefits with patient
  • Reduce dose gradually (e.g., 25-50% every 8-12 weeks)
  • Monitor symptoms closely
  • Aim for lowest dose maintaining control

Specialist Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral (Red Flags)
  • Life-threatening or near-fatal asthma
  • Hospital admission/ED attendance despite treatment
  • Using ≥3 SABA inhalers/year
  • Occupational asthma (refer within 6 weeks)
Routine Referral
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Persistent symptoms despite optimized therapy for 3-6 months
  • Poor control on high-dose ICS
  • Asthma + COPD overlap syndrome
  • Suspected severe/difficult asthma
  • Children <5 years not responding to treatment
  • Food allergy/anaphylaxis + asthma (children)
  • Significant treatment side effects (e.g., faltering growth)

Asthma Control Status

Poor Control: ≥3 SABA/week, night symptoms, ≥2 OCS courses/year, time off work/school Partial Control: Some symptoms, occasional reliever use, 1 exacerbation/year Good Control: No day/night symptoms, no exacerbations, normal activity, PEF >80% predicted
