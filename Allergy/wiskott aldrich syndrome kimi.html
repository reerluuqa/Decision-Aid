Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome

Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Clinical evaluation and initial presentation assessment

Key Presentation Features

Thrombocytopenia is universal at birth. Eczema and immunodeficiency develop variably over time.

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Family history of WAS Common

    Diagnosis may be suspected if there is a family history of thrombocytopenia, bleeding problems, or male infant deaths.

  • Easy bruising and petechiae Common

    Extent depends on degree of thrombocytopenia.

  • Recurrent infections Common

    Most often ear and respiratory infections.

  • Eczema Common

    Variable severity. Often generalized if moderate or severe.

Other Diagnostic Factors

  • Bruises and petechiae - Petechiae or bleeding often seen at sites of excoriation
  • Lymphadenopathy - May be present, particularly if skin is inflamed
  • Perforated tympanic membranes - Associated with past and current infections
  • Serious bleeding Uncommon - Can be spontaneous or post-traumatic
  • Serious/life-threatening infection Uncommon
  • Autoimmunity Uncommon - Affects 26-72% of individuals at some point

Risk Factors

  • Maternal WAS gene mutation carrier Strong

    WAS is inherited in an X-linked manner.

  • Male sex Strong

    Primarily affects males due to X-linked inheritance.

Diagnosis

Investigations and diagnostic criteria

Definitive Diagnosis

WAS protein (WASp) gene mutation analysis is the definitive test for diagnosis.

Initial Diagnostic Investigations

FBC with platelet volume Essential

Thrombocytopenia with small platelet volume is universal unless splenectomy has been performed.

WAS protein (WASp) analysis Specialized

Performed by Western blot, flow cytometry, or fluorescence-activated cell sorter. Available at selected centers.

WAS protein (WASp) gene mutation analysis Definitive

Sequence analysis for mutation in the WASp gene detects mutations in ~99% of affected males.

Pre-treatment baseline tests

Liver function tests, Hepatitis C PCR, EBV/CMV PCR, and serum storage for future analysis.

Subsequent Investigations

  • Immunoglobulin levels - Extremely variable: often normal IgG; raised IgA; low IgM in older patients
  • Vaccine responses - May have normal or low responses to protein antigens, more commonly low antibodies to polysaccharide antigens
  • Isohaemagglutinins - Commonly low
  • Lymphocyte subsets - Classically low CD8 T-cell levels
  • T-cell proliferation assays - Classically low anti-CD3 stimulation

Differential Diagnoses

Idiopathic Thrombocytopenia (ITP)

Differentiating features: Unlike WAS, ITP is not present from birth and is not familial. Platelet size is below normal range in WAS.

Test: Evidence of autoimmune basis is not a discriminator, as this can be present in WAS too.

Autoimmune Lymphoproliferative Syndrome (ALPS)

Differentiating features: ALPS is usually associated with more striking generalized lymphadenopathy than seen in WAS.

Test: Double negative T-cells are raised in ALPS and normal in WAS.

Clinical Classification

Severe WAS

Severe clinical course with significant morbidity and mortality without curative bone marrow transplantation.

Genetics: Mutations resulting in absent protein product.

High Risk
Attenuated WAS (X-linked thrombocytopenia)

Milder clinical course with good prognosis.

Genetics: Mutations preserving reduced protein product.

Better Outlook

Treatment

Management pathways and therapeutic interventions

Treatment Approach

Management differs significantly between attenuated and severe WAS. HSCT is curative for severe disease.

Treatment Pathways

Attenuated WAS

Observation and symptom-based management. No treatment required if asymptomatic.

Key interventions: Prophylactic antibiotics, immunoglobulin if needed, eczema management, bleeding control.

Conservative
Severe Classical WAS

Optimal prophylactic treatment until curative HSCT is available.

Key interventions: Prophylactic antibiotics, immunoglobulin, HSCT when donor available.

Curative Intent

Specific Management Areas

Infection Prophylaxis Essential

Prophylactic Antibiotics

First-line: Trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole 2.5 mg/kg twice daily

Alternative: Azithromycin 10 mg/kg once daily for 3 days every 14 days

Immunoglobulin Replacement

Indicated for frequent/severe infections or abnormal vaccine responses.

Subcutaneous administration is well-tolerated and facilitates home care.

Eczema Management Symptom Control

Step-wise approach similar to atopic eczema, but often requires rapid progression to stronger treatments.

  1. Emollients - First-line with bath emollients and soap substitutes
  2. Topical corticosteroids - Mild to moderate strength depending on severity
  3. Topical tacrolimus - Corticosteroid-sparing agent for children >2 years
  4. Systemic corticosteroids - Prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day for severe cases

Dermatology Referral

Early referral recommended as WAS-related eczema often responds suboptimally to standard treatment.

Bleeding and Bruising Management Critical

Goal: Prevention of serious bleeding episodes (intracranial, gastrointestinal).

Acute Bleeding

Aminocaproic acid: 100-200 mg/kg loading dose, then 100 mg/kg every 4-6 hours

Platelet transfusion: First-line treatment, check increment

Refractory Bleeding

Combination therapy:

  • Systemic corticosteroids (2 mg/kg/day)
  • High-dose immunoglobulin
  • Rituximab (anti-CD20)

Urgent Assessment Required

Mucosal bleeding (mouth/rectal) or significant trauma, especially head injury, requires urgent evaluation.

Autoimmunity Management Reclassifies to Severe

Development of autoimmunity automatically classifies patient as severe WAS - consider HSCT.

  • Autoimmune cytopenias: Rituximab is generally effective
  • Vasculitis/Colitis: Multidisciplinary approach required
  • All autoimmunity: Consultant input essential, HSCT assessment indicated

Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation Curative

HSCT should be performed where a matched sibling or matched unrelated donor is available.

Donor Selection

Preferred: Matched sibling or matched unrelated donor

Survival rates: >90% overall survival

Avoid: Haploidentical transplant (historically poor outcomes)

Pre-HSCT Considerations

Complete infection screening (EBV, CMV, adenovirus PCR)

Document pre-immunoglobulin antibody levels

Continue prophylaxis until successful engraftment

Emerging Therapies

  • Gene Therapy: Lentiviral hematopoietic stem cell gene therapy in clinical trials
  • Thrombopoietin receptor agonists: Eltrombopag may increase platelet counts in some patients

Available at Specialist Centers

Gene therapy is an alternative for patients lacking suitable bone marrow donors. Consult specialized centers for trial availability.

Follow-up

Monitoring schedule and long-term prognosis

Monitoring Frequency

Asymptomatic attenuated WAS: Annual follow-up. Severe/symptomatic: Tailor to clinical need.

Monitoring Schedule

Attenuated WAS (Asymptomatic)
  • Annual clinical monitoring
  • Check infection, eczema, bleeding history
  • Annual investigations:
    • Complete blood count
    • Immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgA, IgM)
    • Lymphocyte subsets
    • Proliferative responses
Annual
Severe or Symptomatic WAS
  • Tailor regularity to clinical need
  • Monitor infections, eczema, bleeding episodes
  • Check for autoimmunity or suspicious lymphadenopathy
  • Annual laboratory investigations (as above)
  • Additional tests as clinically indicated
Variable

Complications

Autoimmunity High Risk

Affects 26-72% long-term. Wide variety of autoimmune syndromes. Development classifies as severe WAS.

Eczema High Risk

Variable severity. Usually worse in severe WAS. Early dermatology input recommended.

Bleeding Medium Risk

Intracranial and GI bleeding are main manifestations. Risk increases with autoimmune thrombocytopenia.

Infection Medium Risk

Bacterial, fungal, viral, and opportunistic infections. Splenectomy increases sepsis risk significantly.

Malignancy Medium Risk

Lymphomas and myelodysplastic syndromes complicate 10-20% of cases. Often EBV-driven.

Prognosis

Attenuated WAS

Long-term outlook is believed to be good. Complications include risk of severe bleeding and autoimmunity.

Favorable

Requires ongoing monitoring for transformation to severe phenotype.

Severe WAS

Survival into third decade unlikely without transplant. Major causes of mortality: severe bleeding, serious infections, autoimmunity.

Poor without HSCT

HSCT Outcomes

Excellent Survival Rates

Five-year survival rates approach 90% for matched sibling donors and fully matched unrelated donors. Significantly improved over past decade.

Matched Sibling Donor

~90% overall survival

Preferred

Matched Unrelated Donor

~90% overall survival

Excellent

Mismatched Family Donor

Historically 50-55% survival, improving

Avoid if possible

Haploidentical

Generally avoided due to poor outcomes

Not Recommended

Patient Education & Red Flags

When to Seek Urgent Care

  • Fever without focus: Seek prompt medical attention (especially severe WAS)
  • Mucosal bleeding: Mouth or rectal bleeding requires urgent assessment
  • Significant trauma: Especially head injury - always assess after trauma
  • Clinical deterioration: Early follow-up if any aspect worsens

Lifestyle Modifications

Patients with thrombocytopenia should avoid contact sports. Appropriate malaria prophylaxis required if traveling.
