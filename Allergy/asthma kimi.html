Assessment

Comprehensive evaluation of suspected asthma

‚ñº

ü©∫ Clinical History

Key Symptoms: Wheeze, noisy breathing, cough, breathlessness, chest tightness

  • Episodic and variable pattern (worse at night, early morning, seasonal)
  • Triggers: exercise, viral infections, allergens, temperature changes, NSAIDs, beta-blockers
  • Impact on daily activities, school/work attendance
  • Previous diagnoses or hospital admissions

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors & Triggers

Atopy: Personal/family history of asthma, eczema, allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps
  • Comorbid rhinitis/sinusitis (10-40% have asthma)
  • Food allergy (4x increased risk in children)
  • Smoking (active/passive including e-cigarettes)
  • Environmental: pollution, moulds, occupational exposure
  • Obesity (difficult to control)
  • Viral respiratory tract infections
Occupational asthma: 1 in 6 adult cases

üëÅÔ∏è Examination

  • Vital signs: Temperature, SpO‚ÇÇ, RR, HR, accessory muscle use
  • Chest: Expiratory polyphonic wheeze (may be absent)
  • Nose: Allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps
  • Growth: Height, weight, BMI (children)
  • Alert: Normal exam does NOT exclude asthma

üß™ Objective Tests

Adults & Young People (‚â•16 years)

  1. Blood eosinophils or FeNO:
    • Eosinophils > lab range OR FeNO ‚â•50 ppb ‚Üí Diagnose asthma
  2. If normal: Bronchodilator reversibility (BDR)
    • FEV‚ÇÅ increase ‚â•12% AND ‚â•200mL ‚Üí Diagnose asthma
  3. If unavailable: PEF twice daily for 2 weeks
    • Variability ‚â•20% ‚Üí Diagnose asthma
  4. Still uncertain: Specialist referral for bronchial challenge test

Children (5-16 years)

  1. FeNO: FeNO ‚â•35 ppb ‚Üí Diagnose asthma
  2. If normal/unavailable: BDR with spirometry
    • FEV‚ÇÅ increase ‚â•12% ‚Üí Diagnose asthma
  3. If unavailable: PEF twice daily for 2 weeks (variability ‚â•20%)
  4. Still uncertain: Skin prick testing to house dust mite OR total IgE + eosinophils

Children Under 5 years

Diagnosis is challenging - no good reference standards

  • Trial of low-dose ICS twice daily for 8-12 weeks
  • Reassess response and symptoms
  • Arrange objective testing at age 5 years
üîç

Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis and rule out differentials

‚ñº

üéØ Diagnostic Criteria

Asthma is likely if: Suggestive clinical history + supportive objective test
  • Variable respiratory symptoms + variable expiratory airflow limitation
  • Symptoms resolve spontaneously or with medication
  • Code as 'suspected asthma' until diagnosis confirmed

üíº Occupational Asthma

Suspect if:
  • Working age adult with NEW asthma symptoms
  • Reappearance of childhood asthma
  • Deteriorating control
  • Symptoms better away from work/on holiday
  • Employed in high-risk job (baking, healthcare, spray painting, welding, etc.)

üö´ Differential Diagnosis

Adult Considerations

  • COPD: Age >35, >30 pack-years smoking, fixed airflow obstruction
  • Heart failure: Orthopnea, PND, peripheral edema, cardiac history
  • Bronchiectasis: Persistent productive cough, recurrent infections, finger clubbing
  • Lung cancer: Persistent cough, hemoptysis, weight loss, smoking history
  • Pulmonary embolism: Acute onset, pleuritic pain, hemoptysis
  • Vocal cord dysfunction: Recurrent 'exacerbations' without objective evidence

Child Considerations

  • Recurrent viral RTI: Cough <10 days, asymptomatic between infections
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux: Postural symptoms, vomiting, faltering growth
  • Cystic fibrosis: Persistent moist cough, GI symptoms from birth, finger clubbing
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia: Recurrent infections, chronic ear disease, situs inversus
  • Inhaled foreign body: Sudden onset, unilateral wheeze, stridor

‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags for Alternative Diagnosis

  • Prominent systemic features (fever, weight loss, night sweats)
  • Hemoptysis, finger clubbing, cyanosis
  • Focal chest signs or persistent chest X-ray changes
  • Symptoms present from birth or perinatal lung problems
  • Faltering growth (children)
Consider specialist referral

Treatment

Stepwise pharmacological management

‚ñº

üéØ Treatment Principles

  • All patients with asthma require ICS-containing therapy
  • Do NOT prescribe SABA alone (without ICS)
  • Individualize inhaler choice based on technique, preference, environmental impact
  • Provide personalized asthma action plan
  • Address modifiable risk factors

üë• Adults & Young People (‚â•12 years)

Step 1: Initial Treatment

As-needed anti-inflammatory reliever (AIR) therapy
  • Low-dose ICS/formoterol combination inhaler taken as needed for symptom relief
  • Examples: Symbicort, Fostair, DuoResp, Fobumix, Luforbec, Wockair
  • Use before exercise or allergen exposure if needed

Step 2: If Highly Symptomatic or Uncontrolled

Low-dose MART (Maintenance And Reliever Therapy)
  • Same ICS/formoterol inhaler for daily maintenance + as-needed relief
  • Continue regular maintenance dose, increase reliever doses as needed
  • Maximum daily licensed dose must not be exceeded

Step 3: If Still Uncontrolled

Moderate-dose MART

Step 4: Add-on Therapies (if eosinophils/FeNO raised)

  • Add LTRA (montelukast 10mg nocte) for 8-12 weeks
  • OR add LAMA (tiotropium 5mcg daily) for 8-12 weeks
  • If improved but inadequate: continue and add other agent
  • If no improvement: stop and try alternative

Step 5: Specialist Referral

If uncontrolled on moderate-dose MART + LTRA + LAMA

üë∂ Children (5-11 years)

Initial Treatment

Twice-daily paediatric low-dose ICS + SABA as needed
  • Examples: beclometasone 100-200mcg/day, budesonide 200mcg/day

If Uncontrolled: Option 1 - MART (off-label)

Consider paediatric low-dose MART if child can manage
  • Use ICS/formoterol combination inhaler
  • If still uncontrolled: increase to paediatric moderate-dose MART

If Uncontrolled: Option 2 - Conventional

  • Add LTRA (montelukast 5mg nocte) for 8-12 weeks
  • If ineffective: stop LTRA
  • Escalate to low-dose ICS/LABA combination inhaler
  • If still uncontrolled: moderate-dose ICS/LABA

Specialist Referral

If uncontrolled on moderate-dose therapy

üë∂ Children Under 5 years

Trial of Treatment: 8-12 weeks of twice-daily paediatric low-dose ICS
  • Indications: Interval symptoms with atopic disorder OR severe acute episodes
  • If symptoms resolve: consider stopping and review after 3 months
  • If symptoms recur: restart and titrate up to moderate-dose
  • If uncontrolled on moderate-dose: add LTRA or refer to specialist

üìù Personalised Asthma Action Plan

Essential Components:
  • Individual triggers and avoidance strategies
  • Recognition of early exacerbation signs
  • Specific instructions for medication changes
  • When and how to use oral prednisolone
  • When to seek urgent medical attention
  • PEF monitoring (if used, document personal best)

Resources: Asthma and Lung UK (www.asthmaandlung.org.uk), Beat Asthma (www.beatasthma.co.uk)

üí® Inhaler Devices

  • Prescribe spacer with pMDI (especially children)
  • Check technique at EVERY consultation and review
  • Consider alternatives if technique poor after training
  • Avoid mixing pMDI and DPI for same patient
  • Consider environmental impact (DPIs have lower carbon footprint)

üõ°Ô∏è Self-Care Measures

  • Smoking cessation (active & passive)
  • Weight management if overweight/obese
  • Regular physical activity (use reliever before exercise)
  • Manage comorbidities (rhinitis, reflux, anxiety/depression)
  • Ensure up-to-date vaccinations (influenza, pneumococcal)

Follow-up & Acute Exacerbation

Monitoring, stepping therapy & emergency management

‚ñº

üìÖ Routine Follow-up

Frequency: At least annually and after any exacerbation

Key Assessment Points:

  • Symptoms: Day/night symptoms, impact on activities, triggers
  • Control indicators:
    • SABA use: >2 inhalers/year ‚Üí poor control
    • Oral corticosteroids: ‚â•2 courses/year ‚Üí poor control
    • Hospital/ED attendance: ‚â•2 visits/year ‚Üí poor control
  • Technique & adherence: Check at every review
  • Inhaler over-ordering: Suggests poor control or overuse
  • Adverse effects: Faltering growth (children), oral thrush
  • Comorbidities: Rhinitis, reflux, obesity, anxiety/depression
  • Modifiable risk factors: Smoking, occupational exposure
  • Validated questionnaires: ACT, ACQ, C-ACT

üìà Stepping Up Treatment

Consider if: Symptoms remain uncontrolled after addressing risk factors
  • Review response after 8-12 weeks
  • Escalate according to age-specific treatment steps
  • Check adherence and technique before escalating

üìâ Stepping Down Treatment

If well controlled for 8-12 weeks: Consider step-down at annual review
  • Discuss risks and benefits
  • Step down to lowest dose that controls symptoms
  • For ‚â•12 years on low-dose ICS + SABA: consider as-needed AIR therapy
  • Allow 8-12 weeks before further reduction
  • Update action plan
  • Monitor and review regularly

ü§∞ Pregnancy

Key Points:
  • Review in early pregnancy and post-partum
  • Maintain good control - do NOT stop ICS/LABA
  • Continue LTRA/LAMA if needed
  • OCS for acute exacerbations - benefits outweigh risks

üè• Acute Exacerbation Management

Assessment of Severity:

üö® Life-threatening (Any one of):

  • Cyanosis, drowsiness, confusion, exhaustion
  • Poor respiratory effort, 'silent chest'
  • SpO‚ÇÇ <92%, hypotension
  • PEF <33% best/predicted
EMERGENCY TREATMENT + HOSPITAL ADMISSION

‚ö†Ô∏è Acute Severe (Any one of):

Adults: Can't complete sentences, SpO‚ÇÇ <92%, RR >25, HR >110, PEF 33-50%

Children: Too breathless to talk/feed, accessory muscles, SpO‚ÇÇ <92%, PEF 33-50%

HOSPITAL ADMISSION

‚úÖ Moderate:

Talking in sentences, SpO‚ÇÇ ‚â•92%, no severe features

MANAGE IN PRIMARY CARE, REVIEW RESPONSE

Treatment Protocol:

Drug Dose (Adults) Dose (Children) Route
Salbutamol 5mg 2.5mg (2-5y), 5mg (>5y) Nebuliser (oxygen-driven)
Ipratropium 0.5mg 0.25mg (2-12y) Nebuliser (if inadequate response)
Prednisolone 40-50mg 10-40mg (age-based) Oral (IM/IV if unable)

If Managing at Home (Moderate Exacerbation):

  • If on AIR/MART: Continue maintenance, increase reliever doses (max licensed dose)
  • If on low-dose ICS: Consider quadrupling dose for up to 7 days (adults)
  • PMDI + spacer: 1 puff every 60 seconds up to 10 puffs, repeat after 10 minutes if needed
  • Continue OCS until recovery (minimum 5 days adults, 3-5 days children)
  • No tapering if <2 weeks
  • Continue ICS when taking OCS
Ring 999 if needing frequent reliever doses without improvement

üìû Post-Exacerbation Review

Review within 2 working days of discharge
  • Assess symptom resolution
  • Check inhaler technique and adherence
  • Identify and manage triggers
  • Update personalized asthma action plan
  • Consider prescribing rescue OCS course for home

üö® Specialist Referral Criteria

URGENT REFERRAL: Near-fatal asthma, life-threatening exacerbation

Routine Referral:

  • Diagnostic uncertainty (adults or children)
  • Suspected occupational asthma ‚Üí occupational lung disease service (within 6 weeks)
  • Persistent symptoms/exacerbations despite optimized treatment for 3-6 months
  • Severe asthma, difficult-to-treat asthma
  • Asthma + COPD overlap
  • Child <5 unable to perform tests and not responding to treatment
  • Preschool child: hospital admission OR ‚â•2 ED visits in 12 months
  • Child with food allergy/anaphylaxis + asthma
  • Significant adverse effects (faltering growth)
