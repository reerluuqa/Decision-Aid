Allergic Rhinitis

Clinical Quick Reference
Definition & Theory

IgE-mediated inflammatory nasal condition resulting from allergen exposure in a sensitised individual.

Epidemiology
  • Up to 30% adults & 40% children in industrialised countries.
  • 80% develop symptoms before age 20.
  • Boys > Girls (early childhood); shifts after puberty.
  • Peak incidence ages 13-14.
Aetiology & Risk
  • Genetic: Highly heritable. Family history (Atopy).
  • Environmental: Aeroallergens (pollen, dust mite, dander), indoor dampness, urban pollution.
  • Microflora Hypothesis: Western lifestyle/antibiotics altering gut biome.
Key Symptoms
  • Nasal: Pruritus, sneezing, rhinorrhoea, congestion (most bothersome).
  • Ocular: Itching, redness, puffiness, watery discharge.
  • Other: Palate/throat itching, fatigue/irritability.
Physical Signs
  • Swollen turbinates & pale mucosa.
  • Clear nasal secretions.
  • Allergic shiners (infraorbital discoloration).
  • Nasal crease (rubbing).
  • Dennie-Morgan lines.
Classification (ARIA)
Intermittent<4 days/week or <4 wks/year
Persistent≥4 days/week AND ≥4 wks/year
MildNo sleep/work/sport impact
Mod/SevereImpact on sleep/school/work
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Differentiating Features
Non-allergic Rhinitis No IgE. Less itching/sneezing. Onset >20 yrs more likely. Triggers: smoke, odors.
Acute Sinusitis Discoloured mucus, facial pain/pressure, fever. Usually < 2 weeks.
Chronic Sinusitis >12 weeks. Hyposmia/anosmia. Inflammation > Infection.
Infectious Rhinosinusitis Sore throat, myalgia, fever. Viral onset.
Diagnostic Tests

Trial of Therapy (1st Line): Antihistamine or Intranasal Corticosteroid (1-2 weeks).


Specific IgE Testing (If trial fails):

  • Skin Prick: Fast, cost-effective, high sensitivity. Stop antihistamines first.
  • In Vitro (IgE): Use if eczema, dermatographism, or cannot stop meds.
IMMEDIATE ACTIONS REQUIRED
Cerebrospinal Fluid Leak

Sign: Unilateral rhinorrhoea (clear fluid).

ACTION: Refer to ENT/Neurosurgery immediately. Do not treat as simple rhinitis.

Infectious Process

Signs: Fever, facial pain/pressure, discoloured nasal mucus.

ACTION: Evaluate for Acute/Chronic Sinusitis. Treat underlying infection.

Structural Anomaly

Sign: Unilateral nasal congestion (not explained by mucosal swelling).

ACTION: Rule out polyps or deviated septum. ENT Referral.

Medication Warnings
  • Montelukast: Risk of neuropsychiatric events. Use only if other agents fail/intolerant.
  • Decongestants (Ongoing): No evidence to support ongoing use. Rebound congestion risk.
  • 1st Gen Antihistamines: Sedation, paradoxical hyperactivity in children.
Allergen Avoidance (First Step)
  • Pollen: Keep windows closed, AC on recycle, avoid outdoor activities during high counts.
  • Dust Mites: Wash bedding >60°C weekly. Barrier covers. Remove soft toys/carpets. Reduce humidity.
  • Pets: Removal is best. Levels decline slowly (months). Hypoallergenic dogs are a myth.
  • Mould: Fix leaks, extractor fans, clear gutters, remove indoor plants.

Pharmacotherapy Algorithm

Mild / Intermittent

1st Line Options (Monotherapy):

  • Intranasal Corticosteroid (INCS)
    (Preferred if congestion present)
  • Oral Antihistamine
    (2nd Gen Non-sedating, e.g. Cetirizine)
  • Intranasal Antihistamine
    (Fast onset, <15 min)

If no improvement in 5-7 days, switch to alternative monotherapy or combine classes.

Persistent / Mod-Severe

1st Line: Intranasal Corticosteroid (INCS) is most effective.

  • Add Oral Antihistamine if still symptomatic.
  • Add Intranasal Antihistamine if still symptomatic.

Reassess in 5-7 days. Step down dose once controlled.

Advanced Options

Immunotherapy (SLIT / SCIT)

For patients with persistent symptoms despite pharmacotherapy. Induces long-term remission. SCIT prevents asthma in children. SLIT is safer but may be less effective.

Oral Corticosteroids

Short course (7 days) only if symptoms are severe and immunotherapy unavailable. Not routine.
