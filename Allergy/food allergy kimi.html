Food Allergy Quick-Scan

Red Flags – Immediate Action
  • Respiratory distress, wheeze, stridor, hoarseness
  • Hypotension, collapse, loss of consciousness
  • Generalised flushing, urticaria, angio-edema
  • Symptoms within minutes of exposure

When to Suspect IgE Food Allergy

FeatureTiming
Skin: itch, urticaria, erythema≤ 2 h
GIT: nausea, colic, vomiting, diarrhoea≤ 2 h
Resp: cough, wheeze, rhinitis≤ 2 h
CV: hypotension, syncope≤ 2 h

Top Triggers

Peanut Tree nuts Hen’s egg Cow’s milk Shellfish / Fish Wheat Soy Sesame

Cross-Reactivity Clues

  • Pollen-food syndrome: raw apple, carrot, hazelnut → birch pollen
  • Latex-fruit: banana, avocado, kiwi, chestnut
  • Tree-nut cluster: cashew-pistachio, walnut-pecan
  • Shellfish cluster: shrimp-crab-lobster

First-Line Management

  • Remove allergen / stop eating
  • Oral antihistamine (child <2 y: chlorphenamine; ≥2 y: cetirizine 5–10 mg)
  • Observe ≥1 h for progression
  • Give adrenaline if ANY systemic features

Referral → Allergy Specialist

  • Any systemic / severe reaction
  • Co-existent asthma (even mild)
  • Multiple food allergy suspected
  • Uncertainty in diagnosis
  • Need for challenge or tolerance check

Anaphylaxis Protocol – click to expand
  1. Call 999 – state “anaphylaxis”
  2. Adrenaline IM:
    • Child <6 y: 0.15 mg (EpiPen Jr / Jext 150)
    • ≥6 y: 0.3 mg (EpiPen / Jext 300)
    • Repeat after 5 min if no improvement
  3. Lie flat / elevate legs; loosen tight clothing
  4. Give high-flow O₂ if available
  5. If hypotensive: IV fluid challenge 20 mL/kg crystalloid
  6. Second dose adrenaline & nebulised salbutamol if wheeze persists
  7. Transfer to ED – minimum 6 h observation
