What Is Social Prescribing?

Healthcare approach linking patients with non-medical community support to improve health and wellbeing.

Core Concept: "What matters to you?" - holistic, patient-centered support beyond medication
  • Free and confidential service
  • Link Worker conducts holistic assessment
  • Personalized plan co-created with patient
  • Connects to community activities and services

When NOT to Use

Immediate medical attention required for:
  • Acute psychiatric emergencies
  • Active suicidal ideation with plan
  • Medical emergencies (chest pain, stroke symptoms)
  • Immediate safeguarding concerns

Social prescribing complements medical care - it does not replace emergency treatment.

Quick Referral Decision Tree

Step 1: Assess the Issue

Is the patient's problem primarily medical or social/determinant-based?

Step 2: Check Eligibility

Does patient live in Manchester OR have a Manchester GP?

Step 3: Identify Needs

Loneliness, mental health, practical issues (housing/finances), lifestyle changes, long-term conditions

Step 4: Make Referral

Electronic referral, email, phone, or warm handoff to Link Worker

Step 5: Follow Up

Link Worker provides feedback; GP incorporates into care plan

Referral Best Practices

  • Use warm handoff when possible - improves engagement significantly
  • Ensure accurate patient contact information
  • Schedule first appointment before patient leaves if possible
  • Set expectation: "You'll receive a call within days"
  • Provide consent for feedback loop between Link Worker and GP
  • Document patient's goals and concerns in referral

When Social Prescribing May Not Be Appropriate

Not Suitable For:

  • Patients requiring specialist mental health services
  • Active addiction requiring specialist treatment
  • Complex psychosis requiring psychiatric care
  • Patients not willing to engage with community services

Consider Instead:

  • IAPT services for moderate-severe anxiety/depression
  • Specialist addiction services
  • CMHT for complex mental health needs
  • Social prescribing as ADD-ON to specialist care

How to Discuss Social Prescribing with Patients

‚úÖ Do Say

"We have a service that can give you extra support with [issue]. They can help you find activities or advice to improve how you're feeling. I think it could really benefit you."
  • Frame as additional support, not a dismissal
  • Emphasize "what matters to you" and patient goals
  • Share brief success stories from other patients
  • Normalize it as part of NHS care

‚ùå Don't Say

"I can't do anything more for you medically, so I'll refer you to social prescribing."
  • Don't imply you're "brushing them off"
  • Don't present it as a last resort
  • Don't make it sound optional/less important
  • Don't be ambivalent - show confidence in the service

Engagement Strategies That Work

Warm Handoff

Introduce Link Worker directly in or immediately after consultation. Significantly improves uptake.

Quick Follow-up

Link Worker calls within days. Speed matters - delays reduce engagement.

Success Stories

Share patient testimonials. "Another patient felt isolated, joined art class, mood improved."

Reassure on Voluntary Nature

"Give it a try - if not helpful, we'll look at other options. It's free and confidential."

Team-wide Culture

Receptionists, nurses all encourage. Make Link Worker visible in practice.

Follow Up

"How did it go with the link worker?" Positive reinforcement at next appointment.

Addressing Patient Concerns

Patient Concern How to Respond
"Is this just because you can't help me?" "This is part of NHS care to address all aspects of your health, not just physical."
"Will I still see you for my medical issues?" "Absolutely - this is additional support. You'll still see me for your medical care."
"I don't have time for another appointment." "Link Workers are flexible - they can meet at convenient times and places, even by phone."
"What if it doesn't help?" "Give it a try - many people find it really helpful, but if not, we'll explore other options."
