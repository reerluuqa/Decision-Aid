Hyperhidrosis Disease Severity Scale (HDSS)

  1. 1 — Sweating not noticeable; doesn’t interfere with daily activities.
  2. 2 — Tolerable; sometimes interferes.
  3. 3 — Barely tolerable; frequently interferes.
  4. 4 — Intolerable; always interferes.

Interpretation: 1–2 = mild–moderate; 3–4 = moderate–severe.

Night sweats → think infection (e.g., TB) or malignancy (e.g., Hodgkin’s). Always exclude serious causes in generalized sweating.

Definition & Impact

  • Hyperhidrosis = chronic condition due to overactive eccrine sweat glands.
  • True prevalence unknown (often under‑reported/under‑diagnosed).
  • Significant impact on quality of life, relationships, school/work performance; risk of skin complications and psychosocial distress.
Primary care focus From age ≥12 Consider QOL impact

Classification

By distribution

  • Localized (focal): scalp/face, axillae, palms, soles.
  • Generalized: whole body; usually secondary.

By cause

  • Primary hyperhidrosis: onset childhood/teens; family history in 30–50%; sympathetic overactivity; stops during sleep; may improve with age.
  • Secondary hyperhidrosis:
    • Localized: anxiety; stroke/brain or peripheral nerve lesions; gustatory sweating.
    • Generalized: obesity; diabetes (incl. hypoglycaemia episodes); menopause; infections (TB, HIV, malaria); cancers (Hodgkin’s/lymphoma, carcinoid); endocrine (thyroid, phaeochromocytoma); cardiovascular/respiratory disease; Parkinson’s; drugs (antidepressants incl. SSRIs/SNRIs/TCAs, trazodone, mirtazapine; cholinesterase inhibitors; steroids; opioids; propranolol; others) and alcohol/illicit drugs or withdrawal.

Assessment & Investigations

History

  • Distribution, symmetry, frequency, duration (>6 months?), triggers (heat, stress, caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods), sleep‑onset.
  • Onset age; psychosocial impact; comorbidities; drug/alcohol history; family history.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, palpitations).

Score severity with the HDSS.

Exam & Tests

  • Examine distribution; BMI; complications (intertrigo, maceration, infections).
  • Baseline labs (if secondary cause suspected or non‑specific features): FBC, ESR/CRP, U&Es, LFTs, HbA1c, TFTs.
  • Chest X‑ray — key practice point.
  • As indicated: HIV/TB tests; blood film (malaria risk); 24h urine catecholamines/metanephrines ± 5‑HIAA; consider CXR already noted.
Generalized hyperhidrosis is usually secondary — actively exclude serious causes before symptom‑only treatment.

Diagnostic Criteria — Primary Focal Hyperhidrosis

  • Focal, visible, excessive sweating in ≥1 site (axillae, palms, soles, craniofacial) and duration ≥6 months, no apparent cause, and ≥2 of:
    • Bilateral & relatively symmetrical
    • Interferes with ADLs
    • ≥1 episode/week
    • Onset < 25 years
    • Positive family history
    • Stops during sleep

If duration <6 months or onset ≥25 yrs, primary disease is still possible — use clinical judgement to exclude secondary causes.

One‑Page Management Algorithm

1
Assess & Triage

History, exam, HDSS. Identify focal vs generalized. Red flags: night sweats, weight loss, fever, systemic signs.

2
Investigate if secondary suspected

Baseline labs (FBC, ESR/CRP, U&Es, LFTs, HbA1c, TFTs), Chest X‑ray; targeted tests (HIV/TB, malaria film; 24h catecholamines/metanephrines ± 5‑HIAA) as indicated.

3
Primary focal hyperhidrosis diagnosed

Proceed to lifestyle + topical therapy; manage comorbid anxiety/depression; review at 6 weeks.

4
First‑line (All focal sites): Aluminium chloride 20%

Apply nightly to dry skin → wash off AM; taper to PRN (up to q6w). Manage irritation with emollient/reduced frequency ± 1% hydrocortisone (short course).

5
Insufficient response after 6 weeks?

Consider off‑label oral antimuscarinic in primary care (e.g., oxybutynin 2.5 mg OD → titrate to 5 mg BD if tolerated; check local guidance) or refer to Dermatology.

6
Specialist escalation

Topical glycopyrrolate (craniofacial, off‑label), higher‑strength aluminium salts, oral glycopyrronium/oxybutynin (off‑label), propantheline (licensed for generalized), iontophoresis (palms/soles), botulinum toxin A (axilla licensed), surgery (select cases).

7
Long‑term plan

Maintenance (e.g., iontophoresis every 1–4 wks; repeat Botox q6–9 mo). Reassess triggers; revisit secondary causes if phenotype changes.

Primary Care Management

Lifestyle / Self‑care

  • Loose, natural‑fibre clothing; avoid tight man‑made fabrics.
  • Triggers: heat, stress/anxiety, caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods.
  • Axillae: antiperspirants (not deodorants), underarm pads; prefer white/black clothing to mask marks.
  • Feet: moisture‑wicking socks; change ≥2×/day; absorbent powder; leather/open shoes; alternate pairs daily.
  • Resources: International Hyperhidrosis Society; BAD leaflet; NHS information page.

Topical Aluminium Chloride (20%)

  • Nightly to dry skin (avoid after shaving/depilatories; avoid eyes/mucosa/broken skin); wash off in AM.
  • Then PRN maintenance (as infrequent as every 6 weeks once controlled).
  • Up to ~80% effective in mild–moderate cases.
  • Irritation: reduce frequency, add emollients; short course hydrocortisone 1% up to 2 weeks if needed.
Oral therapy in primary care (off‑label) — consider with local guidance
  • Oxybutynin: start 2.5 mg once daily → titrate to 5 mg twice daily as tolerated. Counsel on anticholinergic AEs (dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention). Modified‑release may improve tolerability.
  • Review efficacy and side‑effects within 4–6 weeks; stop if ineffective or poorly tolerated.

Specialist Options (Dermatology)

Iontophoresis — palms/soles best evidence
  • Immersion or wet‑pad electrode with weak current; may add glycopyrronium to water if needed.
  • Maintenance every 1–4 weeks; recurrence common after stopping.
  • AEs: transient discomfort, erythema, vesicles.
Botulinum toxin A — axillae licensed; other sites off‑label
  • Multiple intradermal injections; effect ~6–9 months.
  • AEs: injection pain, compensatory sweating; transient weakness/fine motor issues (palms).
Topical/Oral agents
  • Higher‑strength aluminium salts; topical glycopyrrolate (craniofacial, off‑label).
  • Oral antimuscarinics: oxybutynin, glycopyrronium (both off‑label for focal). Propantheline is licensed for generalized hyperhidrosis.
Surgery — last resort
  • Localized axillary sweat gland resection/laser for small areas.
  • Endoscopic thoracic sympathectomy (ETS) for severe palmar/axillary/craniofacial cases refractory to all else.
  • Common complication: compensatory hyperhidrosis (e.g., back/legs), often 6–12 months post‑op; others: gustatory sweating, pneumothorax, atelectasis, bleeding.

Follow‑up

  • Review at 6 weeks after starting aluminium chloride; continue long‑term if effective.
  • Address anxiety/depression where present; note some psych meds can worsen sweating.
  • Reassess phenotype; if atypical/systemic signs emerge, re‑investigate for secondary causes.

Key Takeaways

  • Generalized hyperhidrosis is usually secondary → exclude serious causes first.
  • Assessment includes CXR + baseline bloods where secondary suspected.
  • First‑line: lifestyle optimisation + 6‑week trial of 20% aluminium chloride.
  • If unsuccessful: consider off‑label oxybutynin in primary care (per local guidance) or refer to dermatology.
  • Multiple options exist (iontophoresis, Botox, surgery) — set expectations re. maintenance and recurrence.
  • Prognosis: craniofacial/axillary often improve with age; palmar may persist/worsen at puberty.
