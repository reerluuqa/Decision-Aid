Hyperhidrosis Management Algorithm for Primary Care

Definition: A chronic condition of overactive sweat glands that can significantly affect a patient's quality of life, relationships, and work.

1. Initial Assessment: Differentiate Primary vs. Secondary

Primary Focal Hyperhidrosis

  • Distribution: Focal (axillae, palms, soles, craniofacial)
  • Onset: Childhood/Adolescence (<25 years)
  • History: Family history common (30-50%)
  • Pattern: Stops during sleep; improves with age
  • Cause: Idiopathic (overactive thermoregulation)

Secondary Hyperhidrosis

  • Distribution: Generalized (whole body)
  • Onset: Adulthood (>25 years)
  • History: No typical family history
  • Pattern: Occurs during sleep (night sweats)
  • Cause: Underlying condition or medication
RED FLAG: Night sweats warrant urgent investigation to rule out infection or malignancy.

2. Diagnostic & Investigative Pathway

If Secondary Hyperhidrosis is Suspected...

Generalized hyperhidrosis must be investigated to exclude serious underlying causes.

Baseline Investigations:
  • Full blood count (FBC)
  • Urea & Electrolytes (U&Es), LFTs
  • CRP / ESR
  • HbA1c
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs)
  • Chest X-ray

If Primary Focal Hyperhidrosis is Suspected...

No routine investigations are needed if criteria are met.

Diagnostic Criteria:

Focal, visible sweating for ≥ 6 months with no cause AND at least TWO of the following:

  • Bilateral and relatively symmetrical
  • Impairs daily activities
  • At least one episode per week
  • Onset before age 25
  • Positive family history
  • Stops during sleep

3. Management in Primary Care

Step 1: Lifestyle & First-Line Treatment

Self-Care Advice: Advise avoiding triggers, wearing loose natural-fibre clothing, using moisture-wicking socks/insoles, and considering underarm sweat pads.

First-Line Treatment: Topical Aluminium Chloride 20%

  • Instructions: Apply nightly to completely dry skin; wash off in the morning.
  • Efficacy: Up to 80% effective for mild-to-moderate cases.
  • Trial Period: Advise a six-week trial.
  • Side Effects: If skin irritation occurs, reduce frequency and consider a mild topical steroid.

Step 2: Second-Line Treatment (if first-line fails)

Second-Line Treatment: Oral Oxybutynin Off-label

  • Indication: NICE-endorsed option when topical treatments are ineffective.
  • Dosing: Start 2.5mg OD, titrate slowly up to 5mg BD as tolerated.
  • Monitoring: Watch for anticholinergic side effects (e.g., dry mouth, constipation).

4. Review & Referral Pathway

If Treatment is... Next Step
Effective & Tolerated (Topical or Oral) Continue long-term management in primary care.
Ineffective or Not Tolerated (after trials) Refer to a Dermatologist for specialist management.

5. Overview of Specialist Treatments (Secondary Care)

  • Medications: Higher-strength topicals; other oral agents (e.g., glycopyrronium, propantheline).
  • Iontophoresis: Electric current passed through water (mainly for palms/soles).
  • Botulinum Toxin Injections: Licensed for severe axillary hyperhidrosis; effect lasts 6-9 months.
  • Surgical Options: Sweat gland removal/laser. ETS is reserved for extreme cases due to high risk of compensatory sweating.
Key Reassurance Point: Multiple effective treatment options exist, even in severe cases.
