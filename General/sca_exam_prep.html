üìã Exam Overview

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Management domain is worth 43% of marks (4.5/10.5 marks) - ALWAYS leave at least 5 minutes for management!

Exam Format

  • 12 consultations of exactly 12 minutes each
  • 3 minutes reading time between cases
  • 9 video cases, 3 telephone cases
  • Takes place in your own surgery (97%+ of candidates)
  • Professional role players (actors) simulate patients
  • Recorded and marked later by different examiners
  • Standard: newly qualified independent GP

Three Marking Domains

1. Data Gathering (3 marks) - First half of consultation
2. Clinical Management (4.5 marks) - Second half - WEIGHTED MORE
3. Relating to Others (3 marks) - Throughout the consultation
Grading: Clear Pass, Pass, Fail, or Clear Fail for each domain

‚è±Ô∏è Time Management (CRITICAL!)

üö® MOVE TO MANAGEMENT BY 6.5-7 MINUTES MAXIMUM - THIS IS CRUCIAL!
Why This Matters:
  • Management domain = 43% of marks
  • Safety netting and follow-up often get missed if you run out of time
  • The hardest part of the exam is consulting in exactly 12 minutes

Suggested Time Allocation

  • 0-20 seconds: Brief introduction
  • 20 seconds - 4 minutes: Patient's story, impact, ICE (open questions)
  • 4-6.5 minutes: Doctor's medical questions, red flags, clarification
  • 6.5-12 minutes: Shared management, safety netting, follow-up
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Time Trap: Spending too long on data gathering and running out of time for management. Remember: you get MORE marks for management!

üîÑ Roger Neighbor's 3-Part Consultation Structure

1Ô∏è‚É£ Patient's Part (Early Consultation)

  • Get the patient's story in their own words
  • Use open questions extensively
  • Assess impact on their life
  • Explore Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • DON'T interrupt with medical questions yet
  • Sequence: Story ‚Üí Impact ‚Üí ICE

2Ô∏è‚É£ Doctor's Part (Middle Consultation)

  • Ask your medical questions
  • Red flag questions
  • Closed questions for specific details
  • Fill in knowledge gaps
  • Before moving on, ask: "Is there anything else you wanted to tell me?"

3Ô∏è‚É£ Shared Part (Management - by 6.5-7 minutes)

  • Think out loud
  • Discuss options together
  • Address BOTH medical issues AND patient's ICE
  • Safety netting
  • Follow-up plans
  • Summarize the plan
üí° ICE IS NOT JUST ABOUT ASKING THE QUESTION - You get marks for ADDRESSING ICE in your management!

ICE Tips

  • If patient volunteers ICE early, be grateful - don't need to ask again
  • Make explicit links between ICE and your management plan
  • Show you've listened by incorporating their concerns into the plan

‚úÖ Key Consultation Skills

‚úÖ DO

  • Give patients your full attention at the start
  • Use lots of open questions early on
  • Stay with open questions as long as possible
  • Listen actively and show you're listening
  • Think out loud when uncertain
  • Be your natural self
  • Summarize at transitions
  • "Steer" the consultation gently through the three parts
  • Address the patient's main concern in management
  • Verbalize diagnoses and drug names clearly

‚ùå DON'T

  • Type during consultations (especially telephone)
  • Try to be a "perfect" doctor
  • Rush through like a hospital clerking
  • Perform for the examiner
  • Ask checklist questions
  • Guess medication doses - say you'll check
  • Spend too long on introductions (20 seconds is enough)
  • Use more than one monitor

üéØ Roger's Golden Rule

"Shut up and listen" at the start

The closer you understand what it's like to BE the patient, the better your management plan will be.

When Things Go Wrong

  • Think out loud - this solves most problems
  • Share uncertainty with the patient
  • If patient interrupts, it's usually important - listen
  • If stuck, explain the dilemma honestly
  • Don't pretend or try to hide difficulties

Results Cases

  • Don't spend too long on background
  • Give results early and clearly
  • Allow time for emotional processing
  • Ask: "What's going through your mind right now?"
  • Most of consultation is picking up the pieces and forward planning

Safety & Red Flags

  • Always ask red flag questions when relevant
  • Demonstrate safe management options
  • Show you can recognize when patients need urgent care/referral
  • Default: patients can be safely managed in general practice

üìñ Practical Preparation

Before the Exam

  • Record and review consultations weekly ("Get the video out")
  • See patients across the whole curriculum
  • Practice 12-minute appointments
  • Master the IT/camera setup
  • Choose your exam room early and use it regularly
  • Do out-of-hours work (excellent preparation)
  • Review Good Medical Practice guidance
  • Familiarize yourself with all professionals who visit your practice
  • Get honest feedback from your trainer
