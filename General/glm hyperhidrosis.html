Hyperhidrosis Clinical Reference

Comprehensive guide for assessment and management of excessive sweating

Definition Classification Assessment Management Key Points

Definition & Impact

What is Hyperhidrosis?
Chronic condition caused by overactive sweat glands, producing sweating beyond thermoregulatory needs
Prevalence
True prevalence unknown (underreported/underdiagnosed). Estimated 1-3% of population affected
Impact
Significant reduction in quality of life, affects relationships, work performance, and causes psychological distress

Classification

By Distribution
Localized (Focal): Specific areas (scalp, face, axillae, palms, soles)
Generalized: Whole body involvement (usually secondary)
Primary Hyperhidrosis
• Onset in childhood/teens
• Family history in 30–50%
• Improves with age
• Stops during sleep
• Linked to overactive hypothalamic thermoregulation
Secondary Hyperhidrosis
Localized causes: Anxiety, stroke, brain tumour, nerve damage, gustatory sweating

Generalized causes: Obesity, diabetes, menopause, infections (TB, HIV, malaria), cancers (lymphoma, carcinoid), endocrine disorders, cardiovascular/respiratory disease, Parkinson's, medications

Assessment & Investigations

History
• Distribution, onset age, triggers
• Psychosocial impact
• Drug/family history, comorbidities
• Red flags: Night sweats (infection/malignancy), weight loss, palpitations
Examination
• Signs of underlying disease
• Complications (intertrigo, infection)
• HDSS Scoring (1-4 scale)
• Distribution of sweating
Baseline Investigations
Essential: FBC, U&Es, CRP, LFTs, TFTs, HbA1c, Chest X-ray

Consider: HIV/TB tests, 24h urine catecholamines if indicated

Management Algorithm

1
Lifestyle/Self-Care Measures
• Loose, natural-fibre clothing
• Leather/open shoes, moisture-wicking socks/insoles
• Antiperspirants, underarm pads
• Avoid triggers (heat, stress, spicy foods)
2
First-Line: Topical Aluminium Chloride (20%)
• Apply nightly to DRY skin, wash off AM
• 80% effective for mild-moderate cases
• 6-week trial recommended
• Irritation: Reduce frequency + mild topical steroids
3
Secondary Primary Care Option
• Oral oxybutynin (off-label but NICE-endorsed)
• Start 2.5 mg OD → titrate to 5 mg BD if tolerated
• Monitor for anticholinergic side effects
4
Review at 6 Weeks
Satisfactory response: Continue long-term
Poor response/intolerance: Refer to dermatology
5
Specialist Treatments (Dermatology)
• Higher strength aluminium chloride (up to 50%)
• Other oral agents (beta-blockers, glycopyrronium, CCBs)
• Iontophoresis (palms/soles)
• Botox injections
• Surgery (sweatland removal, laser, ETS - last resort)

Key Clinical Pearls

Red Flag Recognition
Generalized hyperhidrosis = usually secondary → Must exclude serious causes
Essential Investigation
Always include chest X-ray in baseline investigations
First-Line Management
Lifestyle changes + 6-week trial of topical aluminium chloride
Primary Care Option
Oxybutynin before referral (off-label but NICE-endorsed)
Patient Reassurance
Multiple treatment options exist even in severe cases
Complication Screening
QoL impact, anxiety, depression, bromhidrosis, skin infections
