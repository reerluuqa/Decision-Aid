Red Flags - Urgent Referral Required

General Red Flags

  • Significant unintentional weight loss (5% of body weight over 6â€“12 months)
  • Fever, night sweats
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Persistently elevated inflammatory markers
  • Muscle or joint pain with systemic symptoms
  • Localizing or focal neurological signs

Immediate Actions

Urgent Referral

Consider urgent specialist referral for suspected malignancy, infection, or serious underlying condition.

Priority Investigations

Order urgent blood tests including FBC, ESR/CRP, LFTs, TFTs, and consider CXR if clinically indicated.

Assessment

History
Examination

Key Questions to Ask

  • Onset, severity, frequency, duration, fluctuations
  • Effect of rest, sleep, and activity on symptoms
  • Daytime sleepiness (may indicate sleep apnoea)
  • Muscle weakness (may suggest neuromuscular cause)
  • Post-exertional malaise, sleep difficulties, cognitive problems (possible ME/CFS)
  • Stress, anxiety, depression symptoms
  • Impact on daily life, work, relationships
  • Diet, weight history, smoking, alcohol, drug use
  • Medications (including OTC, supplements, herbal remedies)

Physical Examination

  • General appearance and nutritional status (BMI)
  • Pallor (possible anaemia or malignancy)
  • Lymphadenopathy (possible infection or malignancy)
  • Goitre (possible thyroid disease)
  • Pulse, blood pressure (including postural hypotension)
  • Heart sounds (murmurs)
  • Oedema (possible cardiac or liver disease)
  • Muscle weakness (possible neuromuscular cause)
  • Neurological assessment
  • Mental state examination

Common Causes of Fatigue

Medical Conditions

  • Anaemia (iron, B12, folate deficiency)
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Thyroid disorders (hypo/hyperthyroidism)
  • Sleep disorders (obstructive sleep apnoea)
  • Cardiac disease (heart failure, arrhythmias)
  • Respiratory disease (COPD)
  • Renal disease
  • Chronic infections
  • Malignancy
  • Autoimmune/rheumatological conditions

Special Considerations

ME/CFS

Consider myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome if fatigue is accompanied by post-exertional malaise, unrefreshing sleep, and cognitive difficulties.

Long COVID

Consider long COVID if fatigue follows COVID-19 infection and persists for more than 4 weeks.

Psychological Factors

  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Stress
  • Bereavement
  • Burnout
  • Adjustment reactions
  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder

Social Factors

  • Poor sleep hygiene
  • Shift work
  • Caring responsibilities
  • Social isolation
  • Financial stress
  • Work-related stress

Medications That May Cause Fatigue

  • Benzodiazepines
  • Antidepressants
  • Antihistamines
  • Antihypertensives
  • Antipsychotics
  • Antivirals
  • Beta-blockers
  • Muscle relaxants
  • Opiates
  • Statins
  • Corticosteroids (withdrawal)
  • Diuretics

Physiological Factors

  • Pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding
  • Poor sleep quality
  • Inadequate rest
  • Excessive exercise
  • Poor nutrition

Shift Work

Night shift workers are particularly at risk of fatigue because their day sleep is often lighter, of shorter duration, and more easily disturbed.

Investigation Strategy

First-line investigations should be performed in all patients with fatigue. Second-line tests should be considered based on clinical features and initial results. Specialist tests should be arranged if specific conditions are suspected.

Management Approach

General Management

  • Address identified underlying causes
  • Review and adjust medications if appropriate
  • Provide information about fatigue and self-management strategies
  • Advise on sleep hygiene
  • Discuss balanced diet and hydration
  • Recommend appropriate physical activity
  • Address psychosocial factors

When to Refer

Specialist Referral

  • Suspected serious underlying condition
  • Abnormal investigation results requiring specialist input
  • Suspected ME/CFS (refer to specialist service)
  • Persistent fatigue despite initial management
  • Complex psychosocial factors

Patient Advice

Explain that fatigue is common and often self-limiting. Encourage a gradual return to normal activities, balanced with rest periods. Provide information on pacing activities and energy conservation techniques.

Prognosis

General Prognosis

  • Almost three-quarters of people with fatigue in primary care do not require further follow-up
  • Between a third and a half recover within one year

Factors Associated with Better Recovery

  • Male sex
  • Reduced severity and shorter duration of fatigue
  • No expectation of fatigue becoming chronic
  • Perception of better general health and mental health
  • Lower levels of pain
  • Good social support and no carer responsibilities
  • Willingness to attribute fatigue to psychological factors

Potential Complications

  • Negative impact on quality of life
  • Reduced work performance and productivity
  • Increased risk of accidents
  • Development of anxiety or depression
  • Cognitive impairment

Mortality Risk

A UK prospective cohort study found that after adjusting for confounders, the hazard ratio for all-cause mortality was 1.4 for people who reported highest compared with lowest fatigue levels during a mean follow-up period of 16.6 years. The association was particularly strong for deaths related to cardiovascular disease.
