DA: Primary Care Reference

Definition (DA Act 2021)

Abusive behaviour between individuals aged 16+ who are personally connected (partners/family). Regardless of gender or sexuality.

Includes: Physical, sexual, emotional, psychological, controlling, coercive, economic, online/digital abuse. Also honour-based abuse, forced marriage, stalking.
Why Victims Stay

Understanding barriers helps empathy:

  • Want to end abuse, not relationship.
  • Fear of worse violence/homicide.
  • Worried about losing children (social services).
  • Financial/Emotional dependence.
  • Shame, low self-esteem, cultural barriers.
  • Simply don't recognise it as abuse.

Ask about DA if you see:

The LIVES Framework (BMJ 2021)
RESPONSE PROTOCOL

L - Listen

Listen without judgment. Let them tell their story at their own pace. Create a safe space.

I - Inquire

Ask about their needs and concerns. "What would help you feel safer?"

V - Validate

"I believe you." "It is not your fault." Acknowledge the courage it took to speak.

E - Enhance Safety

Assess immediate risk. Are there children? Vulnerable adults? Safety planning.

S - Support

Offer referrals to specialist agencies. MARAC if high risk. SARC for sexual assault.

‚õî Critical Warning
NEVER advise a victim to leave their partner without support in place. The point of leaving is the most dangerous time (risk of injury or murder spikes).
Immediate Risk
LEVEL 1
  • Doesn't feel safe to go home.
  • Threat of immediate serious harm ("He's going to kill me").
  • Planning to leave.
Actions:
  • Support to call Police 999.
  • Urgent Safeguarding (Children/Adults).
  • Referral to MARAC.
  • Discuss with a colleague.
Possible High Risk
LEVEL 2
  • Severe/escalating abuse.
  • Access to weapons.
  • Strangulation/Suffocation attempts.
  • Stalking/Sexual assault.
  • Pregnancy.
  • Threats to kill children or suicide.
  • Abuse of pets.
Actions:
  • Share 999 Silent Solution.
  • Refer to Local DA Agency.
  • Consider MARAC.
  • Safeguarding referral.
  • Arrange follow-up.
Standard/Low Risk
LEVEL 3

No immediate signs of high risk or danger.

Actions:
  • Discuss type/severity of abuse.
  • Ask what support they want.
  • Check for children/vulnerable adults.
  • Refer to local specialist services for safety planning (locks, legal aid, refuge).

üì± 999 SILENT SOLUTION (Click to reveal)

For patients who can't speak on the phone.

  1. Dial 999.
  2. Listen to the operator (don't hang up).
  3. Respond by coughing or tapping the handset if you can.
  4. If prompted by the automated system, press 55.
  5. This lets the operator know it is a genuine emergency and transfers you to the Police.
üìù Best Practice
  • Code: "History of Domestic Abuse".
  • Words: Document patient's exact words (factual/objective).
  • Visibility: CRITICAL: Hide consultation from online patient access.
  • Present: Record name of anyone present during consult.
  • Perpetrator: Do NOT enter info in perpetrator's record unless they disclosed it. Link records if possible.
üîí Confidentiality & Sharing

Consent is ideal, but you can share without it if:

  • Risk of serious harm/homicide to patient or others.
  • Safeguarding concern for children/vulnerable adults.
  • MARAC referral (consent not required as risk is established).
Warning: Ensure sharing doesn't increase risk. Document reasons for overriding confidentiality.
ü§ñ AI Scribes & Technology

If using Ambient Scribes (e.g., Nuance, Abridge):

  • Extra caution required.
  • Consider turning scribe OFF for sensitive parts of the consult.
  • Manually check/edit the summary to ensure it is factual and doesn't create a dangerous record.
  • Ensure printed summaries (referrals) don't expose sensitive info to the abuser.
üë∂ Impact on Children
SAFEGUARDING

Under the DA Act, children who see, hear, or experience the effects of abuse are victims in their own right.

CAPVA (Child to Parent Violence/Abuse):

  • Adolescent using harmful behaviors to control parents.
  • Onset usually 14-16 years.
  • Parents feel ashamed/afraid to ask for help.

Teen Relationship Abuse:

Often overlooked. Young people may not recognize unhealthy behavior if exposed to abuse at home.

Action: Safeguarding referral required for children displaying harmful behavior.
ü©∫ Clinicians as Victims
WORKPLACE

Health professionals have higher rates of DA than the general population.

Barriers to seeking help:

  • Fear of stigma/judgment/GMC referral.
  • Concerns about professional registration.
  • Time pressure/Lack of private space.
  • Perpetrators may threaten career or disrupt work (e.g., deprive sleep).

How to help a colleague:

  • Speak privately ("You seem under pressure...").
  • Don't investigate/counsel.
  • Signpost to Occupational Health/Domestic Abuse Leads.
