üìù History Taking

  • Timing and exact cause of injury
  • Past medical history: cardiac, respiratory, hepatic disease; diabetes; pregnancy; immunocompromise
  • Predisposing factors: seizures, falls, blackouts, substance misuse
  • Tetanus immunization status
  • Allergies (latex, anesthetics, antibiotics)

‚ö†Ô∏è Complications Assessment

  • Infection signs: increased pain, odor, exudate, fever, erythema
  • Hypotension or inhalation injury (singed hairs, hoarse voice, stridor, wheeze)
  • Other trauma: crush injuries, fractures, head injuries
‚ö†Ô∏è Inhalation Injury Red Flags: Singed eyebrows/nasal hairs, black carbon in sputum, hoarse voice, stridor, wheeze, oropharyngeal carbon

üéØ Burn Severity Assessment

Location & Size: Document exact location and calculate Total Burn Surface Area (TBSA)

üìè TBSA Estimation Methods:

Method Description Notes
Lund & Browder Chart with age-adjusted body regions Most accurate; accounts for age differences in head/legs proportion
Rule of Nines Multiples of 9 for different body areas Adults: Head 9%, Each arm 9%, Each leg 18%, Torso 36%
Rule of Palm Patient's palm ‚âà 1% BSA Best for small/scattered burns

üö® Red Flags for Non-Accidental Injury

IN CHILDREN:
  • Burn on non-mobile child
  • Injury on unusual areas (backs of hands, soles, buttocks, back)
  • Implement-shaped injury (cigarette, iron)
  • Forced immersion signs: glove/stocking distribution, symmetrical, sharp borders
  • Delayed presentation, inconsistent history, unrelated adult presenter
  • Multiple injuries of different ages

Treatment & Management
‚ñº

üöë Immediate First Aid

THERMAL BURNS:
  • Stop burning process (drop & roll, smother flames)
  • Remove non-adherent clothing/jewelry (DO NOT remove tar)
  • Cool with running water 15-30 min within 20 min of injury
  • Keep patient warm to prevent hypothermia
  • Cover with cling film (loosely, not circumferential)
  • Elevate affected area
  • Give analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen ¬± codeine)
ELECTRICAL BURNS:
  • DO NOT approach if high-voltage (>1000V)
  • Turn off power or remove with non-conductive material
  • Arrange immediate A&E admission
CHEMICAL BURNS:
  • Remove contaminated clothing
  • Brush off dry chemical
  • Irrigate copiously with water for ~1 hour
  • DO NOT neutralize (causes heat injury)
  • Arrange immediate A&E admission

üè• Admission & Referral Criteria

üö® IMMEDIATE A&E ADMISSION
  • All complex burns (electrical, chemical, >15% TBSA adult, >10% child)
  • All full-thickness burns
  • Deep dermal burns >5% TBSA (adults) or any deep dermal (children)
  • Circumferential deep dermal burns
  • High-pressure steam injuries
  • Suspected non-accidental injury
  • Burns with sepsis
  • Critical area burns: face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, flexural surfaces
  • Suspected inhalation injury
  • Significant comorbidities or at risk of complications
  • Associated significant trauma
‚ö†Ô∏è SPECIALIST BURNS UNIT REFERRAL
  • Uncertainty about referral need
  • Inadequate pain control or dressing difficulties
  • Not healing within 2 weeks
  • Special social/emotional/rehabilitative needs
‚úÖ PRIMARY CARE MANAGEMENT
  • Non-complex superficial epidermal burns
  • Superficial dermal burns <10% TBSA (not in critical areas)

üè† Managing Non-Complex Burns

Superficial Epidermal Burns (e.g., sunburn):

  • Cool baths/showers or cold compresses
  • Emollient massage (emulsifying ointment)
  • Analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen)
  • Maintain hydration
  • Sun protection (SPF 30-50, clothing)
  • Urgent review if blisters develop

Superficial Dermal Burns:

Management by trained professional (practice/district/tissue viability nurse):
  • Clean wound (lukewarm water or 0.9% saline)
  • Apply non-adherent dressing (paraffin gauze, silicone, hydrocolloid)
  • Secondary absorbent dressing + conforming bandage
  • Review at 48 hours, then every 3-5 days
  • Leave blisters intact; de-roof large (>1cm¬≤) or joint blisters
  • Analgesia before dressing changes

ü¶† Wound Infection Management:

If infection suspected (increased pain, odor, exudate, fever, erythema):
  • Take wound swab
  • Start empirical oral antibiotics
  • Review in 7 days; change antibiotics if no response
  • DO NOT use prophylactic antibiotics

Follow-up & Monitoring
‚ñº

üìÖ Review Schedule

  • 48 hours: Dressing change, infection check
  • Every 3-5 days: Dressing changes until healed
  • 2 weeks: If not healed ‚Üí specialist referral
  • 3-6 months: Scar maturation monitoring

üß¥ Scar Management

After Wound Healing:
  • Daily emollient massage (3-6 months) to reduce hypertrophic scarring
  • Sun protection (SPF 30-50 or clothing) for 1-2 years
  • Monitor for contractures, neuropathic pain, itch

‚ö†Ô∏è When to Escalate Urgently

  • Systemic symptoms: fever, tachycardia, hypotension
  • Signs of toxic shock syndrome (children)
  • Contractures limiting movement
  • Severe pain uncontrolled with analgesia
  • Signs of child maltreatment
