Key Principle

Treatment focuses on presenting symptoms rather than anticipated consequences. Symptomatic and supportive care is the mainstay.

Assessment

Initial evaluation & risk assessment

Critical First Step

Identify the plant if possible. Contact local poison control centers, universities, or use FDA PLANTOX database. Plant identification guides management but treatment is symptom-based.

Historical Factors

  • Amount ingested: Small amounts in children vs large amounts in adults
  • Method: Raw ingestion, brewed tea, concentrated extracts, smoked
  • Timing: Onset of symptoms (most appear within hours)
  • Context: Accidental (children) vs intentional (adults) vs foraging misidentification

Key Diagnostic Factors

Tachycardia

Cardiac stimulants, antimuscarinics, nicotine-like alkaloids

Hypertension

Stimulants, antimuscarinics, nicotine-like alkaloids

Hypotension

Digitalis-like glycosides, internal hemorrhage

Miosis

Muscarinic effects, opiates

Mydriasis

Antimuscarinics, sympathomimetics

Bradycardia

Digitalis-like glycosides, muscarinics, vagal overstimulation

Neurological Signs

Seizures

Water hemlock, strychnine, nicotine, thujone, camphor

Hallucinations

Jimson weed, morning glory, peyote, nutmeg

Neuromuscular weakness

Curare alkaloids, grayanotoxin, karwinskia toxins

Fever

Antimuscarinic, nicotine-like alkaloids

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Cardiotoxicity: Any dysrhythmia, heart block, hypotension
  • Neurotoxicity: Status epilepticus, altered mental status
  • Hepatotoxicity: Jaundice, coagulopathy, acidosis
  • Hemorrhage: Visible or internal bleeding
  • Respiratory: Pulmonary edema, respiratory depression

Pediatric Considerations

Children typically ingest small amounts of known household plants. Most lethal plants are uncommon in homes. Poinsettia is minimally toxic despite common concerns.

Diagnosis

Investigations & differential diagnosis

1st Line Investigations

ECG

All patients - detect dysrhythmias, heart block, QRS/QT changes

FBC

Bone marrow suppression, hemorrhage

Electrolytes

Dehydration, renal function, acidosis

LFTs & INR

Hepatotoxicity, coagulopathy

Specific Tests

  • ABG: Acidosis from cyanogenic plants or post-seizure
  • Troponin: Prognostic for colchicine-induced cardiac failure
  • Serum digoxin level: Not reliable for plant cardiac glycosides (poor cross-reactivity)
  • Chest X-ray: Non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema (muscarinic plants)
  • Physostigmine test: Diagnostic aid for antimuscarinic toxicity
  • Glucose: Hypoglycemia from ackee fruit

Plant Classification & Toxicity

Cardiotoxic

Plants: Oleander, foxglove, lily of the valley, yew, monkshood
Toxins: Cardiac glycosides, aconitine, taxine

Hepatotoxic

Plants: Ragwort, kava, green tea extract, pennyroyal
Toxins: Pyrrolizidine alkaloids, atractyloside

Neurotoxic

Plants: Water hemlock, strychnine, Datura, nicotine
Toxins: Cicutoxin, strychnine alkaloids, belladonna

Gastrotoxic

Plants: Pokeweed, holly berries, mistletoe, ipecac
Effects: Severe vomiting, diarrhea, cramping

Myelotoxic

Plants: Autumn crocus, periwinkle, mayapple, castor bean
Toxins: Colchicine, vinca alkaloids, podophyllum

Dermatotoxic

Plants: Nettles, chili peppers, philodendron, ginkgo
Effects: Irritation, contact dermatitis, photosensitivity

Differential Diagnoses

  • Viral gastroenteritis: Fever, constitutional symptoms, self-limiting
  • Bacterial gastroenteritis: Spoiled food ingestion, positive cultures
  • Acute MI: Cardiac history, ischemic ECG changes, elevated troponin
  • Epilepsy: Known seizure history, EEG abnormalities
  • Cyanide poisoning: Occupational exposure, cherry-red lips
  • Paracetamol poisoning: Overdose history, liver injury pattern

Treatment

Management protocols & antidotes

Universal Principle

Symptomatic and supportive care is the mainstay. Very few plant exposures require specific antidotes. DO NOT give activated charcoal routinely - most plants cause vomiting and binding is unproven.

Treatment Algorithm Overview

1
Observation & Supportive Care

All patients require observation for several hours. Efforts to identify plant.

Minimum 4-6 hours
2
System-Specific Management

Treat based on presenting symptoms and toxicity pattern

3
Antidotes (Rarely Needed)

Digoxin Fab (cardiac glycosides), Cyanide antidotes, Physostigmine (antimuscarinic)

GI Symptom Management

Anti-emetics & GI Protection

  • Prochlorperazine: 10 mg PO/IV q6h (adults) - Avoid in seizure/conduction delay plants
  • Ondansetron: 0.15 mg/kg IV q8h (children & adults)
  • IV Fluids: Rehydration for vomiting/diarrhea
  • Antacids/H2 Blockers: Famotidine 20-40 mg BID
Most resolve within 24h

Cardiotoxicity Management

Bradycardia/Hypotension

Atropine: 0.5 mg IV q3-5min (max 3mg)
Digoxin Fab: Empiric 5-30 vials for cardiac glycosides

Tachycardia/Hypertension

Benzodiazepines: Lorazepam 2mg IV
Reduce catecholamine outflow

Aconitine Arrhythmia

Magnesium: 1-2g IV, then 3-20 mg/min
Amiodarone: First choice for VT (unless prolonged QT)

Seizure Management

First-Line Anticonvulsants

  • Benzodiazepines: Lorazepam 2mg IV q1-2h PRN
  • Phenobarbital: 15-20 mg/kg IV for refractory seizures
  • Propofol: 5 mcg/kg/min titrated to EEG burst suppression
  • GABA agents: Synergistic with benzodiazepines
Most plant seizures are multiple/refractory

Specific Antidotes

Digoxin Immune Fab

Indication: Cardiac glycoside plants (oleander, foxglove)
Dose: 5-30 vials based on severity
Endpoint: Resolution of dysrhythmia, hypotension

Cyanide Antidotes

Indication: Cassava, Prunus seeds (chewed)
Hydroxocobalamin: 5g IV (CyanoKit)
OR Sodium nitrite + thiosulfate

Physostigmine

Indication: Severe antimuscarinic toxicity (delirium)
Diagnostic & therapeutic use
Check ECG first - risk of arrhythmias

N-Acetylcysteine

Indication: Hepatotoxic plants (pyrrolizidines, pennyroyal)
Dose: 150 mg/kg IV load, then 70 mg/kg q4h
Continue until liver failure resolves

Hematological Toxicity

Management

  • Stop exposure: Most important intervention
  • Transfusions: PRBC, platelets, FFP as needed
  • Vitamin K: For coumarin-induced coagulopathy (plant-derived)
  • Growth factors: Erythropoietin, G-CSF as adjuncts
  • Monitor for infection: Neutropenia causes immunocompromise
Bone marrow failure may be delayed 5-7 days (autumn crocus)

Dermatological Toxicity

Decontamination & Treatment

  • Soap and water: Immediate decontamination
  • Topical emollients: Moisturizers for irritation
  • Topical steroids: Hydrocortisone 1-2.5% TID-QID
  • Analgesics: For pain from oxalate crystals, capsaicin

Contraindicated Treatments

  • Activated charcoal: Rarely beneficial, aspiration risk
  • Gastric lavage: Not recommended routinely
  • Ipecac: Do NOT induce vomiting
  • Phenothiazines: Avoid in seizure/conduction delay plants
  • Physostigmine: Contraindicated in TCA co-ingestion

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications & prognosis

Monitoring Requirements

  • Observation period: Minimum 4-6 hours for asymptomatic patients
  • Symptom resolution: All symptoms must resolve before discharge
  • Liver function: Monitor LFTs until normalization (hepatotoxic plants)
  • Cardiac monitoring: Until ECG normalizes (cardiotoxic plants)
  • Delayed effects: Bone marrow suppression 5-7 days (autumn crocus)

Complications

Cardiac arrest

Cause: Yew, monkshood, oleander, foxglove
Prevention: Early recognition, antidotes, supportive care

Liver failure

Cause: Pyrrolizidine alkaloids, kava, green tea extract
Action: N-acetylcysteine, transplant referral

Respiratory failure

Cause: Nicotine-like alkaloids, pulmonary edema
Support: Mechanical ventilation if needed

Severe hemorrhage

Cause: Coumarin-containing plants
Treatment: Vitamin K, transfusions

Myelosuppression

Cause: Colchicine, periwinkle, castor bean
Action: Stop exposure, transfusions, growth factors

Contact dermatitis

Cause: Nettles, oxalate crystals, furocoumarins
Treatment: Topical steroids, emollients

Prognosis

Overall Outcomes

  • Majority: Clinically inconsequential with complete resolution
  • US Data: 62 plant-related fatalities 1983-2014 (rare)
  • Thailand Data: 0.9% mortality (2901 cases, 2001-2010)
  • Most lethal: Water hemlock, Datura, monkshood, autumn crocus, oleander (rare exposures)
  • Good prognosis: With aggressive supportive care and antidotes when indicated

Key Prognostic Factors

  • Early recognition: Prompt identification of toxidrome improves outcomes
  • Supportive care quality: ICU monitoring for severe cases
  • Antidote availability: Digoxin Fab, cyanide kits, physostigmine
  • Co-ingestions: Multiple substances increase complexity
  • Underlying health: Cardiac, hepatic disease worsens prognosis

Patient Education & Prevention

Primary Prevention

  • Avoid foraging: Unless expert in plant identification
  • Houseplants: Avoid toxic plants with small children/pets
  • Safe preparation: Pokeweed leaves safe after parboiling
  • Herbal supplements: Caution with unregulated products
  • Storage: Keep concentrated extracts/teas secure

Patient Discussion Points

  • Reassurance: Most exposures cause mild/no symptoms
  • Warning signs: When to seek urgent care
  • Follow-up: Importance of delayed symptom monitoring
  • Prevention: Strategies to avoid future exposures
  • Resources: webPOISONCONTROL for prevention info

Documentation Requirements

  • Plant identification: Photographs, samples, description
  • Exposure details: Amount, time, preparation method
  • Symptom timeline: Onset, progression, interventions
  • Investigations: ECG, labs, imaging results
  • Treatment: Medications, antidotes, responses
  • Consultations: Poison control, toxicology, specialists
