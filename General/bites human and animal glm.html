Bites: Human & Animal

Revised: Aug 2024

  • Stop active bleeding: Apply direct pressure.
  • Airway compromise: Manage immediately if neck/facial bite affects airway.
  • Sepsis signs: Systemic sepsis requires urgent IV antibiotics (hospital).
  • Structural damage: Fractures, tendon rupture, or joint penetration need specialist care.
  • Child Maltreatment: Suspect if bite mark unlikely from a child. Refer per local safeguarding policy.

Clinical Goals

  • Immediate first aid & wound cleaning.
  • Reduce risk of infection (prophylaxis).
  • Treat established infection.
  • Prevent Tetanus & Rabies.
  • Manage Hepatitis/HIV risk.

Infection Risk Factors

Higher risk if:

  • Deep/puncture/crush wounds.
  • Location: Hands, feet, face, genitals.
  • Presentation > 8 hours post-bite.
  • Comorbidities: Diabetes, Asplenia, Immunosuppression.
  • Prosthetic joints or valves.

General Assessment

  • Document: Who, when, and circumstances.
  • Examine: Size, depth, foreign bodies (teeth?), nerve/tendon function.
  • Vitals: Monitor if sepsis suspected.
  • Fist Bites: Examine hand through range of motion; check for tendon extension.

Human Bite Specifics

Occlusal Bites

Actual biting trauma. Common on breasts/genitals in women, arms/shoulders in men.

Clenched-Fist ("Fight Bites")

High infection risk! Wound over MCP joint. Often damages tendon sheath/metacarpal head.

Blood-Borne Virus Risk

Risk is LOW but requires assessment.

  • Source: Assess status of biter if known.
  • Hep B: Check vaccination/antibody status. Post-exposure prophylaxis may be needed.
  • Hep C / HIV: Risk < 1 in 10,000 (if skin broken). Test baseline. Offer PEP if high risk.
  • No Risk: If skin not broken.

Wound Management

  • Cleaning: Irrigate thoroughly with saline or water.
  • Closure: Generally do not close human bites primarily (high infection risk). Secondary closure or healing by secondary intention.
  • Face: Facial cosmetic concerns may require specialist advice for closure.

Rabies Risk Assessment

UK Context

Indigenous rabies absent in terrestrial animals. Bats are a risk anywhere in the UK.

Imported Animals

Consider rabies if bitten by quarantined animals or recently imported dogs/cats (especially PETS scheme).

Abroad

Risk significant in endemic countries. Seek urgent specialist advice (e.g. NaTHNaC) for any animal bite abroad.

Dog Bites

  • Mechanism: Crushing/tearing. High force.
  • Infection Risk: Lower than cats (5-25%), but high for crush injuries.
  • Common Pathogens: Staph aureus, Pasteurella canis, Capnocytophaga canimorsus.
  • Note: Capnocytophaga can cause severe sepsis in asplenic/immunocompromised/elderly.

Cat Bites

  • Mechanism: Deep, narrow puncture wounds. Inoculate bacteria deep into tissue/bone/joints.
  • Infection Risk: Very High (30-50%).
  • Key Pathogen: Pasteurella multocida (fast-spreading).
  • Action: Almost ALL cat bites warrant antibiotic prophylaxis.

Antibiotic Prophylaxis (First Choice)

Patient Group Drug Dose Duration
Adults & Children (12y+) Co-amoxiclav 500/125 mg TDS 3 days (Cat), 5-7 days (High risk)
Children (1mo - 11y) Co-amoxiclav Weight based (refer BNFC/Clinical Knowledge) 3-7 days

Penicillin Allergy / Alternatives

  • Adults (12y+): Doxycycline 100mg OD OR Metronidazole 400mg TDS + Clarithromycin 500mg BD (if Doxy unsuitable).
  • Children (<12y): Co-trimoxazole (Off-label) OR Metronidazole + Clarithromycin.
  • Cat Bite Specifics: If using alternatives for cat bites, ensure cover for Pasteurella (Doxycycline good; Co-trimoxazole variable).

Tetanus Prophylaxis

Assess if wound is tetanus-prone (puncture, soil/manure, devitalized tissue).

  • Fully Immunized (5 doses): None usually needed.
  • Primary Course incomplete/None: Give Tetanus vaccine +/- Immunoglobulin depending on wound status.
