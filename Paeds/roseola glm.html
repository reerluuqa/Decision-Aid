Roseola (HHV-6/7)

Pediatric Updated: Oct 2022
  • Clinical
  • Dx
  • Rx
  • Diffs
RED FLAG: Febrile Seizures occur in 10-15% of infants. Encephalitis is rare but possible. Monitor CNS status closely.
Clinical Snapshot

Definition

Common febrile viral illness of early childhood.

HHV-6B (primary), rarely HHV-7.

Typical Age

6 – 24 months

Rare before 3 mo or after 4 yrs.

Fever Pattern

High (>39.5°C)

Sudden onset. Lasts 3–7 days. Peaks in evening.

Rash Onset

With Defervescence

Discrete pink-red macules/papules. Trunk → Extremities.

Associated Symptoms

  • Irritability
  • Mild Diarrhea (65%)
  • Nagayama's Spots (Red papules on soft palate)
  • Peri-orbital oedema
  • Bulging anterior fontanelle (25%)

Lab Findings (Non-Critical)

  • Initial: Elevated WBC
  • Later: Low WBC, Neutropenia, Atypical lymphocytosis
  • Sterile Pyuria possible

*Lab investigation seldom necessary for classic cases.

Diagnosis

Primary Diagnostic Approach

Clinical Diagnosis

Based on physical exam and history (especially timing of rash with fever resolution). Laboratory investigation is seldom necessary.

When to Investigate / Use PCR

  • Immunosuppressed patients (post-transplant)
  • Severe Encephalitis (viral panels for CSF)
  • Uncertain diagnosis where complicating factors exist
Note: PCR can distinguish active vs latent (chromosomally integrated) infection.
Management (Acute)
1
Symptomatic Control & Hydration
Encourage oral hydration. Treat fever/irritability.
2
Antipyretics
Paracetamol: 10-15 mg/kg orally q4-6h (max 75mg/kg/day)
Ibuprofen: 5-10 mg/kg orally q6-8h (max 40mg/kg/day) [>6 months only]
!
CRITICAL WARNING
Avoid Aspirin due to risk of Reye's syndrome.
3
Immunocompromised / Severe Encephalitis
Consider antivirals (Ganciclovir, Valganciclovir, Foscarnet). *Efficacy not fully evaluated in trials, but used in transplant populations.

Parental Advice & Follow-up

Reassure parents this is a benign, self-limited condition. Rest and fluids are key. No follow-up needed for uncomplicated cases.

Key Differentials
Condition Differentiating Signs/Symptoms Key Action/Test
Measles Prodrome of cough, coryza, conjunctivitis. Koplik's spots (grey-white buccal papules). Rash lasts ~1 week. Serology / Nasopharyngeal swab
Meningococcemia Rapidly progressing purpuric eruption, sepsis, meningeal signs. (EMERGENCY) Blood/CSF Cultures
Rubella Rash starts on Face → Trunk. Tender occipital/cervical LN. Joint involvement in older pts. Serology (IgM/IgG)
Enterovirus Aseptic meningitis. Herpangina or vesicular lesions. PCR / Clinical exam
Epstein-Barr Virus Rash often after ampicillin/antibiotics. Atypical lymphocytes on smear. Monospot / EBV-specific antibodies
