Scarlet Fever

Quick Reference Guide | Updated Apr 2024
RED FLAGS - Immediate Action Required
EMERGENCY: Signs of invasive GAS infection
  • High fever (>39Â°C) with rapid progression
  • Hypotension or signs of shock
  • Severe pain disproportionate to examination
  • Skin necrosis or bullae formation
  • Respiratory distress or tachypnea
  • Altered mental status or confusion
Diagnostic Criteria
  • Classic Sandpaper rash + fever + sore throat
  • Age 1-10 years (peak 3-6 years)
  • Blanching erythematous rash
  • Fever >38Â°C
  • Pharyngitis with exudate
  • Strawberry tongue
  • Pastia's lines in flexures
First-Line Treatment
Phenoxymethylpenicillin
10 days oral therapy
Children: 125-250mg QID (by age)
Adults: 250-500mg QID
Penicillin allergy:
Azithromycin 5 days or Clarithromycin 10 days
Management Algorithm
Clinical Presentation Action Notes
Classic Suspected scarlet fever Start antibiotics immediately No need to wait for culture
Uncertain diagnosis Throat swab for culture Start antibiotics pending results
Outbreak setting Notify public health Mandatory in UK/Europe
Contact with vulnerable Prophylaxis consideration Healthcare workers, immunocompromised
Complications to Monitor
Short-term: Abscess, cellulitis, sepsis
Medium-term: Rheumatic fever (20 days)
Long-term: Glomerulonephritis (1-3 weeks)
Watch for: Persistent fever, joint pain, dark urine
Patient Advice
  • Stay home 24h after antibiotics
  • Complete full 10-day course
  • Good hygiene practices
  • Expect skin peeling (normal)
  • Seek help if symptoms worsen
Key Differentials

Viral Mimics

  • Measles (Koplik spots, cough)
  • Rubella (post-auricular nodes)
  • Fifth disease (slapped cheeks)

Bacterial

  • Staph scalded skin
  • Toxic shock syndrome
  • Kawasaki disease
