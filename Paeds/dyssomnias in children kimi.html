Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk stratification
Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)
Most common sleep-related breathing disorder
Caution
Key Symptoms:
  • Snoring, gasping, choking while asleep
  • Night sweats, mouth breathing
  • Hyperextended neck during sleep
  • Secondary nocturnal enuresis
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness (older/ obese children)
  • Difficulty awakening in morning
Chronic Insomnia Disorder
Previously behavioral insomnia of childhood
Manageable
Key Features:
  • Difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep
  • Resistance to going to bed
  • Need for caregiver presence
  • Daytime impairment (fatigue, behavior, cognition)
  • Occurs ≥3 nights/week for ≥3 months
  • Peak incidence: ≤5 years
Delayed Sleep-Wake Phase Disorder
Circadian rhythm disorder, common in adolescents
Chronic
Key Features:
  • Inability to fall asleep at desired time
  • Inability to awaken at socially acceptable time
  • Erratic sleep schedule (weekday vs weekend)
  • Heightened evening light sensitivity
  • Prevalence: 1-16% in adolescents
Narcolepsy
Central disorder of hypersomnolence
Urgent Referral
Key Features:
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness
  • Cataplexy (sudden muscle tone loss)
  • Sleep attacks
  • Hypnagogic/hypnopompic hallucinations
  • Sleep paralysis
  • Prevalence: 25-50/100,000
Detailed Assessment History
1
Sleep History & Diary

Obtain detailed 24-hour schedule including:

  • Bedtime routine timing and components
  • Sleep onset time and latency
  • Night awakenings (frequency, duration, interventions)
  • Wake-up time (weekday vs weekend discrepancies)
  • Nap schedule and refreshment
  • Sleep environment (darkness, noise, electronics, pets)
  • Behavior with different caregivers
2
Physical Examination

Focus on OSA risk factors:

  • Craniofacial abnormalities (midface hypoplasia, retrognathia)
  • Adenotonsillar hypertrophy
  • Macroglossia
  • Obesity (BMI calculation)
  • Signs of allergic rhinitis
  • Blood pressure measurement
3
Risk Factor Assessment
Strong OSA Risk Factors
  • Down syndrome (30-60% develop OSA)
  • Craniofacial anomalies
  • Adenotonsillar hypertrophy
  • Obesity
  • Neuromuscular disorders/hypotonia
Insomnia Risk Factors
  • Inconsistent parenting/limit-setting
  • Sleep onset associations
  • ADHD or neurodevelopmental disorders
  • Stressful life events
  • Poor sleep hygiene

Diagnosis

Investigations and diagnostic criteria

First-line Investigations

Attended Polysomnography (PSG)
Gold standard for OSA diagnosis
Essential
Indications:
  • Confirm OSA diagnosis
  • Differentiate OSA from other sleep disorders
  • Assess severity (AHI ranges 1-5 for children)
  • Evaluate for co-existing disorders
  • Validate MSLT for narcolepsy

Note: Unattended PSG not recommended in children

Sleep Diary/Actigraphy
Primary tools for circadian & insomnia disorders
Essential
Duration: Minimum 7 days, preferably 14 days
Includes: School days and free days
Measures: Sleep timing, duration, quality, circadian patterns
Use when: Discrepancy between complaints reported
Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT)
Diagnostic test for narcolepsy
Essential
Protocol:
  • Follow full-night PSG (≥7 hours sleep)
  • Off REM-suppressing medications
  • 5 naps at 2-hour intervals
  • Measure sleep latency and SOREMPs

Positive: Mean sleep latency ≤8 min + ≥2 SOREMPs

Diagnostic Criteria

Chronic Insomnia Disorder (ICSD-3-TR)
A
Sleep Difficulty

Difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep, early awakening, resistance to bed, or need for caregiver presence

B
Daytime Impairment

At least one symptom: fatigue, impaired concentration/performance, mood disturbance, daytime sleepiness, or behavioral problems

C
Frequency & Duration

Occurs ≥3 nights/week for ≥3 months

D
Exclusion

Not explained by inadequate sleep opportunity, other sleep/medical/mental disorders, or substances

Pediatric OSA Criteria
1
Parent Report

At least one of: snoring, labored breathing, sleepiness, hyperactivity, behavioral/cognitive problems

2
PSG Findings

≥1 obstructive events/hour OR obstructive hypoventilation pattern (≥25% TST with PaCO₂>50mmHg + snoring/flow limitation)

3
Exclusion

Not better explained by other sleep, medical, neurological disorders, or medications/substances

Differential Diagnosis

Primary Snoring

Snoring without diminished airflow, desaturations, or arousals on PSG. Unclear impact on behavior/cognition.

Central Sleep Apnea

Pauses in breathing >20 seconds without respiratory effort on PSG. Differentiate from OSA.

Anxiety/PTSD

Refusal to sleep alone due to underlying anxiety. Differentiate from limit-setting insomnia.

Restless Legs Syndrome

Vague evening discomfort relieved by movement. Often co-occurs with periodic limb movements.

Treatment

Evidence-based management strategies

Treatment Algorithm Overview

1
Obstructive Sleep Apnea
First-Line: Adenotonsillectomy
For adenotonsillar hypertrophy
Curative

Success Rate: 79-92% resolution of OSA

Less Efficacious: Obese children, Down syndrome, craniofacial abnormalities

Additional Benefit: Improved behavior and quality of life

Note: Watchful waiting may be appropriate for mild OSA in healthy children

Adjunct: CPAP
For persistent OSA or non-surgical candidates
Supportive

Indications: AHI >1, residual OSA post-surgery, <24 months old

Requirements: Patient cooperation, multidisciplinary approach, close follow-up

Considerations: Need heated humidifier, proper mask fit, manage allergies/rhinitis

Adjunct: Anti-inflammatory Therapy
Montelukast ± Intranasal Budesonide
Caution

Dosing: Montelukast 4mg (2-5yr) or 5mg (>5yr) daily; Budesonide 32mcg/spray per nostril 1-2× daily

Benefit: May improve mild residual OSA post-surgery or OSA worsened by allergy

Warning: FDA Boxed Warning for montelukast (serious behavior/mood changes)

Evidence: Short-term benefit only, insufficient evidence for steroids alone

Craniofacial Intervention
For structural abnormalities
Specialized

Options: Maxillary/mandibular advancement, maxillary expansion

Indications: Underlying craniofacial abnormalities, CPAP intolerance

Effectiveness: Orthodontic interventions can effectively treat OSA

2
Chronic Insomnia Disorder
First-Line: Behavioral Modification
Mainstay of treatment
Highly Effective

Success Rate: 94% improvement, 80% sustained at 3-6 months

Key Components:

  • Consistent bedtime routine
  • Regular sleep schedule (nighttime & naps)
  • Age-appropriate time in bed
  • Consistent parenting approach
  • Positive sleep environment
Behavioral Intervention Options
Extinction

Put child to bed awake, close door, leave to cry self to sleep

Modified Extinction

Check on child at set intervals, reassure without picking up

Extinction with Parental Presence

Parent sits in room with back to child, no interaction

Faded Bedtime with Positive Routines

Put child to bed later with positive routines, gradually advance

Adjunct: Melatonin
Specific circumstances only
Caution

Not recommended: First-line for typical chronic insomnia

May be helpful: ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, circadian component

Important: Behavioral interventions first, melatonin may add small benefit

Dosing: Consult specialist (unregulated, variable between manufacturers)

Warning: Long-term effects in children largely unknown

3
Delayed Sleep-Wake Phase Disorder
First-Line: Light Exposure Regulation
Core component of treatment
Essential

Morning: Bright light exposure to advance circadian phase

Evening: Reduce bright light exposure ≥2 hours before bedtime

Consistency: Fixed wake-up time (weekdays & weekends, school & holidays)

Education: Wake-up = time to get out of bed, not lie awake for hours

Adjunct: Chronotherapy
For extreme phase delays
Advanced

Method: Delay bedtime by 3-hour increments around the clock until desired time reached

Rationale: Easier to delay than advance circadian rhythm

Caution: Morning light may worsen delay if phase shift is large

Global Measures: Sleep Hygiene
Essential for all patients
Foundational
  • No caffeine 8 hours before sleep
  • Remove electronics from bedroom
  • Keep bedroom dark, quiet, cool
  • No pets in bedroom
  • Avoid falling asleep with headphones
Adjunct: Melatonin
To advance circadian phase
Adjunct

Timing: Evening dose to advance sleep phase

Dosing: Consult specialist (variable, unregulated)

Emphasis: Behavioral/schedule changes are primary; pill alone won't solve problem

Supervision: Initiation and dosing should be supervised by specialist

4
Narcolepsy
First-Line: Scheduled Naps
Frequent planned daytime sleep
Supportive

Strategy: Scheduled naps throughout day to manage excessive sleepiness

Integration: Part of comprehensive management plan

Adjunct: Pharmacotherapy
Specialist-prescribed medications
Specialist Only

Stimulants: Methylphenidate, dexamfetamine, modafinil, armodafinil

Other Agents: Sodium oxybate, pitolisant, solriamfetol

Cataplexy: Fluoxetine, venlafaxine, clomipramine

Important: Prescribed under specialist supervision only

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications, and prognosis

Monitoring Requirements

1
Obstructive Sleep Apnea
Post-CPAP Monitoring
Close follow-up essential
Critical

Frequency: Regular reassessment, especially in younger children/infants

Parameters to Monitor:

  • Body weight changes (affect pressure requirements)
  • Muscle tone and airway compliance
  • Adenotonsillar size changes
  • Pressure requirement adjustments
  • Possible CPAP discontinuation if resolution

Low Threshold: Re-evaluate if symptoms re-emerge despite normal prior PSG

Consider Repeat PSG When
  • Failure to respond to therapy
  • Recurrence of symptoms
  • Significant weight gain/loss
  • Before discontinuing CPAP
  • High-risk populations (Down syndrome, craniofacial abnormalities, low muscle tone)

Complications of Untreated/Residual OSA

Hypertension
Medium likelihood, variable timeframe
Monitor

Monitoring: Regular blood pressure checks

CPAP Effect: Shown to lower blood pressure in adults with OSA-related hypertension

Action: Close compliance monitoring if on CPAP

Behavioral Disturbances
Medium likelihood, variable timeframe
Reversible

Manifestations: Hyperactivity, behavioral problems, poor school performance

Improvement: Often profound improvement following OSA treatment

Cognitive Deficits
Medium likelihood, variable timeframe
Reversible

Impact: Poor school performance, learning difficulties

Recovery: Shown to improve following OSA treatment

Cardiovascular Complications
Low likelihood, severe cases
Serious

Right-sided Heart Failure: Relatively uncommon, seen in severe OSA

Pulmonary Hypertension: Uncommon, more likely in severe OSA or Down syndrome

Faltering Growth
Medium likelihood, variable timeframe
Reversible

Mechanism: Increased work of breathing, energy expenditure

Recovery: Growth improves following OSA treatment

Obesity
Low likelihood, variable timeframe
Association

Mechanism: Derangement of leptin and ghrelin levels → increased caloric intake

Intervention: Weight management alongside OSA treatment

Prognosis

A
Obstructive Sleep Apnea
Post-Adenotonsillectomy
Generally excellent prognosis
Good

Resolution: 79-92% of children cured

Recurrence Rate: 8.5% at 3 years in general population

Lower Success: Obese children, Down syndrome, craniofacial abnormalities

Weight Gain: Clinically significant weight gain post-surgery (overweight at baseline)

Untreated OSA
Risk of ongoing morbidity
Poor

Neurocognitive: Continued cognitive and behavioral impairment

Cardiometabolic: Risk of hypertension, glucose metabolism issues

Growth: Potential faltering growth

Quality of Life: Reduced academic and social functioning

Behavioral Interventions

Excellent

Key Factor: Parental consistency and adherence to intervention
