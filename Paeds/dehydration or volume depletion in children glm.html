Overview & Theory

Definition: Volume depletion is a deficit in water and solutes from the extracellular component of total body fluid. This is distinct from dehydration, which indicates total body water deficit.

Why Kids are Vulnerable
  • Higher body surface area to mass ratio.
  • Higher percentage of total body water (Newborns ~75%) vs Adults (60%).
  • Higher metabolism = increased need for water.
  • Dependence on others for fluid intake.
Common Causes
  • Gastroenteritis: Most common (Vomiting/Diarrhoea).
  • Haemorrhage: Trauma or occult bleeding.
  • Renal Losses: DKA, Diabetes Insipidus, Diuretics.
  • Skin Losses: Burns, heat stroke.
  • Third Spacing: Sepsis, Anaphylaxis, Obstruction.

Critical Concept

Hypotension is a LATE sign in children. It often heralds overt circulatory collapse. Do not wait for low BP to diagnose shock.

Diagnosis & Assessment

Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Focus on history and physical exam. Lab tests are for direction, not diagnosis.

Quick Severity Checker

Key Indicators (All Stages)
  • General: Irritability, lethargy, decreased activity.
  • Cardio: Tachycardia (Early sign). Weak pulses.
  • Skin: Decreased turgor (pinch abdominal skin), delayed capillary refill (>2-3s). Note: Skin turgor is unreliable in malnutrition.
  • Resp: Tachypnoea (Compensatory for metabolic acidosis).
  • Urine: Decreased output (Except in renal losses like DKA).

Management Algorithm

Step 1 Assess & Stabilize
Check ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation).
Immediate Action: If hypotension, altered mental status, or shock present -> GO TO STEP 3.
Step 2 Mild to Moderate Depletion
Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT)
  • Fluid: WHO Recommended ORS (75 mEq/L Sodium, 75 mmol/L Glucose, 245 mOsm/L).
  • Administration: Small volumes frequently (e.g., 5mL every 2-5 mins).
  • Goal: Re-establish hydration and decrease nausea/vomiting.
Step 3 Severe Hypovolaemia / Shock

Immediate IV Resuscitation

  • Fluid: Isotonic Crystalloid (e.g., Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate).
  • Dose: 20 mL/kg bolus.
  • Reassess: Repeat bolus if signs of shock persist.
  • Monitor: Glucose (Hypoglycaemia is common in ill infants).
Step 4 Ongoing Management
Replace ongoing losses (vomiting/diarrhoea). Monitor urine output. Treat underlying cause (e.g., Insulin for DKA, Antibiotics for Sepsis).
Labs to Consider
  • Serum Electrolytes: Determine Na+ (Hypo/Hyper/Iso) to guide fluid choice.
  • Bicarbonate: Low in metabolic acidosis (DKA/shock).
  • Glucose: Rule out Hypoglycaemia or DKA.
  • Urea/Cr: Ratio >20:1 suggests renal hypoperfusion.
Red Flags in History
  • History of Trauma (Suspect internal bleed).
  • Burns >10% BSA.
  • Diagnosis of Type 1 Diabetes (Risk of DKA).
  • Signs of neglect/abuse (Bruises inconsistent with history).

Key Differentials

Condition Key Differentiators
Sepsis Fever, signs of systemic inflammation, may have "warm shock" initially. Requires blood culture.
DKA Hyperglycaemia, Kussmaul breathing (deep/rapid), polyuria/polydipsia history. Ketones present.
Acute Glomerulonephritis Hypertension, Edema (fluid retention), "Cola-colored" urine.
Intussusception Severe colicky abdominal pain, "Red currant jelly" stool, sausage-shaped mass.
Adrenal Insufficiency Hyperpigmentation, Hyperkalaemia, Hyponatraemia. Very gradual onset usually.
