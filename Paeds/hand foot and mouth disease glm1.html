Overview & Diagnosis

Definition

An acute viral illness characterized by vesicular eruptions in the mouth and papulovesicular lesions of the distal limbs. Usually mild and self-limiting.

üëÑ Oral Lesions (Enanthem)
  • Appear 1-2 days after prodrome.
  • Start as erythematous macules/papules (2-8mm).
  • Common sites: Hard palate, tongue, buccal mucosa.
  • Progress to vesicles → shallow yellow-grey ulcers with erythematous halo.
‚úã Skin Lesions (Exanthem)
  • Macules/papules on hands and feet.
  • Central greyish vesicle (2-5mm).
  • Sides of fingers, dorsum of hands, margins of heels.
  • Buttocks/groin may be affected (esp. infants).
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Distinguishing Feature
Herpangina High fever, oral lesions, no skin rash.
Chickenpox Very itchy, centripetal rash (trunk & extremities).
Herpes Simplex Gingivostomatitis, common in children/immunocompromised.
Erythema Multiforme Target lesions on palms/soles, mucous membrane involvement.

Management Plan

‚úÖ Advice & Treatment

Reassurance

Usually a mild, self-limiting illness. Resolves in 7‚Äì10 days.

Self-Care
  • Fluids: Ensure adequate intake. Watch for dehydration (reduced urine, lethargy).
  • Diet: Soft diet if mouth ulcers painful. Avoid hot/spicy/acidic foods.
  • Hygiene: Wash hands after toilet, before eating. Dispose of tissues.
Pharmacological
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol or Ibuprofen for fever/pain.
  • Topical: Consider oromucosal antiseptic sprays, corticosteroid lozenges, or anaesthetic gels for pain.
  • Blisters: Let dry naturally. Do not pierce.

‚õî Do NOT Prescribe / Exclusion

Prescribing
  • Antibiotics: Not indicated unless secondary bacterial infection suspected.
  • Antivirals: Not indicated.
School/Nursery

No exclusion required for infection control.

Child should stay home only if too unwell to attend.

No need to isolate household contacts or investigate them.

Follow-up

Not routinely required.

Seek advice again if: Dehydration, worsening condition, or oral ulcers persist > 3 weeks.

Background & Transmission

Virology

Cause: Enteroviruses (Picornavirus family).

Main Serotypes: Coxsackie A16 (most common), A6 (atypical, severe), Enterovirus 71 (rare, severe neurological).

Incubation: 3‚Äì5 days.

Transmission
  • Direct contact (nasal/throat secretions, coughing).
  • Contact with blister fluid.
  • Faeco-oral route.
  • Contagious Period: Most contagious in first 7 days. Respiratory shedding up to 30 days, Faecal shedding up to 10 weeks.
Complications & Prognosis
Common:
  • Dehydration (due to oral pain).
  • Nail shedding (onychomadesis) - weeks later (esp. Coxsackie A6).
Rare:
  • Secondary bacterial infection.
  • Neurological (EV71): Meningitis, Encephalitis, Pulmonary oedema.
  • Pregnancy issues (miscarriage/stillbirth - causality not proven).
