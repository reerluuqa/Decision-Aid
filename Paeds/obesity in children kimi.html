Assessment & Screening

Initial evaluation and risk stratification

Key Diagnostic Factors

The most widely accepted method to screen for excess adiposity is calculation of body mass index (BMI): weight in kilograms divided by height in metres squared.

BMI Percentile Classification (Children >2 years)

  • Underweight: <5th percentile
  • Normal weight: 5th to 84th percentile
  • Overweight: 85th to 94th percentile
  • Obesity: ‚â•95th percentile

Severe Obesity Classification

  • Class II: BMI 120% to 139% of 95th percentile, or ‚â•35 kg/m¬≤ (whichever is lower)
  • Class III: BMI ‚â•140% of 95th percentile, or ‚â•40 kg/m¬≤ (whichever is lower)

For children <2 years: Weight-for-height values above the 95th percentile can be categorized as overweight.

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors
  • Parental obesity (‚â•1 parent with obesity)
  • Rapid weight gain in infancy and early childhood
  • Non-Hispanic black or Hispanic ethnicity
  • Poor socioeconomic status
  • Sedentary lifestyle (<60 minutes physical activity/day)
  • Screen time >2-3 hours/day
Additional Risk Factors
  • Maternal gestational diabetes
  • Exposure to corticosteroids, antibiotics, or acid-suppressing drugs <2 years
  • Short sleep duration
  • Urban environment with high pollution
  • Maternal smoking during pregnancy
  • Diet high in ultra-processed foods

History & Examination

Key areas to assess:

  • Medical history: Family history of obesity/diabetes, birth weight, maternal gestational diabetes
  • Dietary history: Sugar-sweetened beverages, portion sizes, fast food consumption
  • Physical activity: Duration, intensity, type of activity
  • Screen time: Television, computer, video games
  • Comorbidities: Hypertension, dyslipidaemia, type 2 diabetes, sleep apnoea, orthopedic issues
  • Psychosocial: Bullying, self-esteem, depression, anxiety
  • Physical signs: Acanthosis nigricans, hypertension, short stature (suggesting hormonal cause)

Investigations

Screen all children with obesity/overweight
Test Indication Frequency
Fasting glucose or HbA1c Screen for type 2 diabetes (age ‚â•10 years or puberty) Every 2 years minimum
Fasting lipids Screen for dyslipidaemia (age ‚â•10 years) Every 2 years (BMI ‚â•85th percentile)
Liver function tests Screen for MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease) Every 2 years (BMI ‚â•85th percentile)
Additional tests based on symptoms
  • Thyroid function tests (if short stature, fatigue)
  • Urinary free cortisol (if violaceous striae, buffalo hump)
  • Genetic testing (if severe early-onset obesity with developmental delay)

Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis and rule out secondary causes

‚ñº

Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnosis is based on BMI percentile adjusted for age and sex, combined with clinical assessment:

Primary Diagnosis

BMI ‚â•95th percentile for age and sex = Obesity

BMI 85th-94th percentile = Overweight

BMI <5th percentile = Underweight

Accurate measurement of height and weight is crucial. Use standardized equipment and plotted on WHO or CDC growth charts.

Differential Diagnosis

Rule out secondary causes of obesity:

Red Flags for Secondary Obesity

  • Short stature with attenuated growth velocity
  • Dysmorphic features
  • Developmental delay
  • Hypogonadism
  • Violaceous striae, buffalo hump (Cushing syndrome)
  • Hypotonia and feeding difficulties in infancy (Prader-Willi)
Conditions to Exclude
  • Hypothyroidism: Low free T4, elevated TSH
  • Cushing syndrome: Elevated urinary free cortisol, violaceous striae
  • Genetic syndromes: Prader-Willi, Bardet-Biedl, monogenic obesity
  • Hypothalamic obesity: History of craniopharyngioma or hypothalamic surgery
  • Medication-induced: Corticosteroids, atypical antipsychotics

Comorbidity Screening

Children with obesity are at high risk for multiple comorbidities:

High Prevalence Comorbidities
  • Metabolic: Type 2 diabetes, impaired glucose tolerance, metabolic syndrome, dyslipidaemia
  • Cardiovascular: Hypertension, early atherosclerosis
  • Hepatic: MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease)
  • Respiratory: Obstructive sleep apnoea, obesity hypoventilation syndrome
  • Orthopedic: Slipped capital femoral epiphysis, Blount's disease
  • Endocrine: Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
  • Psychosocial: Depression, anxiety, bullying, low self-esteem
Assessment Tools
  • Blood pressure measurement (use appropriate cuff size)
  • Fasting glucose/HbA1c and lipid panel
  • Liver function tests (ALT/AST)
  • Polysomnography if sleep apnoea suspected
  • Psychosocial screening questionnaire

Treatment & Management

Age-appropriate interventions and escalation

‚ñº

Treatment Approach Overview

The cornerstone of treatment is lifestyle modification. Pharmacotherapy and metabolic/bariatric surgery may be considered as adjuncts in severe cases.

Key Principles

  • Family-based approach: Parents and family must adopt healthy habits
  • Intensity matters: ‚â•26 contact hours over 3-12 months for effectiveness
  • Age-specific goals: Weight maintenance in younger children, gradual weight loss in adolescents
  • Shared decision-making: Motivational interviewing approach

Age-Specific Treatment Goals

üçº Children <6 years (BMI ‚â•95th percentile)

Goal: Weight maintenance
  • Weight loss up to 1 lb/month (0.5 kg/month) acceptable if BMI 21-22 kg/m¬≤
  • Treat comorbidities concurrently
  • Motivational interviewing recommended
  • Intensive health behavior treatment (consider ‚â•26 hours)

üë¶ Children 6-12 years (BMI ‚â•95th percentile)

Goal: Gradual weight loss (1 lb/month or 0.5 kg/month)
  • Continue linear growth while lowering BMI percentile
  • Intensive health behavior and lifestyle treatment recommended (‚â•26 hours)
  • Family-based multi-component intervention
  • Refer for pharmacotherapy if genetic obesity (setmelanotide)

üë± Adolescents 12-18 years (BMI ‚â•95th percentile)

Goal: Weight loss up to 2 lb/week (0.9 kg/week)
  • Intensive lifestyle modification plus adjunctive therapies as needed
  • Pharmacotherapy recommended alongside lifestyle treatment
  • Consider surgery referral for severe obesity with comorbidities
  • Address psychosocial issues (body image, peer relationships)

Lifestyle Modification Components

Dietary Interventions

  • ‚úÖ Eliminate sugar-sweetened beverages - single most effective change
  • ‚úÖ Decrease portion sizes - use smaller plates, measure portions
  • ‚úÖ Limit energy-dense and fast foods
  • ‚úÖ Increase fruits and vegetables - at least 5 servings/day
  • ‚úÖ Regular family meals - associated with healthier intake
  • ‚úÖ Remove unhealthy foods from home
  • ‚úÖ Avoid grazing/snacking - structured meal times

Physical Activity

  • ‚úÖ ‚â•60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity daily
  • ‚úÖ Age-appropriate and fun activities - play, sports, active transport
  • ‚úÖ Limit screen time - <1 hour/day for 2-5 years, parent-monitored for older
  • ‚úÖ Remove screens from bedrooms
  • ‚úÖ Family involvement - model active behaviors
  • ‚úÖ Adapt for barriers - musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, incontinence

Behavioral Therapy

  • ‚úÖ Motivational interviewing - shared decision-making
  • ‚úÖ Goal setting - specific, measurable, achievable
  • ‚úÖ Self-monitoring - food logs, activity trackers
  • ‚úÖ Reward systems - non-food rewards
  • ‚úÖ Identify triggers - stress, boredom, screen time
  • ‚úÖ Parental modeling - family-wide behavior change

Pharmacotherapy

Recommended as adjunct to lifestyle modification in adolescents ‚â•12 years with obesity, and in children 8-11 years with additional risk factors.

First-line Options
Medication Mechanism Age Approval Key Considerations
Liraglutide (GLP-1 agonist) Delays gastric emptying, central appetite suppression ‚â•12 years 3 mg daily SC; 43% achieve ‚â•5% BMI reduction
Semaglutide (GLP-1 agonist) Weekly GLP-1 receptor agonist ‚â•12 years 16% BMI reduction; delayed gastric emptying caution
Orlistat (lipase inhibitor) Reduces fat absorption ‚â•12 years 120 mg TID with meals; GI side effects limit use
Specialized Options
  • Setmelanotide: MC4 receptor agonist for genetic obesity (POMC, PCSK1, LEPR deficiency, Bardet-Biedl syndrome) - approved ‚â•2 years
  • Phentermine/Topiramate: Combination anorectic - approved 12-17 years in US only
  • Metformin: Not approved for obesity, but may help with weight gain in specific cases

Pharmacotherapy Cautions

  • All GLP-1 agonists delay gastric emptying - aspiration risk during anesthesia
  • Monitor for depression/suicidal ideation with weight loss medications
  • Topiramate: contraindicated in pregnancy (oral cleft risk)
  • Insurance coverage limitations may affect access

Metabolic/Bariatric Surgery

Consider for severe obesity with comorbidities

Surgery Criteria (AAP/ASMBS Guidelines)

  • ‚úÖ BMI ‚â•40 kg/m¬≤ OR ‚â•140% of 95th percentile (whichever is lower)
  • ‚úÖ BMI ‚â•35 kg/m¬≤ OR ‚â•120% of 95th percentile WITH clinically significant comorbidities
  • ‚úÖ Age: Data limited for <12 years; additional research needed
  • ‚úÖ Requirements: Failed intensive lifestyle modification, Tanner stage 2+, skeletal maturity not required
Clinically Significant Comorbidities
  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension
  • Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH)
  • Blount's disease
  • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease
  • Hypertension

Surgical Options

  • Vertical sleeve gastrectomy (VSG): Most common, fewer nutritional complications
  • Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB): More effective but higher complication risk
  • Outcomes: Durable weight loss (7-10 years), comorbidity resolution, improved quality of life

Surgery Contraindications

  • Medically correctable cause of obesity
  • Substance abuse or unstable psychiatric illness (relative)
  • Current or planned pregnancy within 12-18 months
  • Inability to adhere to nutritional supplementation
  • Lack of family/social support

Managing Overweight (BMI 85th-94th percentile)

Primary: Lifestyle Modification
  • Goal: Weight maintenance or slowing weight gain velocity
  • Intensive health behavior treatment (‚â•26 hours recommended)
  • Close monitoring of BMI trajectory
  • More intensive approach if additional risk factors present:
    • Family history of type 2 diabetes
    • Non-white ethnicity
    • Signs of insulin resistance (acanthosis nigricans)
    • Hypertension, dyslipidaemia, PCOS
    • Small for gestational age at birth

High-Risk Monitoring
  • Type 2 diabetes: Screen with pancreatic autoantibodies to exclude type 1
  • PCOS: Screen adolescents with irregular menses, hirsutism, acne
  • Sleep apnoea: Polysomnography if snoring, daytime somnolence
  • Orthopedic issues: Hip/knee pain (slipped capital femoral epiphysis)

Long-term Complications

Obesity in childhood has significant health implications in adulthood.

High Likelihood Complications
  • Adult obesity: 55% of children ‚Üí obese adolescents ‚Üí 80% ‚Üí obese adults
  • Type 2 diabetes: Risk increases with BMI and duration of obesity
  • Metabolic syndrome: Cluster of hypertension, dyslipidaemia, glucose intolerance
  • Cardiovascular disease: Early atherosclerosis, increased stroke risk in adulthood
  • Hypertension: 13% have elevated systolic, 9% diastolic BP
  • MASLD: Can progress to fibrosis and cirrhosis
  • PCOS: Common in adolescent girls with obesity
Medium Likelihood Complications
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea (30-60% of children with obesity)
  • Orthopedic problems (slipped capital femoral epiphysis, Blount's disease)
  • Cholelithiasis (gallstones)
  • Psychosocial morbidities (depression, anxiety, social discrimination)
  • Asthma (increased severity and reduced control)
Long-term Cancer Risk

Adolescent obesity associated with increased cancer risk in adulthood (HR 1.26-1.27 for various cancers).

Prognosis & Outcomes

Treatment Success Factors

  • ‚úÖ Family involvement: Parents as agents of change
  • ‚úÖ Intensity: ‚â•26 contact hours over 3-12 months
  • ‚úÖ Younger age: Better outcomes with earlier intervention
  • ‚úÖ Comprehensive approach: Diet + activity + behavior therapy
  • ‚úÖ Addressing barriers: Tailored to individual/family circumstances

Challenges & Barriers

  • ‚ùå High relapse rate: Weight regain common after initial loss
  • ‚ùå Access disparities: Culturally diverse, disadvantaged populations underserved
  • ‚ùå Medication access: Insurance coverage limitations
  • ‚ùå Surgical eligibility: Young age group (<12 years) lacks robust data
  • ‚ùå Stigma: Weight bias affects engagement and outcomes
Long-term Outlook

With sustained lifestyle changes and appropriate medical/surgical intervention when indicated, many complications can be prevented or reversed. Early intervention is key to improving long-term outcomes.

When to Escalate Care

Refer to Pediatric Endocrinology
  • Abnormal screening tests (glucose, lipids, liver function)
  • Suspected secondary cause (hormonal, genetic)
  • Severe obesity (Class II/III)
  • Need for pharmacotherapy discussion
  • Bariatric surgery evaluation
Consider Referral
  • Dietitian for intensive nutrition counseling
  • Psychology/psychiatry for mental health support
  • Sleep specialist for suspected sleep apnoea
  • Hepatology for MASLD
  • Orthopedics for musculoskeletal complications
  • Gynecology for PCOS management
