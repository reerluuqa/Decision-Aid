Erythema Infectiosum

Parvovirus B19

Immediate Red Flags

Pregnancy

  • Risk: Fetal anaemia, Hydrops fetalis, Intrauterine death.
  • High Risk Window: 9-20 weeks gestation.
  • Action: Immediate OB-GYN referral for serial ultrasounds/MCA Dopplers.
  • Maternal transmission rate: 17%-33%.

Immunocompromised

  • At-risk: HIV, Chemotherapy, Transplant patients.
  • Risk: Persistent infection >3 weeks, Chronic pure red cell aplasia.
  • Note: May NOT present with rash (often just anaemia).
  • Action: IVIG therapy; Haematology consult.

Hemolytic Anemia

  • At-risk: Sickle cell, Thalassaemia, Hereditary spherocytosis.
  • Risk: Transient Aplastic Crisis (TAC).
  • Symptoms: Pallor, tachycardia, shortness of breath.
  • Action: Urgent FBC/Reticulocyte count; transfusion support.
Clinical Appearance
Stage 1: "Slapped Cheek"
Stage 2: Lacy/Reticular Extremities

Key Features

  • Bilateral bright red macules on cheeks, sparing nasal ridge & peri-oral areas.
  • 1-4 days later: Lacy, reticular erythema on torso/extremities (1-3 weeks).
  • Adults: Arthralgia/Arthritis (small joints: hands, wrists, knees).
  • Prodrome: Mild fever, headache, pharyngitis (often absent in children).

Patient Discussion

Warning: The rash may intensify with heat (bathing, exercise) or sunlight. This is normal and does not mean the infection has returned.

Infectivity: Once the rash appears, the patient is usually no longer contagious.

Management Algorithm

Pyrexia / Discomfort

Paracetamol: 10-15 mg/kg (children) or 500-1000mg (adults) q4-6h.

Adjunctive Pain

Ibuprofen: 4-10 mg/kg (children) or 200-400mg (adults) q6-8h. Use if fever uncontrolled.

Arthritis / Arthralgia

NSAIDs: Ibuprofen or Naproxen. Self-limiting in small joints.

Supportive Care

Maintain hydration and rest. No specific antiviral required.

High-Risk Management

Pregnancy (Confirmed/Suspected)

Refer to Obstetrics. Serial Ultrasounds + MCA Dopplers for up to 10-12 weeks. Consider intrauterine transfusion if hydrops severe.

Immunocompromised / Persistent Infection

IVIG: 400 mg/kg/day IV for 5 days.

Reduce immunosuppression if possible. RBC transfusion support if needed.

Investigations (High Risk Only)

Serology: IgM+ (Acute), IgG+ (Immune). PCR DNA if immunocompromised.

Quick Epidemiology
  • Age: Most common 6-10 years.
  • Season: Late winter/Spring.
  • Spread: Respiratory droplets.
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Differentiating Signs Key Test
Measles Prodrome: Cough, coryza, conjunctivitis. Koplik spots (white buccal mucosa). Cephalocaudal spread. PCR swab / Serology
Rubella Rose-pink macules. Tender cervical/occipital lymphadenopathy. Rubella IgM/IgG
Scarlet Fever Preceded by pharyngitis. Sandpaper texture, Pastia's lines, circumoral pallor, desquamation. Throat swab (GAS)
Roseola Infantum Sudden high fever 3-5 days, rash appears at defervescence. Affects younger infants (6mo-3yr). HHV-6 Serology/PCR
Drug Hypersensitivity History of recent drug intake. May have eosinophilia. Clinical history / CBC
