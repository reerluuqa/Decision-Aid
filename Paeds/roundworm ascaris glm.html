Roundworm (Ascaris lumbricoides)

Last Revised: February 2025 | NICE CKS Summary

Age: 1 month+

URGENT ADMISSION REQUIRED

If signs of intestinal or biliary obstruction, or severe respiratory distress (Löffler syndrome) are present.

Assessment & Diagnosis

Key History Questions

  • Travel to or migration from endemic areas (tropics/subtropics).
  • Respiratory symptoms (cough, wheeze, dyspnoea) - suggests larval migration.
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms (pain, distension, nausea).

Investigations

Stool Microscopy (Gold Standard)

Collect 3 specimens on different days to improve detection.

  • Eosinophilia: Check FBC if >0.4 x 10⁹/L.
  • CXR: If Löffler syndrome suspected.
Management (Confirmed Uncomplicated)
  1. Step 1: Prescribe Antihelminthic

    Offer Mebendazole (unless contraindicated).

  2. Step 2: Advice & Hygiene
    • Wash hands before eating and after toilet.
    • Wash, peel, or cook raw vegetables.
    • Advise regarding transmission prevention.
  3. Step 3: Follow Up

    Cure rates are high (92-97%). Seek specialist advice if unsure or symptoms persist.

Drug: Mebendazole
Adult & Child 2-17y

Standard Regimen

100mg

Twice daily for 3 days

OR

500mg

Single dose (Off-label)

Child under 1y

Specialist Advice Recommended

100mg

Twice daily for 3 days

  • Pregnancy: Contraindicated. Do not prescribe.
  • Breastfeeding: Excreted in small amounts. Manufacturer advises caution. Seek specialist advice.
  • Age under 2: Unlicensed. Seek specialist advice.
Drug Effect
Carbamazepine, Phenytoin, Phenobarbital, Primidone, Ritonavir Reduced Mebendazole levels
Cimetidine Increased Mebendazole levels (Monitor for adverse effects)

No dose adjustment needed for intestinal infections (unlike systemic).

Common:

  • Abdominal pain

Uncommon/Rare:

  • Diarrhoea, flatulence, nausea/vomiting.
  • Dizziness, rash, convulsions.
  • SJS, Toxic epidermal necrolysis (Rare).
Background
  • Organism: Ascaris lumbricoides (Largest intestinal nematode).
  • Transmission: Fecal-oral via contaminated soil/food.
  • Prevalence: Common in tropics/subtropics with poor sanitation. Rare in developed nations (usually imported).
  • Life Cycle: Eggs ingested → Larvae migrate to lungs (Löffler syndrome) → Swallowed → Adult worms in intestine.
Differentials
Asthma Cough, wheeze, dyspnoea.
Gastroenteritis Watery diarrhoea/vomiting (rare in Ascariasis).
Appendicitis RLQ pain, movement worsens pain.
Strongyloides Abdo pain, weight loss, skin changes.
Pancreatitis Severe radiating pain.

Prognosis

Generally excellent with treatment. Most infections are asymptomatic.

Complications: Rare but include malnutrition (children), bowel obstruction, or biliary issues (heavy worm burden).
