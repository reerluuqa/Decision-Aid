Infantile Colic Management

Based on BMJ Best Practice Guidelines | Last updated: July 18, 2024

For infants <5 months with paroxysmal crying in otherwise healthy, well-fed infants

Assessment & History

Key History Elements

  • Age at symptom onset and crying pattern (duration, frequency, time of day)
  • Feeding details: position, duration, burping, bottle teat type, maternal diet (if breastfeeding)
  • Exposure to cigarette smoke (during pregnancy and after)
  • Stooling and urination patterns
  • Sleep patterns and any alleviating/aggravating factors
  • History of fever, vomiting, or trauma
  • Parental psychosocial factors (stress, depression, anxiety, coping mechanisms)

Physical Examination Essential

  • Plot weight, length, and head circumference on standard growth charts
  • Vital signs - fever indicates underlying infection
  • Examine tympanic membranes for otitis media
  • Check for intussusception, fracture, corneal abrasion, incarcerated hernia, anal fissure
  • Confirm infant is otherwise healthy and thriving

Red Flag Features Rule out organic causes

  • Fever
  • Faltering growth
  • Malodorous urine (possible UTI)
  • Recurrent vomiting
  • Blood in stool
  • Abdominal distension
  • Lethargy or altered responsiveness

Risk Factors for Infantile Colic

  • Age <5 months (peaks at 6 weeks, resolves by 3-4 months)
  • Food sensitivity - cow's milk proteins, maternal ingestion of eggs, chocolate, citrus, seafood
  • Exposure to cigarette smoke (doubles prevalence)
  • Lack of breastfeeding
  • Parental psychosocial factors (stress, depression, anxiety, poor parenting skills)
  • Increased parental age and higher education level
  • Low birth weight or small for gestational age

Diagnosis Clinical diagnosis

Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria Gold standard

For clinical diagnosis, ALL must be present:

  1. Infant <5 months of age when symptoms start and stop
  2. Recurrent and prolonged crying/fussing/irritability without obvious cause, cannot be prevented/resolved by caregivers
  3. No evidence of faltering growth, fever, or illness

For clinical research, additionally requires:

  1. Carer reports infant cried/fussed ≥3 hours/day on ≥3 days in 7 days
  2. 24-hour crying + fussing confirmed ≥3 hours by prospective behavior diary

Wessel's Criteria (Rule of 3s)

  • Crying episodes lasting >3 hours per day
  • On >3 days per week
  • For >3 weeks

Investigations

Not routinely required - diagnosis is clinical. <5% of distressed infants have identifiable medical conditions.

  • Consider only if diagnosis is in doubt or red flags present:
  • Urinalysis and urine culture (if UTI suspected)
  • Plain X-ray (if fracture suspected)
  • Abdominal X-ray (if intestinal obstruction suspected)

Differential Diagnoses Must exclude

  • UTI: Malodorous urine, fever, poor feeding, poor weight gain
  • Otitis media: >6 months, fever, ear tugging, bulging hyperemic TM
  • Gastroesophageal reflux: Effortless regurgitation from birth, worse after meals
  • Intussusception: Colicky pain, currant-jelly stool, vomiting, palpable mass
  • Fracture: History of trauma, localized tenderness
  • Pyloric stenosis: 3-6 weeks, projectile non-bilious vomiting, olive-shaped mass
  • Anal fissure: Blood on stool surface, constipation

Treatment & Management

General Recommendations First-line

  • Avoid overfeeding or underfeeding
  • Feed in semi-upright position to prevent aerophagia
  • Adequate burping after feeds
  • Avoid excessive stimulation
  • Parental reassurance and education Critical
  • Encourage parents to discuss feelings and support each other

Breastfed Infants

Continue breastfeeding

Formula-fed Infants

Check mechanical factors

Breastfeeding Management

Continue breastfeeding - breastfed infants have lower colic rates.

Maternal Diet Modification

For severe colic or infants with atopic features:

  • Eliminate cow's milk from maternal diet
  • Avoid potentially allergenic substances: caffeine, chocolate, eggs, nuts
  • Consider low-FODMAP diet Emerging evidence

Formula-feeding Management

Mechanical Factors First check

  • Ensure bottle teat hole is correct size for infant's feeding speed
  • Consider bottles with collapsible bags to decrease air swallowing
  • Review proper feeding technique and positioning

Formula Modification For severe cases

Hypo-allergenic formulae may benefit infants with severe colic or atopic features

  • Whey hydrolysates or casein hydrolysates
  • Periodic food challenges at monthly intervals
  • Monitor for improvement vs. natural resolution

AVOID These Formulas

  • Soya formulae: Important allergen, potential long-term reproductive health effects, especially avoid in premature infants and those with hypothyroidism
  • Fibre-supplemented formula: No proven effect

Pharmacological Treatment Not recommended

Medications to AVOID

  • Dicycloverine (Dicyclomine): Contraindicated in infants <6 months - risk of respiratory difficulties, apnea, coma, death
  • Simethicone: No evidence as pain-relieving agent

Emerging Treatments Limited evidence

  • Probiotics (Lactobacillus reuteri): Mixed evidence - some studies show reduced crying in breastfed infants, but 2019 Cochrane review found no clear benefit for prevention. UK guidance does NOT recommend.
  • Baby massage: Poor-quality evidence, may reduce crying/fussing time

Persistent Colic

If no response to behavioral/dietary modifications:

  • Provide continued parental reassurance
  • Emphasize infant will outgrow condition and continue to thrive
  • Offer support resources (e.g., NHS Choices: colic)

Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring

  • Benign, self-limiting condition - no long-term follow-up necessary
  • Spontaneous resolution by 3-4 months of age in most infants
  • Persists into 4th-5th month in up to 30% of cases
  • Growth and development should remain normal

Potential Complications Important to address

Negative Psychosocial Impact on Parents Medium likelihood

  • Parental stress, anxiety, fatigue, depression
  • Anger, hostility, marital disharmony
  • Domestic violence, guilt, helplessness
  • Poor parent-child interaction
  • Role ambivalence and relationship dissatisfaction

Child Abuse Risk Low but serious

May result from parental stress, anger, hostility, depression. Emphasize need for emotional support.

Long-term Associations Low likelihood

  • Recurrent abdominal pain (mixed evidence)
  • Allergic disorders: asthma, rhinitis, eczema, food allergy (mixed evidence)
  • Childhood psychological disorders: sleep disorders, challenging behaviors
  • Family dysfunction at 12 months (communication difficulties, unresolved conflicts)

Prognosis Excellent

  • Resolves spontaneously by 3-4 months in most infants
  • Infant continues to thrive and develop normally
  • Reassure parents that condition is temporary
  • No long-term sequelae in majority of cases

When to Re-evaluate

Return for medical review if:

  • Symptoms persist beyond 5 months
  • Poor weight gain or faltering growth
  • Development of fever or other concerning symptoms
  • New onset vomiting, diarrhea, or blood in stool
