Acute Pharyngitis

Clinical Decision Support Tool (BMJ Based)
Last Updated: June 2024

Immediate Triage (Rule Out Emergencies)

Scan these symptoms first. If present, patient requires urgent assessment.

Airway Obstruction

Suspect: Epiglottitis, Retropharyngeal Abscess

  • Drooling of saliva
  • Tripod/Sniffing posture (sitting forward, neck extended)
  • Muffled voice ("Hot potato" voice)
  • Stridor or respiratory distress
Action: DO NOT examine throat Call ENT/Anesthesia

Sepsis / Deep Neck Infection

Suspect: Lemierre's syndrome, Septic thrombophlebitis

  • Severe unilateral neck pain & swelling
  • Trismus (inability to open mouth)
  • Septic appearance / Toxic look
  • Persists > 3 days despite antibiotics
Action: Urgent CT Neck IV Antibiotics

Specific Viral/Systemic

Look for pathognomonic signs indicating non-strep aetiology.

  • Koplik spots (blue-white on buccal mucosa) ‚Üí Measles
  • Grey Membrane (bleeds on touch) ‚Üí Diphtheria
  • Conjunctivitis + Cough ‚Üí Viral (Adenovirus/COVID)
Action: Isolate if measles Report Diphtheria

Diagnostic Approach

Clinical Scoring (Centor/McIsaac)

Select findings to estimate GAS probability.

Viral vs. Bacterial Features

Feature Likely Bacterial (GAS) Likely Viral
Cough / Coryza Absent Present
Throat Exudate, severe pain Mild erythema, ulcers
Nodes Tender Anterior Posterior (often)
Season Winter/Early Spring Summer/Autumn (Enterovirus)

*Presence of clear viral symptoms (rhinorrhoea, cough, hoarseness, oral ulcers, conjunctivitis) = No GAS testing needed.

Investigations

  • 1st Line: Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT). Point of care.
  • Gold Standard: Throat Culture (90-95% sensitive).
  • Algorithm: If RADT negative but high clinical suspicion ‚Üí Culture (especially in children).
  • Note: Do not treat asymptomatic carriers (positive test + no symptoms).

Red Flag History

  • Sexual activity/abuse: Consider Gonorrhoea/Chlamydia/HIV.
  • Immunocompromised: Consider Candida (Thrush).
  • Wild game meat: Consider Tularaemia (penicillin resistant).

Management Pathway

Viral Pharyngitis

Goal: Symptom relief. No antibiotics.

Analgesics (NSAIDs/Paracetamol) Salt water gargle Topical anesthetics (Lozenges)

Avoid Aspirin in children (Reye's syndrome).

Confirmed GAS Pharyngitis

Goal: Prevent Rheumatic Fever, reduce transmission.

First Line: Penicillin V (Oral) 10 days Benzylpenicillin (IM Single Dose)
Penicillin Allergy: Macrolide Cephalosporin (Caution)
Note: Amoxicillin often used in children for palatability, but AVOID if infectious mononucleosis is suspected (risk of rash).
Prophylaxis: Only in patients with history of Rheumatic Fever.

Antibiotic Stewardship & Complications

Complications to Monitor

  • Acute Rheumatic Fever: Rare, preventable with early antibiotics.
  • Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy): Severe throat pain, trismus, "hot potato" voice.
  • Post-streptococcal Glomerulonephritis: Hematuria, edema (weeks later).
  • Lemierre's Syndrome: Sepsis, jugular vein thrombosis.

Follow-up

  • Test of cure is not routine.
  • No need for follow-up culture unless high risk (renal failure history) or recurring symptoms.
  • Advise patient: Non-infectious ~24 hours after starting antibiotics.

Aetiology & Theory

Bacterial

Group A Streptococcus (GAS)

Responsible for 20-30% of sore throats in children, 5-15% in adults. Spread via respiratory droplets. Incubation 2-5 days.

Others: Group C/G, Arcanobacterium, Fusobacterium necrophorum.

Viral

Most Common Cause

SARS-CoV-2, Influenza, Adenovirus, Parainfluenza, Rhinovirus, Enterovirus.

Infectious Mononucleosis (EBV): Often accompanied by lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly.

Other

Fungal: Candida albicans (Immunocompromised, inhaled steroids).

STIs: Gonorrhoea, Chlamydia, HIV (Adolescents).

Zoonotic: Tularaemia (Undercooked wild meat).
