Visual Inspection

Examine the baby in bright, preferably natural light:

  • Look at the skin of the whole body
  • Blanch the skin (gently press on the nose) to assess for jaundice
  • Examine the sclerae and gums
  • Be aware that jaundice may be more difficult to see in darker skin
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: If jaundice is suspected, the diagnosis must be confirmed by measurement of serum bilirubin in secondary care. Do not rely on visual inspection alone to estimate bilirubin level.

History Taking

Review personal child health record (red book) and hospital discharge documentation:

  • Obstetric history: Mother's Rhesus status, blood group, maternal red cell antibodies
  • Timing: Age at onset and duration of jaundice
  • Feeding history: Type of feeding, intake adequacy, number of wet/dirty nappies
  • Urine/stool color: Dark urine or pale chalky stools (may indicate liver disease)
  • Signs of illness: Lethargy, fever, vomiting, weight loss, irritability
  • Family history: Haemolysis, G6PD deficiency, siblings requiring phototherapy

Physical Examination

Assess for:

  • Signs of illness (fever, lethargy)
  • Appropriate weight gain
  • Evidence of bruising (cephalhaematoma)
  • Extent of jaundice (cephalocaudal progression)

Risk Factors for Significant Hyperbilirubinaemia
  • Decreased gestational age/preterm delivery
  • Low birth weight
  • Development of jaundice within first 24 hours
  • Sibling or parent with jaundice requiring treatment
  • Exclusive breastfeeding with suboptimal intake
  • Visible bruising or scalp haematoma
  • Maternal age >25 years
  • East Asian or Mediterranean ethnicity
  • Dehydration
  • Down syndrome
  • Macrosomic infant of diabetic mother
  • Family history of G6PD deficiency
‚úÖ If risk factors present: Re-examine during first 48 hours and ensure close monitoring

Diagnostic Approach

Base diagnosis on clinical observation at every contact, particularly within first 72 hours:

  • Perform visual inspection in bright natural light
  • Check skin, sclerae, and gums for yellow discoloration
  • Do not measure bilirubin routinely in non-jaundiced babies
  • Confirm suspected jaundice with serum bilirubin measurement in secondary care
üìä Diagnosis must be confirmed by: Transcutaneous bilirubinometer or serum bilirubin level measurement

Differentiating Physiological vs Pathological

Physiological Jaundice (Common & Usually Benign):

  • Appears at 2 days of age
  • Peaks on days 3-5
  • Decreases by day 10 (term) or 3 weeks (preterm)
  • Baby otherwise well and feeding adequately

Pathological Jaundice (Requires Investigation):

  • Appears in first 24 hours of life
  • Conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia
  • Persistent beyond 14 days (term) or 21 days (preterm)
  • Associated with signs of illness

Investigations in Secondary Care

Additional investigations to determine underlying cause:

  • Full blood count, packed cell volume, blood film
  • Blood group (mother and baby)
  • DAT (Coombs' test) for isoimmunization
  • Liver function tests
  • Metabolic screening (including hypothyroidism)
  • G6PD levels (consider ethnicity)
  • Microbiological cultures (if infection suspected)

Prolonged Jaundice

Definition: Jaundice persisting beyond 14 days in term babies (‚â•37 weeks) or 21 days in preterm babies (<37 weeks)

Common causes:

  • Breast milk jaundice (usually harmless, resolves by 12 weeks)
  • Liver disease (conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Infection

Secondary Care Treatments

Options depend on bilirubin level and underlying cause:

  • No treatment: For physiological or breastmilk jaundice with bilirubin below threshold
  • Phototherapy: Light converts unconjugated bilirubin to excretable products
  • Exchange transfusion: For signs of bilirubin encephalopathy or high kernicterus risk
  • Treat underlying illness: Sepsis, metabolic disorders
  • Early surgical treatment: For biliary atresia
üí°
Primary Care Management

All neonates with visible jaundice need bilirubin measurement:

  • Arrange urgent referral to secondary care (usually immediate/same-day)
  • Transcutaneous bilirubinometry not generally available in primary care
  • Follow local protocols for appropriate urgency level

If transcutaneous measurement available:

  • Record level within 6 hours
  • Manage according to local protocols and threshold tables

Parent Information & Reassurance

Provide within 24 hours of birth:

  • How to check baby for jaundice
  • What to do if jaundice is suspected
  • Importance of seeking urgent attention if jaundice in first 24 hours
  • Check nappies for dark urine or pale chalky stools
  • Reassurance that neonatal jaundice is common and usually transient/harmless

Breastfeeding Support

Encourage and support continued breastfeeding:

  • Breastfeeding can usually continue during jaundice
  • Advise frequent feeding (8-12 times per 24 hours)
  • Wake baby for feeds if necessary
  • Ensure sufficient access to breastfeeding support

Signs of adequate intake:

  • ‚â•4 wet nappies per day by 72 hours
  • 3-4 stools per day by fourth day

Monitoring & When to Reassess

Close monitoring required for:

  • Babies with risk factors for significant hyperbilirubinaemia
  • Preterm or low birth weight infants
  • Those with bruising or birth trauma
  • Infants with suboptimal intake

Parents should seek urgent medical attention if:

  • Jaundice appears within first 24 hours
  • Baby is unwell or feeding poorly
  • Jaundice is worsening or spreading
  • Dark urine or pale stools noted
  • Jaundice persists beyond 2 weeks (term) or 3 weeks (preterm)
