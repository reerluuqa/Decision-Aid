Red Flags & Emergency Actions

URGENT: Immediate Action Required

PHACE(S) Syndrome

Segmental cervicofacial haemangiomas may indicate underlying structural anomalies.

  • Posterior fossa anomalies
  • Haemangioma (segmental, facial)
  • Arterial lesions (cerebral)
  • Cardiac abnormalities / coarctation
  • Eye abnormalities
  • Sternal clefting / supraumbilical raphe

ACTION REQUIRED

Ophthalmological exam + Echo + CNS imaging

LUMBAR Syndrome

Segmental perineal/genital haemangiomas signal potential underlying defects.

  • Lower body infantile haemangioma
  • Urogenital anomalies & ulceration
  • Myelopathy / tethered cord
  • Bony deformities
  • Anorectal malformations
  • Renal anomalies

ACTION REQUIRED

MRI of spine - TEST OF CHOICE

Airway Involvement (Beard Distribution)

Haemangiomas on lower face/neck may be associated with laryngeal haemangioma.

Immediate ENT referral + possible endoscopy

High-Output Cardiac Failure

Large visceral haemangiomas can cause high-output heart failure.

Strong Risk Factors

  • HIGHLow birth weight (<1500g: 14% risk)
  • HIGHPrematurity
  • MODWhite ethnicity
  • MODFemale sex (2:1 to 5:1 ratio)
  • Advanced maternal age
  • Multiple gestation

Key Diagnostic Features

  • Onset: First few weeks (not usually at birth)
  • Appearance: Pink/red/blue macules ‚Üí rapid growth
  • Growth phase: 80% complete by 3 months
  • Involution: Begins 6-12 months, continues years
  • GLUT1 positive: Distinguishes from congenital haemangiomas

High-Risk Locations Requiring Early Intervention

Lip

Feeding difficulties, cosmetic deformity

Asymptomatic / Low Risk

Observation and reassurance - most haemangiomas involute spontaneously.

  • Education and parental reassurance
  • Regular monitoring (1-2 monthly)
  • Document with photographs
  • No functional impairment
  • Low risk of cosmetic deformity

Functional / Cosmetic Concerns

Active treatment indicated for the following:

  • Periorbital lesions (amblyopia risk)
  • Airway involvement
  • Ulceration with pain/bleeding
  • Large facial lesions (PHACE risk)
  • Lip lesions affecting feeding
  • Risk of permanent disfigurement

Ulcerated Haemangioma Management

Wound Care

  • Burow's solution compresses
  • Petrolatum-impregnated gauze

Topical

  • Metronidazole 0.75% BID
  • Mupirocin 2% TID if infected

Analgesia

  • Paracetamol 10-15 mg/kg
  • Lidocaine 3% cream PRN

Adjuncts

  • Becaplermin 0.01% daily
  • Pulsed dye laser

Side Effects to Monitor

Cushingoid facies, irritability, growth retardation, HPA axis suppression. Rebound growth common during taper.

Monitoring & Follow-up

Routine Cases

Low-risk lesions: Follow-up every 1-2 months or as needed.

  • Monitor growth rate
  • Photographic documentation
  • Parental education

Ulcerated Lesions

High complication risk: Follow-up every 1-2 weeks initially.

  • Monitor wound healing
  • Check for secondary infection
  • Pain management review

On Beta-Blockers

Active treatment: Close monitoring required for adverse effects.

  • Hypoglycemia (feed with food)
  • Bronchospasm (stop if wheezing)
  • Hypotension, bradycardia
  • Rebound growth on cessation
