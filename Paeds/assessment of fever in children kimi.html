Assessment of Fever in Children

Assessment
Urgent Considerations

Use ABCDE approach. Consider sepsis in any febrile child with tachycardia, tachypnea, altered mental status, or poor perfusion. Administer antibiotics within 1 hour if sepsis suspected.

History Critical

Fever Characteristics
  • Height & duration of fever
  • Measurement method
  • Response to antipyretics (does NOT differentiate viral vs bacterial)
  • Pattern: Night sweats suggest TB or lymphoma
Accompanying Symptomatology
  • Rash: Characterize type (maculopapular, petechial, urticarial, purpuric)
  • Cardiorespiratory: Cough, breathlessness, chest pain
  • GI: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain
  • CNS: Confusion, seizures, altered mental state, focal deficits
  • MSK: Arthralgia, myalgia, bone pain
  • ENT: Pharyngitis, otalgia, nasal discharge
  • Urinary: Dysuria, frequency, hematuria
Exposure & Risk Factors
  • Recent travel to endemic areas (malaria, dengue, TB, typhoid)
  • Contact with ill individuals
  • Animal exposure (cats - cat-scratch disease)
  • Vaccination status
  • Recent medications/drug exposures
  • Immunocompromised status
  • Heat exposure (heat illness)

Physical Examination Detailed

General Appearance & Vital Signs
  • Toxic appearance: Ill-appearing children ↑ risk of serious bacterial infection
  • Temperature: Confirm with accurate measurement
  • Heart rate: Persistent tachycardia suggests thyroid storm or endocarditis
  • Respiration: Tachypnea may indicate pneumonia
  • Capillary refill: ≥3 seconds = intermediate risk (NICE traffic light)
  • Blood pressure: Hypertension in inflammatory/vasculitic disorders
System-Specific Findings
  • Skin: Turgor, rashes (petechial, purpuric, erythema migrans), desquamation
  • Eyes: Conjunctivitis, papilledema, uveitis, proptosis
  • Joints: Swelling, warmth, tenderness, decreased ROM
  • Lymph nodes: Cervical, generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Abdomen: Hepatosplenomegaly, tenderness, masses
  • Neurological: Kernig's/Brudzinski signs, bulging fontanelle, focal deficits

Diagnostic Testing Age-Based

Children < 2 months: Higher risk - comprehensive evaluation required
Required Tests < 2 months
  • Full blood count
  • Blood culture
  • Urine microscopy & culture
  • CSF studies & culture
  • Chest X-ray (if respiratory symptoms)
  • Stool analyses (if diarrhea)
Selective Tests > 2 months
  • FBC (if bacterial concern)
  • Urine microscopy & culture
  • Blood culture (if high fever >39°C)
  • CSF testing (if CNS signs)
  • Chest X-ray (if respiratory distress)
  • Procalcitonin (better predictor of SBI)
Fever of Unknown Origin Workup
  • FBC with peripheral smear
  • ESR, CRP, Procalcitonin
  • Blood culture
  • Urinalysis & culture
  • Electrolytes, LFTs, creatinine
  • Serological tests (EBV, CMV, HIV)
  • Chest X-ray
  • NAAT for tuberculosis
  • Additional tests as indicated (stool, bone marrow, imaging)
Diagnosis

Differential Diagnoses Comprehensive

Infections (Most Common)
Common Infections
Viral Syndromes Self-limited

Features: Non-specific symptoms, maculopapular rash, low-grade fever

Tests: Clinical diagnosis; rapid tests available for influenza, RSV, COVID-19

Urinary Tract Infection Common

Features: Dysuria, frequency, suprapubic/flank tenderness, fever >24h

Tests: Urine dipstick (leukocyte esterase/nitrites), microscopy, culture

Pneumonia Respiratory

Features: Cough, chest pain, respiratory distress, tachypnea

Tests: Chest X-ray, procalcitonin (if bacterial suspected)

COVID-19 Endemic

Features: Mild cough, sore throat; may have minimal symptoms despite pneumonia on imaging

Tests: RT-PCR, rapid antigen test

Meningitis Emergency

Features: Irritability, lethargy, seizures, bulging fontanelle, meningeal signs

Tests: CSF cell count >22 WBC/mcL (infants) or >7 WBC/mcL (older), Gram stain, culture, PCR

Serious Bacterial Infections
Sepsis Critical

Features: Tachycardia, tachypnea, fever/hypothermia, poor perfusion, altered mental status

Tests: Blood cultures, lactate, CBC, CRP, urine microscopy

Action: Immediate empiric antibiotics & fluids

Septic Arthritis Urgent

Features: Joint swelling, redness, painful ROM, inability to bear weight

Tests: Joint fluid WBC >50,000/mcL, Gram stain, culture

Action: Urgent joint drainage

Osteomyelitis Urgent

Features: Bone pain, refusal to move extremity, limp

Tests: ESR/CRP, blood culture, X-ray (may be normal early), MRI preferred

Inflammatory/Vasculitic Disorders
Key Inflammatory Conditions
Kawasaki Disease Coronary Risk

Criteria: Fever ≥4 days + ≥4 of: extremity changes, polymorphous rash, conjunctivitis, oral changes, cervical lymphadenopathy

Tests: ESR/CRP, CBC, LFTs, urinalysis, echo (for coronary aneurysms)

Treatment: IVIG + aspirin

Rheumatic Fever Jones Criteria

Major: Carditis, polyarthritis, subcutaneous nodules, chorea, erythema marginatum

Minor: Fever, arthralgia, ↑ESR/CRP, prolonged PR interval

Requires: Recent GAS infection + 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor

JIA Chronic

Features: Arthritis ≥6 weeks, morning stiffness, low-grade fever

Tests: ANA, rheumatoid factor, ESR/CRP

Malignancies
Leukemia/Lymphoma Urgent

Features: Weight loss, night sweats, bone pain, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly

Tests: FBC with smear (abnormal cells), LDH, bone marrow biopsy

Action: Urgent hematology referral

Key Diagnostic Clues
  • Petechial rash + fever: Consider meningococcemia, leukemia, RMSF
  • Conjunctivitis + fever >4 days: Consider Kawasaki disease
  • Bone pain + pallor: Consider leukemia, osteomyelitis
  • Recent travel + fever: Consider malaria, dengue, typhoid, TB
  • Heart murmur + fever: Consider endocarditis
Treatment
Sepsis: Time-Critical Treatment

Within 1 hour: Broad-spectrum antibiotics + crystalloid fluids

Blood cultures before antibiotics if doesn't delay treatment >45 minutes

Condition-Specific Treatment Targeted

Bacterial Infections
Meningitis Emergency

Empiric Abx: 3rd gen cephalosporin (e.g., ceftriaxone) ± vancomycin

Dexamethasone: Consider if bacterial meningitis confirmed

Duration: 7-21 days depending on organism

Pneumonia Moderate

Empiric Abx: Amoxicillin (community-acquired)

Supportive: Oxygen if SpO2 <92%

Duration: 5-7 days typical

UTI Common

Empiric Abx: Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or cephalosporin

Cystitis: 3-5 days

Pyelonephritis: 10-14 days

Inflammatory/Vasculitic
Kawasaki Disease Coronary Protection

IVIG: 2 g/kg single dose

Aspirin: High dose (80-100 mg/kg/day) until fever resolves, then low dose (3-5 mg/kg/day)

Follow-up: Echocardiogram at diagnosis & 2-6 weeks

Supportive Care
  • Antipyretics: Paracetamol 15 mg/kg q4-6h OR Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg q6-8h
  • Fluids: Maintain hydration; oral preferred, IV if needed
  • Monitoring: Temperature, vital signs, fluid balance
  • Isolation: For suspected meningococcal disease, TB, COVID-19

Management by Age Age-Specific

< 2 months

High risk - Empiric antibiotics recommended until cultures negative

  • Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV
  • Consider ampicillin if Listeria risk
  • Admit for observation
> 2 months

Selective approach - Based on clinical appearance and risk factors

  • Well-appearing: Observation ± limited workup
  • Ill-appearing: Full workup + empiric antibiotics
  • Consider procalcitonin level
Red Flag Conditions Requiring Immediate Treatment
Meningitis, Sepsis, Septic Shock, Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever, Thyroid Storm
Follow-up

Disposition & Monitoring Essential

Admission Criteria
  • Ill-appearing or toxic
  • Suspected sepsis or serious bacterial infection
  • Age < 1 month with fever ≥38°C
  • Immunocompromised
  • Social concerns (unreliable follow-up)
  • Intractable vomiting/dehydration
  • Persistent fever despite treatment
Discharge Criteria
  • Well-appearing with stable vitals
  • No identified serious bacterial infection
  • Reliable caregivers with good follow-up
  • Able to maintain hydration
  • Appropriate antipyretic response
  • Clear return precautions provided

Reassessment Timeline Important

Phone Follow-up
  • 24-48 hours after discharge
  • Assess fever resolution
  • Check medication tolerance
  • Review culture results if available
Clinic Re-evaluation
  • 48-72 hours if fever persists
  • 72 hours for UTI follow-up
  • 1 week for pneumonia
  • 2 weeks for Kawasaki disease (echo)

Return Precautions Critical

Return Immediately If:
  • Worsening fever or persistent >3 days
  • New rash (especially petechial/purpuric)
  • Lethargy, confusion, or seizures
  • Difficulty breathing or cyanosis
  • Signs of dehydration (no tears, dry mucous membranes, oliguria)
  • Stiff neck or severe headache
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Severe pain
  • Poor oral intake

Prevention & Education Proactive

Prevention Strategies
  • Vaccination: Up-to-date immunizations (pneumococcal, Hib, meningococcal, influenza)
  • Hand hygiene: Regular handwashing
  • Vector control: Mosquito/tick prevention in endemic areas
  • Safe food/water: When traveling
  • Avoid overheating: Appropriate clothing, hydration in hot weather
Caregiver Education
  • Appropriate use of antipyretics (dosing, frequency)
  • Adequate fluid intake
  • Lightweight clothing, tepid sponging
  • When to seek medical attention
  • Importance of follow-up appointments
  • Never give aspirin to children (Reye syndrome risk)
Referral Pathways
Emergency: Sepsis, meningitis, severe dehydration, respiratory distress
Urgent: Suspected Kawasaki disease, pneumonia requiring oxygen, complicated UTI
Routine: Persistent viral illness, managed conditions (otitis media, mild UTI)

Key Temperatures
  • Fever: ≥38.0°C (100.4°F)
  • Hyperpyrexia: ≥40.0°C (104°F)
  • Hypothermia: <36.0°C (96.8°F)
Red Flags
  • Ill appearance, lethargy
  • Poor perfusion, prolonged CRT
  • Respiratory distress
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Non-blanching rash
Key Scores
  • NICE Traffic Light: Risk stratification
  • Centor/McIsaac: Strep pharyngitis
  • Rochester/KCH: Infant febrile criteria
