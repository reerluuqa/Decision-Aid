JIA QuickRef

BMJ Best Practice • Jul 2024
EMERGENCY DIFFERENTIAL: SEPTIC ARTHRITIS

Rule out immediately if: Single joint, high fever, severe pain, erythema, or refusal to bear weight. DO NOT delay for rheumatology if infection is suspected.

Definition Core
  • Onset < 16 years
  • Arthritis in ≥1 joint (swelling, warmth, limited ROM)
  • Duration ≥ 6 weeks
  • Exclusion of other causes
UVEITIS ALERT High Risk

10-20% of JIA patients develop anterior uveitis. It is typically asymptomatic.

Action: Refer to Ophthalmology immediately.
High Risk: Young, Female, ANA+, Oligoarticular.
TREATMENT GOAL

Achieve clinically inactive disease, prevent joint damage/disability, and minimize treatment side effects.

Clinical Approach
Morning Stiffness Gel phenomenon (stiffness improving with activity)
Limp/Gait Worse in mornings; "Refusal to walk"
Uveitis Usually asymptomatic (red eye is rare)
Systemic (SoJIA) Quotidian spiking fever + evanescent salmon rash
Key Investigations
ANA Risk Strat: Positive = Uveitis risk. Not diagnostic.
Rheumatoid Factor (RF) Positive in Polyarticular JIA (older girls). Aggressive disease.
ESR / CRP High in Systemic/Moderate in Poly. Normal/Mild in Oligo.
FBC Anemia/Thrombocytosis/Leukocytosis in Systemic JIA.
Crucial Differentials Rule Out
Sign JIA Septic Arthritis Malignancy (Leukemia) Acute Rheumatic Fever
Pain Mild/Moderate (worse with motion) Severe, Constant Severe, night pain often Migratory, severe
Fever Only in Systemic JIA (spiking) High, persistent Low grade or high (B symptoms) High, continuous
Joint Look Swelling/Warmth Erythema / Marked Swelling Normal or peri-articular swelling Swelling moves between joints
Action Refer Rheum Urgent Ortho/Emergency Urgent Oncology Treat + Cardiac Echo
Systemic JIA Emergency

Key: Fever + Rash (+/- Arthritis)

  • Quotidian fever (spikes daily)
  • Evanescent salmon rash
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Risk of Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)
Oligoarticular Most Common

Key: ≤4 joints (first 6 mo)

  • Large joints (Knee, Ankle)
  • Young age (<6), Female
  • High Uveitis Risk (ANA+)
  • Limb length discrepancy (longer leg on affected side)
Polyarticular RF- Common

Key: ≥5 joints (first 6 mo)

  • Symmetric involvement
  • Small joints (hands/feet)
  • Young/Teen girls
  • Moderate disease severity
Polyarticular RF+ Aggressive

Key: Like Adult RA

  • Teenage girls
  • Rheumatoid Nodules
  • Erosive disease
  • Worse prognosis
Enthesitis-Related Boys

Key: Inflammation at tendon insertion

  • Boys > 6 years
  • HLA-B27 Positive
  • Sacroiliac pain
  • Acute (painful) Anterior Uveitis risk
Psoriatic Skin+Joint

Key: Psoriasis + Arthritis OR 2 of:

  • Dactylitis (Sausage digits)
  • Nail pitting / Onycholysis
  • Family history of Psoriasis
Supportive Care First Line
  • NSAIDs: Naproxen or Ibuprofen (symptom control, does not alter disease course).
  • Intra-articular Steroids: Triamcinolone hexacetonide. Highly effective for Oligoarticular JIA.
  • Physiotherapy: Crucial to prevent contractures. Active participation encouraged.
  • Vaccinations: Update before starting biologics (avoid live vaccines on treatment).
Systemic Therapy (Polyarticular)
1
Methotrexate

First line conventional synthetic DMARD.

2
Biologic: TNF Inhibitors

Etanercept / Adalimumab. Add if MTX fails or high risk features.

3
Alternative Biologics

Tocilizumab (IL-6) or Abatacept if anti-TNF fails.

Systemic JIA (SoJIA) Treatment

NSAIDs are insufficient. Use Corticosteroids for acute control, followed rapidly by:

Anakinra (IL-1 blocker) Canakinumab (IL-1 blocker) Tocilizumab (IL-6 blocker)
Monitoring & Safety
Methotrexate FBC, LFTs, Renal q3-4 months. Give Folic Acid.
Biologics (TNF) TB Screen (Quantiferon/PPD), Hep B Screen before start. Watch for infection reactivation.
Tocilizumab LFTs (Risk of hepatotoxicity). Lipids can elevate.
Ophthalmology High risk (ANA+): Every 3-4 months. Lower risk: Every 6-12 months.
