When to Suspect an Eating Disorder

Consider an eating disorder in patients presenting with:

  • Unusual BMI (low/high) or rapid weight loss
  • Changes in eating behavior causing concern
  • Mental health issues (anxiety, depression, social withdrawal)
  • Disproportionate concern about weight/shape
  • Poor control of diet-affected chronic diseases (diabetes, celiac)
  • Menstrual/endocrine disturbances
  • Unexplained GI symptoms or electrolyte imbalance
  • Physical signs of malnutrition or compensatory behaviors
Remember:

Eating disorders can occur in anyone at any age and may present in people of average or above normal weight. Risk is highest between ages 13-17.

Clinical Features by Type

Anorexia Nervosa
  • Low BMI (<18.5 adults, <5th percentile children)
  • Intense fear of weight gain
  • Body image disturbance
  • Food restriction, excessive exercise, purging
  • Physical signs: Bradycardia, hypotension, lanugo hair, amenorrhea
Bulimia Nervosa
  • Recurrent binge eating (≥1/week for 3 months)
  • Compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, fasting)
  • Weight usually normal or above
  • Physical signs: Russell's sign, dental erosion, parotid swelling
Binge Eating Disorder
  • Recurrent binge eating (≥1/week for 3 months)
  • NO compensatory behaviors
  • Often overweight or obese
  • Eating rapidly, until uncomfortably full, when not hungry
Atypical Eating Disorders
  • Symptoms of above disorders but don't meet full criteria
  • Example: All criteria for anorexia met but weight within normal range
  • Over-concern with body weight and shape generally present
  • May progress to full diagnostic criteria over time

Assessment Approach

History

Eating patterns, weight history, compensatory behaviors, body image, psychological factors

Physical Examination

Weight, height, BMI, vital signs, signs of malnutrition

Investigations (as indicated)

FBC, U&Es, LFT, glucose, TFT, ECG if indicated, pregnancy test if applicable

Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral

Physical Red Flags
  • Rapid weight loss
  • BMI <15
  • Bradycardia (<50 bpm)
  • Hypotension
  • Hypothermia
  • Electrolyte abnormalities
Psychological Red Flags
  • Severe depression
  • Suicidal ideation
  • Self-harm
Children/Adolescents
  • Faltering growth
  • Delayed puberty

Management in Primary Care

  • Monitor: Regular weight checks, vital signs, mental state
  • Coordinate care: Liaise with specialist services
  • Manage complications: As appropriate (e.g., electrolyte replacement)
  • Support: Provide information, involve family/carers (with consent)
Important:

All suspected eating disorders require specialist assessment. Primary care focuses on monitoring, coordination, and managing complications while awaiting specialist input.

Referral Guidelines

Urgent Referral (within 24 hours)

Severe physical or mental health deterioration

Routine Referral

All suspected eating disorders require specialist assessment

Target Timeframe

Children/young people within 4 weeks; adults per local agreements

Follow-up

Regular monitoring based on severity:

  • Physical: Weight, vital signs, complications
  • Psychological: Mood, motivation, engagement with treatment
  • Social: Functioning, support systems
Prognosis Note:

Eating disorders can persist for decades if untreated. Early intervention significantly improves outcomes, especially in adolescents.
