Equinovarus Foot (Clubfoot)

Last Updated: Aug 31, 2023 | Based on BMJ Best Practice
Clinical Quick Scan
Immediate Action Required
Refer patient to a Paediatric Orthopaedic Surgeon trained in the Ponseti method. Do not delay.
Snapshot
Epidemiology
  • Incidence: 1 in 1000 live births.
  • Gender: Boys > Girls (2:1).
  • Risk: 17x higher in 1st-degree relatives.
Prognosis

With non-operative Ponseti method:

Excellent

(78% good/excellent results at 30 years)

Diagnosis & Assessment
Red Flags for Syndromic/Neuromuscular Cause

If present, prognosis may be poorer and standard Ponseti might fail:

Arthrogryposis Spina Bifida Cerebral Palsy Dimples/Hairy patch on spine Hip Dysplasia (Screen US recommended)

Differential Diagnosis Comparison

Condition Heel Position Correctability Distinguishing Feature
Idiopathic Clubfoot Varus Rigid / Non-correctable Classic 4 deformities present.
Postural Clubfoot Varus Fully Correctable No rigidity/atrophy. Simple stretching may fix.
Metatarsus Adductus Neutral/Valgus Flexible No equinus. Just forefoot deviation.
Vertical Talus Valgus Rigid "Rocker-bottom" foot. Heel in valgus.
Management: Ponseti Method
1

Manipulation & Casting

Weekly casting (every 4-7 days). Correct cavus/adductus first, then varus. Equinus is corrected last.

Typical course: 4-6 casts.
2

Achilles Tenotomy

Required in >85% of cases if dorsiflexion <15-20¬∞ after casting.

Complication: Bleeding/Aneurysm (Rare)

Final cast worn for 3 weeks post-tenotomy.

3

Bracing (Crucial for Maintenance)

Full-time (23hrs) x 3 months
→
Night/Naps until 4-5 years

Non-compliance is the #1 cause of relapse.

Monitoring & Relapse
Signs of Relapse
  • Development of equinus
  • Tight heel cord
  • Dynamic forefoot supination (when walking)
  • Intoeing gait
Action: Recasting + Rebracing.
Schedule
Start Brace 2-3 weeks check
Monitoring Every 3-6 months (age 3-4)
Long term Every 1-2 years (to maturity)
Classification Details
  • Idiopathic: Rigid, not correctable. May be moderate/severe.
  • Postural: Fully correctable, not a true clubfoot.
  • Teratological: Severe, high failure rate, associated with neuromuscular disease.
Pathophysiology
Ligaments are thickened with densely packed collagen fibers. Talus is plantar flexed and medially rotated. Tendons (tibialis anterior, extensors) are medially displaced.
