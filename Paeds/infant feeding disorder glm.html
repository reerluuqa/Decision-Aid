Infant Feeding Disorders Quick Reference

CRITICAL RED FLAGS (Immediate Action Required)

Sudden changes or specific symptoms indicate underlying pathology that is not a simple feeding disorder.

  • Bilious vomiting (Green): Indicates upper GI obstruction. Surgical emergency in term infants. (Common in premature establishing feeds).
  • Sudden onset vomiting: Exclude infection first (Meningitis, UTI, Sepsis).
  • Projectile vomiting: Pyloric stenosis (non-bilious).
  • Apparent Life-Threatening Event (ALTE): Associated with recurrent regurgitation. Investigate immediately.
  • Faltering Growth: Crossing down 2 centiles requires investigation beyond simple feeding advice.
Definition & Overview

Conditions in children <1 year leading to impaired oral intake, associated with medical, nutritional, feeding skill, and/or psychosocial dysfunction.

25-35% Children with normal dev
80% With developmental delay
<30m Efficient feed duration
Key Risk Factors
  • Prematurity: <37 weeks (impaired suck/swallow/breathe coordination).
  • Developmental Delay: 2x risk vs normal dev.
  • Anatomical Abnormalities: Cleft lip/palate, tongue-tie.
  • Neonatal Surgery: Cardiac or GI surgery history.
Aetiology: Common Causes
GORD

Most common. Regurgitation, arching, irritability. Physiological if growing well.

Cows' Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA)

~2% infants. Symptoms: Vomiting, colic, eczema, constipation/diarrhoea.

Anatomical

Cleft lip/palate, ankyloglossia (tongue-tie), tracheo-oesophageal fistula.

Aetiology: Complex Causes
Neurological

Cerebral palsy, HIE. Poor coordination increases aspiration risk.

Cardiac/Respiratory

Congenital heart disease, Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (tire easily).

Psychosocial

Found in 80% of feeding disorders. Carer stress, depression, or forced feeding.

Diagnostic Approach (History & Exam)
History Focus

Prenatal events (polyhydramnios?), Gestational age, Birth weight, Illness history, Surgery.

Feeding Observation

Observe for 20 mins. Look for: Cues, suck/swallow/breathe coordination, carer interaction.

Red Flag Symptoms

Coughing/Choking (Aspiration?), Recurrent pneumonia, Apnoea/Bradycardia.

Growth Charts

Plot weight, length, HC. Compare to birth. Faltering growth = Complex issue.

Management & Actions

First Steps

  • Reassure if growth is normal & no red flags.
  • Video recording of feed at home can help diagnosis.
  • Treat underlying conditions (e.g., GORD medication).

Dietary Trials

  • CMPA: Exclude cow's milk for 2-4 weeks. Rechallenge to confirm.
  • Lactose: Trial lactose-free formula if suspected intolerance.
  • Thickened Feeds: May be used for reflux (ensure professional guidance).

Behavioural

  • Reduce stress at mealtimes.
  • Stop forced feeding; avoid distraction techniques.
  • Involve Speech & Language Therapy (SLT) or Occupational Therapy (OT) for oral skills.
Investigations
Indication Test
Suspected Aspiration Chest X-ray, VFSS
Anatomical Abnormality Upper GI Contrast Study
Refractory GORD pH Impedance Monitoring
Coeliac / Allergy IgA tTG, Skin Prick
Physical Exam Checklist
  • Growth: Weight, Length, Head Circumference (Plot on chart).
  • Craniofacial: Cleft palate, Micrognathia (Pierre Robin), Tongue-tie.
  • Neurology: Tone, posture, reflexes, developmental milestones.
  • Respiratory: Stridor, wheeze, work of breathing.
  • Abdomen: Distension, tenderness, organomegaly.
  • Skin: Eczema (atopy/CMPA).
