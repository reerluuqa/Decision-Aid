When to Suspect Conduct Disorder

Core Features (ICD-11 Criteria):

  • Persistent pattern markedly defiant, disobedient, provocative, or spiteful behavior
  • Duration ‚â• 6 months for ODD, ‚â• 12 months for Conduct-Dissocial Disorder
  • Significant impairment in personal, familial, social, or educational functioning
  • Behaviors more frequent than typical for age/developmental level

Age-Specific Presentations

üßí Ages 3-7 Years
  • General defiance of adults' wishes
  • Angry outbursts with temper tantrums
  • Physical aggression (especially to siblings/peers)
  • Destruction of property
  • Arguing and blaming others
üë¶ Ages 8-11 Years
  • All of the above plus:
  • Swearing and persistent lying
  • Stealing outside the home
  • Breaking rules persistently
  • Bullying and cruelty to animals
  • Setting fires
üë§ Ages 12-17 Years
  • All of the above plus:
  • Hurting other people
  • Committing crimes (assault, robbery, vandalism)
  • Running away from home
  • Truancy from school
  • Alcohol and drug misuse

Comprehensive Assessment Components

Essential Information Sources: Child, families, schools, and other caregivers
  • Core conduct disorder symptoms (see above)
  • Current functioning at home, school, and with peers
  • Parenting style (positive/negative aspects, coercive discipline)
  • Family situation and parental wellbeing
  • History of mental/physical health problems
  • Concurrent stresses (bereavement, bullying, parental separation)
  • Screen for comorbidities (ADHD, autism, depression, anxiety, PTSD)
  • Assess immediate safety risks

Child Maltreatment Recognition

Always Consider Maltreatment: Especially with marked behavior changes without plausible explanation
  • Unusual/marked change in behavior/emotional state
  • Frequent attendance to healthcare services
  • Injuries with features suggesting maltreatment
  • Evidence of sexual activity in child/young person
  • Harmful parent-child interactions
  • Refusal to allow child to speak alone
  • Signs of neglect (infestations, poor hygiene, failure to thrive)

ICD-11 Diagnostic Criteria

Key Principle: Isolated antisocial or criminal acts are NOT sufficient for diagnosis
üéØ Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)
  • Persistent pattern of markedly defiant, disobedient, provocative, or spiteful behavior
  • More frequent than typical for age/developmental level
  • Duration ‚â• 6 months
  • Results in significant impairment
  • Subtypes: with/without chronic irritability-anger
‚ö†Ô∏è Conduct-Dissocial Disorder
  • Repetitive and persistent pattern violating basic rights or societal norms
  • Multiple behaviors: aggression, destruction, deceit, rule violations
  • Duration ‚â• 12 months with multiple incidents
  • Significant impairment in functioning
  • Onset: childhood (<10 years) or adolescent (‚â•10 years)
üìã Other Specified Disruptive Behavior Disorders
  • Share features but don't meet full criteria
  • Persistent behavior problems across settings
  • Duration ‚â• 6 months
  • Significant impairment present

Diagnostic Thresholds

Behavioral Symptoms Must Be:

  • Persistent and repetitive (not isolated incidents)
  • Present across multiple settings (home, school, social)
  • Developmentally inappropriate for age/culture
  • Causing significant functional impairment
  • Present for specified duration (6-12 months minimum)

Comorbidity Considerations

üß† Neurodevelopmental
  • ADHD (>40% comorbidity)
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder
  • Learning disabilities
  • Communication disorders
üò¢ Mental Health
  • Depression and anxiety
  • PTSD (especially with trauma history)
  • Substance misuse disorders
  • Self-harm behaviors
üö® Severity Markers
  • Callous-unemotional traits
  • Early onset (<10 years)
  • Severe, frequent, varied behaviors
  • Low intelligence/IQ

Prognostic Indicators

Poor Prognosis Associated With:

  • Early onset (childhood vs adolescent)
  • Callous-unemotional traits
  • Severe, frequent, and varied behaviors
  • Hyperactivity and attention problems
  • Lower intelligence
  • Parental criminality or alcohol misuse
  • Harsh, inconsistent parenting
  • Low socioeconomic status

Positive Prognostic Factors:

  • Adolescent onset (better than childhood onset)
  • Recognition and treatment of comorbid ADHD
  • Appropriate school placements
  • Positive parenting approaches
  • Absence of callous-unemotional traits

Red Flags for Immediate Referral

URGENT Immediate CAMHS referral if:
  • Significant complicating factors present
  • Coexisting mental health problems (depression, PTSD)
  • Neurodevelopmental conditions (ADHD, autism)
  • Learning disabilities or difficulties
  • Substance misuse issues
  • Immediate safety risks identified
  • Child maltreatment concerns

Referral Criteria

REFERRAL REQUIRED Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS)

Refer to CAMHS if ANY significant complicating factors:

  • Coexisting mental health problem (depression, PTSD, anxiety)
  • Neurodevelopmental condition (ADHD, autism)
  • Learning disability or difficulty
  • Substance misuse issues
  • Child maltreatment concerns
  • Immediate safety risks
  • Complex family circumstances
DIRECT REFERRAL For children without complicating factors, refer directly to appropriate intervention

Age-Based Psychosocial Interventions

üë∂ Ages 3-11 Years: Parent Training Programs
EVIDENCE-BASED First-line intervention for younger children

Group Parent Training:

  • 10-12 parents per group
  • Up to 16 sessions (90-120 minutes each)
  • Skills: modeling, rehearsal, feedback
  • Both parents encouraged to attend

Individual Parent Training:

  • For severe/complex problems or attendance issues
  • Up to 10 sessions (60-90 minutes each)
  • Both parents encouraged to attend
  • Same skill-building approach
üè† Foster Carer/Guardian Training

For children aged 3-11 years in foster care:

  • 8-12 foster carers/guardians per group
  • 12-16 meetings (90-120 minutes each)
  • Both carers encouraged to attend
  • Parenting skills improvement focus
üë¶ Ages 9-14 Years: Child-Focused Programs

Group Social and Cognitive Problem-Solving:

  • 10-18 weekly meetings (2 hours each)
  • Cognitive-behavioral problem-solving model
  • Rehearsal and feedback for behavior improvement
  • Social skills development focus
üë§ Ages 11-17 Years: Multimodal Interventions
INTENSIVE Multisystemic Therapy (MST)
  • Specially trained case manager provides intensive support
  • Interventions across multiple settings:
    • Individual therapy
    • Family therapy
    • School-based interventions
    • Criminal justice system involvement
    • Community interventions
  • 3-4 meetings per week for up to 5 months
  • Comprehensive, individualized approach

Medication Management

IMPORTANT Medications not first-line for conduct disorders alone
  • Methylphenidate or Atomoxetine: Not recommended for conduct disorder alone
  • Consideration: May be appropriate if co-existing ADHD is present
  • Adequate ADHD treatment: Improves prognosis for behavioral disorder
  • Monitoring: Regular follow-up if medications initiated

Parental Support and Education

ESSENTIAL Address parental needs and reduce stigma

Provide:

  • Reading material about conduct disorders (e.g., Royal College of Psychiatrists resources)
  • Assessment of parental needs:
    • Personal, social, and emotional support
    • Support in caring role
    • Practical advice (childcare, housing, finances)
  • Address concerns about being blamed for child's problems
  • Explain reasons and purpose of interventions
SENSITIVITY Parents may feel blamed; approach with empathy and clear communication

Information Sharing and Consent

  • Confidentiality: Seek permission from Gillick-competent young people and parents
  • Information sharing: Before contacting schools or nurseries
  • Multi-agency coordination: Inform social services and other involved agencies
  • Documentation: Include all assessment information in referrals

Accessing Interventions

Local Service Navigation:

  • Contact local authorities through city/county council websites
  • Check safeguarding websites for resources
  • Family and children information services
  • Local CAMHS team for service availability
  • Availability varies by location - check local pathways

Crisis Management

EMERGENCY Immediate risk assessment essential

Immediate Safety Concerns:

  • Risk of harm to child or others
  • Suicidal ideation or self-harm
  • Severe aggression or violence
  • Child maltreatment concerns
  • Substance overdose or intoxication

Action: Urgent mental health assessment or emergency services

Long-term Prognosis

Variable outcomes depending on onset and severity:

üìä Adolescent-Onset (‚â•10 years)
  • Better prognosis: ~85% cease antisocial behavior by early 20s
  • Often limited to adolescent period
  • Lower risk of adult criminality
  • Better educational and occupational outcomes
‚ö†Ô∏è Childhood-Onset (<10 years)
  • Poorer prognosis: ~50% have persistent serious problems
  • Higher risk of adult antisocial personality disorder (~50%)
  • Increased risk of substance abuse, anxiety, depression
  • Poorer academic performance and higher truancy
  • Unemployment and relationship problems
  • Criminal behavior (violent and non-violent)

Factors Influencing Prognosis

‚ö†Ô∏è Poor Prognosis Factors
  • Callous-unemotional traits (early expression)
  • Severe, frequent, and varied behaviors
  • Hyperactivity and attention problems
  • Lower intelligence/IQ
  • Parental criminality or alcohol misuse
  • Harsh, inconsistent parenting
  • Low socioeconomic status
  • Ineffective schooling
‚úÖ Positive Prognostic Factors
  • Recognition and treatment of ADHD
  • Appropriate school placements
  • Positive parenting approaches
  • Absence of callous-unemotional traits
  • Later onset of symptoms
  • Less severe behavioral patterns
  • Higher intelligence
  • Stable family environment

ODD to Conduct Disorder Progression

MONITORING Some cases of ODD progress to Conduct-Dissocial Disorder

Risk Factors for Progression:

  • Severely defiant or spiteful behaviors
  • Co-existing ADHD
  • Chronic irritability and anger
  • Family history of conduct problems
  • Poor response to early interventions

Associated with Later:

  • Depressive disorders
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Substance use disorders
  • Antisocial personality disorder

Red Flags for Re-referral

RE-REFER If deterioration or new concerns emerge
  • Worsening of behavioral problems
  • New onset of self-harm or suicidal ideation
  • Significant decline in school functioning
  • Substance misuse escalation
  • Involvement with criminal justice system
  • New mental health symptoms (depression, anxiety, psychosis)
  • Family crisis or breakdown
  • Child maltreatment concerns

Transition to Adult Services

Planning for Transition (Ages 16-18):

  • Assess ongoing mental health needs
  • Coordinate with adult mental health services
  • Address educational/vocational planning
  • Consider substance misuse services if needed
  • Legal and financial independence planning
  • Housing and support needs assessment
IMPORTANT Ensure continuity of care during transition period

Family Support and Education

Ongoing Parental Needs:

  • Continued parenting support and skills reinforcement
  • Respite care needs assessment
  • Sibling support (often overlooked)
  • Financial and practical support
  • Peer support groups
  • Family therapy if indicated

Prognosis Reporting to Families

COMMUNICATION Provide realistic but hopeful outlook

Key Messages:

  • Early intervention improves outcomes
  • Consistent parenting approaches are crucial
  • Treatment of comorbidities (especially ADHD) is important
  • Adolescent-onset has better prognosis than childhood-onset
  • Many young people improve significantly with treatment
  • Ongoing support may be needed into young adulthood
