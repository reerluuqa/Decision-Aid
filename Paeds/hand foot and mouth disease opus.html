Suspected Syphilis? Refer Immediately

Refer all people with suspected syphilis to a Genito-Urinary Medicine (GUM) clinic or specialist sexual health service.

Clinical Features by Stage

Key Feature Details
Onset 9–90 days after exposure (Mean: 21 days).
Chancre Solitary, painless, indurated ulcer.
Clean base, sharp margin, discharges clear serum.
Site: Genital (penile, labial, cervical), rectal, or oral.
May be atypical if HIV+ (multiple, painful).
Lymph Local lymphadenopathy (often subtle/non-tender).
Duration Heals spontaneously in 3–8 weeks.
Key Feature Details
Onset 3–10 weeks after chancre appears.
Rash Widespread, non-pruritic maculopapular rash.
Characteristically involves palms and soles.
Can be papular, macular, or ulceronodular.
Condylomata Lata Grey/white moist wart-like lesions.
Commonly perineum and anus.
Mouth 'Snail tract' ulcers (mucous patches).
Systemic Generalized lymphadenopathy, low-grade fever, malaise, headache.
Hepatitis, nephritis.
Stage Details
Early Latent < 2 years duration.
Asymptomatic, serological evidence present.
25% have recurrence of secondary symptoms.
Late Latent > 2 years duration.
Asymptomatic, but non-infectious (except pregnant women).
Type Details
Gummatous Granulomatous lesions with necrotic center.
Affect skin and bones. Resolve rapidly with tx.
Cardiovascular Occurs 20–40 years later.
Aortic regurgitation, aortic aneurysm, angina, heart failure.
Neurosyphilis Tabes dorsalis: Ataxia, lancinating pains, Charcot joints.
General paresis: Dementia, personality change, seizures.

Assessment

  • History: Symptoms, full sexual history (partners based on stage), past STIs.
  • Risk Factors: MSM, unprotected sex, HIV+, multiple partners.
  • Examination: Genital, skin (palms/soles), mouth, neurological/eye signs.
  • Tests: Refer for serology, dark-field microscopy, HIV screen.

Complications & Red Flags

High Risk
Neurosyphilis

Can occur at any stage.

  • Early: Meningitis, cranial nerve palsies (optic/vestibulocochlear), hearing loss.
  • Late: Tabes dorsalis (lightning pains), General paresis (dementia).
Ocular Syphilis
  • Uveitis, optic neuropathy.
  • Retinitis, interstitial keratitis.
  • Potential for permanent visual loss.
Cardiovascular
  • Aortic aneurysm.
  • Aortic regurgitation.
  • Angina / Heart failure.
Pregnancy Risks
  • Vertical transmission risk high.
  • Stillbirth, neonatal death.
  • Preterm birth, low birth weight.
  • Congenital syphilis.

Quick Facts & Prognosis

Transmission

Direct contact with infectious lesion (genital/rectal/oral).
Also blood transfusion (rare in UK), needle sharing.
Vertical (Mother to Child).

Untreated Progression

25% develop secondary signs.
1/3 progress to severe late disease (20-40 yrs).
Cardio/Neuro lesions may progress despite tx.

Prognosis

Curable with appropriate antibiotics before complications.
HIV co-infection increases risk of reinfection & ocular/neuro involvement.
