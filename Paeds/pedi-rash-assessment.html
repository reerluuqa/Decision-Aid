Meningococcal Disease

LIFE THREATENING
  • Non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash ‚Äî especially on extremities
  • Rapid progression ‚Äî within 24 hours
  • Fever + malaise Abrupt onset typical with abrupt onset
  • Nuchal rigidity or altered consciousness
  • Shock signs: tachycardia, poor perfusion
‚ö° ACTION: IV antibiotics within 1 hour + fluids

Sepsis

EMERGENCY
  • Systemic inflammatory response to infection
  • Tachycardia, tachypnea, temperature instability
  • Poor capillary refill (>3 sec), mottled skin
  • Hypotension, altered mental status
  • Risk factors: age <1yr, immunocompromise
‚ö° ACTION: Sepsis protocol + broad-spectrum antibiotics

Anaphylaxis

IMMEDIATE
  • Urticaria + angioedema with respiratory compromise
  • Airway: stridor, wheeze, throat tightness
  • Hypotension, tachycardia, collapse
  • Onset: minutes to hours after exposure
  • Common triggers: food, drugs, insect stings
‚ö° ACTION: IM Adrenaline 0.01mg/kg (max 0.5mg)

Stevens-Johnson / TEN

HIGH MORTALITY
  • Mucosal involvement ‚Äî oral, ocular, anogenital
  • Nikolsky sign positive Blistering with lateral pressure on skin ‚Äî skin sloughing with pressure
  • Targetoid lesions, painful skin, acute illness
  • Often drug-induced: anticonvulsants, sulfonamides
  • SJS: <10% BSA | TEN: >30% BSA
‚ö° ACTION: Stop offending drug + burn unit referral

Kawasaki Disease

URGENT ‚Äî Risk of Coronary Aneurysm
  • Fever ‚â•5 days + ‚â•4 of 5 criteria:
  • Bilateral conjunctival injection (no exudate)
  • Oral changes: strawberry tongue, cracked lips
  • Peripheral extremity changes: edema, desquamation
  • Polymorphous rash (maculopapular/erythroderma)
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy (usually unilateral)
‚ö° ACTION: IVIG + Aspirin within 10 days of fever

Toxic Shock Syndrome

URGENT
  • High fever, diffuse erythematous rash
  • Desquamation 1-2 weeks later (palms/soles)
  • Hypotension, multi-organ involvement
  • Associated with: staph/strep infection, wounds
  • More common with abscesses, post-surgical
‚ö° ACTION: IV antibiotics + supportive care

Emergency Action Protocol

SEPSIS Protocol ‚Äî First Hour

  • Recognize: Signs of infection + organ dysfunction
  • Call for help / Activate sepsis pathway
  • Blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics (if no delay)
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour
  • IV crystalloid fluids: 20mL/kg bolus
  • Monitor lactate, urine output, vitals

ANAPHYLAXIS Protocol

  • IM Adrenaline 0.01mg/kg (max 0.5mg) ‚Äî anterolateral thigh
  • Call for help / Start resuscitation if needed
  • High-flow oxygen if available
  • Position patient appropriately (supine if hypotensive)
  • IV fluids if hypotensive
  • Repeat IM Adrenaline every 5-15 min if no response

MENINGOCOCCAL Protocol

  • Do NOT delay antibiotics for LP if unstable or rapid progression
  • Blood cultures FIRST if stable
  • IV Ceftriaxone 50mg/kg (max 2g) OR Cefotaxime
  • Lumbar puncture if no contraindications
  • Fluid resuscitation + monitor for shock

Clinical Assessment Algorithm

1

RED FLAG CHECK Assess for Immediate Life Threats

Look for: Non-blanching rash, respiratory compromise, hypotension, altered consciousness, mucosal involvement with skin detachment.

If present: Activate emergency protocols immediately. Do NOT delay for investigations.

2

VITALS & STABILITY Assess ABCs and Vital Signs

Check: Temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, SpO2, capillary refill, mental status.

If unstable: Resuscitate first, then investigate. Sepsis = time-critical.

3

RASH CHARACTERISTICS Describe Morphology & Distribution

Determine: Primary lesion type (macule/papule/vesicle/pustule/petechiae), pattern (annular/linear/diffuse), distribution (acral/central/mucosal).

Key test: Glass test for blanching. Non-blanching = urgent evaluation.

4

HISTORY Identify Associated Features

Ask about: Fever pattern, pruritus, pain, recent infections, medications (new within 2-6 weeks), exposures (ticks, sick contacts), immunization status, travel.

Generally: Rash without fever or systemic symptoms is NOT urgent.

5

DIFFERENTIAL Generate Differential & Plan

Match rash type + history to likely causes. Use the differentials table above.

Routine: Supportive care, follow-up. Urgent: Labs, antibiotics, admission. Critical: Immediate intervention + ICU.

Key Emergency Medications

Adrenaline (IM) 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.5mg) anterolateral thigh
Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV/IM (max 2g) for meningococcal
Penicillin V 15 mg/kg PO QID (10 days) for scarlet fever
IVIG 2 g/kg total for Kawasaki (within 10 days)
Aspirin 30-100 mg/kg/day for Kawasaki (antiplatelet dose)

When to Discharge / Reassure

  • Well-appearing child
  • Normal vital signs for age
  • Rash is blanching (not petechial)
  • No fever or mild fever only
  • No associated respiratory distress
  • Normal mental status
  • No mucosal involvement
  • Clear exposure history (e.g., viral exanthem)
