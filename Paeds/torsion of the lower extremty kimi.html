Torsion of the Lower Limb in Children

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm based on BMJ Best Practice Guidelines (May 2025)

Most cases are physiological variations that resolve spontaneously

Assessment

History Taking

Key Questions to Ask Parents:

  • Reason for consultation and specific concerns about appearance
  • Age at which the problem was first noticed
  • Progression or changes over time
  • Family history of similar rotational problems
  • Child's sitting habits (W-sitting, sitting on feet)
  • Developmental milestones (sitting by 8 months, walking by 12-18 months)
  • Pain, tripping, falling, or limping
  • Obstetric and birth history

Rotational Profile Measurements:

Foot Progression Angle (FPA)
Angle between foot axis and line of progression. Negative = in-toeing, Positive = out-toeing.
Hip Rotation Arc
Measure internal/external rotation with patient prone, pelvis stabilized, knee flexed 90°.
Thigh-Foot Angle
Angle between longitudinal axes of thigh and foot in prone position.
Transmalleolar Axis
Assesses tibial torsion. Angle between line through femoral condyles and malleoli.
Red Flags: Pain, asymmetric range of motion, limp, delayed walking, recent gait change
Risk Factors
Neuromuscular Disease Intrauterine Positioning Family History Female Sex Ligamentous Laxity
Investigations

Radiographs Indicated For:

  • Hip pain or asymmetric ROM → AP & Frog Lateral Pelvis
  • Child >8 years with gait change/knee pain → AP & Cross-table Lateral Knee
  • Suspected foot deformities → Weight-bearing Foot X-rays
  • Positive cover-up test for Blount's disease → Standing AP Lower Extremities

Advanced Imaging (CT/MRI/EOS):

Reserved for severe/complex deformities or preoperative planning. MRI/EOS preferred over CT due to lower radiation.

Diagnosis

Normal Rotational Values by Age
Hip Internal Rotation
Infant: 60° | Adolescent: 70° | >2 SD = abnormal
Foot Progression Angle
Infant: < -20° | By 8 years: >30° or <-20° = significant
Thigh-Foot Angle
Infant: 5° internal (range -30° to +20°) | >30° at 8 years = LTT
Transmalleolar Axis
Newborn: -4° | >40° at 7-8 years = LTT
Diagnostic Categories

Common Presentations:

Medial Tibial Torsion (MTT)
Most common cause of in-toeing in children <5 years. Usually resolves spontaneously.
Medial Femoral Torsion (MFT)
Most common cause of in-toeing in children >6 years. Decreases through adolescence.
Lateral Tibial Torsion (LTT)
More problematic than MTT. Less common in infancy but more likely to persist.
Metatarsus Adductus
Flexible forefoot adduction. Usually corrects spontaneously or with stretching.
Critical Differentials
Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis Blount's Disease Child Abuse Club Foot Miserable Malalignment Syndrome

Adolescent with Knee/Hip Pain + Out-toeing = SCFE until proven otherwise!

Treatment

Management by Severity

Within 2 SD of Mean (84-98% of children)

Management: Reassurance and observation only

  • Non-surgical interventions (twister cables, night splints, shoe wedges, physiotherapy) are ineffective
  • Bracing may have negative psychological effects
  • Arrange regular follow-up only if parental anxiety persists

Beyond 2 SD - In-toeing (MTT)

Initial Management: Parental reassurance and education

  • Regular follow-up every 6-12 months
  • Surgery (derotational osteotomy) only if:
    • Age >8 years AND
    • Thigh-foot angle >3 SD beyond mean AND
    • Significant gait disruption
  • Rarely indicated in <1% of cases

Beyond 2 SD - Out-toeing (LTT)

More problematic than in-toeing

  • Surgery indicated if:
    • Age >8 years
    • Thigh-foot angle >40° or >3 SD
    • Significant functional deformity

Medial Femoral Torsion (MFT):

Surgery never indicated prophylactically. Consider only if:

  • Severe deformity >3 SD
  • Medial hip rotation 80-90° or lateral rotation 0°
  • Significant functional disability
  • Age >11 years

Miserable Malalignment Syndrome (MFT + LTT):

Conservative first: Physiotherapy for knee pain/instability

Surgery if persistent: Derotation tibial and femoral osteotomy

Neuromuscular Conditions (Cerebral Palsy):

May require botulinum toxin or soft-tissue procedures before bony surgery

Associated Conditions
Metatarsus Adductus
Flexible: Observe/stretching
Rigid: Serial casting
Severe: Surgery if >4 years
Flat Feet
Painless: Reassurance
Painful: Orthotics
Stiff: Orthopaedic referral
Infantile Blount's
<3 years: Observe
Bracing: Limited effectiveness
Severe: Surgery before age 4
Adolescent Blount's
Requires orthopaedic referral
Surgical correction is mainstay
Emerging Treatments

Rotational Guided Growth: Novel technique using growth plate tethering to gradually correct deformity. Avoids weight-bearing restrictions of osteotomy, but requires further study.

Follow-up

Monitoring Schedule

Within Normal Limits: Routine follow-up not recommended if diagnosis is correct and parents are reassured.

Borderline Cases (2-3 SD): Reassess every 6-12 months until improvement or skeletal maturity.

Post-operative: Regular monitoring for deformity recurrence with continued growth.

Natural History & Prognosis
Tibial Torsion
MTT usually corrects 1-2 years after physiological bowing resolves. LTT less likely to resolve spontaneously.
Femoral Torsion
Femoral anteversion decreases from 40° at birth to 20° by age 10 years. Most in-toeing resolves by age 5-6 years.
Functional Impact
No evidence that torsional variations increase risk of falling or impair function. In-toeing may even benefit sprinters!
Long-term Concerns
Severe malalignment may predispose to premature osteoarthritis. More studies needed to confirm association.
Complications

Non-surgical: Minimal complications. Main issue is parental anxiety and unnecessary interventions.

Surgical Complications:

  • Residual deformity (under/over-correction)
  • Recurrence with continued growth
  • Pseudarthrosis
  • Growth disturbance
  • Nerve injury
Parental Counseling Points
  • Reassurance: Most torsional deformities are normal variations that correct with growth
  • No Evidence: Tripping/falling is NOT caused by in-toeing or flat feet in toddlers
  • Shoes/Braces: Do NOT alter natural history and may have negative psychological effects
  • Spontaneous Resolution: Most correct by age 5-6 years without intervention
