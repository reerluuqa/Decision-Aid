Eating Disorders: 1-Page Clinical Algorithm for Primary Care

Based on NICE CKS Guidance, July 2024

1SUSPECT an Eating Disorder

Be alert in patients presenting with:

  • Weight Concerns: Unusually low or high BMI, rapid weight loss, or faltering growth in children.
  • Behavioral Changes: Restrictive dieting, changes in eating habits, disproportionate concern with body weight/shape, excessive exercise.
  • Mental Health: New or worsening anxiety, depression, social withdrawal, or obsessive traits.
  • Physical Symptoms: Fatigue, dizziness, fainting, palpitations, GI symptoms (constipation, reflux), menstrual irregularity/amenorrhoea.
  • High-Risk Groups: Adolescents (peak onset 13-17), athletes, dancers, models.

2INITIAL ASSESSMENT in Primary Care

History

  • Eating/Weight: Dietary restriction, binge eating, purging (vomiting, laxatives), fear of weight gain.
  • Physical Health: Ask about fatigue, syncope, palpitations, GI symptoms, and amenorrhoea.
  • Mental Health: Screen for depression, anxiety, self-harm, and suicide risk.

Examination

  • BMI: Calculate and compare with previous records. Use centile charts for patients <18.
  • Vital Signs: Temperature (hypothermia), Pulse (bradycardia <50 bpm), Blood Pressure (hypotension & postural drop).
  • Muscle Strength: Assess with the Sit upâ€“Squatâ€“Stand (SUSS) test.

Investigations

  • Bloods: Urea & Electrolytes (check for hypokalaemia), FBC, LFTs. Note: Normal bloods do not rule out serious risk.
  • ECG: Essential for anyone with rapid weight loss, purging, bradycardia, hypotension, or electrolyte imbalance.

Seek immediate specialist advice if unsure.

  • Physical Instability: Rapid weight loss (>1kg/week), Bradycardia (<40 bpm), Postural tachycardia, Hypotension, Hypothermia, Poor SUSS test, Significant electrolyte disturbance.
  • Acute Mental Health Risk: Active suicidal ideation or intent, significant self-harm.
  • Social Crisis: Lack of support or unsafe home environment.

IF RED FLAGS PRESENT

URGENTLY ADMIT or discuss with a specialist (Eating Disorder service, Liaison Psychiatry, Paediatrician/Physician) for advice on the most appropriate setting. Consider use of the Mental Health Act if the patient lacks capacity and refuses admission.

IF NO RED FLAGS

REFER IMMEDIATELY to an age-appropriate Eating Disorder Service (e.g., CAMHS for <18s, CMHT for adults). Do not "watch and wait."

4MANAGEMENT While Awaiting Specialist Assessment

  • Monitor Closely: Arrange regular reviews (e.g., weekly for children) to re-assess physical and mental health risk.
  • Coordinate Care: Liaise with specialists, especially for patients with co-morbidities like diabetes.
  • Prescribing Caution: Be aware that malnutrition and purging can increase the risk of medication side effects (especially cardiac).
  • Provide Support: Offer information to the patient and their family/carers from reputable sources (e.g., Beat Eating Disorders, NHS).
