Minimal Change Disease

Quick Reference Guide - Pediatric & Adult

⚠
EMERGENCY RED FLAGS: Severe edema with respiratory distress | BP >95th percentile | AKI (creatinine ↑) | Signs of peritonitis
1
Initial Assessment

Key Symptoms

  • Facial edema (morning)
  • Peripheral edema
  • Recent viral illness
  • Age 1-8 years

First Tests

  • Urinalysis (3+ to 4+ protein)
  • Urine P/C ratio ≥200 mg/mmol
  • Serum albumin <30 g/L
  • Normal C3 complement
2
Treatment Protocol
Prednisolone (Children)
2 mg/kg/day (max 60mg) × 4-6 weeks
Then 1.5 mg/kg alternate days × 4-6 weeks
Prednisolone (Adults)
1 mg/kg/day (max 80mg) × 4-16 weeks
Taper over ≥24 weeks after remission
3
Corticosteroid Response
Response Type Definition Action
SSNS Remission within 4 weeks Continue tapering schedule
Partial Improvement but not remission Extend 2 more weeks
SRNS No remission at 4 weeks
4
Relapse Management

Infrequent Relapse

<2 relapses/6 months OR <4 relapses/12 months

Treatment: Same as initial presentation

Frequent Relapse

≥2 relapses/6 months OR ≥4 relapses/12 months

Add: Corticosteroid-sparing agent

Corticosteroid-Dependent

Relapse during taper or within 2 weeks of stopping

Add: Second-line immunosuppressant

5
Second-line Options
Cyclophosphamide
2 mg/kg/day × 8-12 weeks
Max cumulative: 168 mg/kg
Ciclosporin
4-5 mg/kg/day (children)
3-5 mg/kg/day (adults)
Tacrolimus
0.1 mg/kg/day (children)
0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day (adults)
Rituximab
Consult specialist
For difficult cases
6
Edema Management

Standard Measures

  • Low-salt diet
  • Fluid restriction if overloaded
  • Daily weight monitoring

Severe Edema

  • 25% albumin + furosemide IV
  • Monitor BP and respiratory status
  • Watch for hypertension
7
Complications & Monitoring

Short-term Risks

Complication Signs Action
Peritonitis Fever, abdominal pain IV antibiotics immediately
Thrombosis Swelling, pain Anticoagulation

Long-term Monitoring

  • BP, weight, growth (each visit)
  • Urea, creatinine, urinalysis (6 monthly)
  • FBC, LFTs (on immunosuppression)
  • Vitamin D levels (frequent relapsers)
  • Calcium supplementation
8
Prognosis & Parent Education

Excellent Prognosis

  • 85-90% respond to steroids
  • Most outgrow by teenage years
  • Rare progression to ESRD
  • Normal adult life expected

Key Parent Points

  • Home urine dipstick monitoring
  • Report relapses early
  • No live vaccines on steroids
  • Infection risk when nephrotic
  • Transition to adult care
