Beta-Thalassaemia

Last Updated: Dec 2023
Quick-Scan Clinical Tool
Global Context: High prevalence in Mediterranean, Middle East, India, SE Asia. Always screen ancestry.

Clinical Spectrum

Trait / Minor
ROUTINE

Clinical

  • Asymptomatic
  • Mild microcytic anaemia
  • Normal growth

Key Labs

  • Mild ↓ Hb
  • ↑ HbA2 (>3.5%)
  • ↑ Hb F (sometimes)

Action

DO NOT give iron unless iron deficiency is proven. Genetic counseling required.

Intermedia
VARIABLE

Clinical

  • Diagnosis: Toddler/Older child
  • Moderate anaemia (Hb 7-10 g/dL)
  • Skeletal changes, Hepatosplenomegaly

Pathophys

  • β+ mutations (some β-globin made)
  • Ineffective erythropoiesis
  • Iron absorption ↑ (Even without transfusion)

Action

Transfusion only if symptomatic (infection, pregnancy, failure to thrive). Monitor Iron Overload!

Major (Cooley's)
URGENT / TRANSFUSION DEPENDENT

Clinical

  • Onset: 4-6 months (Hb F switch)
  • Severe anaemia, Failure to thrive
  • Chipmunk facies, Frontal bossing

Pathophys

  • β0 mutations (No β-globin)
  • No Hb A. Hb F high.
  • Death in 1st/2nd year without treatment.

Action

Immediate Haematology referral. Regular transfusions required.

Aetiology & Genetics

Chromosome 11 mutation. Autosomal Recessive. Compound heterozygosity (e.g., HbE + Beta-thal) is common in SE Asia and can be severe.

Screening Tips

Look for Mentzer Index < 13 (Thalassaemia) vs > 13 (Iron Deficiency).

Check RDW: Normal in Thalassaemia trait, High in Iron Deficiency.

Diagnostic Workup

Key History Factors
  • Ancestry: Mediterranean, Middle East, Asian.
  • Family History: Anaemia, Transfusion dependence, Unexplained jaundice.
  • Symptoms: Lethargy, Abdominal distension, Pallor, Failure to thrive.
Physical Exam Signs
  • Skeletal: Frontal/Parietal bossing, Chipmunk facies.
  • Abd: Hepatosplenomegaly (Marked).
  • General: Jaundice (Mild), Gallstones risk.

Differential Diagnoses

Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Differentiate via Ferritin/Iron studies and Mentzer Index.

Sickle Cell / HbE

Confirm via Hb Electrophoresis. Compound heterozygosity is possible (Thal + HbE).

Treatment Strategy

Goal of Management: Maintain Hb > 9.5-10 g/dL for normal growth & suppress ineffective erythropoiesis.
Transfusion Regimen (Major)

Protocol

  • Infants: Split units every 4 weeks.
  • Children (18mo - 2yr): 1 unit packed RBCs every 3-4 weeks.
  • Adults: 2 units every 2-3 weeks.
  • Target: Pre-transfusion Hb > 9.5 g/dL.
Product: Leuko-reduced packed red cells.
Iron Chelation (Critical)
Trigger: Start after 6-8 units transfused (approx. age 2) or MRI shows LIC > 5 mg/g dry weight.

1. Desferrioxamine (SC/IV)

  • First choice.
  • Risk: Auditory/Visual toxicity. Monitor eyes/ears q3mo.

2. Deferasirox (Oral)

  • Once daily. Good adherence.
  • Risk: Renal/Hepatic toxicity. Contraindicated in severe renal failure.

3. Deferiprone (Oral)

  • 3x daily. Good for cardiac iron.
  • RISK: Agranulocytosis / Neutropenia. Monitor CBC weekly.
Curative Therapy: HSCT

Allogeneic Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant

  • Only cure currently available.
  • Best outcome: Matched sibling donor.
  • Timing: Perform early, before organ damage from iron overload.
  • Pesaro Class 1 & 2 have best prognosis.
Intermedia Specifics
  • Transfusion: Only during stress (infection, surgery, pregnancy).
  • Splenectomy: Often considered if massive splenomegaly causing discomfort or cytopenias.
  • Iron: Still monitor iron! Gut absorption is increased even without transfusions.

Red Flags & Complications

Immediate Concerns
  • Severe Anaemia / Heart Failure: High output failure in untreated Major.
  • Sepsis Post-Splenectomy: Overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI).
    Action: Vaccinate & Prompt Antibiotics.
  • Agranulocytosis: If on Deferiprone.
    Action: Stop drug immediately.
Long Term Toxicities
Heart Cardiac Iron Overload (T2* MRI < 20ms = Risk). Leads to Arrhythmia/Failure.
Endocrine Diabetes, Hypothyroidism, Hypoparathyroidism.
Liver Fibrosis, Cirrhosis (LIC > 7 mg/g).
Skeletal Osteoporosis, Pathological fractures.
Monitoring Schedule (Summary)

Transfusion Dependent

  • Every visit: Physical exam, weight/height.
  • Every 3 months: Serum Ferritin, LFTs, Thyroid, Glucose.
  • Annually: Cardiac T2* MRI, Liver Iron Concentration (MRI), Opthalmology/Audiology (if on Desferrioxamine).

Non-Transfusion Dependent (Intermedia)

  • Every 6-12 months: Liver Iron Concentration (MRI) - even without transfusions!
  • Regular: Monitor leg ulcers, pulmonary hypertension, and thrombotic risk.
