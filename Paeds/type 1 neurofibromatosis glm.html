NF1 Clinical Reference

Auto-dominant • Neurofibromin deficiency

Immediate Concerns

Do not miss: Rapidly enlarging mass, unremitting pain, new neurological deficit → Suspect MPNST.
Action: Urgent MRI, Biopsy, Surgical/Oncology Referral.

Key Differential Diagnoses
Condition Key Differentiator from NF1 Genetic/Test Marker
Neurofibromatosis Type 2 (NF2) Bilateral vestibular schwannomas, few/no CAL spots. NF2 gene mutation (Chr 22)
Legius Syndrome (NF1-like) Café au lait spots + freckling, but NO neurofibromas, NO Lisch nodules. SPRED1 gene mutation
McCune-Albright Polyostotic fibrous dysplasia, precocious puberty. GNAS1 gene mutation
Schwannomatosis Multiple schwannomas, no vestibular tumors, little/no skin pigment. SMARCB1/ LZTR1
Congenital MMR Deficiency Ragged café au lait edges, early onset cancers. PMS2, MLH1, MSH6, MSH2
Investigation Strategy

Baseline Imaging

  • MRI Brain: Optic pathway glioma screening.
  • Whole Body MRI: Assess internal tumour burden (especially at transition to adulthood).

Genetics

  • RNA-based testing: Recommended to confirm NF1 mutation.
  • Mosaic NF1: Suspected if segmental findings; test <100% cells.

Oncology Surveillance

  • Women ≥30-35 yrs: Early breast screening (Mammogram/MRI).
  • PET Scan: Differentiate MPNST vs benign neurofibroma.

Patient Discussion Points

  • Inheritance: Autosomal dominant (50% risk to offspring). Variable expressivity.
  • Progression: Condition is progressive and variable. New tumors can appear.
  • Complications: Regular follow-up is crucial to catch MPNST early.
  • Pregnancy: Often causes rapid growth of neurofibromas.
  • Psychosocial: Cosmetic impact of neurofibromas; support groups recommended.
