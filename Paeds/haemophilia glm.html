Management Basics

Critical Alerts

Intracranial Haemorrhage URGENT
Context: 3-5% of newborns with severe haemophilia present with ICH. May be spontaneous or due to trauma.
Signs: Hypoactivity, bulging/tense fontanelle, seizures, irritability, pallor.
Adult Signs: Headache, vomiting, focal neurological deficits.
ACTION: Immediate imaging (CT/MRI) & Factor Replacement.
Iliopsoas Bleed URGENT
Presentation: Severe lower abdominal, upper thigh, and/or lower back pain.
Key Sign: Pain on extension of hip, not on rotation. Characteristic gait (flexed hip, inward rotation).
Risk: Femoral nerve compression (loss of patellar reflex).
ACTION: Urgent factor replacement & ultrasound/CT evaluation.

Typical Presentation

Neonates
  • Prolonged bleeding after heel prick
  • Prolonged bleeding after circumcision (50% occurrence)
  • Cephalohematoma
  • Intracranial haemorrhage
Children
  • Haemarthrosis: Knees, ankles, elbows (Pain, swelling, limited ROM)
  • Easy bruising / Haematomas
  • Bleeding after dental work
  • Excessive bleeding after trauma
Adults / Carriers
  • Menorrhagia (female carriers)
  • Bleeding postpartum/surgery
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding
  • Haematuria

Lab Result Decoder

Follow the workflow to interpret aPTT findings.

Step 1: Initial aPTT

Is the Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (aPTT) prolonged?

Mild Haemophilia Possible
aPTT may be normal in mild cases (>30% Factor levels). If clinically suspected (bleeding history), proceed directly to Factor VIII and IX Assays.

Step 2: Mixing Study

Incubate patient plasma with normal plasma (1:1) for 2 hours at 37Â°C.

Factor Deficiency Confirmed
Proceed to Factor VIII and IX Assays to determine type and severity.

Step 3: Inhibitor Detected

Failure to correct suggests an inhibitor (antibody).

Critical Check: Emicizumab

Is the patient on Emicizumab prophylaxis?

False Positive: Emicizumab interferes with aPTT (shortens it). Do not rely on aPTT. Use Chromogenic Assay with Bovine Reagents.
Action: Perform Bethesda Assay (or Nijmegen-modified) to quantify inhibitor titer (High vs Low responder).

Severity Classification

Grade Factor Level (% of normal) Clinical Presentation Frequency of Bleeds
Severe < 1% Frequent spontaneous bleeding (joints/muscles) Weekly (without prophylaxis)
Moderate 1% - 5% Occasional spontaneous bleeding; severe after trauma Monthly or less
Mild > 5% - 40% Severe bleeding only after trauma or surgery Yearly or less (usually triggered)
Inheritance Pattern

X-linked Recessive

  • Affects almost exclusively males
  • ~30% of female carriers have low factor levels (lyonization)
  • ~1/3 of cases are spontaneous mutations (no family history)
Complications

Inhibitors (Antibodies)

  • Develops in ~30% of severe Haemophilia A
  • Up to 5% in Haemophilia B
  • Risk Factors: High intensity treatment, specific gene mutations (inversions), African-Caribbean/Hispanic ethnicity

Management Overview

Acute Bleed

Immediate Action:

  • Administer Factor VIII or IX concentrate ASAP.
  • Rule: "When in doubt, treat."
  • R.I.C.E. (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) for musculoskeletal bleeds.
Prophylaxis

Primary Prevention:

  • Regular factor infusion to prevent bleeds.
  • Standard of care for severe haemophilia.
  • Converts phenotype from severe to moderate/mild.
Monitoring

Inhibitor Screening: Check regularly (every 3-12 months) or after intensive exposure (5+ days). Screen before surgery.

Physiotherapy: Essential to maintain joint range of motion and prevent arthropathy.
