Otitis Media with Effusion (OME)

Clinical Decision Support Tool | Ages 0-12 years | NICE CKS November 2025

Assessment

History Taking

Newborn hearing screening: Review results from newborn hearing test
Hearing symptoms:
  • Mishearing or asking for repetitions
  • Difficulty communicating in groups
  • Excessively high TV volume
  • Fluctuating hearing loss
Ear symptoms: Mild intermittent ear pain, fullness, 'popping', tinnitus
Discharge: Persistent foul-smelling discharge = URGENT referral
Infection history: Recurrent AOM, URTIs, nasal obstruction, rhinorrhoea
Risk factors: Snoring, thumb/dummy sucking, bottle feeding, household smoking

Examination

Otoscopy findings: Normal-looking TM does NOT exclude OME
Effusion indicators:
  • Abnormal drum color (yellow, amber, blue)
  • Loss/diffuse light reflex
  • Opacification (not from scarring)
  • Air bubbles or fluid level
  • Retracted/concave drum
Predisposing factors: Craniofacial anomalies, adenoid hypertrophy, asthma, eczema

Impact Assessment

Concentration/attention issues Social withdrawal Speech/language delay Balance problems/clumsiness School performance decline

Formal Assessment Required

Hearing testing and tympanometry should be performed by trained staff to confirm diagnosis in children with suspected OME and hearing loss.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Features

Feature Acute Otitis Media Otitis Media with Effusion
Earache/Fever/Irritability Present Usually absent
Middle ear effusion Present Present
Opaque drum Present May be absent
Bulging drum May be present Usually absent; retracted
Impaired drum mobility Present Present
Hearing loss Present, not predominant Usually present

Investigations

Pneumatic otoscopy: Primary diagnostic tool for OME
Tympanometry:
  • Sensitivity: 84-93%
  • Specificity: 70-79%
  • Improves diagnostic accuracy
Audiometry: Development-appropriate testing
  • Visual response audiometry: 8 months to 2.5 years
  • Conventional audiometry: 4+ years

Differential Diagnosis

Acute otitis media (AOM) Mastoiditis Otitis externa Referred pain (teeth/TMJ) Otitic barotrauma Foreign body Impacted earwax Sensorineural hearing loss

Key Differentiator

Pain and systemic upset (fever, irritability) are characteristic of AOM but usually absent in OME. OME often follows AOM.

Treatment & Management

Contraindicated Treatments

NO Evidence for These Treatments

Antibiotics Antihistamines Mucolytics Decongestants Corticosteroids Leukotriene receptor antagonists PPIs / Anti-reflux meds

Also: Homeopathy, cranial osteopathy, acupuncture, dietary modifications, probiotics, massage

Conservative Management

No hearing loss: Reassurance only - OME usually resolves spontaneously within 3 months
With hearing loss: Active observation period 3 months
  • Regular follow-up with repeated history & examination
  • Audiology assessment
  • Speech/language assessment if indicated

Non-Surgical Options

Autoinflation: Consider in cooperative children
  • Usually older children without ear pain
  • Can coordinate device use
Hearing aids: Alternative to surgery when contraindicated/unacceptable
  • Persistent bilateral OME with hearing loss
  • Suitable for children where surgery not appropriate

Surgical Options

Myringotomy & grommets (ventilation tubes): Most common surgical option
  • Small incision in eardrum to drain fluid
  • Hollow tube placed to equalize pressure
  • Water precautions for 2 weeks post-op
  • Grommets fall out as eardrum grows
  • Immediate hearing improvement, less effective at 6-9 months
Adjuvant adenoidectomy: Consider with grommets for children >4 years

Referral Indications

Refer to ENT when hearing loss is associated with significant developmental, social, or educational impact, or for children with craniofacial anomalies.

Follow-up & Parent Advice

Post-Grommet Management

Follow-up schedule:
  • Periodic review and hearing re-assessment
  • Hearing test 1 year post-surgery
  • Refer back to ENT if symptoms recur
Complications to monitor:
Otorrhoea (1 in 5-10 children) Tympanosclerosis (1 in 3 children <5y) Perforation/atelectasis Cholesteatoma
Otorrhoea management: Topical non-ototoxic antibiotics (e.g., ciprofloxacin) for 5-7 days

Parent & Carer Advice

Information provision:
  • Nature, causes, and fluctuating course of OME
  • Impact on hearing, language, behavior, and social wellbeing
  • Prognosis: 95% resolve by age 10 years
  • No proven benefit from medications or alternative therapies
Risk factor counseling: Parental smoking increases risk Breastfeeding reduces risk
Communication strategies:
  • Be close and face child when speaking
  • Minimize background noise
  • Use visual aids
  • Inform teachers and request classroom adjustments

Prognosis

Natural resolution:
  • Usually resolves spontaneously within 3 months
  • 22% resolve at 1 year, 50% at 3 years
  • 70% at 5 years, 95% at 10 years
  • Older children more likely to resolve quickly
Persistent risk factors:
Bilateral OME Winter months Recurrent AOM history Sibling history

Key Message

OME has an excellent prognosis. Most children require only observation and support, with surgery reserved for persistent cases with significant impact.
