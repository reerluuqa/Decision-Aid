Critical Red Flags

  • High fever (>38.5¬∞C / 101¬∞F)
  • Arthralgias & myalgias - joint and muscle pain
  • Hepatosplenomegaly - enlarged liver/spleen
  • Osteolytic bone lesions on X-ray
Action Required

IMMEDIATE REFERRAL to dermatology/inpatient care. May require systemic corticosteroids + isotretinoin (sequential).

Severe Nodulocystic Acne
  • Multiple large nodules (>5mm) and cysts
  • Painful, tender to palpation
  • Risk of scarring - hypertrophic/keloidal
  • Failed conventional therapy after 6+ months
  • Psychological distress - depression/anxiety
Consider Urgent Referral

Oral isotretinoin indicated. Monitor for depression. Requires specialist oversight in patients <18 years.

Patient Education Points

Skin Care Instructions
  • Use non-comedogenic products only (makeup, moisturizers)
  • Gentle cleansing: Twice daily with pH-neutral/slightly acidic cleanser
  • Avoid irritants: No scrubs, astringents, or fragranced products
  • NO picking/squeezing - increases scarring risk
  • Keep hair products away from face
Sun Protection & Expectations
  • Daily broad-spectrum SPF 30+ - some medications increase sun sensitivity
  • Results take 6-12 weeks - manage patient expectations
  • Initial worsening may occur with retinoids (purge period)
  • Continue maintenance therapy after clearance to prevent relapse
  • Diet: High glycemic foods may exacerbate - limited evidence

Special Populations & Considerations

Pregnancy Considerations

SAFE in pregnancy:

  • Topical Clindamycin
  • Topical Erythromycin
  • Azelaic acid 20%
  • Benzoyl peroxide
üö´ AVOID in Pregnancy

Oral tetracyclines (teeth discoloration), oral retinoids (teratogenic), topical retinoids, salicylic acid (high concentration).

Pediatric & Adolescent
  • Age 12+: Most topicals approved (clascoterone, salicylic acid)
  • Oral antibiotics: Tetracyclines avoid <8 years (teeth)
  • Isotretinoin: <12 years = specialist only; 12-17 requires dual prescriber
  • Psychosocial impact: Screen for depression/anxiety
  • Family history: Predicts severity - early intervention recommended
Drug-Induced Acneiform Eruptions

Common culprits:

  • Androgens - testosterone, anabolic steroids
  • Corticosteroids - topical or systemic
  • Anticonvulsants - phenytoin, carbamazepine
  • Lithium, Isoniazid
  • JAK inhibitors, ACTH

If drug-induced, consider discontinuation or substitution if clinically feasible.

PCOS Association
  • Consider PCOS in women with: acne + obesity + hirsutism + irregular periods
  • Screen: Free testosterone, LH/FSH ratio, 17-OH progesterone
  • Don't delay acne treatment while investigating PCOS
  • Hormonal therapy (COC, spironolactone) particularly beneficial
