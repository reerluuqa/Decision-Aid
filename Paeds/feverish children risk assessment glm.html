Feverish Children (0-5y) Risk Assessment

Based on NICE Guidelines [Last revised June 2023]
Quick-scan tool for primary healthcare professionals. Use for assessment and initial management.

Risk Stratification (NICE Traffic Light)

Identify the highest level of risk present. If any feature from a category exists, the child falls into that risk category.

Green (Low Risk)

Colour Normal colour of skin, lips, tongue.
Activity Responds normally to social cues. Content/smiles. Stays awake or awakens quickly. Strong normal cry or not crying.
Respiratory None of the amber or red symptoms.
Circulation Normal skin and eyes. Moist mucous membranes. Warm peripheries.
Hydration Normal urine output, moist mouth.
If no Amber or Red features present.

Amber (Intermediate Risk)

Colour Pallor reported by parent/carer.
Activity Does not respond normally to social cues. No smile. Wakes only with prolonged stimulation. Decreased activity.
Respiratory Nasal flaring.
Tachypnoea (6-12m: >50bpm; >12m: >40bpm).
Saturation ≤95% (air). Crackles on auscultation.
Circulation Tachycardia (<12m: >160bpm; 12-24m: >150bpm; 2-5y: >140bpm). Poor feeding in infants. Dry mucous membranes.
Hydration CRT ≥3 secs. Reduced urine output (dry nappies). Reduced skin turgor.
Other Temp ≥39°C in age 3-6m. Fever ≥5 days. Rigors. Swelling of limb/joint. Non-blanching rash (without other red features). Swelling of limb/joint. Non-weight bearing.

Red (High Risk)

Colour Pale, mottled, ashen, or blue.
Activity No response to social cues. Appears ill to healthcare professional. Does not wake / if roused does not stay awake. Weak, high-pitched or continuous cry.
Respiratory Grunting. Tachypnoea (RR >60). Moderate or severe chest indrawing.
Temperature Temp ≥38°C in infants <3 months.
Critical Signs Non-blanching rash. Bulging fontanelle. Neck stiffness. Focal neurological signs. Focal seizures. Status epilepticus.

Red Features Management

If Red features suggesting serious/life-threatening cause (Sepsis, Meningitis, Pneumonia, Encephalitis):

  • Transfer: Arrange emergency ambulance transfer to A&E.
  • Urgent F2F: If non-life-threatening red features (e.g. isolated fever >38C under 3m without toxicity) were assessed by phone, arrange urgent (within 2 hours) face-to-face assessment.
  • Consider: Meningitis, Sepsis, Herpes Simplex Encephalitis.

Amber Features Management

If Amber features (but no Red):

  • Assessment: Arrange face-to-face assessment (urgency depends on clinical judgement) if done by phone.
  • Consider: Pneumonia, UTI, Septic Arthritis, Kawasaki disease.
  • Specialist Referral: Immediate referral to paediatric specialist for infants <3 months with suspected UTI and no alternative focus.
  • Admission: Consider if parents/carers seeking help repeatedly, illness prolonged/unclear, or high anxiety due to previous bad experience.

Green Features Management

Can be managed at home

  • Assess and manage underlying cause if appropriate.
  • Consider urine dipstick/microscopy if unexplained fever and no focus.
  • Provide safety netting advice (see Meds & Care tab).

Safety Netting

Parents/carers should be advised to seek medical review if:

  • Fever lasts >5 days.
  • Child becomes more unwell.
  • Non-blanching rash / signs of CNS infection.
  • Seizure occurs.
  • Signs of dehydration develop.

Self-Care Advice

  • Fluids: Encourage regular fluids. If breastfeeding, continue as normal.
  • Monitoring: Check child regularly, including at night.
  • Clothing: Dress appropriately for environment. Do not over-wrap.
  • Environment: Keep room ventilated.
  • Sponging: Do NOT use tepid sponging to lower temperature.
  • Rest: Keep away from school/nursery until recovered.

Clinician Tips

  • Parental Perception: Accept reported fever by parents as valid.
  • Temp Measurement: Under 4wks = electronic axilla. 4wks-5y = axilla or infra-red tympanic. Do NOT use forehead strips.
  • Antipyretics: Do not routinely give to prevent febrile seizures. They do not prevent recurrence.
  • Response to Meds: Do not rely on temp decrease (or lack thereof) to differentiate serious vs non-serious illness.
  • Vaccination: Note that some vaccines may induce fever in <3 month olds.
