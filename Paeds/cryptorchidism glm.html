Immediate Action Required

Bilateral Non-palpable testes OR Hypospadias + Cryptorchidism: Refer immediately for DSD (Disorder of Sex Development) workup including Karyotype & Biochemical tests.

Overview
Theory

Cryptorchidism (Undescended Testis): One or both testes not present within the dependent portion of the scrotal sac.

Epidemiology: 2-8% in term newborns; drops to 1-2% after few months. 70% palpable, 30% non-palpable.

Key Timing: Spontaneous descent unlikely after 6 months corrected gestational age.

Do NOT Order Routinely:

  • Ultrasound or MRI for initial diagnosis (low sensitivity).
  • hCG stimulation test (rarely required).
Risk Factors
  • Prematurity (Incidence up to 45%)
  • Low Birth Weight / SGA
  • Family History (up to 23%)
  • Gestational Diabetes / Maternal Smoking
Management Algorithm
1
Referral & Surgery

Refer by 6 months corrected age. Surgery within the next year.

2
Orchiopexy

Inguinal or scrotal incision. Concurrent inguinal hernia repair.

Optimal timing: 9-18 months age.
1
Exam Under Anaesthesia

Locate testis.

2
If Palpable: Proceed to Orchiopexy.
If Not Palpable: Surgical Exploration (Laparoscopic).
3
Findings:
• Intra-abdominal testis: Orchiopexy (1 or 2 stage).
• Nubbin/Vanishing testis: Excise remnant.
• Blind ending vessels: Terminate.
CRITICAL: Check for DSD first
1
Endocrine/Genetic Evaluation

Karyotype, Inhibin B, MIS, FSH.

2
If Anorchia (No testes):
Testosterone supplementation & Fertility counseling.
3
If Testes Present:
Surgical exploration & Orchiopexy.
Risk: Significant increase in malignancy risk if treated after puberty.
1
Options:
  • Orchiopexy: + Biopsy (if spermatogenesis possible).
  • Orchiectomy: Often recommended due to high cancer risk.
2
If Mass Present:

Tumour markers -> Radical Orchiectomy.

3
Palliative:

If >50 yrs or high comorbidity: Observation (risk of anesthesia > risk of neoplasia).

Prognosis & Complications
Risks of Delay
  • Testicular Cancer (3-10x increase)
  • Reduced Fertility
  • Testicular Torsion
Successful Outcome

92-95% success for testes beyond external ring if treated early.

Clinical Pearls
  • Retractile Testis: No surgery, but observe annually for ascent.
  • Acquired Cryptorchidism: "Ascending" testes require referral.
  • Hormonal Therapy: hCG/GnRH generally not recommended first-line.
