Kawasaki Disease

Pediatric Vasculitis • Quick Reference
Updated: Nov 2025
BMJ Best Practice Source

RED FLAGS: COMPLICATIONS & EMERGENCY

Coronary Artery Aneurysm (CAA): Affects 20-25% of untreated patients. Risk is higher in infants <6 months (up to 39% even with treatment).

Kawasaki Shock Syndrome: Vasodilatory shock, hypotension, poor perfusion. Requires ICU care.

Rule of 10: Treatment is most effective if initiated within 10 days of fever onset.

At a Glance

Definition: Acute, self-limiting systemic vasculitis of unknown etiology.

Target Demographic: Children < 5 years (peak 6-24 months). Rare in < 3 months.

Leading cause: Acquired heart disease in children in developed nations.

Key Risk Factors

  • • Asian Ancestry (Highest incidence in Japan/Korea)
  • • Age < 6 months (Higher risk of CAA)
  • • Male Sex (M:F ratio 1.5:1)

Diagnostic Checklist

Interactive

AHA Criteria: Fever (≥5 days) + ≥4 of the criteria below.
(Diagnosis can be made earlier if 4 criteria are met with classic presentation).

Fever
High grade (>39°C), usually ≥5 days, unresponsive to antibiotics.
Conjunctivitis
Bilateral, non-purulent, bulbar injection. (No exudate).
Oral Mucositis
Erythema, "Strawberry tongue", cracked/fissured lips. (No pharyngeal exudate).
Extremity Changes
Acute: Erythema/edema of hands/feet.
Subacute: Periungual desquamation (weeks 2-3).
Rash
Polymorphous, erythematous, maculopapular. No vesicles/crusting.
Cervical Lymphadenopathy
Unilateral, ≥1.5 cm, usually non-suppurative.

Standard Management

Primary Treatment Goal

Reduce inflammation & prevent Coronary Artery Aneurysms (CAA). Start immediately upon diagnosis.

1
IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)

2 g/kg as a single infusion over 10-12 hours.

2
Aspirin

High dose (80-100 mg/kg/day) initially, until afebrile for 48-72h. Then switch to low dose (3-5 mg/kg/day) for 6-8 weeks.

IVIG Resistant?

If fever persists 36h post-IVIG: Consider second dose of IVIG, Corticosteroids, or Infliximab (TNF-alpha inhibitor).

Investigations & Monitoring

Baseline Labs

  • CRP / ESR: Elevated in acute phase. CRP normalizes faster.
  • FBC: Leukocytosis, normocytic anemia. Thrombocytosis in subacute phase (week 2).
  • LFTs: Transaminitis common (40%).
  • Urinalysis: Sterile pyuria (50%).

Echocardiogram Schedule

Timing Action Notes
At Diagnosis Perform Echo Do not delay treatment for Echo. Baseline for Coronary Z-score.
2 Weeks Repeat Echo Check for developing CAA.
4-6 Weeks Repeat Echo If initial echo was normal or labs abnormal.

Differentials & "Incomplete" KD

Consider Incomplete KD if:

Fever ≥ 5 days + 2-3 clinical criteria + abnormal lab markers (CRP ≥3.0, ESR ≥40) OR positive Echo.

High Risk Infants < 6 months often present with only fever. Treat promptly.

Common Mimics

  • Scarlet Fever (Exudative pharyngitis, circumoral pallor)
  • Measles (Koplik spots, cough, coryza)
  • Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome
  • Adenovirus / EBV
  • Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis
