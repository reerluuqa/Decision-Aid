IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura)

The most common vasculitis of childhood. Presents with purpuric rash, abdominal pain, arthritis/arthralgia, and glomerulonephritis.

94% Complete Recovery Rate in Children
2-10 yrs Peak Age of Onset (4-7 yrs)
13.5-27 Cases per 100,000/year
M:F 2:1 Male:Female Ratio
🔍

Diagnostic Criteria (EULAR/PRINTO/PRES)

  • Mandatory Purpura or petechiae with lower limb predominance
  • +1 Required Abdominal pain (diffuse, colicky)
  • +1 Required Histopathology: IgA deposition
  • +1 Required Arthritis or arthralgia (knees, ankles)
  • +1 Required Renal involvement (hematuria, proteinuria)
SENSITIVITY: 87.1% | SPECIFICITY: 87.7%

IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED - Red Flags

🚨

Life-Threatening

⚠

Urgent Hospital Admission Required

  • Pulmonary haemorrhage
  • Cerebral vasculitis
  • Severe organ involvement
  • Shock or cardiovascular collapse

Treatment: IV corticosteroids + immunosuppressants immediately

Consider: Plasmapheresis for severe cases

🍴

Gastrointestinal Emergency

⚠

Exclude Intussusception

  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Rectal bleeding
  • Vomiting (especially bilious)
  • Abdominal mass

Action: Urgent abdominal ultrasound

Most common: Ileocolic intussusception

💰

Renal Emergency

  • ⚠ Severe proteinuria >250 mg/mmol
  • ⚠ Acute kidney injury (rising creatinine)
  • ⚠ Nephrotic syndrome
  • ⚠ Nephritic syndrome
  • ⚠ Hypertension with renal involvement

Action: Urgent nephrology referral

⚠

NSAID Warning

Concerns with NSAID use in IgAV:

  • ⚠ Renal toxicity
  • ⚠ Cardiovascular events
  • ⚠ GI bleeding

Recommendation: Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration. Avoid if renal involvement or GI symptoms present.

📋

Monitoring Protocol

Initial Assessment

Blood pressure, urinalysis, serum creatinine, urine protein:creatinine ratio

First 4 Weeks

Weekly BP checks, urinalysis monitoring

Up to 6 Months

Continue regular urinalysis checks in ALL patients

Renal Biopsy Indicators

Severe proteinuria >4 weeks, persistent moderate proteinuria, or impaired GFR

👥

When to Refer

Urgent (Nephrology)

  • → Moderate-severe nephritis
  • → Acute kidney injury
  • → Nephrotic/nephritic syndrome
  • → Severe proteinuria

Standard (Rheumatology)

  • → Diagnostic uncertainty
  • → Severe extrarenal symptoms
  • → Refractory disease
  • → Relapsing disease
✅

Prognosis

94%
Complete Recovery in Children
  • ✓ Most resolve spontaneously over weeks to months
  • ✓ Rash typically resolves within 3-4 weeks
  • ✓ Arthritis/arthralgia: no long-term problems
  • ✓ Long-term complications rare but CKD risk exists
📈

Risk Factors for Poor Prognosis

  • ⚠ Age >6.5 years at onset
  • ⚠ Severe renal involvement at presentation
  • ⚠ Nephrotic syndrome
  • ⚠ Impaired GFR at onset
  • ⚠ Extensive crescents on biopsy (>50%)
📅

Follow-up Timeline & Key Actions

Acute Phase (0-4 weeks)

  • 🔔 Week 1-2: Symptomatic treatment, monitor for complications
  • 🔔 Week 2-4: Assess renal function, urine protein
  • 🔔 Ongoing: Exclude intussusception if abdominal symptoms

Long-term Monitoring (6+ months)

  • 🔔 Months 1-6: Regular urinalysis monitoring
  • 🔔 BP monitoring: Continue throughout
  • 🔔 Renal follow-up: If nephritis present
