RSV Infection Management

Evidence-based clinical algorithm for respiratory syncytial virus infection management based on BMJ Best Practice guidelines (July 2025)

Assessment

High-Risk Groups

Urgent Attention Required
Strong Risk Factors
  • History of prematurity (<35 weeks)
  • Age <6 months at start of RSV season
  • Chronic lung disease (e.g., BPD)
  • Complex congenital heart disease
  • Immune deficiency
  • Indigenous/American-Indian/Alaska Native infants
  • Down's syndrome
Additional Risk Factors
  • Smoke exposure
  • Family history of asthma
  • Multiple siblings/daycare exposure
  • Birth during first half of RSV season
  • Older adult age (>65 years)

Seasonal Patterns

RSV outbreaks occur worldwide during winter months:

  • Northern Hemisphere: November - February (peak January/February)
  • Southern Hemisphere: May - September
  • Tropical Regions: Prolonged circulation with rainy season

Clinical Features in Infants

  • Early signs: Rhinorrhoea, congestion, cough
  • Progression (2-4 days): Tachypnoea, wheeze, respiratory distress
  • Signs of severity: Apnoea Poor feeding Increased work of breathing Cyanosis
  • Apnoea risk: 20% in infants <6 months, especially <1 month

Clinical Features in Adults

  • Healthy adults: Usually upper respiratory tract infection, self-limiting
  • High-risk adults: Lower respiratory tract disease, pneumonia
  • High-risk groups: Elderly, immunocompromised, chronic cardiopulmonary disease, transplant recipients

Key Assessment Points

  • Determine vaccination/immunoprophylaxis status
  • Assess hydration and feeding adequacy
  • Measure respiratory rate and work of breathing
  • Check oxygen saturation (critical for severity assessment)
  • Identify underlying chronic conditions
Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

RSV diagnosis is primarily clinical based on:

  • Seasonal presentation (winter months)
  • Characteristic symptoms (cough, wheeze, respiratory distress)
  • Physical examination findings
  • Risk factor assessment

Note: Routine laboratory testing is NOT required for most patients according to AAP guidelines.

Diagnostic Testing (Consider if needed)

Pulse Oximetry
Essential

Rapid assessment of hypoxaemia. Recommended for all infants in secondary care.

Viral Testing
Selective

Rapid antigen test: 75% sensitivity, 99% specificity

RT-PCR: Preferred method, higher sensitivity

Use for cohorting/isolation decisions.

Chest X-ray
Limited Use

Reserve for severe disease or failure to improve. May show atelectasis, hyperinflation, peribronchial cuffing.

Blood Tests
Not Routine

Consider in severe disease. Blood cultures if bacterial infection suspected.

Differential Diagnoses

Human Metapneumovirus

Clinically indistinguishable from RSV. Requires PCR testing.

Influenza Virus

Higher fevers (>38.9¬∞C), more pneumonia and bacterial superinfection.

Parainfluenza Virus

10-30% of bronchiolitis. Type III mimics RSV. Often occurs in late spring.

Bacterial Pneumonia

No wheeze, higher fevers, lobar infiltrate on CXR.

Severity Classification

Mild Illness
Stable
  • No hypoxaemia
  • Mildly increased respiratory rate
  • Adequate feeding
  • Safe for outpatient management
Moderate Illness
Admit
  • Oxygen saturations <90-92%
  • Tachypnoea
  • Increased work of breathing
  • Inadequate feeding/dehydration
Severe Illness
ICU Referral
  • Refractory hypoxaemia
  • Progressive respiratory distress
  • Frank respiratory failure
  • Apnoea episodes
Treatment

Core Principle

Treatment is primarily supportive. No therapy shortens disease course or hastens symptom resolution.

Supportive Care Focus

Treatment by Severity

Mild or Self-limiting Illness
Outpatient Management
  • Home care with diligent follow-up
  • Nasal toilet with saline drops/suction
  • Adequate hydration and nutrition
  • Monitor for deterioration

High-risk infants (prematurity, chronic lung disease, CHD, immunodeficiency) may require hospital admission despite mild presentation.

Critical Care
  • Respiratory support: Non-invasive ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP) or intubation
  • Mechanical ventilation: If respiratory failure
  • Rehydration: IV fluids or enteral nutrition
  • Continuous monitoring: Cardiorespiratory monitoring

Adjunctive Therapies

Bronchodilators
Selective Use

NOT recommended routinely. May be tried in patients with:

  • Asthma
  • COPD
  • Severe disease

No evidence for reduced admissions or length of stay.

Nebulised Hypertonic Saline
Hospital Use

Potential benefit in mild-moderate bronchiolitis in hospital setting. Requires prolonged use, not for ED.

Ribavirin ¬± IVIG
Immunocompromised Only

Consider in severe immunodeficiency (HSCT, transplant recipients). Not for routine use in children.

Corticosteroids
Not Routine

NOT effective for RSV bronchiolitis. May benefit patients with:

  • Asthma
  • COPD
  • Atopy
Antibiotics
Limited Indications

Routine use NOT recommended. Risk of bacterial coinfection only 0.2%.

Consider if:

  • Severe disease requiring intubation (26% risk)
  • Confirmed/suspected bacterial infection

Ineffective Therapies
Do Not Use
  • Chest physiotherapy (no benefit)
  • Mucolytics (DNase, acetylcysteine)
  • Surfactant (no benefit)
  • Heliox mixtures (no benefit)
  • Montelukast (no benefit)

Immediate Admission

Adult Treatment

Supportive Care
  • Supplemental oxygen as needed
  • Bronchodilators for underlying COPD/asthma
  • IV fluids if dehydrated
  • Antipyretics for fever
  • Consider ribavirin in severe immunocompromised patients
Follow-up & Prevention

Monitoring

Essential
  • Outpatients: Daily monitoring for deterioration
  • Inpatients: Continuous monitoring until stable
  • Discharge criteria: Stable O‚ÇÇ saturation, adequate feeding, improving symptoms
  • Follow-up: Arrange GP/primary care review within 48 hours

Complications

Bronchiolitis
Common

The primary manifestation of RSV in infants.

Pneumonia
Common

Viral pneumonia is common in severe disease.

Recurrent Wheeze/Asthma
Long-term Risk

Infants with severe RSV LRTI have increased risk of developing asthma later in life.

Apnoea
20% in <6 months

May be sole presentation in very young infants (<1 month).

Prognosis

  • Previously healthy infants: Generally self-limiting, 10-14 days
  • Hospitalized infants: 2-3 days average stay, 2-5% require mechanical ventilation
  • Mortality: <0.1% in developed countries, ~1% in developing countries
  • Worldwide burden: 33.1 million episodes annually in children <5 years, 59,600 in-hospital deaths

Secondary Prevention

Infection Control
  • Hand hygiene: Strict hand washing in clinical and home settings
  • Contact precautions: Gowns, gloves, masks for healthcare workers
  • Isolation: Cohort patients with confirmed RSV
  • Environmental: Clean toys, surfaces regularly
  • Visitor restrictions: Limit visitors during outbreaks

Patient & Parent Education

Critical
  • Warning signs: Worsening breathing, poor feeding, cyanosis, lethargy
  • When to seek care: Immediate medical attention if apnoea, severe distress
  • Home care: Nasal suctioning, adequate fluids, fever control
  • Prevention: Avoid smoke exposure, good hand hygiene
  • Vaccination: Ensure eligible family members vaccinated
