Physical Examination Findings

Miliaria Crystallina:
  • Clear, non-inflammatory vesicles on non-erythematous base
  • Fragile vesicles that rupture easily with friction
  • Typically on trunk and proximal extremities
  • Spontaneous resolution with "branny" desquamation
Miliaria Rubra:
  • Erythematous, non-follicular papules with central vesicle
  • Sites of friction and occlusion
  • Face spared in adults, may involve face in neonates
  • May develop pustules (miliaria pustulosa)
Miliaria Profunda:
  • Flesh-colored, deep-seated papules
  • Associated anhidrosis of affected areas
  • Compensatory hyperhidrosis of face, axillae, hands, feet
  • Usually follows repeated episodes of miliaria rubra

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

Primary diagnostic method: Characteristic history and physical examination findings are usually sufficient for diagnosis. Laboratory testing is typically not required.

Clinical Diagnosis Sufficient Tests Only for Atypical Cases

Investigations (If Needed)

Consider these tests only if clinical presentation is atypical or differential diagnosis needs exclusion:

Microbiology Tests:
  • Tzanck Smear: Rules out herpetic infection (HSV/VZV) - no multinucleated giant cells in miliaria
  • Viral DFA/Culture/PCR: Differentiate from varicella or HSV infection
  • Bacterial Culture: Rules out impetigo or S. aureus infection
  • Fungal Culture/KOH Prep: Rules out candidiasis (important in neonates)
Histopathology:
  • Skin Biopsy: Rarely needed, confirms diagnosis and rules out differentials
  • Miliaria Crystallina: Sub-corneal vesicle over acrosyringeal duct, no inflammation
  • Miliaria Rubra: Spongiosis of intra-epidermal sweat duct with lymphocytic infiltrate
  • Miliaria Profunda: Intra-epidermal spongiotic vesicle with denser inflammatory infiltrate

Differential Diagnosis

Important conditions to consider and differentiate:

Fox-Fordyce Disease
Apocrine Miliaria
  • Intensely pruritic papules in adult women
  • Axillae, groin, pubic region, areola
  • Folliculocentric (vs. non-follicular in miliaria)
  • No relationship to heat/humidity
Varicella/Zoster Infection
Viral Infection
  • Vesicles on inflammatory base, all stages simultaneously
  • Highly contagious, often epidemic
  • Multinucleated giant cells on Tzanck smear
  • Positive viral PCR/culture
Acne Vulgaris
Follicular Disorder
  • Folliculocentric papules, pustules, comedones
  • May involve face, chest, back
  • Not related to heat/sweating
  • Chronic course vs. transient miliaria
Folliculitis
Infectious
  • Folliculocentric pustules
  • May be associated with occlusion
  • Bacterial culture may isolate S. aureus
  • Lesions centered on hair follicles
Transient Acantholytic Dermatosis (Grover's Disease)
Pruritic Eruption
  • Pruritic erythematous papulovesicles on trunk
  • Adult males, more persistent
  • Related to heat/sweating but no anhidrosis
  • Biopsy shows acantholysis with dyskeratosis
Neonatal Considerations
Pediatric
  • Erythema Toxicum Neonatorum: Folliculocentric pustules with eosinophils
  • Transient Neonatal Pustular Melanosis: Non-follicular pustules with neutrophils
  • Congenital Candidiasis: Pustules, not vesicles; KOH positive
Treatment

Core Management Principle

Primary treatment for all forms: Placement in a cool environment to reduce sweating. "The only effective treatment and prevention is to avoid further sweating."

Treatment Algorithm Overview

1
All Patients: Cool environment + Supportive care (moisturizing ointment, absorbent powder, cleansing) First Line
2
Adjunct: Antibacterial agents (topical chlorhexidine, bacitracin, erythromycin) Prophylaxis
3
Febrile Patients: Add antipyretics (paracetamol or ibuprofen) Symptomatic
4
With Pruritus: Add antipruritic agents (calamine, menthol, pramoxine, or mid-potency topical steroid) Symptom Control
5
Miliaria Profunda: Consider isotretinoin (adults: 0.5 mg/kg/day for 2 months) Specialist Use

Supportive Care Measures

Environmental Control:
  • Cool environment (few hours for crystallina, weeks-months for profunda)
  • Cooling fans for febrile patients
  • Cool soothing baths with colloidal oatmeal
  • Avoid heat and humidity whenever possible
Skin Care:
  • Regular washing to remove salt and bacteria
  • Topical absorbent powders (cornstarch)
  • Moisturizing ointments (anhydrous lanolin)
  • Apply before exercise or at onset of eruption
Pharmacological Adjuncts:
  • Ascorbic Acid: 1g orally once daily (prevention and treatment)
  • Antipyretics: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for febrile patients
  • Topical Antibacterials: Chlorhexidine 2-4% rinse, bacitracin, erythromycin

Treatment Timeline Expectations

Miliaria Crystallina: Rapid Resolution

Quick relief within a few hours in cool environment; spontaneous resolution with desquamation

Miliaria Rubra: Gradual Improvement

Improvement after several days in cooler environment; complete resolution may take 2-3 weeks

Miliaria Profunda: Prolonged Course

Requires weeks to months of heat avoidance; eruption phase transient (<1 hour after overheating)

Complications

Heat Exhaustion (Tropical Anhidrotic Asthenia): High Risk
  • Associated with miliaria profunda
  • Symptoms: Weakness, fatigue, dizziness, collapse, hyperpyrexia
  • Most common in soldiers stationed in tropical climates
  • Risk reduction through patient education about occlusive clothing and UV exposure
Hypohydrosis/Anhidrosis: Medium Risk
  • Can persist for up to 3 weeks after clinical resolution
  • More severe and profound in miliaria profunda
  • Episodic in miliaria rubra (less severe)
  • Compensatory hyperhidrosis in unaffected areas

Prognosis

Varies significantly by type:

Miliaria Crystallina: Excellent

Transient condition with prompt spontaneous resolution; no long-term sequelae

Miliaria Rubra: Good

Transient condition; improvement within days, complete resolution in 2-3 weeks; no sequelae

Miliaria Profunda: Variable

Less common, slower to resolve; may have transient sequelae (anhidrosis, heat intolerance); eruption phase brief (<1 hour) but underlying dysfunction persists

Prevention Strategies

No primary prevention other than avoidance: Control heat and humidity to avoid stimulating sweating.

Primary Prevention Secondary Prevention Patient Education
Environmental Modifications:
  • Avoid conditions of high heat or humidity
  • Limit activity in hot environments
  • Wear light, loose-fitting clothing
  • Practice good hygiene with cooling showers/baths
  • Use appropriate sunscreen and reapply every 2 hours
Pharmacological Prophylaxis:
  • Topical Anhydrous Lanolin: Apply before exercise to prevent new lesions
  • Topical Antibacterials: May be effective prophylactically (chlorhexidine, bacitracin)
  • Ascorbic Acid: 1g orally daily for prevention
Special Populations:
  • Infants: Avoid heavy bundling; keep cool and dry
  • Tropical Travelers: Gradual acclimatization before heavy exercise
  • Outdoor Workers: Regular cooling breaks, hydration, protective clothing
  • Military Personnel: Education on tropical anhidrotic asthenia prevention
