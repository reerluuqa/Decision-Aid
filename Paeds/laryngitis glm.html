Laryngitis Quick Ref

Acute Laryngitis < 7 Days

Inflammation of the larynx leading to oedema of true vocal folds.

  • Usually viral (Rhino/Influenza/Parainfluenza).
  • Self-limiting.
  • Clinical Diagnosis (Hoarseness + URI).
Chronic Laryngitis > 3 Weeks

Persistent inflammation. Rule out malignancy.

  • Causes: Reflux, Phonotrauma, Smoking, TB/Fungal.
  • Red Flag: Dysphagia, weight loss, persistent pain.
  • Action: Specialist consult (Laryngoscopy).

AIRWAY EMERGENCY

Assess Airway First. Do not agitate patient if epiglottitis suspected.

Suspect Epiglottitis Life Threat
  • Key Signs: Toxic appearance, tripod positioning, drooling, stridor.
  • Fever: High grade.
  • Cause: Haemophilus influenzae type B.
ACTION: Examine in OR/Controlled Setting. Secure Airway (Intubation/Tracheotomy). Do NOT use laryngoscope in room.
Suspect Diphtheria Global Risk
  • Key Signs: White-grey exudates (tonsils/soft palate) that bleed on removal.
  • History: Unvaccinated, travel to endemic areas (Africa, Asia, etc).
  • Symptoms: Sore throat, progressive SOB, malaise.
ACTION: Isolate + Diphtheria Antitoxin (IMMEDIATE) + Antibiotics. Airway monitoring.

Diagnosis & Triage

Bacterial vs. Viral?

Select findings to assess likelihood of bacterial infection:

High Suspicion of Bacterial Origin. Consider Antibiotics & Culture.
Likely Viral. Supportive care usually sufficient.
Key Risk Factors & History
  • Recent URI: Viral prodrome likely.
  • Vocal Strain: Teachers, singers (Traumatic).
  • Immunocompromise: HIV, Steroids (TB/Fungal risk).
  • Smoking/Irritants: Chronic laryngitis risk.
  • Reflux: Globus sensation, throat clearing.

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Signs
Tonsillitis Less hoarseness. No laryngeal oedema on indirect laryngoscopy.
Infectious Mononucleosis Hepatosplenomegaly, fatigue, creamy exudates (don't bleed).
Laryngeal Carcinoma Chronic hoarseness > 3 wks, weight loss, smoking history. Biopsy required.
GORD Heartburn, regurgitation. Larynx shows posterior oedema (pseudosulcus).
Peritonsillar Abscess "Hot potato" voice, trismus, uvular deviation.

Management

Viral Laryngitis Most Common
  • Voice Rest: 3-14 days. Absolute rest for singers.
  • Hydration: Increase water intake.
  • Humidification: Steam inhalers.
  • Avoid: Caffeine (diuretic), Decongestants.
  • Analgesia: For pain/fever (Paracetamol/NSAIDs).
  • NO Antibiotics: Unless bacterial superinfection.
Bacterial / Specific Tx
  • Antibiotics: If bacterial origin suspected (e.g., Penicillin V, Erythromycin, or based on culture).
  • Diphtheria: Antitoxin + PCN/Erythromycin.
  • TB: Refer to Infectious Disease. Standard anti-TB regimen.
  • Fungal: Reduce steroid inhaler dose; rinse mouth. Antifungals.
When to Refer (ENT)
  • Airway distress
  • Hoarseness > 2-3 weeks.
  • Failed treatment after 4 weeks.
  • Visible laryngeal lesion.
  • Professional voice user.
  • Suspected Malignancy/TB.
