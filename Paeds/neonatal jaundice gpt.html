Neonatal Jaundice – Primary Care Algorithm
Based on NICE CKS (May 2025) • For babies from birth to <1 month

Fast Reference (Clinic Desk Aide)

All visible jaundice requires a bilirubin measurement (TCB/serum). In most primary care settings this means immediate or same‑day referral according to urgency.

Measure within 6 hours if >24 h old Onset <24 h → admit within 2 h Prolonged: >14 d term / >21 d preterm → same‑day referral
Breastfeeding can continue Check urine & pale stools Encephalopathy = 999

Scope & Audience

Primary care assessment of neonatal jaundice; secondary care thresholds/treatments are referenced only for awareness.

  • Applies to newborns under 28 days.
  • Use clinical judgment plus local referral pathways.
  • Provide parents written & verbal information within 24 hours of birth.

Algorithm

1

Initial Check

  • Examine in bright/natural light (skin, sclerae, gums).
  • Document age at onset, duration, feeding adequacy (wet/dirty nappies).
  • Ask about urine/stool colour (dark urine, pale/chalky stools = liver disease).
  • Screen risk factors: preterm/low BW, early onset, bruising/cephalhaematoma, FHx phototherapy, G6PD, exclusive breastfeeding with suboptimal intake, East Asian/Mediterranean ancestry.
2

Red Flags → Emergency

  • Onset <24 hours
  • Unwell or signs of bilirubin encephalopathy (lethargy, poor feeding, irritability, altered tone/posture, high‑pitched cry, apnoea, seizures).
  • Sepsis signs, severe dehydration, significant weight loss.
  • Action: 999 / immediate admission.
3

Urgent Referral

  • Onset <24 h: admit to neonatal/paeds unit within 2 h.
  • All other visible jaundice >24 h: assess in unit within 6 h (use clinical judgment for faster escalation).
  • Prolonged jaundice: >14 d (term) / >21 d (preterm) → same‑day referral (timed by local protocol if otherwise well).
4

Primary Care Actions

  • Arrange bilirubin measurement (TCB or serum) — typically in secondary care.
  • Record examination findings and risk factors.
  • Provide parents info within 24 h of birth and at assessment: how to check, when to seek help, reassurance.

Parental Advice & Support

  • Neonatal jaundice is common; usually transient and harmless.
  • Breastfeeding can usually continue. Encourage frequent feeds; wake baby if needed.
  • Ensure access to breastfeeding support (local services, BfN, La Leche League, NCT, Bliss).
  • Urgent help if jaundice appears in first 24 h, if baby is unwell, or if dark urine / pale stools.

Risk Factors

  • Preterm / low birth weight
  • Early onset (<24 h)
  • Exclusive BF with suboptimal intake
  • Bruising / cephalhaematoma
  • FHx phototherapy / haemolysis (incl. G6PD)
  • East Asian or Mediterranean ancestry
  • Macrosomic IDM, dehydration, trisomy 21

Secondary Care – For Awareness

Investigations

  • Serum bilirubin (± TCB)
  • FBC, Hct, film
  • Maternity & neonatal blood group; DAT (Coombs)
  • LFTs; metabolic screen; TSH as per screening
  • G6PD level when relevant
  • Blood/urine/CSF cultures if infection suspected

Treatment

  • No treatment when below thresholds and well
  • Manage underlying cause (e.g., infection)
  • Phototherapy
  • Exchange transfusion (encephalopathy/high risk or non‑response)
  • Early surgery for obstructive causes (e.g., biliary atresia)
