Pediatric Migraine Quick Reference

Clinical decision support tool for rapid assessment and management

RED FLAGS - EMERGENCY SIGNS
Nocturnal headache
Headache triggered by Valsalva (coughing, straining)
Headache triggered by exercise
Double vision
Changes in gait pattern or falls
Recent onset of behavioural issues
Acute/subacute changes in frequency or character
Positional aggravation
KEY DIAGNOSTIC CRITERIA
Criteria Details
Attacks At least 5 attacks
Duration 2-72 hours (children)
Characteristics At least 2 of: unilateral, pulsating, moderate/severe, aggravated by activity
Associated At least 1 of: nausea/vomiting, photophobia, phonophobia
Criteria Details
Attacks At least 2 attacks
Aura Symptoms Visual, sensory, speech/language, motor, brainstem, retinal
Characteristics At least 3 of: gradual spread, succession, 5-60 min duration, unilateral, positive
Headache Accompanied or followed within 60 minutes
EPIDEMIOLOGY & RISK FACTORS
Prevalence by Age
  • 3-7 years 1-3%
  • 7-11 years 4-11%
  • 15 years 8-25%
Key Risk Factors
Family history (50%)
Female sex (post-puberty)
Poor sleep quality
Stress
TREATMENT APPROACH
Acute Management

First-line: Ibuprofen (preferred) or paracetamol

Second-line: Triptans

Rizatriptan ≥6y Almotriptan ≥12y Zolmitriptan ≥12y
Preventive Treatment

Indications: ≥1 headache/week with school absenteeism

Options:

  • Topiramate (≥12y)
  • Amitriptyline (with CBT)
  • Propranolol
  • Cyproheptadine
Lifestyle Modifications
  • Regular sleep (10h children, 9h teens)
  • Stress management
  • Regular meals
  • Hydration
  • Exercise
SPECIAL MIGRAINE TYPES
Migraine with Brainstem Aura

Symptoms: Dysarthria, vertigo, tinnitus, diplopia, ataxia

Hemiplegic Migraine

Reversible unilateral weakness with typical migraine symptoms

Chronic Migraine

≥15 days/month for >3 months, with ≥8 having migraine features
