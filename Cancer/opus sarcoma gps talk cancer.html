‚ö° Bone Sarcoma Warning Signs
  • Night pain - waking from sleep (suggests periosteal involvement)
  • Non-mechanical pain - deep gnawing pain at rest
  • Escalating analgesia requirements
  • Weight-bearing pain (fracture risk)
‚ÑπÔ∏è Key Facts
  • Can occur at any age
  • Lower limb > upper limb
  • Thigh = most common site
  • 99% of small (<5cm), superficial lumps are benign
‚ö° Clinical Pitfalls
  • Coding danger: Don't code as "lipoma" or "sebaceous cyst" unless confirmed - use "lump" + "presumed"
  • Benign things don't usually recur - if it's back, investigate
  • Lipoma feels smooth but so can sarcoma
  • A "ganglion" that's growing and painful needs imaging
‚ö†Ô∏è Remember
  • Larger tumours = worse prognosis
  • Early detection saves lives and reduces surgical extent
‚ÑπÔ∏è Age Distribution
  • Peak 1: Ages 12-25 (young adults)
  • Peak 2: Over age 65
‚ö†Ô∏è Commonly Misdiagnosed As
  • Shoulder: Frozen shoulder
  • Hip: Arthritis, trochanteric bursitis
  • Knee: Sports injury, effusion
  • Wrist: Ganglion, de Quervain's, tendinitis
‚ö° Differentiating Features
  • Pain doesn't improve with rest (non-mechanical)
  • Night pain - suggests tumour through periosteum
  • 30mg MST BD for "frozen shoulder" = disproportionate
  • Ewing sarcoma may show: ‚ÜëESR, ‚ÜìWCC, night sweats
  • 1 Expose & Compare: Always examine both sides - look for asymmetry
  • 2 Shoulders: Look from behind - you'll see more
  • 3 Palpate: Feel for tenderness directly on bone
  • 4 Depth: Can you lift it between fingers? = Superficial. Hard to define? = Assume deep
  • 5 Knee: Is the whole knee swollen (effusion) or just one area (tumour)?
  • 6 Sciatica: Palpate sciatic nerve path - could be compressed by proximal tumour
Soft Tissue Lumps
  • 1st line: Ultrasound
  • Good for superficial lesions
  • If uncertain ‚Üí urgent referral
  • Specialist may arrange MRI
Bone Lesions
  • 1st line: X-ray
  • Most will show on plain film
  • If normal but concerned ‚Üí repeat at 6 weeks
  • Still suspicious ‚Üí refer even if X-ray normal
‚ö° False Reassurance Traps
  • Normal bloods do NOT exclude sarcoma
  • CT chest/abdomen/pelvis showing no mets doesn't mean the lump is benign
  • Normal X-ray may miss early lesions - repeat if still concerned

  • Often at edge of radiation field
  • Don't assume a bone lesion near previous cancer is a met - could be new primary sarcoma
