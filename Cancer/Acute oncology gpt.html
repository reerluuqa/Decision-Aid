Acute Oncology — GP Quick Reference (UK Primary Care)

Built for rapid consultation in clinic. Use / to focus search · P to print · ? for help.

1) Classify the AO presentation

  • Type 1 — New/suspected cancer presenting via emergency pathway.
  • Type 2 — Toxicity/complication of SACT or radiotherapy.
  • Type 3 — Complication of cancer itself (pleural effusion, ascites, MSCC, SVCO).

Tip: Decide “toxicity vs tumour” early; both can coexist.

2) UKONS RAG triage (what to do)

Use the UKONS 24-hour triage tool. RAG-rate by symptom severity:

GREEN Stop oral SACT if UKONS says so, safety-net, contact AO/CNS same week.

AMBER Phone AO helpline now for same-day plan; likely assessment.

RED Arrange same-day hospital assessment/ED; alert AO team.

Common RED examples
  • Fever/unwell post-chemo → possible neutropenic sepsis.
  • Immunotherapy colitis with ≥6–7 watery stools/day or blood, dehydration.
  • New hypoxia/breathlessness → suspected pneumonitis.
  • Red flags: MSCC, SVCO, uncontrolled vomiting, severe dehydration, confusion.

3) Make the treatment visible

  • Record a problem entry: On active anti-cancer treatment (add drug + start date).
  • Add hospital-only meds to the repeat list (for interaction checks).
  • Ask patient to photo the box/clinic letter and keep on their phone.
  • Save AO helpline & CNS contact in the care plan.

4) Toxicity map (recognise fast)

Classic chemotherapy (DNA damage)
  • Neutropenic sepsis risk; follow NICE CG151 & sepsis NG51.
  • GI toxicity (N/V/D), mucositis; cytopenias.
Targeted/Cell-cycle agents (e.g., CDK4/6 inhibitors like abemaciclib)
  • Pneumonitis/ILD (can be severe). New dry cough/dyspnoea → UKONS, stop drug, speak to AO.
  • Myelosuppression, diarrhoea common; check interactions.
Antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs)
  • Pneumonitis risk (payload can affect lungs). Low threshold to escalate.
Immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors)
  • irAEs can be delayed months after stopping (pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, thyroid/adrenalitis).
  • Use UKONS immunotherapy pages; discuss early with AO—steroids may be required.

5) Initial actions & tests (don’t delay escalation)

  • Identify exact drug/class; check UKONS; call AO helpline early.
  • Baseline: FBC, U&Es, LFTs, CRP; consider TFTs if irAE possible.
  • O2 sats, CXR for respiratory symptoms.
  • Neutropenic sepsis suspected → same-day hospital per NICE; don’t give oral antibiotics unless per local AO protocol.

6) Safety-netting script

“If fever >38°C, rigors, rapidly worsening breathlessness, bleeding, >6 watery stools/day, or you feel acutely unwell, call the AO helpline now or attend ED. Otherwise, if symptoms persist/worsen over 24–48h, contact us or AO again.”

7) When to trigger ACP

  • In incurable cancer, an AO/ED admission is a prognostic inflection point.
  • ~70% 12-month mortality after an AO contact → offer **ACP/TEP**, consider palliative referral, anticipatory meds, and virtual ward.
  • Document patient priorities; share with community teams.

8) Cancer Care Review — mini checklist

  • Exact drug name(s), route, schedule; last cycle/date.
  • Planned duration/next review; who to call if unwell.
  • Screen for toxicities (bowel, breathlessness/cough, rash, fatigue, endocrine).
  • Update meds (hospital-only), interactions, contraception/VTE where relevant.
  • Holistic needs + ACP readiness (especially after any unplanned admissions).
