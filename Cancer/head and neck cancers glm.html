Clinical Context & Prognosis

How it Might Present

  • Laryngeal: Hoarseness (most common), throat pain. Diagnosis by laryngoscopy.
  • Oral: Persistent ulceration, mass, abnormal bleeding. Regional lymphadenopathy in advanced disease.
  • Thyroid: Nodule or diffuse swelling. May present with regional lymphadenopathy.
Note: Definitive diagnosis for all requires biopsy in secondary care.

Prognosis & Prevalence (UK)

Referral Management & Safety Netting

1
Immediate Referral Action

Once decision is made, refer within 1 working day. Use suspected cancer pathway referral (2-week wait) for symptoms identified in Red Flags.

2
Uncertainty? Discuss with Specialist

If symptoms/signs are ambiguous, discuss via telephone or email with a specialist. This communicates urgency and ensures correct pathway.

3
Patient Information & Support

Explain they are being referred to a cancer service. Reassure them most referred people do not have cancer. Provide written info on:

  • Where and how long they wait.
  • What tests to expect.
  • Who to contact if appointment is missed.
4
Safety Netting

For low-risk patients not meeting referral criteria:

  • Explain which symptoms to watch for.
  • Arrange a review if symptoms persist/worsen.
  • Ensure investigation results are reviewed and acted upon (watch for false negatives).
