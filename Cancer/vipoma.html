Urgent Key Clinical Features

  • Profuse watery diarrhea (>700mL/day, typically >3L/day) 100%
  • Diarrhea persists even after 48-72 hours of fasting (secretory diarrhea)
  • Dehydration symptoms: headache, weakness, poor skin turgor, dry mucous membranes
  • Weight loss (36-100% of patients)
  • Flushing secondary to vasodilation (0-33% of patients)

Caution Associated Symptoms

  • Hyperglycemia symptoms (polydipsia, polyuria, fatigue) - affects 20-50%
  • Hypercalcemia due to VIP-stimulated bone resorption
  • Abdominal discomfort from frequent bowel movements
  • Signs of metastasis: hepatomegaly, cachexia

Risk Factors

  • Median age at diagnosis: 51 years (range 11-75 years)
  • Multiple endocrine neoplasia type 1 (MEN1) - reported in <1% of VIPoma patients
  • Equal prevalence in men and women

Physical Examination Findings

  • Muscle weakness and cramps (hypokalemia)
  • Poor skin turgor and dry mucous membranes (dehydration)
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Hepatomegaly (if liver metastases present)

Critical Diagnostic Criteria

VIPoma is characterized by the triad of:

  • Profuse watery diarrhea
  • Hypokalemia
  • Metabolic acidosis (due to bicarbonate loss)
  • PLUS elevated serum VIP levels

1st Line Tests Laboratory Investigations

Basic Metabolic Panel
  • Serum potassium: reduced (often <3 mmol/L)
  • Serum bicarbonate: reduced (metabolic acidosis)
  • Serum calcium: raised (hypercalcemia)
  • Serum glucose: raised (hyperglycemia)
VIP Level Measurement Essential
  • Fasting serum VIP >75 pg/mL may indicate enteropancreatic tumor
  • >200 pg/mL is strongly suggestive of VIPoma
  • Measure by radioimmunoassay during symptomatic period
  • If normal, repeat testing (secretion may be episodic)
Additional Tests
  • Liver function tests: may be abnormal with metastasis
  • Arterial blood gas: confirm metabolic acidosis
  • Gastric pH monitoring: increased pH (6-7) due to hypochlorhydria/achlorhydria
  • Chromogranin A/Pancreatic polypeptide: if VIP normal but suspicion remains

Imaging Tumor Localization

Cross-sectional Imaging 1st

VIPomas are usually solitary, >3cm at diagnosis:

  • CT abdomen: Multi-phase contrast-enhanced
  • MRI abdomen: Multi-phase, superior for hepatic metastases
  • Endoscopic ultrasound: For small elusive tumors
Functional Imaging If CT/MRI Inconclusive
  • Somatostatin receptor PET-CT/MRI: Ga-68 DOTATATE/DOTATOC
  • Somatostatin receptor scintigraphy: Octreotide scan (less commonly used)

If Unresectable Biopsy

Endoscopic ultrasound-guided fine needle aspiration for tissue diagnosis and grading (Ki-67 index).

Differential Diagnosis

  • Infectious gastroenteritis (stool studies)
  • Osmotic diarrhea (resolves with fasting)
  • Gastrinoma (elevated gastrin, acidic diarrhea)
  • Carcinoid syndrome (severe flushing, 5-HIAA)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (endoscopy)
  • Thyroid medullary carcinoma (calcitonin)

Treatment

Acute Presentation Initial Management

1. Aggressive Fluid Resuscitation
  • Saline-based IV fluids for dehydration
  • Correct electrolyte imbalances:
    • Potassium chloride
    • Magnesium sulfate
    • Phosphorus
2. Somatostatin Analogues 1st Line
Octreotide
Short-acting: 200-300 mcg/day SC in 2-4 divided doses
Long-acting depot: 20 mg IM every 4 weeks
Titrate based on response (usual range 150-750 mcg/day)
Lanreotide
Long-acting: 120 mg SC every 4 weeks
  • Start short-acting for rapid symptom control
  • Transition to long-acting after 2 weeks
  • Continue short-acting for 2-4 weeks after starting long-acting to prevent symptom exacerbation
  • Up to 80% achieve clinical response (diarrhea improvement)

Localized Disease Surgical Management

Curative Resection Only Curative Option

Complete surgical resection offers the only chance for cure

  • Pancreaticoduodenectomy: For tumors >2cm in pancreatic head
  • Distal pancreatectomy: For tumors in body/tail (Â± splenectomy)
  • Enucleation/local excision: For small tumors <2cm
  • Lymphadenectomy: Remove regional nodes (peripancreatic)

Metastatic Disease Multimodal Approach

Surgical Candidates
  • Palliative resection: Primary tumor + regional nodes
  • Debulking: Metastasectomy, RFA, cryoablation
  • Liver transplant: Highly selected patients with liver-only metastases
Liver-Directed Therapies Selective
  • Transarterial embolization/chemoembolization

Refractory Disease Salvage Options

  • Corticosteroids: for short-term symptom control
  • Clinical trial enrollment: strongly recommended
  • Supportive care: optimize fluid/electrolyte management

Emerging Therapies

  • Surufatinib: Tyrosine kinase inhibitor (approved in China)
  • Cabozantinib: Significantly improved PFS in phase 3 trials

Long-term Complications

  • Biliary stasis/cholelithiasis: From chronic somatostatin analogue use
  • Consider prophylactic cholecystectomy if long-term therapy anticipated
  • Cachexia from chronic volume depletion
  • Bowel obstruction from large pancreatic mass

Follow-up
â–¼

Monitoring Protocol

Post-Surgical Resection
  • Periodic plasma VIP levels to assess for recurrence
  • Imaging studies as clinically indicated
  • Rising VIP levels or symptom recurrence warrants workup
Medical Management
  • Regular follow-up with fluid/electrolyte monitoring
  • Tumor marker assessment (VIP, chromogranin A)
  • Cross-sectional imaging every 3-6 months
  • Assess treatment response and side effects

Prognosis

Localized Disease Excellent
  • >90% 5-year survival after complete surgical extirpation
  • <50% of patients present with localized disease
Metastatic Disease >50% at diagnosis
  • 5-year survival exceeds 40% even with metastasis
  • Median overall survival: 85 months (SEER data)
  • Slow-growing tumors with relatively good outcomes

Referral Pathways

  • Multidisciplinary team: Surgeons, gastroenterologists, medical oncologists
  • Specialist center: Experience in neuroendocrine tumors
  • Genetic counseling: Consider MEN1 testing if family history suggestive

Patient Education

  • Chronic nature of disease requiring lifelong monitoring
  • Importance of adhering to somatostatin analogue therapy
  • Signs of recurrence to report promptly
  • Nutritional support and hydration strategies
  • Genetic implications for family members (if MEN1-associated)
