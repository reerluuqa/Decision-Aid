Urological Cancers: A GP's Quick Reference Guide

Key clinical takeaways from the "GPs Talk Cancer" podcast featuring consultant urological surgeon Mr. Sotonye Tolofari

Based on the "GPs Talk Cancer" podcast episode on urological cancers

1. Bladder Cancer: Haematuria & Recurrent UTIs

Bladder cancer is the 11th most common cancer in the UK. A key challenge is distinguishing it from urinary tract infections, especially in women.

Scenario 1: Patient with symptoms of a UTI

Prove the Infection:

  • Perform a urinalysis
  • Send a Mid-Stream Urine (MSU) sample for microscopy and culture

Interpreting the MSU Result:

Growth Confirmed:

Treat the infection. Action: Re-check the urine with a urinalysis at least 6 weeks later to ensure the haematuria has resolved. If blood persists, further investigation is needed.

No Growth, but Blood Present (Sterile Pyuria):

This should raise major alarm bells. Do not assume it's a "partially treated" infection. Action: This meets the criteria for an urgent suspected cancer referral to urology, especially in patients over 45.

No Growth, No Blood:

Consider other diagnoses like bladder pain syndrome or interstitial cystitis.

NICE NG12 Referral Guidance (Key Reminders):

  • Visible Haematuria (no UTI): Refer patients aged 45 and over
  • Non-Visible Haematuria (no UTI): Refer patients aged 60 and over with dysuria or a raised white cell count

Clinical Pearl:

For patients under the referral age (e.g., a 29-year-old with visible haematuria), an urgent (non-2WW) referral is still appropriate as they require investigation.

Distinguishing the Source of Bleeding in Women

It is notoriously difficult to determine if blood is from the urethra, vagina, or rectum ("front, middle, or back").

A PV and speculum examination can be helpful but are often inconclusive.

Action:

If a urine sample shows blood, even with potential menstrual contamination, it warrants a urology referral to exclude a urological cause before referring to gynaecology.

2. Renal (Kidney) Cancer: The Incidental Finding

Renal cancer is the 6th most common cancer in the UK, with incidence rates rising due to factors like obesity and an aging population.

Presentation: Over 90% of renal cell carcinomas (RCCs) are found incidentally on scans performed for other reasons. The classic triad of loin pain, a palpable mass, and haematuria is now rare.

Managing Incidental Renal Cysts on Ultrasound Reports:

Simple Cyst:

This is a benign bubble of fluid with zero malignant potential.

Action: No further action or follow-up is needed unless it is very large (>6-7 cm) and causing pain. These are common in over 50% of the adult population.

Complex Cyst:

Described as a collection of bubbles or having internal septations/walls.

Action: Always refer these patients to urology. They require a CT scan to be properly classified (using the Bosniak scale), as some have a high risk of being malignant.

Angiomyolipoma (AML):

A benign fatty lump.

Action: Refer to urology if the AML is over 4cm, as there is a 5-10% risk of significant bleeding. Use "Advice and Guidance" if unsure.

3. Testicular Cancer: Lumps in Young Men

Testicular cancer is the 17th most common cancer in UK males but is one of the most common cancers in men aged 30-34.

Assessment and Action:

  • Take a History: Ask about risk factors such as undescended testes (cryptorchidism), subfertility, or a family history of testicular cancer
  • Examine the Patient: The key is to determine if the lump is arising from the testicle itself (hard, solid, craggy) or is a separate structure

Differential Diagnoses:

  • Epididymal cyst
  • Hydrocele (can't feel the testicle)
  • Varicocele ("bag of worms")

Referral Pathway:

Lump arising FROM the testicle:

This is a suspected cancer referral. Do not delay by requesting an ultrasound in the community. These patients need to be seen by urology immediately.

Lump feels separate from the testicle (e.g., suspected epididymal cyst):

It is appropriate to arrange a routine ultrasound in primary care.

Patient Education:

Signpost patients to resources like the OddBalls Foundation for information on self-examination.

4. Summary of Primary Care Investigations

Investigation Recommended Use Avoid
Urinalysis/MSU Essential for investigating all UTI symptoms and haematuria Reassuring a patient if an MSU shows no growth but contains blood
Blood Tests Renal function (U&Es) is a standard part of the workup -
Ultrasound KUB Useful for investigating suspected benign scrotal lumps or as a baseline for recurrent, proven UTIs Using it as a first-line investigation for a suspicious testicular mass (this delays referral)
X-Ray KUB - No longer recommended. Has very low sensitivity for stones and does not diagnose cancer. Mr. Tolofari: "What is this, 1990?"
