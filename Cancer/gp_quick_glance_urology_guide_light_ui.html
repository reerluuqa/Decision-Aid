1) Haematuria: act first, then explain

Red Flag Follow NG12

Urgent actions

  • Visible haematuria (any sex) ≥45 yrs → Suspected cancer referral after infection excluded or if blood persists post‑UTI treatment.
  • Non‑visible haematuria ≥60 yrs → Suspected cancer referral.
  • Any age with palpable bladder mass, constitutional symptoms (weight loss, bone pain, new back pain in men ≥50, hypercalcaemia, AKI/obstruction signs) → Urgent evaluation.
UTI vs cancer—simple rule
  • UTI + blood: Treat UTI → repeat urinalysis at ~6 weeks. If blood persists, repeat once at 2–4 weeks; if still present → investigate/refer.
  • Blood but no growth (sterile pyuria or clear haematuria): Don’t serially re‑test. Refer.
  • Menstrual/uncertain source ("front, middle, back"): consider PV/speculum; repeat urine after menses. Proven urine blood with uncertain source → urology referral; add gynae if history suggests vaginal origin.

Primary care tests (when applicable)

  • Urinalysis → MSU if infection suspected.
  • U&Es.
  • Avoid X‑ray KUB (low yield, radiation).
  • Ultrasound KUB: not routine for cancer triage; consider in difficult, proven recurrent UTIs to check hydronephrosis/mass.
Below threshold but high‑risk? Smoking, occupational exposures, strong FHx, persistent visible bleeding, recurrent stones → justify urgent urology or Advice & Guidance even if below NG12 age cut‑offs.

2) Renal cancer: what to remember

  • Mostly incidental on imaging nowadays.
  • Classic triad (loin pain, mass, haematuria) is uncommon.
  • Haematuria due to RCC is <10% of RCC presentations.
  • Watch for constitutional clues: weight loss, bone pain, hypercalcaemia; consider obstructive uropathy from lower tract disease too.

3) Recurrent UTIs in women (post‑menopausal)

Stepwise care
  1. Conservative: hydration, post‑coital voiding, avoid bladder irritants; D‑mannose/cranberry if patient wishes.
  2. Topical vaginal oestrogen if atrophic symptoms.
  3. Antibiotic strategies: patient‑initiated, prophylactic low‑dose, or cyclical (document cultures).
  4. Still recurrent despite above (and infections proven) → order U&Es; consider US KUB (hydronephrosis, bladder lesion, large residual); then urology referral (cystoscopy ± intravesical options; rule out CIS/TCC).

Remember Any haematuria outside a proven/in‑treatment UTI → follow the haematuria referral rules.

4) Renal cysts: decoding the report

Reassure (green)

  • Simple renal cyst clearly stated → No cancer risk; usually asymptomatic unless >6–7 cm.
  • Small simple cysts (1–2 cm) → no referral.

Act / Refer (red)

  • Complex cyst (septations/solid elements/“not simple”) → urology referral for CT & Bosniak class.
  • Bosniak II: usually monitor (often discharge if stable 3–4 yrs).
  • Bosniak III–IV: ~50%+ malignant or malignant → consider partial nephrectomy.
  • Angiomyolipoma (AML) ≥4 cm → bleeding risk 5–10% (Wunderlich) → refer (embolisation/partial nephrectomy options).
  • Incidental solid renal mass → refer.

5) Testicular lumps: don’t delay

Expedite Seen within days where possible
  • Peak incidence: 20s–30s (non‑seminoma ~20s; seminoma ~30s).
  • Hard, solid, intratesticular mass → Suspected cancer referral immediately (don’t wait for community US).
  • Likely benign scrotal lumps (epididymal cyst, hydrocele, varicocele, hernia) → primary‑care US reasonable; refer if uncertain.

Risk factors to ask

  • Undescended testis/orchidopexy, subfertility, prior pelvic/inguinal radiotherapy, family history.

Metastatic pattern Retroperitoneal nodes most common; lungs next.

Patient education: Encourage monthly self‑exam; signpost to national charities (e.g., OddBalls) for how‑to guides and awareness.

6) Special cohort note

Sickle cell disease/trait: very rare association with renal medullary carcinoma. No screening; maintain heightened vigilance for haematuria. Low threshold for urinalysis ± renal US if symptomatic.

8) Do / Don’t

Do

  • Document infection status before/with referral decisions.
  • Safety‑net with time‑bound rechecks (e.g., repeat urine ~6 wks).
  • Use Advice & Guidance for sub‑threshold but high‑risk cases.

Don’t

  • Serially repeat MSUs for non‑visible haematuria with no growth in eligible ages—refer.
  • Order X‑ray KUB for cancer work‑up or stones screening.
  • Delay testicular mass referrals while awaiting community US if cancer suspected.

9) Phrases for your notes

Copy‑ready Clinic use
  • Visible haematuria, MSU no growth. No UTI. Meets NG12 → 2WW urology.
  • UTI with haematuria treated. Plan: repeat urinalysis in 6 weeks; safety‑net given.
  • Complex renal cyst on US ("not simple") → urology referral for CT/Bosniak.
  • Hard, intratesticular mass on exam → urgent suspected testicular cancer referral; safety‑net provided.

10) Patient messages (safety‑net lines)

  • “If you see blood again, pain worsens, develop fever/rigors, or feel unwell, contact us urgently or 111/ED.”
  • “Any new testicular lump or rapid change → same‑day contact.”
