Acute Oncology Quick Reference Guide

For Primary Care Clinicians

Types of Acute Oncology Patients

Type 1

Patients with undiagnosed cancer presenting with non-cancer related symptoms (e.g., pneumonia found to have underlying lung cancer)

Type 2

Known cancer patients presenting with treatment toxicities (most common type seen in acute oncology)

Type 3

Known cancer patients presenting with complications of the cancer itself (e.g., pleural effusion, ascites)

Systemic Anti-Cancer Therapy (SACT) Management

Overview
Drug Classes
Side Effects

SACT prescriptions are increasing by 8% per year. There's a 15-20% chance of emergency admission due to SACT toxicity.

When a patient on SACT presents with symptoms:

  1. Identify if patient is on SACT
  2. Determine the specific drug name (ask patient to show photo on phone)
  3. Research the drug class and common side effects
  4. Consult UKONS guidelines for management
  5. Use cancer helplines for urgent advice

SACT is no longer just chemotherapy. It spans multiple drug classes with different mechanisms and side effects:

Common SACT Classes:

  • Traditional Chemotherapy: DNA damaging agents causing hematological and GI toxicity
  • Cell Cycle Inhibitors: Can cause pneumonitis (e.g., abemaciclib)
  • Monoclonal Antibodies: Target specific receptors on cancer cells
  • Antibody Drug Conjugates: Antibodies with attached chemotherapy
  • Immunotherapy: Activates host immune system against cancer

Be aware of both immediate and delayed side effects of cancer treatments:

Key Side Effects to Monitor:

  • Pneumonitis: Dry cough, breathlessness (can occur with cell cycle inhibitors)
  • Immunotherapy-related "itis": Colitis, dermatitis, hepatitis, etc.
  • Delayed side effects: Can occur months to years after treatment completion
  • Traditional toxicities: Neutropenic sepsis, nausea, diarrhea

UKONS Guidelines and RAG Rating

The UK Oncology Nursing Society (UKONS) provides guidelines for managing acute oncology emergencies with a RAG rating system:

RED

Severe symptoms requiring immediate hospital admission

Examples: Grade 3-4 toxicities, high fever, severe diarrhea

AMBER

Moderate symptoms requiring urgent review

Examples: Grade 2 toxicities, worsening symptoms

GREEN

Mild symptoms manageable in community

Examples: Grade 1 toxicities, stable symptoms

Using UKONS in Practice:

  1. Access UKONS guidelines online (search "UKONS guidelines")
  2. Identify the relevant section based on suspected toxicity
  3. Assess severity using the RAG rating criteria
  4. Follow management recommendations for each RAG category
  5. For RED and AMBER: Contact cancer helpline or acute oncology service
  6. For GREEN: Consider temporary treatment cessation and outpatient review

Prognosis After Emergency Admission

Mortality Rate After Acute Oncology Admission

70%

of patients with incurable cancer die within 12 months of an emergency oncology admission

Implications for Practice:

  • Emergency admissions in incurable cancer patients are a significant red flag
  • Multiple emergency admissions suggest declining prognosis
  • Consider initiating advanced care planning discussions
  • Collaborate with acute oncology teams when prognosis appears worse than communicated

Documentation and Communication

Improving Cancer Treatment Documentation:

  • During cancer care reviews, specifically document the exact cancer treatment name
  • Code patients as being on "active treatment" and specify the drug name
  • Encourage patients to take photos of their medications on their phones
  • Update records when treatments change
  • Consider requesting "ongoing treatment summary" rather than "end of treatment summary"

Resources and Contacts

Cancer Helplines

Most cancer patients have access to a cancer helpline for urgent advice. These are staffed by nurses trained in UKONS guidance.

Acute Oncology Services

Each acute hospital with an A&E department has an acute oncology service. Establish contact with your local service for advice and support.
