Key Points

  • Always ask about family history in cancer consultations
  • "Rule of thumb": 2+ family members with same cancer type under age 60 OR 3+ at any age should raise concern
  • Document family history carefully in patient records
  • Remember that family histories can change over time

Terminology

  • Genomics = all of our DNA (not just genes)
  • Variant = change in DNA (replacing the term "mutation")
  • Germline variant = present in all cells, can be inherited
  • Somatic variant = present only in specific tissue/cancer cells
  • Pathogenic variant = variant with clinical consequences
Patients may be confused about "genetic" cancers - somatic variants are not inherited

Family History Assessment

  • Always ask about family history in cancer consultations
  • Document family history carefully in patient records
  • Remember that family histories can change over time
  • Ask about: type of cancer, age at diagnosis, relationship to patient
  • Consider both maternal and paternal sides
If a patient comes to see you and a first degree relative of theirs has got cancer, clinical genetics will want to know the details of the relative that's affected

Red Flags for Inherited Cancer Predisposition

General Red Flags

  • Cancer diagnosed at young age
  • Multiple family members affected
  • One person with multiple cancers or bilateral cancers
  • Rare cancer types
  • Specific cancer combinations
  • Certain ethnic backgrounds

Cancer Combinations

  • Breast/Ovarian Strong association
  • Bowel/Endometrial/Ovarian Consider Lynch syndrome
  • Breast/Prostate Association exists
  • Bowel/Gastric/Gallbladder Consider Lynch syndrome
  • Ureteric/Renal Consider Lynch syndrome
Rule of thumb: 2+ family members with same cancer type under age 60 OR 3+ at any age should raise concern and prompt referral
Only around 5% of cancers have an inherited basis, but identifying these patients is crucial for them and their families

High-Risk Populations

  • Ashkenazi Jewish population: Higher prevalence of BRCA1/2 variants
  • Early-onset cancers: Particularly solid tumours in childhood
  • Multiple primary cancers: In the same individual
Population screening programs may exist for certain high-risk groups (e.g., Ashkenazi Jewish for BRCA)

When to Refer

  • Patients meeting red flag criteria
  • When uncertain about risk assessment
  • Use advice and guidance pathways where available
  • Include as much detail as possible in referrals (ages, cancer types)
Clinical genetics services may have access to information that primary care cannot access

Referral Process

  • Include detailed family history with ages at diagnosis
  • Specify your concerns and reasons for referral
  • Consider if any affected relatives could be tested first
  • Check local guidelines for specific referral criteria

Direct-to-Consumer Testing

  • Encourage patients to seek information from test providers
  • Follow normal processes for risk assessment and referral
  • Be aware of RCGP/BSGM joint guidelines on handling these results
Avoid testing "for reassurance" as results may be difficult to interpret and may not qualify for further NHS investigation

Management of Patients with Known Genetic Predispositions

Key Responsibilities

  • Ensure they understand implications for family members
  • Provide appropriate surveillance information
  • Discuss risk-reducing measures (lifestyle, medications, surgery)
  • Advise on symptom vigilance
  • Consider specific needs (e.g., contraception for Lynch syndrome)

Surveillance

  • Population screening programs (breast, cervical, bowel)
  • More intensive surveillance for known genetic conditions
  • Research programs (e.g., EUROPAC for pancreatic cancer)

Risk Reduction

  • Lifestyle advice (smoking, alcohol, diet, exercise)
  • Risk-reducing medications (e.g., aspirin for Lynch syndrome)
  • Risk-reducing surgery (e.g., mastectomy for BRCA)
Clinical genetics will usually organize surveillance programs for inherited cancer predisposition syndromes

Family Implications

  • Explain inheritance patterns (usually autosomal dominant)
  • Discuss predictive testing for at-risk relatives
  • Explain that testing is usually offered from age 18 onwards
  • Encourage communication between family members
Most inherited cancer predisposition syndromes are inherited in an autosomal dominant way, meaning first-degree relatives have a 50% risk

Key Takeaways for Primary Care

  • Family history is our main tool for genomic risk assessment
  • Always ask about family history in cancer consultations
  • Remember the "rule of thumb" for red flags
  • Know when to refer and what information to include
  • Understand the difference between germline and somatic variants
  • Be aware of resources available for support
