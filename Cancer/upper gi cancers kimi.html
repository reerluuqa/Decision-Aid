Risk Factors

Age

Risk increases significantly after age 55 for oesophageal/stomach cancers, after 40 for pancreatic cancer

Gender

Two-thirds of oesophageal and stomach cancers occur in males

Smoking & Alcohol

Significant risk factors for oesophageal cancer

Chronic Conditions

Cirrhosis (liver cancer), Barrett's oesophagus (oesophageal cancer)

Diagnostic Investigations

Appropriate imaging and endoscopic procedures

Primary Diagnostic Modalities

Upper GI Endoscopy

Indications: Dysphagia, dyspepsia with alarm features (age 55+)

Timeline: Urgent (within 2 weeks) or non-urgent

Advantages: Direct visualization + biopsy capability

Gold Standard

CT Scan

Indications: Suspected pancreatic cancer, staging

Timeline: Urgent (within 2 weeks)

Advantages: Comprehensive assessment of pancreas and surrounding structures

Preferred for Pancreas

Ultrasound

Indications: Gall bladder/liver masses, pancreatic cancer (if CT unavailable)

Timeline: Urgent (within 2 weeks)

Advantages: Readily available, no radiation

Alternative Modality

Biopsy

Indications: Confirm diagnosis, guide treatment

Timeline: During endoscopy or image-guided

Location: Performed in secondary care

Definitive Diagnosis

Investigation Timelines

Urgent (2-week wait)

  • Upper GI endoscopy for dysphagia or weight loss with GI symptoms (age 55+)
  • CT scan for suspected pancreatic cancer (age 60+ with weight loss)
  • Ultrasound for upper abdominal masses (gall bladder/liver)

Non-urgent

  • Upper GI endoscopy for treatment-resistant dyspepsia (age 55+)
  • Upper GI endoscopy for haematemesis
  • Follow-up investigations for low-risk symptoms

Key Diagnostic Principles

  • 1. Age thresholds: 55+ for oesophageal/stomach cancer investigation, 40+ for pancreatic cancer referral
  • 2. Symptom combinations: Weight loss + GI symptoms significantly increases suspicion
  • 3. False negatives: Be aware of possibility of false-negative chest X-rays and faecal occult blood tests
  • 4. Specialist discussion: Consider discussing uncertain cases with specialists by phone/email

Referral Pathways

Appropriate referral routes and timelines

Cancer-Specific Referral Pathways

Oesophageal Cancer

Urgent Endoscopy (2 weeks):

  • Dysphagia (any age)
  • Age 55+ with weight loss + upper abdominal pain/reflux/dyspepsia
2-Week Wait

Pancreatic Cancer

Suspected Cancer Pathway:

  • Age 40+ with jaundice (immediate)
  • Age 60+ with weight loss + abdominal/back pain/diarrhoea/new diabetes
28-Day Diagnosis Target

Stomach Cancer

Referral Options:

  • Urgent endoscopy (2 weeks): dysphagia, weight loss with GI symptoms (55+)
  • Suspected cancer pathway: palpable epigastric mass
2-Week Wait

Liver & Gall Bladder

Urgent Ultrasound (2 weeks):

  • Upper abdominal mass consistent with enlarged liver/gall bladder
  • Further investigation in secondary care
Urgent Imaging

Referral Process Requirements

Essential Steps

  • Make referral within 1 working day of decision
  • Include all relevant clinical information
  • Specify urgency level clearly
  • Use local referral proformas if available
  • Communicate concerns and sense of urgency
  • Ensure maximum waiting periods for non-urgent referrals
  • Track patients who miss appointments

Specialist Discussion

Consider discussing uncertain cases with specialists:

  • Phone consultation: For urgent clarification of symptoms
  • Email discussion: For non-urgent advice on investigation
  • Communicate urgency: Ensure secondary care understands clinical concern

Follow-up & Safety Netting

Patient support, monitoring, and safety protocols

Patient Information & Support

Information to Provide

  • Where they are being referred to and why
  • Expected waiting times for appointments
  • How to obtain further information or help before specialist appointment
  • What to expect from the service and diagnostic procedures
  • Timeline for receiving results and diagnosis
  • Whether they can bring someone to the appointment
  • Contact details if appointment confirmation is not received
  • Reassurance that most referrals do not result in cancer diagnosis

Safety Netting Protocols

Safety Netting Involves:

  • Timely review and action after investigations
  • Active monitoring of symptoms in people at low (but not no) risk
  • Clear explanation of which symptoms to watch for
  • When to return for re-evaluation
  • Providing written information when appropriate

Review Criteria

  • Planned review within agreed timeframe
  • Patient-initiated review if new symptoms develop
  • Return if symptoms recur, persist, or worsen
  • Continued concerns despite negative initial assessment

Monitoring & Audit

Audit Requirements

Local arrangements should ensure:

  • Non-urgent referrals are assessed by specialists
  • Patients can be seen more urgently if needed
  • Maximum waiting periods are established
  • Missed appointments are tracked and followed up

Professional Development

Healthcare professionals should:

  • Participate in continuing education
  • Engage in peer review activities
  • Maintain clinical consulting and diagnostic skills
  • Improve early cancer identification

Special Considerations

Pediatric Patients

When cancer is suspected in a child:

  • Discuss referral decision with parents/carers
  • Involve the child appropriately based on age
  • Consider specialized pediatric oncology pathways

Cultural Sensitivity

Provide information that is:

  • Appropriate for language and ability
  • Culturally sensitive to beliefs about cancer
  • Available in various formats
  • Respectful of confidentiality preferences

Critical Safety Points

  • False Negatives: Be aware that chest X-rays and faecal occult blood tests can yield false-negative results
  • Responsibility: The healthcare professional who orders investigations must take responsibility for reviewing and acting on results
  • Communication: Explicitly pass on responsibility if another clinician will follow up results
