Leukaemia & Lymphoma Quick Reference Guide for GPs

Actionable educational material for General Practitioners to quickly reference and act upon when suspecting leukaemia or lymphoma in a patient.

Red Flag Symptoms & Patient Profiles

Lymphoma

  • Painless Lump: Most commonly in the neck (including supraclavicular area), axilla, or groin.
Pea-sized

Unlikely to be worrying; monitor.

Grape-sized (1-1.5cm+)

Needs investigation.

Satsuma-sized

Very worrying.

  • Overwhelming Fatigue: Described as "coming home and collapsing." Patients stop extracurricular activities (sports, socializing) and only manage the absolute basics.
  • Itching (Pruritus): Can be a presenting symptom.
  • Vague/Unusual Symptoms: Lymphoma is a great "mimic."
    • Mediastinal Mass: Can cause facial swelling (Superior Vena Cava obstruction), shortness of breath, or a dry cough.
    • Abdominal Mass: Vague symptoms like weight loss and sweating. As it grows, it can cause pain, discomfort, or renal dysfunction from ureter compression.

Patient Profile:

  • Hodgkin's Lymphoma: Peaks in young adults (16-24) and older adults.
  • Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma: More common with increasing age.
  • Immunosuppressed Patients: Those with long-term immunosuppression (e.g., rheumatology patients, post-transplant) are at a higher risk. Have a lower threshold for investigation in this group.

Acute Leukaemia

  • Rapid Onset: History can be as short as 3-4 weeks from feeling "not quite right" to being very unwell.
  • Bone Marrow Failure Symptoms:
    • Anaemia: Tiredness and pallor are key signs.
    • Thrombocytopenia: Spontaneous or easy bruising, gum bleeding.
    • Neutropenia: Infections, fever.
  • Patient Profile: Can occur in any age group, including young adults who present as "tired and pale" after a holiday.

Chronic Leukaemia

  • Often Asymptomatic: Frequently picked up as an incidental finding on routine blood tests.
  • Slow Onset: Symptoms develop over many years or even decades.
  • Patient Profile: Typically found in middle-aged or older adults during routine health checks.

Initial Investigations in Primary Care

Symptom / Suspected Condition Recommended Initial Investigations
Persistent Neck Lump Refer to Head & Neck (2WW): They often have one-stop clinics for ultrasound and same-day biopsy, which is the fastest route to diagnosis. A biopsy is essential for diagnosis.
Fatigue, Itching, Vague Symptoms (Suspected Lymphoma)
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Note: FBC is often NORMAL in lymphoma. Its main role is to rule out other causes of symptoms (e.g., iron deficiency). Do not be reassured by a normal FBC if a lump persists.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): To check for jaundice if the patient is itchy.
  • EBV/CMV Serology: Consider in young adults with lumps and fatigue.
Tiredness & Pallor (Suspected Acute Leukaemia)
  • Urgent Full Blood Count (FBC) with blood film: This is the key diagnostic test. Look for pancytopenia (low haemoglobin, low platelets, low/high white cells).
Incidental Abnormal FBC (Suspected Chronic Leukaemia)
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Will show raised lymphocytes (CLL) or raised neutrophils (CML).
Vague Chest/Abdominal Symptoms
  • Chest X-ray: For unexplained cough or shortness of breath. A mediastinal mass may show up but can be missed.
  • Relevant Bloods (FBC, U&Es, LFTs)
Children Have a very low threshold for an FBC in children with unexplained bruising, pallor, fatigue, or reduced activity levels.

Interpreting Blood Results & Next Steps

Blood Result Finding Potential Diagnosis Action for GP
Pancytopenia (Low Hb, Platelets, WCC) Acute Leukaemia The lab should phone this result through. Contact the on-call haematologist immediately for a management plan. The patient needs to be seen in haematology the next morning.
Isolated Lymphocytosis Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia (CLL) / MBL
  • Lymphocyte count > 5: Likely CLL. Refer routinely to haematology. No urgent treatment is needed if asymptomatic.
  • Lymphocyte count < 5: This is Monoclonal B-cell Lymphocytosis (MBL), a benign precursor. Do not refer. Manage in primary care with a yearly FBC.
Isolated Neutrophilia Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML)
  • Neutrophil count < 15: Monitor in primary care.
  • Neutrophil count > 15: Refer to haematology. These patients will be treated.
Normal FBC with a persistent lump/concerning symptoms Lymphoma Do not be reassured. A normal FBC is common. The patient still needs referral (e.g., Head & Neck 2WW for a neck lump) for a biopsy.

Urgent Management & Safety Netting

Suspected Acute Leukaemia

After an urgent FBC result is phoned through:

1
Assess the Patient: Contact the patient immediately. Ask about:
  • Fever/Infection: Are they febrile or unwell?
  • Bleeding: Any active bleeding (gums, bruising)?
  • Symptoms: How are they feeling?
2
When to Send to A&E vs. See Next Day:
Send to A&E if:
  • Febrile/Sepsis symptoms (Neutropenic Sepsis)
  • Actively bleeding
  • Haemoglobin <60 (will likely need an urgent transfusion)
Manage as Outpatient if:
  • Patient is clinically well, afebrile, and not bleeding
  • Hb >70, Platelets >20
  • Action: Safety net carefully. Instruct them to go to A&E if they develop a fever or bleeding overnight. Arrange for them to be seen in the haematology clinic the next morning.
3
Communicating with the Patient:
  • Explain that their blood counts are low and need urgent assessment by a specialist.
  • It is appropriate to mention this could be a serious condition, like a blood cancer, to prepare them for the urgent appointment.

Key Clinical Pearls

Lymphoma

A persistent lump needs a biopsy. Normal blood tests do not rule it out. Think of it when symptoms are vague and persistent.

Acute Leukaemia

Presents acutely and is diagnosed via FBC. It is a medical emergency.

Chronic Leukaemia (CLL)

Often an incidental finding. Asymptomatic patients are monitored, not treated.

Continuity of Care

For patients with vague, persistent, or evolving symptoms, continuity with the same GP is vital for spotting patterns.
