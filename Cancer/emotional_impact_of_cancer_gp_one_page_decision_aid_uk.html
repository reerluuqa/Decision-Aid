Rapid Triage (\~60–120s)

Acknowledge Validate Normalise Individualise Safety‑net

1) Openers (say this)

build trust
  • “Many people feel anxious or low at points on the cancer pathway. How has it been for you recently?”
  • “What’s your biggest worry today?”
  • “Shall we make a brief plan for the next few weeks, and who to call if things dip?”
  • With carers: “How is this affecting you? What support do you have?”
Many people feel anxious or low with cancer. How has it been for you? What is your biggest worry today? Would it help to sketch a plan together and agree who to contact if things dip? How is this affecting you as a carer? What support do you have?

2) Screen quickly

mood/anxiety
  • PHQ‑2 / GAD‑2 as a starter. If positive, complete PHQ‑9 (0–27) and GAD‑7 (0–21). Cut‑offs commonly used: PHQ‑9 ≥10; GAD‑7 ≥10 for clinically significant symptoms (consider context).
  • Distress Thermometer (0–10) for a quick oncology‑friendly snapshot.
  • Ask directly about risk: suicidal ideation, self‑harm, psychosis, safeguarding, neglect, capacity concerns.
Note: Adjust interpretation around chemotherapy, steroids, pain and sleep disruption. Repeat scores to track change.

3) Stratify & act

stepped care

Low–moderate distress

  • Validate/normalise emotions; give self‑management strategies (see Scanxiety).
  • Offer NHS Talking Therapies (self‑referral or GP referral).
  • Social prescribing: activity, community, financial/benefits advice (Macmillan), carers’ support, Maggie’s Centres.

Moderate–severe or persistent

  • Medication if indicated (see “Psychotropic prescribing in cancer”).
  • Refer to Talking Therapies (CBT, counselling) or local psycho‑oncology.
  • Co‑manage with oncology/CNS; consider fit note, reasonable adjustments at work.

Red flags → same‑day action

  • Active suicidal intent / plans or high self‑harm risk.
  • New psychosis, severe agitation, or inability to care for self.
  • Safeguarding concerns (dependent children, vulnerable adults).
  • Acute delirium (infection, metabolic, meds).
Use local crisis pathways; document capacity, risks, and safety‑netting.

Psychotropic prescribing in cancer (GP quick notes)

  • First‑line SSRIs often considered: sertraline, citalopram/escitalopram (check QT and interactions). Avoid paroxetine/fluoxetine with tamoxifen (↓ efficacy via CYP2D6). Always check the BNF & oncology meds.
  • Mirtazapine: helpful for poor appetite, weight loss, insomnia, nausea; sedating at lower doses.
  • Venlafaxine: may help vasomotor symptoms; watch BP and interactions.
  • Avoid St John’s wort and unverified supplements (interactions).
  • Start low, titrate; review 2–4 weeks; monitor hyponatraemia in older adults.
Coordinate with oncology/CNS; consider steroid‑related mood change, opioid effects, antiemetics, and QT‑prolonging regimens.

“Scanxiety” & living with uncertainty

Name itMake a planLimit doom‑scrollingSleep hygieneActivity
  1. Normalise: fear spikes before tests/results; most people cope better once a plan is clear.
  2. 5–20 min “worry window” each morning; write worries; act on solvable items; park the rest until tomorrow.
  3. Anchor to the present: brief walk, cooking, simple chores, time with children/pets, hobbies, breath work (4–6 breathing).
  4. Information diet: agree limits on searching/forums; steer to reputable sources.
  5. Results plan: who will call, by when, and how to chase; offer a follow‑up to debrief.

Carers & family dynamics

  • Invite carers in: “How is this affecting you?” Add to GP carer register; offer carer’s assessment; give respite/benefits signposting.
  • Teens: ask their understanding and what would help; grandparents can be a bridge; encourage open but age‑appropriate communication.
  • At progression/near EoL: acknowledge pain and hopes; avoid collusion; centre the patient’s wishes and capacity.

Touchpoints across the cancer pathway – what primary care can do

Suspicion & 2WW referral

uncertainty high
  • Explain why you’re referring; avoid over‑ or under‑reassurance. Offer concrete timelines and how results arrive.
  • Give scanxiety tips; provide contact route if anxiety escalates.
  • Flag vulnerabilities (severe MH history, social isolation, safeguarding).
  • Document shared understanding and safety‑netting.
  • Offer a brief check‑in while waiting (phone or message).

Diagnosis & active treatment

plan brings control
  • Early call/letter: “We’re here if needed.” Build continuity.
  • Manage symptoms (pain, sleep, nausea, bowels, menopausal sx) and mood; coordinate with CNS.
  • Fit notes / work advice; welfare/benefits signposting (Macmillan).
  • Consider Talking Therapies; social prescribing for movement and connection.
  • Medication if indicated; check interactions (tamoxifen, QT, CYP effects).

End of treatment / surveillance

distress often ↑
  • Normalise post‑treatment dip; fear of recurrence is common.
  • Agree a follow‑up plan for new or persistent symptoms & emotional dips.
  • Offer activity plans; consider mirtazapine/SSRI if indicated.
  • Encourage gradual return to life goals; address avoidance (“I can’t plan”).
  • Invite carers back for support review.

Progression / palliative phase

be honest & kind
  • Acknowledge reality and hopes; centre patient’s wishes; avoid hallway collusion.
  • Discuss priorities, anticipatory meds, OOH plan, and carer support.
  • Consider DNACPR/ReSPECT, ACP, and hospice/palliative referrals.
  • Review mood, sleep, spiritual concerns; offer bereavement support early.
  • Protect continuity: named GP where possible.

Documentation aide‑mémoire

SUBJECTIVE: mood/anxiety today; biggest worry; sleep; appetite; pain; treatment phase. SCALES: PHQ‑2/9: __ / __ | GAD‑2/7: __ / __ | Distress Thermometer: __/10. RISK: SI/SH ___ ; psychosis ___ ; safeguarding/capacity ___ ; supports at home ___. CARERS: identified/registered? carer’s assessment signpost given. ASSESSMENT: normal reaction vs GAD/MDD; contributing meds (steroids/opiates/antiemetics) ; co‑morbidities. PLAN: self‑management given; resources provided; Talking Therapies referral/self‑referral; social prescribing; meds started/adjusted (interaction checked); follow‑up in __ weeks; crisis plan. SAFETY‑NET: when/how to seek urgent help; who will call with results, when; agreed contact if worse.

Trusted resources to signpost

  • NHS Talking Therapies (self‑referral & GP referral)
  • Macmillan Cancer Support – information, benefits advice, local services
  • Maggie’s Centres – drop‑in psychological & practical support
  • Carer’s Assessment (local authority)
  • BNF / NICE – check interactions (e.g., tamoxifen & SSRIs)
  • Distress Thermometer (oncology screening)
  • Book: The Cancer Survivor’s Companion (recommended for post‑treatment coping)
