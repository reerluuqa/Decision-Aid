Chronic Pain Syndromes

Updated: May 13, 2025 ‚Ä¢ BMJ Best Practice Reference

‚õî IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED If any of the following are present, initiate urgent investigation or specialist referral immediately.

Neurological Red Flags

  • New headache or significant change in character within 2 years.
  • Abnormal neurological symptoms or signs.
  • Back pain radiating to lower extremity with neurological deficit.
  • History of head trauma with persistent symptoms (Post-traumatic headache).

Systemic / Vascular Flags

  • Patient > 50 years with new onset headache (Rule out GCA).
  • Temporal artery tenderness, jaw claudication, visual disturbance.
  • Posterior head or neck pain (Rule out dissection/trauma).
  • Constitutional symptoms (weight loss, fever) suggesting malignancy/infection.

Management Principles

‚úÖ FIRST-LINE & BENEFICIAL
  • Non-pharmacological: Stretching exercises, relaxation techniques, activity modification.
  • Pharmacological (Varies): Antidepressants (e.g., TCAs, SNRIs) and Anticonvulsants (e.g., Gabapentinoids) for many neuropathic/musculoskeletal conditions.
  • Treat underlying cause: e.g., Prednisolone for Polymyalgia Rheumatica (rapid response).
  • Comorbidities: Aggressively treat depression and anxiety.
‚õî AVOID / CAUTION
  • Opioids: Do NOT use as first-line or routine therapy. Evidence is limited; risks include overdose, misuse, fractures (falls), and hyperalgesia.
  • Medication Overuse: Watch for drug overuse headache (‚â•15 days/month analgesic use).
  • Normalizing Pain: In older patients, do not consider chronic pain (e.g., arthritis, spinal stenosis) "normal ageing".

Classification & Diagnosis

Myofascial Pain

Skeletal muscle and fascia/connective tissue pain. Most common in speciality pain clinics.

Key Features

  • History of muscle tenderness/spasm.
  • Pain related to physical activity.
  • Restricted Range of Motion (Active).
  • Sleep/mood disturbances common.

Exam Findings

  • Taut bands: Palpable contracted cord-like muscle fibres.
  • Trigger Points: Point tenderness; Active causes referred pain, Latent is local only.
  • Twitch Response: Involuntary contraction on palpation/insertion.
Common Examples: Lateral epicondylitis, Quadratus lumborum syndrome, Piriformis syndrome.

Musculoskeletal (Mechanical) Pain

Pain arising from bone, joint, muscle, vertebral column, tendon, or soft tissue.

Key Features

  • Joint pain, morning stiffness.
  • Crepitus with joint movement.
  • Osteoarthritis: High-use, weight-bearing, asymmetrical.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: Symmetrical, inflammatory, responds to NSAIDs.

Exam Findings

  • Restricted Passive ROM.
  • Joint crepitus.
  • Joint inflammation (swelling, warmth, tenderness).
  • Joint instability.

Neuropathic Pain

Pain caused by a lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system.

Hallmarks

Burning Allodynia Hyperalgesia
  • History of diabetes, shingles, or trauma.
  • Numbness/Sensory loss (Dermatomal or Stocking/Glove).

Specific Conditions

  • Postherpetic Neuralgia: Pain > 1 month after zoster onset.
  • CRPS: Excessive guarding, after injury/immobilization.
  • Diabetic Neuropathy: Up to 25% of diabetics.

Fibromyalgia

Chronic, widespread pain with significant emotional distress or functional disability.

Strong Risk Factors

Demographic & Social

  • Female sex
  • Age > 45 years
  • Pregnancy
  • Low socioeconomic status / Deprived area
  • Unemployment or work dissatisfaction

Medical & Psychological

  • Personal/Family history of chronic pain
  • Comorbid mood disorder (Depression/Anxiety)
  • History of trauma or acute back pain with neuro signs
  • Smoking (Nicotine use)

Key Differentials

  • Clinical diagnosis (DSM-5 criteria). No specific organic test.
  • Elevated CK. Positive ANA. EMG changes. Muscle Biopsy.
    • Condition Differentiating Signs/Symptoms Key Tests/Findings
    Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) Neck/shoulder/hip girdle stiffness & pain. Age >50. Morning stiffness. Rapid improvement with Prednisolone (24-48h). Elevated ESR/CRP.
    Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) New unilateral headache, jaw claudication, visual disturbance, temporal artery tenderness. Elevated ESR/CRP. Temporal Artery Biopsy (+ Corticosteroid trial).
    Hypothyroidism Weakness, fatigue, weight change. Delayed relaxation of DTRs. Elevated TSH, low T4.
    Depression Anhedonia, sleep disturbance, low energy, guilt.
    Polymyositis Symmetrical weakness of shoulder/pelvic girdles.

    Definitions

    • Acute Pain: Decreases during healing, resolves within 3 months.
    • Chronic Pain: Persists longer than 3 months (or recurs). Due to persistent activation of neural pathways and muscle spasm.
    • Allodynia: Perception of non-noxious stimuli as painful.
    • Hyperalgesia: Noxious stimuli produce exaggerated/prolonged pain.
