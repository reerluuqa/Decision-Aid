Guillain-Barre Syndrome

Quick Reference Clinical Tool

Last Updated: Nov 10, 2022
EMERGENCY: 20-30% of patients develop respiratory failure requiring ventilation. Monitor vital capacity every 6 hours initially.
Key Facts

Definition: Acute inflammatory polyneuropathy classified into axonal and demyelinating forms.

Incidence: 0.6-1.9 per 100,000 population.

Gender: Slightly more common in males (ratio 1.78:1).

Age: Mean onset ~40 years, increases with age.

Progression: 80% reach nadir by 2 weeks, 97% by 4 weeks.

Common Subtype: AIDP (85-90% of cases in North America/Europe).

Association: Linked to Zika virus outbreaks and rarely COVID-19.

Prognosis: Most recover, but 20% have residual disability.

Red Flags
  • Rapid progression of weakness over days
  • Respiratory distress or vital capacity <20 mL/kg
  • Bulbar dysfunction (facial weakness, dysphagia)
  • Autonomic dysfunction (arrhythmias, blood pressure fluctuations)
  • Inability to lift head or cough effectively
Typical Disease Timeline
Preceding Infection

2/3 of patients have viral or bacterial infection 1-3 weeks before onset.

Progressive Phase

Symptoms progress over days. 80% reach nadir by 2 weeks.

Plateau Phase

Symptoms stabilize for days to weeks before recovery begins.

Recovery Phase

Gradual improvement over weeks to months. Most patients recover.

Quick Actions
Common Triggers
Campylobacter jejuni (13-39%)
CMV (5-22%)
EBV (1-13%)
Mycoplasma (5%)
Zika virus
COVID-19 (rare)
Diagnostic Approach
Required Features: Progressive weakness of >1 limb, areflexia
Supportive Features: Progression over <4 weeks, relative symmetry, mild sensory signs, cranial nerve involvement, autonomic dysfunction, elevated CSF protein without cells
Confirmatory: Neurophysiology showing demyelination or axonal features
Key Investigations
Test Expected Findings
Nerve Conduction Studies Prolonged distal/F-wave latencies, reduced conduction velocities
CSF Analysis Elevated protein with normal cell count (albuminocytological dissociation)
Spirometry Reduced vital capacity, inspiratory/expiratory pressures
Liver Function Tests Transient elevation of aminotransferases
GBS Subtypes
AIDP Most common (85-90% in Western countries)
AMAN Pure motor form, common in Japan/China
AMSAN Sensory and motor deficits, axonal loss
Miller-Fisher Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia
Bickerstaff's Similar to MFS but with encephalopathy/hyper-reflexia
Pharyngeal-cervical-brachial Arm weakness, swallowing dysfunction, facial weakness
Acute pandysautonomia Autonomic dysfunction without motor weakness
Pure sensory Sensory loss, ataxia, areflexia without motor involvement
Differential Diagnosis
Transverse Myelitis Sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction, hyper-reflexia
Myasthenia Gravis Extraocular involvement, fatigability, preserved reflexes
Lambert-Eaton Slower onset, dry mouth, potentiation after exercise
Botulism Descending paralysis, fixed dilated pupils
Polymyositis Muscle tenderness, elevated CK, preserved reflexes
Disease-Modifying Therapy
First-Line Options
IV Immunoglobulin (IVIg)

2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days. Equally effective as plasma exchange.

Plasma Exchange (PLEX)

5 exchanges over 2 weeks. Most effective if started within 2 weeks of onset.

Note: IVIg and PLEX have equivalent efficacy. No benefit of combining them or giving IVIg after PLEX.

Supportive Care
Respiratory Support
  • Monitor vital capacity every 6 hours
  • Early intubation if VC <20 mL/kg
  • Consider non-invasive ventilation
Autonomic Management
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring
  • IV fluids for hypotension
  • Short-acting agents for hypertension
Pain Management
  • Neuropathic pain agents
  • Avoid opioids if possible
  • Consider gabapentinoids
Rehabilitation
  • Early physiotherapy
  • DVT prophylaxis
  • Psychological support
Special Considerations
Miller-Fisher Syndrome

Usually self-limiting. Consider IVIg if severe or prolonged.

Pregnancy

IVIg preferred over PLEX. Both can be used if benefits outweigh risks.

Children

Similar treatment approach. Weight-adjusted dosing for IVIg.

Campylobacter-associated GBS

Poorer prognosis. Consider antibiotics if persistent fecal excretion.

Avoid These
  • Corticosteroids - Not effective in GBS
  • Combining IVIg and PLEX - No additional benefit
  • Delayed treatment - Less effective after 2 weeks
  • Vaccination during acute phase - Avoid if possible
Respiratory Monitoring
Parameters to Monitor
Vital Capacity Every 6 hours initially. ICU admission if <20 mL/kg
Max Inspiratory Pressure Every 6 hours. ICU admission if worse than -30 cmH₂O
Max Expiratory Pressure Every 6 hours. ICU admission if <40 cmH₂O
Pulse Oximetry Continuous. Not reliable alone - late sign
Arterial Blood Gases Only if clinically indicated. Late sign
Intubation Criteria: VC <20 mL/kg, MIP < -30 cmH₂O, MEP <40 cmH₂O, rapid decline, bulbar dysfunction, or inability to clear secretions.
Autonomic Monitoring
Cardiovascular
  • Continuous ECG monitoring
  • Hourly blood pressure checks
  • Treat arrhythmias promptly
  • Avoid QT-prolonging drugs
Other Systems
  • Daily weight
  • Input/output monitoring
  • Bowel and bladder function
  • Skin integrity checks
Prognosis & Recovery
Expected Recovery Timeline
Plateau Phase

2-4 weeks after onset, symptoms stabilize

Early Recovery

Weeks to months - gradual improvement

Majority Recover

70-80% achieve full functional recovery

Prolonged Recovery

20% have residual disability after 1 year

Poor Prognosis Factors

Older age, rapid progression, severe axonal loss, Campylobacter infection

Follow-up Schedule
Discharge Arrange outpatient neurology follow-up within 2-4 weeks
1 Month Assess functional recovery, need for ongoing therapy
3 Months Neurophysiology if recovery slower than expected
6 Months Assess for residual deficits, long-term rehabilitation needs
12 Months Final assessment of recovery status
Clinical Case Example
20-year-old woman with GBS

Presentation: Lower back pain and bilateral tingling in feet and hands progressing over 4 days to inability to mobilize legs. History of coryzal symptoms 2 weeks ago.

Examination: 0/5 power in lower extremities with areflexia, no sensory deficits despite paraesthesias.

Investigations: Elevated aminotransferases, lumbar puncture with mildly elevated protein and no cells.

Respiratory Function: VC 1300 mL, MIP -30 cmH₂O, MEP 35 cmH₂O (patient weighs 70 kg).

Red Flags: Rapid progression, severe weakness, borderline respiratory parameters.
Actions: Admit to ICU, consider IVIg or PLEX, monitor respiratory function closely.
