Insomnia Quick Reference

Last updated: Oct 21, 2025

Definition

Difficulty initiating sleep, maintaining sleep, or early-morning awakening despite adequate opportunity for sleep.

Frequency: 3+ nights/week

Duration: 3+ months

Results in clinically significant distress or impairment

Epidemiology

  • 30% of general population report sleep disruption
  • 10-15% have insomnia disorder meeting diagnostic criteria
  • More common in women than men
  • Reports increase with age

Pathophysiology

Disorder of hyperarousal involving:

  • Physiological components
  • Cognitive components
  • Emotional components

Consequences

Consequence Risk Level
Reduced quality of life High
Psychiatric disorders High
Workplace accidents High
Cardiovascular disease Moderate

Risk Factors

Strong
  • Female sex
  • Advanced age
  • Chronic medical conditions
  • Psychiatric illness
  • Alcohol/substance misuse
Weak
  • Certain medications
  • Jet lag
  • Night work

3P Model

  • Predisposing: Family history, medical/psychiatric disorders
  • Precipitating: Stressful life events, hospitalization
  • Perpetuating: Maladaptive behaviors/cognitions, sleep anxiety

Diagnostic Approach

  • Medical and sleep history are often sufficient
  • Sleep diaries are essential tools
  • Actigraphy may aid diagnosis when history is unreliable
  • Consider polysomnography for suspected sleep apnea or PLMD

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Delayed sleep onset (30+ minutes)
  • Multiple or long awakenings (30+ minutes)
  • Early morning awakening (30+ minutes before desired)
  • Impairment of functioning
  • Presence of risk factors

Assessment Tools

Tool Purpose
Sleep Diary Primary assessment tool
ISI Insomnia severity
PSQI Sleep quality
ESS Daytime sleepiness
PHQ-9 Depression screening

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiator
Restless Legs Syndrome Uncomfortable leg sensations
Periodic Limb Movement Rhythmic limb movements
Obstructive Sleep Apnea Breathing disturbances
Circadian Rhythm Disorders Sleep timing issues

First-Line Treatment

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)

  • Best balance between efficacy and safety
  • Recommended for most patients
  • Components include sleep hygiene, stimulus control, sleep restriction

Pharmacological Options

Medication Class Indications
Benzodiazepine Receptor Agonists Short-term use
Melatonin Receptor Agonists Sleep onset difficulties
Orexin Receptor Antagonists Sleep maintenance
Antidepressants Comorbid depression

Treatment Algorithm

1
Assessment

Identify comorbid conditions, medications, and contributing factors

2
First-line Treatment

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)

3
Second-line Treatment

Pharmacological therapy (short-term) when CBT-I unavailable or insufficient

4
Follow-up

Monitor response, adjust treatment as needed

Emergency Situations

Immediate attention required for patients with severe insomnia who present with suicidal ideation or severe impairment in functioning.

Red Flags

  • Suicidal ideation
  • Severe depression
  • Impaired driving leading to accidents
  • Severe daytime impairment
  • Substance misuse

Emergency Actions

  • Immediate psychiatric evaluation for suicidal ideation
  • Consider temporary disability from work if driving impaired
  • Refer to sleep specialist for refractory cases
  • Consider short-term use of sedating medications under close supervision

When to Refer

  • Suspicion of sleep apnea or PLMD
  • Treatment-resistant insomnia
  • Complex comorbidities
  • Need for specialized CBT-I

Follow-up

  • Monitor treatment response
  • Assess for side effects of medications
  • Evaluate daytime functioning
  • Consider tapering off medications after improvement
