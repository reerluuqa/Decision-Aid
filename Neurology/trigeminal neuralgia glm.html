Trigeminal Neuralgia - Quick Clinical Reference

Instant access to critical diagnostic and treatment information

Definition

Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) is a facial pain syndrome in the distribution of â‰¥1 divisions of the trigeminal nerve, characterized by paroxysms of sharp, stabbing, intense pain lasting up to 2 minutes.

Epidemiology
  • Incidence: 4-29 cases per 100,000 person-years
  • Female predominance (3:2 female to male ratio)
  • Mean age of onset: 53-57 years
  • Lifetime prevalence: 0.16% to 0.3%
Classification
  • Classical TN (75%): Intracranial vascular compression
  • Secondary TN (15%): Tumor, MS, or other pathology
  • Idiopathic TN (10%): No identifiable cause
Key Pathophysiology

Focal demyelination and resultant conduction aberrations (ephaptic transmission) at the trigeminal nerve root entry zone. In MS patients, demyelinating plaques in the pons encompassing the root entry zone are common.

Typical Presentation
Pain Characteristics:
  • Unilateral paroxysms of facial pain
  • Sharp, stabbing, intense quality
  • Lasts seconds to minutes
  • Stereotyped in the individual
Common Triggers:
  • Tooth brushing
  • Eating
  • Cold exposure
  • Light touch to face
Diagnostic Approach

Trigeminal neuralgia is primarily a clinical diagnosis. Key components include:

Detailed History

Focus on pain characteristics, triggers, and distribution

Physical Exam

Should be normal in classical TN; sensory changes suggest secondary cause

MRI

Recommended for all patients to exclude secondary causes

ICHD-3 Diagnostic Criteria
Recurrent paroxysms of unilateral facial pain in the distribution(s) of one or more divisions of the trigeminal nerve
Pain characteristics:
  • Duration: fraction of a second to 2 minutes
  • Severity: intense
  • Quality: electric shock-like, shooting, stabbing, or sharp
Precipitated by innocuous stimuli within the affected trigeminal distribution
Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis
Key Diagnostic Factors
Factor Significance
Stereotyped paroxysms of facial pain Required for diagnosis
Attacks triggered by facial/oral stimulation Common finding
Bilateral or side-alternating pain Rare, suggests underlying disorder
Sensory/motor changes Suggests secondary cause
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Differentiating Features
Dental caries/fracture Localized to specific tooth, confirmed with intra-oral x-rays
Temporomandibular joint syndrome Often bilateral, jaw opening may be restricted
Migraine Presence of preceding aura, associated with photophobia, nausea
Glossopharyngeal neuralgia Pain in distribution of glossopharyngeal nerve (oropharyngeal and otic)
Post-herpetic neuralgia Typically continuous pain in V1 distribution, often with tactile allodynia
Temporal arteritis Often continuous, can be bilateral, associated with jaw claudication
First-Line Medical Therapy
Carbamazepine
200 mg/day orally initially (1-2 divided doses), usual maintenance: 400-1200 mg/day in 2 divided doses
Only anticonvulsant with efficacy proven in RCTs. Watch for allergic rash (may precede Stevens-Johnson syndrome).
Oxcarbazepine
300 mg/day orally initially, usual maintenance: 600-1200 mg/day in 2 divided doses
Derivative of carbamazepine with fewer drug-drug interactions and lower adverse effect profile.
Second-Line Medical Therapy
Lamotrigine
Consult specialist for dosing guidance
Can be used as monotherapy or add-on therapy. Watch for allergic rash.
Gabapentin
300 mg once daily initially, maintenance: 300-1800 mg/day in 3 divided doses
Less effective than carbamazepine but associated with fewer adverse events.
Pregabalin
75 mg twice daily for 1 week, then 150 mg twice daily for 1 week, then 300 mg twice daily
Alternative to gabapentin with similar efficacy profile.
Baclofen
10 mg three times daily initially, maintenance: 40-80 mg/day
May be helpful for TN in patients with MS who are using it for spasticity.
Surgical Options for Refractory Cases
Microvascular Decompression

First-line procedural treatment for classical TN

  • Targets the presumed pathological initiator
  • 62-89% of patients pain-free at 3-11 years follow-up
  • Severe complications rare (death, stroke, meningitis)
  • Other complications: cranial nerve palsy (4%), hearing loss (1.8%), facial hypoaesthesia (3%)
Ablative Procedures

Considered for idiopathic TN or patients who don't want open surgery

  • Stereotactic radiosurgery: 30-66% achieve long-term remission
  • Radiofrequency gangliolysis: 26-82% achieve remission
  • Glycerol injection: 19-58% achieve remission
  • Balloon compression: 55-80% pain-free at 4-11 years
Treatment Algorithm
1 Newly diagnosed TN: Start with carbamazepine or oxcarbazepine
2 If first-line ineffective or not tolerated: Try lamotrigine, gabapentin, or pregabalin
3 For medicine-unresponsive classical TN: Consider microvascular decompression
4 For medicine-unresponsive idiopathic TN: Consider ablative surgery
5 For medicine-unresponsive secondary TN: Individualized therapy based on underlying cause
Emergency Red Flags
Immediate Referral Required
Conditions requiring urgent evaluation:
  • Bilateral or side-alternating pain - suggests underlying disorder
  • Sensory or motor deficits on neurological examination
  • Pain in V1 distribution with corneal involvement (risk of keratitis)
  • Sudden onset of severe facial pain
  • Associated neurological symptoms (weakness, vision changes, gait disturbance)
Secondary Causes to Consider
Condition Key Indicators Immediate Action
Multiple Sclerosis Younger patients, bilateral symptoms, other neurological signs Urgent MRI with contrast
Posterior Fossa Tumor Progressive symptoms, sensory deficits, gait disturbance Urgent MRI with contrast
Temporal Arteritis Age >50, jaw claudication, visual changes, elevated ESR Urgent ESR, CRP, temporal artery biopsy
Dental Abscess Fever, swelling, localized to tooth Urgent dental evaluation
Treatment Complications
Medication Side Effects Requiring Attention:
  • Allergic rash with carbamazepine or lamotrigine - may precede Stevens-Johnson syndrome
  • Dizziness, ataxia, confusion - may require dose adjustment
  • Hepatotoxicity - monitor liver function with carbamazepine
  • Hyponatremia - monitor sodium levels with carbamazepine or oxcarbazepine
Surgical Complications:
  • Corneal hypoaesthesia - risk of keratitis, requires ophthalmology referral
  • Hearing loss - after microvascular decompression
  • Anesthesia dolorosa - severe complication of ablative procedures
  • Cranial nerve palsies - after surgical procedures

Multidisciplinary Approach

Consider referral to specialist when:

  • Diagnosis is uncertain
  • First-line treatments are ineffective
  • Surgical intervention is being considered
  • Atypical features or red flags are present
