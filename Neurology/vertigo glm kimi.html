Vertigo Quick Reference

Clinical Decision Support Tool
Last updated: Jan 05, 2024

EMERGENCY ALERT

Central causes of vertigo (vascular and neoplastic) are emergencies that should not be overlooked. Immediate imaging required for suspected cases.

Definition

Vertigo is the sensation that the environment is spinning around relative to oneself (objective vertigo) or vice versa (subjective vertigo). True vertigo is described as a rotary sensation of the patient or surroundings, and is often of vestibular origin.

Red Flags

Central signs, inability to stand, vertical nystagmus

Benign Signs

Positional triggers, brief duration, normal neurological exam

Key Tests

Dix-Hallpike, Head-impulse test, Neurological exam

Red Flags & Emergency Actions

Central Vertigo Red Flags

  • Inability to stand without support (even with eyes open)
  • Vertical or direction-changing nystagmus
  • New onset severe headache
  • Focal neurological deficits
  • Significant cerebrovascular risk factors
  • Negative head-impulse test

Emergency Actions

  • Request urgent MRI for all patients with red flags
  • Immediate neurology consultation
  • Consider stroke protocol if cerebellar stroke suspected
  • Admit for observation if central cause cannot be ruled out
  • CT scan if MRI unavailable or contraindicated
Common Vertigo Conditions
Cerebrovascular Causes
Central

Cerebellar stroke may present similarly to peripheral causes but with additional neurological signs.

Sudden onset
Duration: Hours to days
Associated: Nystagmus, ataxia
Diagnosis: MRI
Neoplastic Causes
Central

Intracranial tumors and vestibular schwannomas may present with vertigo and signs of increased intracranial pressure.

Progressive onset
Duration: Weeks to months
Associated: Headache, gait abnormality
Diagnosis: CT/MRI
Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)
Peripheral

Sudden-onset, severe attacks of vertigo usually lasting <30 seconds and precipitated by specific head movements.

Sudden onset
Duration: <30 seconds
Associated: Positional triggers
Diagnosis: Dix-Hallpike test
Meniere's Disease
Peripheral

Episodic vertigo with hearing loss, roaring tinnitus, and ear pressure. Usually affects adults in their fourth decade.

Episodic onset
Duration: Minutes to hours
Associated: Hearing loss, tinnitus
Diagnosis: Clinical, audiometry
Vestibular Migraine
Peripheral

Most common cause of spontaneous episodic vertigo. Affects approximately 20% of patients with migraine.

Episodic onset
Duration: Seconds to days
Associated: Photophobia, phonophobia
Diagnosis: Clinical history

Central Vertigo

  • Gradual onset
  • Severe, persistent symptoms
  • Vertical or direction-changing nystagmus
  • Negative head-impulse test
  • Cannot stand without support
  • Associated neurological symptoms
  • Focal neurological signs

Peripheral Vertigo

  • Sudden onset
  • Brief, episodic symptoms
  • Horizontal unidirectional nystagmus
  • Positive head-impulse test
  • Can stand with eyes open
  • Associated auditory symptoms
  • Normal neurological exam
Management Actions

Emergency Management

  • Immediate neuroimaging for suspected central causes
  • Stroke protocol if cerebellar infarction suspected
  • Urgent neurology consultation
  • Hospital admission for observation
  • Antithrombotic therapy for ischemic stroke
  • Neurosurgical referral for hemorrhagic stroke or tumors

Outpatient Management

  • Canalith repositioning maneuvers for BPPV
  • Vestibular rehabilitation therapy
  • Dietary modifications for Meniere's disease
  • Prophylactic medications for vestibular migraine
  • Short course of vestibular suppressants for acute symptoms
  • Follow-up with ENT or neurology as appropriate
