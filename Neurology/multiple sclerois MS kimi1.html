When to Suspect Multiple Sclerosis

Typical Age: 20-50 years (can present <0.5% aged ‚â•60)
‚ö†Ô∏è Caution Do NOT routinely suspect MS if: Main symptoms are fatigue, depression, or dizziness without focal neurological symptoms/signs
  • History of previous neurological symptoms
  • Symptoms evolve over >24 hours, persist days/weeks, then improve
  • MS can affect nearly any part of CNS

Common Initial Presentations

üö® Urgent Optic Neuritis: Bilateral optic neuritis requires urgent exclusion of neuromyelitis optica
1. Optic Neuritis (20-30% of initial presentations)
  • Partial/total unilateral visual loss developing over days
  • Pain behind eye (especially on eye movement)
  • Loss of colour discrimination (particularly reds)
  • Fundoscopy often normal; may see pale/swollen disc
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect
2. Transverse Myelitis
  • Sensory symptoms (paraesthesia) or motor symptoms (weakness) below inflammation level
  • Develop over hours/days
  • Tight band sensation around trunk at inflammation level
  • Lhermitte's phenomenon (shock-like sensation on neck flexion)
  • Urinary symptoms: urgency, frequency, retention
3. Cerebellar-Related Symptoms
  • Ataxia, vertigo, clumsiness, dysmetria
  • Abnormalities with finger-to-nose testing
  • Walking heel-to-toe difficulties
4. Brainstem Syndromes
  • Eye movement abnormalities ‚Üí diplopia, oscillopsia, nystagmus, internuclear ophthalmoplegia
  • Bulbar muscle problems ‚Üí dysarthria, dysphagia
  • Ataxia

Key Clinical Features

  • Fatigue: Affects >80%, may precede onset by years
  • Spasticity: Affects up to 80%, ranges from mild tightness to severe contractures
  • Ataxia/Tremor: Common, severe disabling tremor in ~6%
  • Visual Problems: Optic neuritis, internuclear ophthalmoplegia, nystagmus, diplopia
  • Reduced Mobility: 85% report gait disturbance as main complaint
  • Pain: Prevalence 29-86% (neuropathic or musculoskeletal)
  • Bladder Problems: >90% in progressive MS
  • Sexual Dysfunction: 50-90% men, 40-80% women
  • Mental Health: Depression/anxiety up to 50% lifetime prevalence
  • Cognitive Impairment: 43-70% during disease course

Risk Factors

  • Genetics: Complex; >200 alleles contribute small risk effects
  • Vitamin D deficiency: May have immunomodulatory role
  • Cigarette smoking: Increases risk and disease progression
  • Diet/Obesity: Early life obesity associated with 2-fold increase
  • Latitude: Prevalence increases with distance from equator
  • EBV infection: May have disease-triggering role
  • Female gender: 2-3x more common in women

Diagnosis & Confirmation
‚ñº

Confirming the Diagnosis

üö® Immediate Refer promptly to consultant neurologist - Early diagnosis and treatment may improve prognosis
  • Only a consultant neurologist should diagnose MS using revised McDonald criteria
  • Establish lesions at different times and locations (RRMS)
  • Establish progressive deterioration over ‚â•1 year (PPMS)

Diagnosing a Relapse

Relapse Definition: New/worsening symptoms lasting >24 hours, after stable period of ‚â•1 month, in absence of infection/other cause
  • Rule out infection (especially UTI, respiratory)
  • Discriminate between relapse vs disease fluctuations/progression
  • Consider other conditions with similar features
  • Do NOT routinely diagnose relapse if symptoms >3 months

Differential Diagnosis

  • Demyelinating diseases: Neuromyelitis optica (urgent!), transverse myelitis, ADEM
  • Metabolic: Vitamin B12 deficiency, diabetes, hypocalcaemia, hypothyroidism
  • Infections: Lyme disease, syphilis, HIV, tropical spastic paraparesis
  • Vascular: Primary CNS vasculitis, ischaemic stroke
  • Systemic inflammatory: SLE, Beh√ßet's, sarcoidosis
  • Neoplastic: Primary/metastatic lesions, paraneoplastic syndromes

Disease Patterns

  • RRMS (85% at onset): Episodes of relapse/remission
  • SPMS: Gradual accumulation of disability, relapses become less frequent
  • PPMS (10-15%): Steady progression from onset without remissions

Specialist Team Role

  • Confirm diagnosis using McDonald criteria
  • Consider disease-modifying therapy (DMT)
  • Organize multidisciplinary management
  • Provide support at diagnosis (NICE Quality Standard)
  • Face-to-face follow-up within 6 weeks
  • Annual comprehensive review

Treatment & Management
‚ñº

Managing a Relapse

üö® Timely Steroid treatment: Offer as early as possible, within 14 days of symptom onset
  • Rule out infection before treating
  • Consider hospital admission if severe, comorbidities, or oral steroids failed
  • Standard treatment: Oral methylprednisolone 0.5g daily for 5 days
  • Do NOT use lower dose than above
  • Benefits: Reduces relapse duration by average 13 days, may reduce severity
  • Risks: Mental health effects (depression, confusion, agitation), worsening glucose control
  • Do NOT give home supply for future self-administration
  • Each relapse should be discussed with specialist team
  • Expected recovery: significant improvement within 2-3 months, can continue up to 12 months
  • Residual disability occurs in 30-50% of relapses

Fatigue Management

  • Assess for sleep difficulties, anxiety, depression, anaemia, thyroid disease
  • Explain precipitating factors: heat, overexertion, stress
  • Advise planning activities, prioritizing, regular short rests
  • Encourage regular aerobic, balance, stretching exercises including yoga
  • Specialist options:
    • Mindfulness-based training
    • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)
    • Fatigue management programmes
    • Supervised exercise programmes
    • Vestibular rehabilitation
  • Drug treatment (specialist):
    • Amantadine (off-label) - may help but not effective for all
    • Modafinil (off-label) - unless pregnancy likely/planned
    • SSRIs (off-label) - lowest effective dose

Spasticity Management

  • Assess/treat aggravating factors: skin irritation, posture, constipation, infections, pain
  • Warning: Some patients use spasticity for standing/walking - treatment may worsen function
  • First-line: Baclofen (see prescribing info below)
  • Second-line: Gabapentin if baclofen not tolerated/inadequate
  • Increase dose gradually every 2 weeks
  • Try maximum tolerated dose before stopping
  • Combination therapy may be tried
  • Encourage self-management within agreed limits
  • Refer to physiotherapy
  • Refer to specialist spasticity service if insufficient control

Ataxia & Tremor Management

  • No specific recommended treatment for MS-related tremor/ataxia
  • Refer to physiotherapist for posture advice
  • Refer to occupational therapist for:
    • Activity adaptations (eating, drinking)
    • Assistive technology
    • Computer modifications

Oscillopsia Management

  • Can cause significant distress and functional limitation
  • First-line (specialist): Gabapentin (off-label)
  • Second-line (specialist): Memantine (off-label)
  • If treatment fails: refer to neuro-ophthalmologist

Mobility Problems

  • Assess/treat aggravating factors: fatigue, spasticity, visual problems
  • Refer for assessment by rehabilitation specialists/physiotherapists
  • Consider:
    • Supervised exercise programmes
    • Vestibular rehabilitation
    • Mobility aids
    • Home adaptations
  • Encourage continued exercise after programme ends
  • Fampridine may benefit some but not cost-effective currently

Mental Health Management

  • Depression/Anxiety: Manage as usual, consider medication interactions
  • CBT: Offer for adjustment difficulties
  • Emotional lability: Consider amitriptyline (specialist)
  • Cognitive impairment:
    • 65% prevalence in MS
    • Assess for anxiety, depression, sleep, fatigue
    • Refer to OT and neuropsychologist

Pain Management

  • Musculoskeletal pain: Common, secondary to mobility/posture problems
    • Refer to physiotherapy
    • Consider NSAIDs (see CKS guidelines)
  • Neuropathic pain: See NICE neuropathic pain guidance
    • Refer to pain services if appropriate

Sexual Problems

  • Common but under-reported (50-90% men, 40-80% women)
  • Thorough assessment required
  • Discuss/refer to secondary care
  • Consider medication for erectile dysfunction
  • Offer relationship or psychosexual counseling

Bladder Problems

Complex management: Discuss with MS team, refer to urology/neurourology/urogynaecology
  • Storage dysfunction most common: urinary frequency, urgency, incontinence
  • Urinary urgency (62%), frequency (50%), urge incontinence (45%), nocturia (33%)
  • See CKS topics on LUTS, urinary incontinence, UTI

Pregnancy Considerations

  • Relapse rates reduce during pregnancy
  • ~25% experience relapse in first 3 months postpartum
  • Pregnancy does not increase disease progression risk
  • If planning pregnancy:
    • Discuss with specialist team
    • Review fertility, child MS risk, vitamin D use
    • Review medication safety (some DMTs contraindicated)
    • Discuss pain relief, breastfeeding, fatigue management
  • UTIs more frequent in pregnancy - advise prompt treatment
  • Increased risk postpartum depression - ensure appropriate management

Follow-up & Ongoing Care
‚ñº

General Management Principles

  • Patient under care of consultant neurologist and multidisciplinary MS team
  • MS nurses are key link between primary/secondary care and community services
  • Offer lifestyle advice for general good health:
    • Encourage regular exercise - beneficial for MS
    • Advise smoking cessation - may increase disability progression
    • Optimize comorbidities (depression, anxiety, vascular risk factors)
    • Be aware of medication interactions with DMTs
  • Vaccinations:
    • Live vaccines may be contraindicated with DMTs - seek specialist advice
    • Offer flu vaccination per national guidance
    • Discuss possible relapse risk after flu vaccination in RRMS
  • Provide information:
    • Patient organizations: MS Trust, MS Society
    • DVLA notification requirements
    • Legal rights (social care, employment, benefits)
    • Social care needs assessment

Monitoring & Review

‚úì Annual Comprehensive review: At least annually by healthcare professionals with MS expertise
  • Usually arranged in secondary care
  • During any consultation, check review occurred in preceding 12 months
  • Discuss cognitive problems and importance of regular assessment
  • Provide information on advance care planning and power of attorney
  • Manage symptoms and end-of-life care in conjunction with MS team and palliative care services

Prognosis & Counselling

  • No cure currently available
  • Prognosis varies widely
  • Neurological disability gradually accumulates over time for most
  • RRMS: 85-90% start with relapsing course; 10-20 years after onset, >50% develop SPMS
  • PPMS: 10-15% have progressive course from onset; faster progression to disability
  • Poor prognostic factors:
    • Male sex, older age at onset
    • Multifocal presentation
    • Pyramidal/cerebellar involvement
    • Partial recovery from relapses
    • High relapse rate in first 2 years
    • High lesion burden on MRI
  • Early intervention with DMTs may reduce relapse risk and delay disability

Quality of Life & Support

  • MS is most common cause of serious physical disability in working-age adults
  • Significant detrimental effect on quality of life for patients and families/carers
  • Provide psychological and social support throughout disease course
  • Encourage engagement with patient organizations for peer support
  • Address employment issues early
  • Consider referral to:
    • Occupational therapy for daily living adaptations
    • Neuropsychology for cognitive issues
    • Pain services
    • Psychosexual counseling

When to Re-refer to Specialist

üö® Urgent Neuromyelitis optica: High mortality if not diagnosed/treated quickly
  • Severe relapse requiring hospital admission
  • New or worsening symptoms not responding to treatment
  • Pregnancy planning or confirmed pregnancy
  • Significant cognitive decline
  • Mental health crisis
  • Uncertainty about diagnosis
  • Consideration of disease-modifying therapy changes

Prescribing Information - Key Drugs
‚ñº

Baclofen (for Spasticity)

Dosing:
  • Start: 5mg three times daily
  • Maintenance: Usually 60mg/day in three divided doses
  • Maximum: 100mg/day in divided doses
  • Adjust based on individual response
  • If no benefit after 6 weeks at maximum dose, stop
  • Withdrawal: Reduce gradually over ‚â•1-2 weeks
Cautions:
  • Cerebrovascular disease, Parkinson's
  • Diabetes, elderly
  • Respiratory, hepatic, renal impairment
  • History of peptic ulceration
  • Epilepsy, severe psychiatric disorders
  • Risk of misuse, abuse, dependence
  • Pregnancy: only if benefit outweighs risk
Adverse Effects:
  • Common: drowsiness, nausea, fatigue, confusion, dizziness, hallucination, depression
  • Rare: abdominal pain, erectile dysfunction, hepatic dysfunction
  • Withdrawal symptoms: anxiety, psychosis, hyperthermia, seizures, tachycardia
Drug Interactions:
  • Alcohol, muscle relaxants, opiates, anxiolytics, hypnotics - increased sedation/respiratory depression
  • TCAs - enhanced muscle relaxation, additive drowsiness
  • Antihypertensives - potentiated hypotensive effect
  • NSAIDs - may decrease baclofen excretion

Gabapentin (for Spasticity/Oscillopsia)

‚ö†Ô∏è Class C Controlled Important: Evaluate history of drug abuse before prescribing. Monitor for abuse and dependence.
Considerations:
  • Off-label use for MS spasticity and oscillopsia
  • Second-line after baclofen for spasticity
  • First-line for oscillopsia
  • See NICE neuropathic pain guidance for details

Methylprednisolone (for Relapses)

Standard Regimen:
  • 0.5g orally once daily for 5 days
  • Do NOT use lower dose
  • Offer within 14 days of symptom onset
  • Reduces relapse duration by average 13 days
Complications:
  • Temporary mental health effects (depression, confusion, agitation)
  • Worsening blood glucose control in diabetes
  • Do NOT give supply for home self-administration

Amitriptyline (for Emotional Lability)

  • Off-label use for MS-related emotional lability
  • See NICE neuropathic pain guidance for details
  • Monitor for side effects: drowsiness, anticholinergic effects
