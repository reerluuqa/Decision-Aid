MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) is a medical emergency. Immediate treatment with high-dose glucocorticoids may prevent permanent vision loss.

Visual Symptoms - URGENT ACTION
New visual loss (transient or permanent)
Double vision
Visual field defects
Changes to colour vision
IMMEDIATE ACTION:
  • Arrange urgent (same day) assessment by an ophthalmologist
  • Consider one-off high dose corticosteroid treatment while awaiting transfer
  • Intravenous glucocorticoid therapy if acute visual loss
  • If IV not possible: 60-100 mg oral prednisolone for up to 3 consecutive days

Diagnosis & Symptoms

Key Diagnostic Criteria
Age 50 years or older
New-onset headache (most common symptom)
Temporal artery abnormality (tenderness, thickening, nodularity)
Visual disturbances
Scalp tenderness (50% of cases)
Intermittent jaw claudication
Systemic features (fever, fatigue, weight loss)
Polymyalgia rheumatica features (40% of cases)
Primary Care Investigations
Essential Blood Tests:
  • Full blood count - normochromic normocytic anaemia, elevated platelet count
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - typically elevated
  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) - often >50 mm/hour
Important: Do not delay referral while waiting for blood test results if GCA is strongly suspected.
Additional Tests if Needed:
  • Baseline urea and electrolytes, HbA1c, calcium and liver function tests
  • Serum protein electrophoresis if ESR elevated out of proportion to CRP
  • Screening for osteoporosis risk

Management

Initial Management
Standard Initial Treatment:
40-60 mg oral prednisolone per day for GCA without visual symptoms
For Visual Symptoms:
60-100 mg oral prednisolone for up to 3 days if IV not possible
Intravenous glucocorticoid therapy if acute visual loss
Additional Considerations:
  • Provide blue steroid card
  • Discuss potential adverse effects of corticosteroids
  • Consider bone and/or gastrointestinal protection
  • Advise on infection risk and live vaccine contraindications
Follow-up & Monitoring
Review Schedule:
  • Every 2-8 weeks during first 6 months
  • Every 12 weeks during second 6 months
  • Every 12-24 weeks during second year
  • At any point if relapse occurs
Each Review Should Include:
  • Full history for symptoms of disease control, relapse, and complications
  • Targeted examination
  • Blood monitoring (FBC, ESR, CRP)
  • Enquiry about adverse effects of corticosteroids
Long-term Monitoring:
  • Consider monitoring for late complications such as aortic aneurysm
  • Arrange on a case-by-case basis by specialist
Managing Relapse
Signs of Relapse:
  • Recurrence of GCA symptoms or signs
  • Ischaemic complications
  • Symptoms of polymyalgia rheumatica
  • Rise in ESR or CRP (though relapse can occur with normal inflammatory markers)
Management Approach:
  • Refer to specialist for assessment
  • May require increase in glucocorticoid dose
  • Consider steroid-sparing agents (methotrexate or tocilizumab)
  • Reassess tapering schedule

Quick Reference

RED FLAGS:
  • Any visual symptoms
  • Jaw claudication
  • Temporal artery abnormalities
  • Systemic features in patients >50 years
IMMEDIATE ACTIONS:
  • Start high-dose steroids if strongly suspected
  • Urgent referral (same day for visual symptoms)
  • Arrange blood tests but don't delay referral
