Myasthenia Gravis

Clinical Reference & Protocol

Myasthenic Crisis: Immediate Action Required

Definition: Exacerbation requiring mechanical ventilation.

Ventilate if FVC ≤ 15 mL/kg
Ventilate if NIF ≤ 20 cm H₂O
Acute Treatment IVIg / PLEX

Caution: Do not wait for ABG changes (pCO₂ rises late). Common triggers: Infection, aspiration, high-dose steroids, surgery, or failure of adherence.

Overview

Chronic autoimmune disorder of the post-synaptic membrane at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ).

Key Sign: Muscle weakness that increases with exercise (fatigue) and improves on rest.

Antibodies: AChR (80-90%), MuSK (3-7%), LRP4, Agrin.
Onset: Women <40, Men >60.

Key Diagnostic Factors

Ocular

  • • Ptosis (Drooping)
  • • Diplopia (Double Vision)
  • • Better in morning, worse at night

Oropharyngeal / Resp

  • • Dysarthria (Slurring)
  • • Dysphagia (Swallowing trouble)
  • • Shortness of breath

Generalized

Proximal limb weakness (arms/legs). No muscle wasting (except tongue in MuSK). Reflexes normal.

Physical Exam Tip: "Ice Pack Test" improves ptosis in >95% of MG patients (2-5 mins of cooling).

Acute Management

!

Myasthenic Crisis

Airway protection & Mechanical Ventilation.

1

IV Immunoglobulin (IVIg)

or Plasma Exchange (PLEX). Rapid improvement usually within 1 week.

2

Identify Triggers

Treat infection, stop offending drugs (e.g., certain antibiotics, beta-blockers), check aspiration.

Chronic Treatment

S

Symptomatic: Pyridostigmine

Anticholinesterase inhibitor. Initial tx for most. Note: MuSK+ patients often respond poorly.

I

Immunosuppression

Prednisolone: Start low, titrate up.
Azathioprine: First-line steroid-sparing agent.
Others: Tacrolimus, Mycophenolate, Ciclosporin.

Sx

Thymectomy

Required if Thymoma present.
Effective in generalized AChR+ patients without thymoma.

N

Newer Therapies (AChR+)

Complement C5 inhibitors (Eculizumab), FcRn antagonists (Efgartigimod).

Key Differentials

Condition Signs that differentiate from MG
Lambert-Eaton (LEMS) Proximal weakness that improves with exercise. Autonomic symptoms (dry mouth). Associated with SCLC.
Botulism Descending paralysis, hypotension, constipation/urinary retention. Pupils often involved.
Primary Myopathies Gradual progressive weakness, not fatigable. Elevated CK.
Stroke / CNS Lesion Sudden onset, usually unilateral, hyperreflexia/spasticity (initially), no fatigability.

MGFA Clinical Classification (Quick Ref)
