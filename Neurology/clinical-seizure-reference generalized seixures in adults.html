Definition

Generalised Tonic-Clonic Seizure (GTCS) is characterised by:

  • Loss of consciousness
  • Phasic tonic stiffening of limbs (symmetric or asymmetric)
  • Repetitive clonic jerking movements
  • Suppressed level of arousal post-event
Key Point: Vast majority are self-limited without intervention

Key Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors:
  • Family history of generalised-onset epilepsy
  • Previous CNS infection (encephalitis, meningitis)
  • Head trauma (prolonged LOC or penetrating)
  • CNS lesions (tumours, vascular malformations)
  • Stroke (most common preventable cause in high-income countries)
  • Antenatal/perinatal brain insult

Urgent Imaging - When to CT First

Head CT should be performed URGENTLY if:
  • Focal neurological deficits on exam
  • Patient not recovering to baseline
  • Suspected intracranial haemorrhage
  • Known brain tumour

Note: CT is more readily available in emergency. MRI is superior for detecting cortical dysplasia, migrational defects, small neoplasms.

Critical: Differentiate Seizures from Mimics

Up to 20-30% of patients referred for "refractory epilepsy" have non-epileptic events

Differentiation Guide

Condition
Key Features
Diagnostic Tests
Functional (Non-Epileptic) Seizures
Signs: Variable consciousness, eye closure, side-to-side head shaking, pelvic thrusting, prolonged activity, no benzodiazepine response
Tongue bite: Tip of tongue (vs lateral in epileptic)
Video-EEG: Normal or muscle artefact, no ictal changes
Consider: Psychological comorbidities (50%+ have anxiety/depression)
Convulsive Syncope
Signs: Brief convulsive activity, rapid return to baseline, triggered by stress/pain/position
Key: No prolonged confusion
Clinical history: Most important
Tests: Tilt-table, serial CK (may help differentiate)
Cardiac Arrhythmia
Signs: Sudden LOC, palpitations/anxiety before, abrupt onset/offset, rapid return to baseline
Note: No prolonged convulsions
ECG: Essential initial test
Further: Holter monitor, loop recorder if normal ECG
TIA (Limb-Shaking)
Signs: Focal motor movements, sudden onset, often with carotid disease
Key: Not true convulsions
CT/MRI: Vascular assessment
EEG: No epileptiform activity
Panic Attacks
Signs: Subjective dread, autonomic symptoms (palpitations, sweating, paresthesias), no LOC
Duration: 10-30 minutes
Neuroimaging/EEG: Normal
Video-EEG: No ictal changes
Tic Disorders
Signs: Head/neck/shoulder movements, suppressible, not impairing consciousness
Key: Rhythmic vs seizure movements
EEG: Normal, no epileptiform features
Clinical: Diagnosis based on history/exam
Red Flags for Non-Epileptic Events:
  • Continuous limb shaking without true unconsciousness
  • Eye closure during event (rare in true seizures)
  • Variable/prolonged course (>5 minutes typical)
  • No response to benzodiazepines
  • Side-to-side head shaking or pelvic thrusting
  • Waxing and waning course
  • Tip-of-tongue biting (vs lateral)

Pregnancy Considerations - Critical

Multidisciplinary care required. Balance seizure risks vs teratogenicity.

üö´

Avoid in Pregnancy

Contraindicated due to teratogenicity:
  • Valproic acid - Major malformations, neurodevelopmental disorders, neural tube defects
  • Topiramate - Cleft lip, neurodevelopmental disorders, SGA
  • Phenobarbital - Major malformations, neurodevelopmental effects
  • Phenytoin - Major malformations, neurodevelopmental effects
Valproic Acid Restrictions:
  • Not for females of childbearing potential unless no alternative
  • Requires pregnancy prevention programme
  • Male patients: potential risk 3 months before conception
‚úÖ

Preferred in Pregnancy

Consider (per AAN):
  • Lamotrigine - Not associated with increased malformations
  • Levetiracetam - Not associated with increased malformations
  • Oxcarbazepine - Generally safe option
Important Notes:
  • Monitor drug levels - pharmacokinetics change in pregnancy
  • May need increased doses
  • Anatomic ultrasound 14-18 weeks + serum AFP
  • Pre-conception counselling essential
  • Folic acid supplementation recommended
