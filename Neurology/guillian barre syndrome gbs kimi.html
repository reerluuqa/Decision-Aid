Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk stratification

‚ñº

Key Clinical Features Red Flag

Progressive Weakness

Ascending, symmetrical muscle weakness affecting lower limbs before upper limbs, proximal before distal

Areflexia/Hyporeflexia

Absent or reduced deep tendon reflexes, typically in weakest limbs

Sensory Symptoms

Paraesthesias in hands and feet, often preceding weakness. Usually mild objective sensory loss

Autonomic Dysfunction

Sinus tachycardia, labile BP, postural hypotension, urinary retention, ileus

Cranial Nerve Involvement

Facial weakness (50-60%), bulbar dysfunction, extraocular muscle weakness (15%)

Pain

Back and leg pain common, especially prominent in children

‚ö†Ô∏è Respiratory Warning Signs

20-30% of patients develop respiratory muscle weakness requiring mechanical ventilation. Monitor for:

  • Bulbar dysfunction (odds ratio 17.5 for intubation)
  • Rapid disease progression
  • Bilateral facial weakness
  • Dysautonomia
  • Inability to lift head or cough

Risk Factors & Triggers

Preceding Infection

Two-thirds have viral (CMV, EBV, hepatitis E) or bacterial (C. jejuni) infection 1-3 weeks prior

Campylobacter jejuni

Precedes 60-70% of AMAN/AMSAN cases, 30% of AIDP. 100-fold increased risk in 2 months post-infection

Viral Infections

Zika virus (1.23% develop GBS), COVID-19, dengue, chikungunya, Japanese encephalitis

Demographics

Slight male predominance (1.78:1), incidence increases with age (mean 40 years)

Respiratory Assessment Critical

20/30/40 Rule for Intubation

Consider ICU admission and elective intubation if ANY of the following are present:

  • Vital capacity < 20 mL/kg (odds ratio 15.0)
  • Maximal inspiratory pressure worse than -30 cmH‚ÇÇO
  • Maximal expiratory pressure < 40 cmH‚ÇÇO
  • Reduction of 30% or more in any parameter from baseline

Monitor: Bedside spirometry every 6 hours initially. Do NOT rely on pulse oximetry or arterial blood gases alone (late signs).

Diagnosis

Diagnostic criteria and investigations

‚ñº

Diagnostic Criteria

Required Features

  • Progressive weakness in both arms and legs
  • Areflexia or hyporeflexia

Supportive Features

  • Progression over days to 4 weeks
  • Relative symmetry
  • Mild sensory signs or symptoms
  • Cranial nerve involvement, especially bilateral facial weakness
  • Recovery beginning 2-4 weeks after progression ceases
  • Albuminocytological dissociation in CSF
  • Electrophysiological evidence of demyelination

Features That Rule Out Diagnosis

  • Recent diphtheria infection
  • Abnormal porphyrin metabolism
  • Hexacarbon abuse or lead intoxication
  • Poliomyelitis, botulism, hysterical paralysis

First-Line Investigations Order Immediately

Nerve Conduction Studies

Prolonged distal & F-wave latencies, reduced conduction velocities, H reflex prolonged/absent. Test 3 sensory & 3 motor nerves bilaterally.

Lumbar Puncture

CSF: Elevated protein with normal cell count (<5 cells/mm¬≥). May be normal in first 2 weeks. 15% have mild pleocytosis (5-50 cells/mm¬≥).

S pirometry

Bedside spirometry every 6 hours initially. Monitor vital capacity, maximal inspiratory/expiratory pressures.

Liver Function Tests

Aminotransferases may be elevated (up to 500 U/L) in first few days, normalizing by 1-2 weeks. Correlates with disease severity.

Additional Investigations

Anti-ganglioside Antibodies

Limited diagnostic value. Consider for variant syndromes: anti-GQ1b (MFS, 90% positive), anti-GT1a (pharyngeal-cervical-brachial).

Serology & Stool Culture

C. jejuni, CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma for epidemiological purposes. C. jejuni positive serology associated with worse prognosis.

Spinal MRI

Consider if diagnosis unclear. May show nerve root enhancement (83% sensitive). Exclude spinal cord pathology (abscess, myelitis).

HIV Testing

Indicated if high risk or CSF pleocytosis >10 cells/mm¬≥.

Differential Diagnosis

Transverse Myelitis

Sensory level, early bowel/bladder dysfunction, hyperreflexia, MRI focal demyelination

Myasthenia Gravis

Fluctuating weakness, preserved reflexes, no sensory loss, positive edrophonium test

Botulism

Descending paralysis, fixed dilated pupils, constipation, EMG shows incremental response

Polymyositis

Muscle pain/tenderness, preserved reflexes, no dysautonomia, elevated CK

Treatment

Disease-modifying & supportive therapy

‚ñº

Principles of Treatment

Multidisciplinary approach combining supportive therapy and disease-modifying therapy with either IVIG or plasma exchange. Start treatment within 2 weeks of symptom onset for optimal outcomes.

Disease-Modifying Therapy Time Critical

First-Line Treatment Options

IVIG and plasma exchange are equally efficacious. Choice depends on availability, contraindications, and institutional preference.

Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG) Preferred

Indication: Ambulatory patients ‚â§2 weeks from onset, or non-ambulatory ‚â§4 weeks

Dose: 400 mg/kg/day for 5 days (total 2 g/kg)

Advantages: Peripheral IV access, fewer complications, easier administration

Contraindications: IgA deficiency (anaphylaxis risk), severe renal failure

Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis)

Indication: Non-ambulatory patients within 4 weeks of onset

Regimen: 50 mL/kg every other day for 7-14 days (total 5 exchanges)

Advantages: Preferred in IgA deficiency or renal failure

Disadvantages: Requires central venous access, more complications (infection, hypotension)

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Notes

  • Combination therapy (plasma exchange + IVIG) is NOT recommended
  • Second IVIG course NOT recommended even for poor prognosis (increased adverse events)
  • Corticosteroids do NOT significantly improve outcomes and may delay recovery

Supportive Therapy Critical Care

Respiratory Management

ICU monitoring for at-risk patients. Early intubation for bulbar dysfunction or VC <20 mL/kg. Monitor with serial PFTs.

Cardiovascular Monitoring

Continuous telemetry for dysautonomia. Treat hypertension with short-acting agents (labetalol, esmolol).

DVT Prophylaxis

Anticoagulation (LMWH or DOAC) + compression stockings for non-ambulatory patients.

Pain Management

Gabapentin, carbamazepine, amitriptyline for neuropathic pain. Use opioids cautiously (worsen autonomic dysfunction).

Rehabilitation

Start gentle strengthening exercises in acute phase:

  • Isometric, isotonic, isokinetic exercises
  • Manual resistive and progressive resistive exercises
  • Proper limb positioning and posture
  • Orthotics and nutrition support
  • Multidisciplinary approach improves outcomes

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications & prognosis

‚ñº

Disease Course Monitoring

Progressive Phase

Symptoms worsen for up to 2 weeks (80% reach nadir by 2 weeks, 97% by 4 weeks)

Plateau Phase

Persistent, unchanging symptoms lasting 2-4 weeks

Recovery Phase

Recovery begins 2-4 weeks after progression ceases. Most regain independent walking by 6 months

Complications Monitor Closely

Respiratory Failure

20-30% require mechanical ventilation. Monitor with serial PFTs. Mean ventilation duration: 15-43 days.

Likelihood:
Medium
Autonomic Dysfunction

Cardiac arrhythmias, labile BP, ileus, urinary retention. Monitor with telemetry and fluid balance.

Likelihood:
Medium
Deep Vein Thrombosis

Risk from immobility and IVIG-induced hypercoagulability. Use prophylactic anticoagulation.

Likelihood:
Medium
Fatigue

Common long-term sequela (high likelihood). Supervised exercise programs recommended.

Likelihood:
High
Psychological Problems

Depression, anxiety, PTSD. Early recognition and referral to psychologist/psychiatrist important.

Likelihood:
Medium
Neuropathic Pain

Chronic pain affects many patients. Manage with gabapentin, carbamazepine, amitriptyline.

Likelihood:
High

Prognosis

Overall Prognosis: GOOD

  • 85% make good functional recovery
  • 80% walk independently by 6 months
  • Miller-Fisher syndrome: most recover completely within 6 months without treatment
  • Recovery may be prolonged in severe disease but most regain independent walking
Poor Prognostic Factors
  • More severe weakness at nadir
  • Rapid onset of symptoms
  • Older age (>75 years)
  • Muscle wasting
  • Electrically inexcitable nerves
  • Preceding diarrheal illness (C. jejuni)
  • Need for mechanical ventilation (20% mortality in this group)

Follow-up Schedule

Recommended Follow-up Timeline

  • Within 2 weeks: Evaluate for relapse (consider plasma exchange if relapse occurs)
  • Every 4-6 weeks: For 6 months (monitor recovery progress)
  • Every 6 months: For 1 year
  • Then annually: Long-term monitoring

Patient Education & Resources

Advice for Patients

  • Contact physician immediately for worsening weakness, numbness, breathing/swallowing difficulty, or bladder changes
  • Continue physiotherapy and occupational therapy
  • Be aware of possible long-term effects: fatigue, pain, psychological problems
  • Seek psychological support if needed
  • Avoid immunizations during acute phase and up to 1 year after onset
