Suspected TIA?

DEFINITION

Transient neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischemia, without acute infarction.

Duration: < 24 hours (usually resolves within 1 hour).

Referral Protocol

Refer URGENTLY (within 24 hours)

All people presenting with suspected TIA within the last week should be referred to a stroke physician without risk stratification.

Immediate Treatment (While awaiting specialist assessment)

  • Give a single dose of Aspirin 300 mg immediately (if not already taking anticoagulation). Note: NICE advises this carries low risk of bleeding even in people on anticoagulants.

Background & Risks

Key Risk Factors

Hypertension Atrial Fibrillation Diabetes Mellitus Smoking Hyperlipidaemia Alcohol Misuse Physical Inactivity Obesity Age > 55 Migraine (with aura) Sickle Cell Disease Family History

Causes: ~85% Ischaemic (Thrombus/Embolus), ~15% Haemorrhagic (Intracerebral/Subarachnoid).

Complications

Early Period (0-30 days)
  • Cerebral oedema
  • Haemorrhagic transformation
  • Seizures
  • Venous thromboembolism (DVT/PE)
  • Aspiration pneumonia
  • Cardiac complications (Arrhythmias, MI)
Long-term Mobility & Physical
  • Hemiparesis/Hemiplegia (80% of people)
  • Falls (73% of severe stroke patients)
  • Spasticity and contractures
  • Shoulder pain (subluxation)
Cognitive & Sensory
  • Post-stroke dementia (1/3 develop post-recurrent stroke)
  • Neuropathic pain (Central post-stroke pain)
  • Visual problems (Hemianopia, diplopia)
  • Dyspraxia
Daily Living
  • Continence problems (Urinary/Faecal)
  • Dysphagia (Swallowing difficulties) - Dehydration risk
  • Fatigue (Not relieved by rest)
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Depression / Mood disorders

Source: NICE CKS Stroke and TIA. Last revised November 2025.
