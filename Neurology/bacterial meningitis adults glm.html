Bacterial Meningitis in Adults

Last updated: Nov 25, 2025
Quick Reference Evidence-Based
EMERGENCY: Suspect bacterial meningitis in adults with fever, headache, neck stiffness, and altered consciousness. Start empirical antimicrobial therapy promptly.

Red Flag Combination

  • Fever - Present in 77% of cases
  • Headache - Present in 87% of cases
  • Neck stiffness - Present in 83% of cases
  • Altered consciousness - Present in 69% of cases

Note: The classic triad (fever, neck stiffness, altered mental status) occurs in only 41-51% of patients. 95% have at least two of the four symptoms.

Within 1 Hour of Arrival

Immediately
Initial assessment by senior clinical decision-maker
Within 15 minutes
Blood cultures (even if antibiotics given in community)
Within 30 minutes
Lumbar puncture (unless contraindicated) - at least 15 mL of CSF
Within 60 minutes
Start empirical antimicrobial therapy promptly

Do NOT routinely perform neuroimaging before lumbar puncture.

Perform cranial imaging before LP if:

  • Reduced or fluctuating level of consciousness (GCS <9)
  • New focal neurological signs, including seizures
  • Abnormal pupillary reactions or papilloedema

Delay LP but Give Antibiotics If

  • Shock or severe respiratory/cardiac compromise
  • Sepsis or rapidly progressing rash
  • Confirmed/known bleeding or high risk of bleeding
  • Raised intracranial pressure signs

Initial Management

Empirical Therapy
Targeted Therapy

For immunocompetent adults:

  • Third-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone or cefotaxime)
  • Vancomycin (if local resistance rates high)

For immunocompromised adults or >50 years:

  • Ampicillin (to cover Listeria monocytogenes)
Pathogen Treatment
Streptococcus pneumoniae Penicillin G or ceftriaxone ± vancomycin
Neisseria meningitidis Penicillin G or ceftriaxone
Haemophilus influenzae Ceftriaxone or cefotaxime
Listeria monocytogenes Ampicillin ± gentamicin

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors
Weak Risk Factors
  • Advanced age - >60 years due to impaired immunity
  • Crowding - College dormitories, military camps
  • Immunocompromising conditions - Diabetes, alcohol misuse
  • Cranial anatomical defects or VP shunts
  • Cochlear implants
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Contiguous infection - Sinusitis, pneumonia, mastoiditis
  • Genetic predisposition - Complement deficiencies

Epidemiology

Incidence:

  • Western countries: 0.7-0.9 per 100,000/year
  • African countries: 10-40 per 100,000/year

Most common pathogens:

  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Neisseria meningitidis
  • Haemophilus influenzae type b

Mortality: About 1 in 5 cases of pneumococcal meningitis in adults result in death.
