Assessment

Quick Tips

  • Vertigo = false sensation of movement/spinning
  • Differentiate from light-headedness or disequilibrium
  • Always consider stroke in acute vestibular syndrome
  • Perform targeted examination based on history

History Taking

Timing

  • Duration: Seconds, minutes, hours, continuous?
  • Onset: Sudden vs gradual
  • Frequency: Single episode vs recurrent

Triggers

  • Positional: Triggered by head movement (BPPV)
  • Spontaneous: Occurs without provocation
  • Movement: Worsened by head motion

Associated Symptoms

  • Ear symptoms: Hearing loss, tinnitus, fullness
  • Neurological: Diplopia, dysarthria, weakness, ataxia
  • Autonomic: Nausea, vomiting, sweating
Relevant Medical History
  • Recent upper respiratory tract infection (vestibular neuronitis)
  • Migraine history (vestibular migraine)
  • Head trauma (BPPV, perilymphatic fistula)
  • Cardiovascular risk factors (stroke)
  • Ototoxic medications (aminoglycosides, furosemide)
  • Alcohol use
  • Family history of migraine or Ménière's disease

Physical Examination

Ear Examination

  • Inspect external ear canal and tympanic membrane
  • Look for discharge, vesicular eruptions (herpes zoster)
  • Check for cholesteatoma signs (retraction pocket)

Eye Examination

  • Inspect for nystagmus (note direction, effect of gaze fixation)
  • Perform fundoscopy
  • Check for diplopia or visual field defects
Special Tests

Romberg's Test

  1. Patient stands with feet together
  2. Ask to close eyes
  3. Positive: Loss of balance with eyes closed
  4. Interpretation: Proprioceptive or vestibular problem (not specific)

Dix-Hallpike Manoeuvre

Caution

Avoid if neck/back problems or cardiovascular issues
  1. Patient sits upright, head turned 45° to one side
  2. Rapidly lie patient down, head extended 20-30°
  3. Hold position 30 seconds, observe for nystagmus
  4. Positive: Torsional upbeating nystagmus after latency of 5-20 seconds
  5. Fatigable within 1 minute

Head Impulse Test

Caution

Assess neck movement first; avoid in cervical spine disease
  1. Patient fixes gaze on examiner's nose
  2. Examiner rapidly turns head 10-20° to side
  3. Normal: Eyes stay fixed on target
  4. Positive: Corrective saccade back to target
  5. Interpretation: Peripheral vestibular hypofunction on side tested

Alternate Cover Test

Tests for skew deviation (suggests central lesion)

  1. Patient looks at examiner's nose
  2. Alternate covering each eye
  3. Positive: Vertical correction when uncovered
  4. Interpretation: Stroke until proven otherwise

Unterberger's Test

  1. Patient marches on spot with eyes closed
  2. Positive: Rotation >30° to one side
  3. Interpretation: Labyrinthine dysfunction on side of rotation

Red Flags

Suspect Central Cause if:

  • Persistent vertigo >24 hours with hyperacute onset
  • Normal head impulse test
  • New onset headache (especially occipital)
  • Severe ataxia or inability to walk
  • New unilateral deafness
  • Cranial nerve palsies or limb weakness/sensory loss
  • Direction-changing nystagmus on lateral gaze
  • Vertical or purely torsional nystagmus

Diagnosis

Differentiating Peripheral vs Central Vertigo

Peripheral Vertigo

  • Can walk (may be unstable)
  • May have hearing loss/tinnitus
  • No other neurological symptoms
  • Horizontal nystagmus with torsional component
  • Nystagmus suppressed by fixation
  • Head impulse test often positive

Central Vertigo

  • Often unable to stand/walk
  • Uncommon hearing loss (but possible)
  • Usually other neurological symptoms
  • Direction-changing or vertical nystagmus
  • Nystagmus not suppressed by fixation
  • Head impulse test usually normal

Specific Diagnoses

Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

Typical Features

  • Episodes triggered by head position change
  • Duration: seconds to <1 minute
  • Latency: symptoms begin 5-20 seconds after movement
  • Fatigable on repeated testing

Nystagmus Characteristics

  • Torsional upbeating nystagmus
  • Geotropic (beats toward lower ear)
  • Transient (<1 minute)
  • Fatigues with repeated testing

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Typical history of positional vertigo
  • Positive Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre
  • Nystagmus characteristics consistent with BPPV
Vestibular Neuronitis/Labyrinthitis

Clinical Course

  • Acute onset, severe vertigo
  • Persists for days, gradually improves
  • Often follows viral infection
  • May have gait instability

Hearing Status

  • Neuronitis: No hearing loss
  • Labyrinthitis: Hearing loss + tinnitus
  • No ear fullness (unlike Ménière's)

Examination Findings

  • Spontaneous nystagmus initially
  • Positive head impulse test (unilateral)
  • Romberg test may be positive
  • May have gait ataxia
Ménière's Disease

Episode Characteristics

  • Spontaneous onset (not positional)
  • Duration: 30 minutes to several hours
  • Recurrent episodes
  • May have clusters of attacks

Triad of Symptoms

  • Vertigo
  • Fluctuating hearing loss
  • Tinnitus
  • Ear fullness

Important Note

Can be difficult to distinguish from vestibular migraine early in the disease course. Requires long-term follow-up for definitive diagnosis.

Vestibular Migraine

Diagnostic Features

  • Current or previous migraine history
  • Vestibular symptoms (5 min to 72 hours)
  • Photophobia or phonophobia
  • Visual aura may be present

Differentiation

  • Associated migraine features
  • May have headache during vertigo
  • Less prominent ear symptoms
  • History of motion sickness

Treatment

Central Vertigo Management

Urgent Action Required

Any suspicion of central cause requires immediate referral or admission

Immediate Referral Criteria

  • Isolated persistent vertigo >24 hours with hyperacute onset
  • Normal head impulse test
  • New onset headache (especially occipital)
  • Cranial nerve palsies or limb weakness/sensory loss
  • Severe ataxia or inability to walk
Emergency

Referral Process

Urgently refer to:

  • Neurology (suspected stroke)
  • ENT/Audiovestibular medicine (suspected central cause)
  • Direct to Emergency Department if critically unwell

Symptomatic Treatment

Consider short-term treatment while awaiting specialist review

Peripheral Vertigo Management

BPPV Management

Canalith Repositioning

Epley manoeuvre for posterior canal BPPV

  • Effective in >80% of cases
  • May need 1-3 treatments
  • Can be taught for home use
Primary Treatment

Short-term Medications

For severe symptoms during repositioning

Short course only

Refer if:

  • Refractory after 3 attempts
  • Atypical nystagmus
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Patient unable to tolerate manoeuvre
Vestibular Neuronitis/Labyrinthitis

Acute Phase (First 3 days)

  • Vestibular suppressants for severe symptoms
  • Antiemetics for nausea/vomiting
  • Encourage gentle mobilisation

Recovery Phase

  • Vestibular rehabilitation exercises
  • Gradual return to normal activities
  • Avoid prolonged use of vestibular suppressants

Expected Course

  • Acute symptoms: 1-3 days
  • Gradual improvement: 1-3 weeks
  • Full recovery: 4-6 weeks (may have residual symptoms)
Ménière's Disease

Acute Attack

  • Vestibular suppressants (short course)
  • Anti-emetics if needed
  • Rest in quiet, dark room

Long-term Management

  • Salt restriction (1.5-2g sodium/day)
  • Diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide)
  • Betahistine (may reduce frequency)
  • Avoid caffeine, alcohol, nicotine

Referral

All suspected cases to ENT/Audiovestibular medicine

Specialist care

Symptomatic Drug Treatment

Important Considerations

  • Avoid long-term use (>1 week) - impairs vestibular compensation
  • Caution in elderly (sedation, falls risk)
  • Avoid driving/operating machinery
  • Review after 1 week if symptoms persist

Follow-up

Patient Education

Key Points to Discuss

  • Explanation of diagnosis
  • Expected course and prognosis
  • Importance of medication compliance
  • Avoid driving during acute symptoms
  • Strategies to prevent falls
  • When to seek urgent review

Driving Advice

DVLA Guidance:

  • Stop driving during acute vertigo episodes
  • May resume when symptoms controlled
  • Group 2 drivers (HGV/bus) require specialist review
  • Notify DVLA if condition is chronic or recurrent

Follow-up Schedule

1 Week Review

All patients with persistent symptoms

  • Assess symptom improvement
  • Review medication use
  • Check for red flag symptoms
  • Consider referral if not improving

At 2-4 Weeks

If symptoms persist:

  • Reconsider diagnosis
  • Refer if not already done
  • Vestibular rehabilitation if peripheral cause

Ongoing Follow-up

Specialist clinics will arrange:

  • Vestibular testing (VNG, caloric testing)
  • Audiometry
  • Imaging if indicated (MRI brain/IAM)
  • Specialist treatments (e.g., intratympanic injections)

Vestibular Rehabilitation

Exercise Programs

Gaze Stabilisation

  • Focus on target while moving head
  • Progress from slow to fast movements
  • Different target distances

Habituation

  • Repeated exposure to provoking movements
  • Graded progression
  • Reduces symptom over time

Balance Training

  • Static balance exercises
  • Dynamic balance activities
  • Dual task training

Home Program

  • Brandt-Daroff exercises for BPPV
  • Graded daily activities
  • Progressive walking program

Duration

  • Typically 6-12 weeks
  • Daily practice essential
  • May need specialist physiotherapy referral

When to Seek Urgent Review

Red Flags Requiring Immediate Attention

  • New neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, speech changes)
  • Severe headache or neck pain
  • Double vision or visual loss
  • Sudden severe hearing loss
  • Inability to stand or walk
  • Persistent vomiting/dehydration
  • Chest pain or palpitations

Referral Pathways

Neurology

For suspected stroke/TIA, multiple sclerosis, or other central causes

Urgent if acute

ENT/Audiovestibular

For BPPV, Ménière's disease, chronic peripheral vertigo

Routine unless severe
