Migraine

Primary care management for adults & children 12y+
Age 12+ Updated Aug 2025

Definition

Common primary headache disorder characterized by attacks of moderate or severe headache (often unilateral/pulsating).

Photophobia Phonophobia Nausea Vomiting

Epidemiology

  • Global: 1 in 7 people.
  • UK: ~10 million people.
  • Sex: 2-3x more common in women.
  • Age: Most common 25-55 years.

Common Triggers

Lifestyle

Disturbed sleep, missed meals, lack of exercise, stress.

Dietary

Excessive caffeine intake.

Hormonal

Menstruation (common in 18-25% of female migraineurs).

Migraine without Aura

Requires at least 5 attacks fulfilling:

Duration: 4–72 hours (adults) or 2–72 hours (adolescents).
Two of: Unilateral, Pulsating, Moderate/Severe, Aggravated by activity.
One of: Nausea/Vomiting OR Photophobia + Phonophobia.

Migraine with Aura

Requires at least 2 attacks fulfilling:

Fully reversible aura symptoms (Visual, Sensory, Speech).
Three of: Spreads gradually ≥5 min, ≥2 symptoms in succession, lasts 5-60 min, Unilateral, Positive symptoms.
Headache accompanies or follows within 60 mins.

Classification by Frequency

Episodic

Less than 15 days/month.

Chronic

≥15 days/month (with migraine features on ≥8 days) for >3 months.

Menstrual-related

Occurs predominantly between 2 days before and 3 days after menstruation start.

IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

Investigate urgently or refer if secondary headache disorder is suspected. Exclude meningitis, giant cell arteritis, intracranial hemorrhage, or malignancy.

Atypical Aura (Requires Specialist/Admission)

  • Motor weakness
  • Double vision
  • Visual symptoms affecting only one eye
  • Poor balance
  • Decreased level of consciousness

"Thunderclap" or Secondary Signs

  • Sudden onset (reach peak < 5 mins)
  • Fever, neck stiffness (meningism)
  • Papilledema
  • New onset after age 50 (unless clear history)
  • Systemic symptoms (weight loss, jaw claudication - suspect GCA)

Differential Diagnosis

Primary Headaches

Tension-type headache, Cluster headache, Paroxysmal hemicrania.

Secondary Headaches

Head trauma, Vascular disorders (SAH, thrombosis), Infection, Substance withdrawal.

Other

Glaucoma, Sinusitis, TMJ disorder, Trigeminal neuralgia.

Clinical Goals

Primary care objectives for managing migraine:

  • Recognize and diagnose migraine accurately.
  • Identify possible trigger factors.
  • Manage acute attacks effectively.
  • Prevent Medication Overuse Headache (MOH).
  • Consider preventative treatments for chronic migraine.
  • Refer to secondary care where appropriate.

Treatment Principles

Acute: Combination therapy with triptan + NSAID or Paracetamol.
Preventative: Consider Propranolol, Topiramate (note pregnancy restrictions), or Candesartan.
Children: Management of young people (12y+) follows similar pathways but monitor growth.

Pregnancy Considerations

Most women (up to 80%) improve in 2nd/3rd trimester.

  • Risk: Migraine with aura may increase ischemic stroke risk.
  • Medication: Seek specialist advice re: anti-emetics.
  • Topiramate: High risk of fetal malformations; ensure effective contraception.

Complications to Monitor

Medication Overuse Headache Chronic Migraine Migrainous Infarction Status Migrainosus (>72h) Depression
