CRPS Rapid-Ref

Clinical Decision Support

EMERGENCY: Rule Out Immediately

Compartment Syndrome Pain out of proportion, tense swollen tissue, pain with passive movement → Urgent surgical consult
Arterial Compromise Colour/temperature changes, cramping, dystonias, burning pain, gangrene → Vascular imaging urgent
Severe Infection Hot, red, swollen, painful extremity → ESR, CRP, blood cultures, consider aspiration/swab
Deep Vein Thrombosis Swelling, pain, discoloration (NOT burning/electrical) → Compression ultrasound urgent

Key Risk Factors

Trauma (fractures, sprains, surgery)
Immobilisation (casting)
Female sex (2-4x higher)
Minor procedures (phlebotomy, injections)

Treatment Algorithm

1

Physiotherapy + Occupational Therapy

Reduce oedema, decrease muscle guarding, improve function. Include desensitisation, active/passive exercises, stress loading.

FIRST-LINE
2

Psychological Therapies

Part of comprehensive interdisciplinary treatment. Address anxiety, depression, and barriers to recovery.

CONCOMITANT
3

Pharmacotherapy

NSAIDs → Anticonvulsants (gabapentin/pregabalin) → TCAs → Bisphosphonates (if osteopenia)

ADJUNCT
4

Consider Corticosteroids

Short course for early CRPS (without contraindications). IV therapy shows improvement up to 12 months.

EARLY ONLY
1

Continue Rehabilitation

Physiotherapy + OT remains essential. Graded motor imagery and mirror visual feedback may help.

ONGOING
2

Optimise Pharmacotherapy

Anticonvulsants, TCAs, SNRIs, topical lidocaine. Consider ketamine infusions (limited evidence).

MULTI-MODAL
3

Interventional Therapies

Sympathetic blocks (limited evidence), somatic nerve blocks, epidural infusions. Reserve for refractory cases.

SECOND-LINE
4

Neuromodulation

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) or Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG) stimulation for refractory CRPS.

ADVANCED

Differential Diagnoses - Key Differentiators

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Infection Hot, red, swollen extremity. ESR/CRP elevated.
DVT Swelling, pain, discoloration. NOT burning/electrical. Ultrasound diagnostic.
Arterial Compromise Colour/temp changes, cramping, gangrene possible. Vascular imaging required.
Gout Sudden onset, joints affected (often big toe). Monosodium urate crystals.
Peripheral Neuropathy Symmetrical, distal lower extremities first. No trauma history. Nerve conduction studies.
Lymphoedema Insidious onset, non-pitting oedema, aching pain (not burning).
