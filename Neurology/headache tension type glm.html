Headache - Tension-Type

Safety First: Exclude Red Flags & Secondary Causes before diagnosing tension-type headache. If red flags exist -> Admit or Refer Urgently.

üîç Clinical Features

Must have at least two of:

  • Bilateral location (both sides).
  • Pressing/tightening quality (non-pulsating). "Like a vice or tight band".
  • Mild or moderate intensity.
  • Not aggravated by routine physical activity (walking, stairs).

Associated symptoms: No nausea or vomiting. No more than one of photophobia (light sensitivity) or phonophobia (sound sensitivity).

  • Secondary causes: Giant cell arteritis, raised ICP, infection, glaucoma, cervical spine issues.

üö® When to Refer / Admit

  • Red Flags Present: Suspected secondary cause (e.g., thunderclap headache, neurological signs, systemic illness).
  • Uncertainty: Diagnosis unclear or atypical symptoms.
  • Refractory: Symptoms persist despite optimal primary care management.
Do NOT: Refer for neuroimaging solely for reassurance if diagnosis is clear tension-type headache.
Treatment Pathway

Management Plan

Warning: Advise all patients on the risk of Medication Overuse Headache (MOH). Avoid frequent/excessive use of acute analgesia (Opioids, Triptans, or simple analgesics >10-15 days/month).

üíö Acute Episodic Tension-Type Headache

First-Line Treatment

Advise taking OTC analgesia as soon as possible after onset.

  • Paracetamol
  • Aspirin
  • NSAID (e.g., Ibuprofen, Naproxen)

Avoid / Cautions

  • Do NOT recommend opioids for acute treatment.
  • Review triggers: Stress, sleep disorders, poor posture, neck pain, diet.

üíô Frequent Episodic or Chronic

Consider non-pharmacological interventions first.

Physiotherapy
Consider for some people (esp. with neck pain/postural issues).
Regular Exercise
Aerobic exercise can be beneficial.
CBT & Relaxation
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy or relaxation techniques.

Prescribing Information

Amitriptyline Hydrochloride

Note: This is a TCA (Tricyclic Antidepressant). Even at low doses used for headache, adverse effects and interactions apply.

üõë Contraindications (Do Not Prescribe)

  • Recent Myocardial Infarction (MI)
  • Arrhythmias (especially heart block)
  • Acute Porphyria
  • Severe Hepatic Impairment
  • Manic phase of Bipolar Disorder

‚ö†Ô∏è Cautions / Monitor

  • Cardiovascular disease (Bradycardia, Heart Failure)
  • History of Bipolar/Psychosis
  • Risk of Suicide (especially under 25s)
  • Glaucoma (Increased intraocular pressure)
  • Urinary Retention / Prostatic hypertrophy
  • Epilepsy/Seizures
  • Thyroid disease (Hyperthyroidism)

Common Adverse Effects

System Effects
Anticholinergic Dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, urinary retention.
CNS Drowsiness (very common), dizziness, tremor, fatigue.
Cardiac Palpitations, tachycardia, postural hypotension.
Psychiatric Agitation, confusion (esp. elderly), sleep disturbances.
Weight Increased appetite / weight gain.

*Adverse effects are often dose-dependent. Slow titration may improve tolerance.

Key Drug Interactions

  • MAOIs: Do not give within 2 weeks of stopping MAOIs.
  • Arrhythmics: Increased risk of arrhythmias (e.g., Quinidine, Flecainide).
  • Antihypertensives: TCAs may reduce the effect of clonidine, reserpine, etc.
  • Sympathomimetics: Risk of hypertension (e.g., Ephedrine, Phenylephrine).
  • Alcohol: Increased CNS depression.
  • Warfarin: May increase INR (monitor).
