FND

Functional Neurological Disorder

Primary Care Clinical Reference

NICE CKS Sept 2024
Rule-In, Not Rule-Out: Diagnosis requires positive signs of FND on examination. Do not use as a diagnosis of exclusion.
Red Flags / Alternatives

Consider these organic pathologies first:

Sign/Symptom Action
Sudden onset weakness/speech issues Urgent Stroke/TIA
Progressive gait disturbance/headache Urgent CNS Cancer
Typical epileptic seizure features Refer Epilepsy
Giant cell arteritis symptoms Urgent GCA
Key Positive Signs (Rule-In FND)
Hoover's
Hip extension weakness improves with contralateral hip flexion.
Entrainment
Tremor changes rhythm to copy a movement in the other limb.
Give-Way
Sudden loss of resistance (not pain related).
Variable
Symptoms change with attention or task.
Examination Checklist
  • Motor: Discordance between voluntary and automatic movement.
  • Gait: Monoplegic leg dragging (like wood), sudden knee buckling, slowness/caution.
  • Sensory: Variable boundaries, not following anatomical distribution.
  • Visual: Excessive blinking, effortful facial expression.
  • Seizures (Video if possible): Pelvic thrusting, side-to-side head, arching back.
  • Seizures (Consciousness): Preserved awareness (eyes responsive), recall of event.
  • Recovery: Rapid recovery without post-ictal confusion.
  • Tremor: Distractibility/Entrainment.
Primary Care Action Plan
1
Suspect FND? Identify positive signs on exam. Do NOT investigate for all organic causes if clear positive signs are present.
2
Explain to Patient: Use honest, positive language. "A problem with how the nervous system functions, not structure." Avoid terms like "pseudo" or "all in your head."
3
Refer to Neurology: For confirmation of diagnosis and initial management.
4
Multidisciplinary Team (MDT): Arrange referral to Physiotherapy, Occupational Therapy, Speech & Language Therapy, and/or Mental Health services based on clinical need.
Avoid / Caution
  • Avoid repeated/invasive investigations if positive signs are clear (can reinforce illness beliefs).
  • Avoid diagnosing by exclusion alone.
  • Do not dismiss symptoms as "fake" or malingering.
  • Avoid excessive medication reviews for non-contributory drugs without specialist advice.
Support & Prognosis
  • Prognosis: Variable. Can be relapsing-remitting. Early diagnosis improves outcome.
  • Factors associated with poor outcome: Long duration of symptoms, severe comorbid depression/chronic pain, fixed dystonia, lack of social support.
  • Signpost: Provide info on neuro-rehab and self-management strategies.
Definition

Functional Neurological Disorder (FND) is a condition in which a person experiences neurological symptoms caused by a disorder of neurological system functioning, rather than a structural disease.

  • Considered a "brain network disorder."
  • Can affect motor, sensory, balance, and speech systems.
  • Diagnosis is clinical (positive signs).
Prevalence
6%
Of new neurology outpatients
2nd
Most common reason for referral (after headache)

More common in women (approx 3:1). Peak incidence 35-50 years.

Risk Factors & Associations

Note: These are not necessary for diagnosis. Many patients have no identifiable trigger.

Comorbidities:
  • Anxiety / Depression
  • Chronic Pain / Fibromyalgia
  • Migraine / IBS
  • Fatigue
History:
  • Physical trauma (accident/head injury)
  • Neurodivergence (ADHD/Autism)
  • Pre-existing neurological condition
Psychosocial:
  • Childhood/adult trauma
  • Stressful life events (not always present)
