Headache Assessment Algorithm

Evidence-based clinical decision support tool for primary care | NICE CKS March 2022

Assessment

Comprehensive evaluation of headache presentation

History Taking

Onset & Pattern: Sudden vs gradual, duration, frequency, temporal pattern
Pain Characteristics: Location, severity (0-10), quality (throbbing, pressure, stabbing)
Associated Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, photophobia, phonophobia, autonomic features
Triggering Factors: Valsalva maneuvers, stress, menstrual cycle, medications
Impact: Effect on daily activities, need to rest vs agitation
Medication History: Acute treatments used, frequency, response, over-the-counter medications
Medical History: Comorbidities, immunocompromise, malignancy, pregnancy
Red Flag Screening: Ask about thunderclap onset, neurological symptoms, fever, visual changes

Physical Examination

Vital Signs

BP, pulse, temperature, O₂ saturation

Fundoscopy

Papilledema, pupillary asymmetry

Neurological

Cranial nerves, limb strength, gait

Head & Neck

Temporal arteries, sinuses, TMJ, meningeal signs

Investigations

Investigations are NOT routinely required for primary headache disorders. Order based on clinical suspicion.

When to Order Tests

  • Any red flag present → Urgent referral (may include baseline bloods)
  • Age >50 with new headache: ESR/CRP for giant cell arteritis
  • Pregnancy: Blood pressure, urinalysis for pre-eclampsia
  • Household/cluster symptoms: Carbon monoxide level

Diagnosis

Classify headache using ICHD-3 criteria

Red Flags - Urgent Referral Required

Immediate emergency referral if any of these are present

URGENT Emergency Red Flags

  • Thunderclap onset (max intensity within 5 minutes)
  • New focal neurological deficit
  • Altered consciousness or confusion
  • Fever with neck stiffness or rash
  • Papilledema on fundoscopy
  • Recent head trauma (within 3 months)
  • Pregnancy with hypertension/visual changes

Orange Flags - Specialist Referral

Urgent but non-emergency specialist evaluation needed

CAUTION Orange Flags

  • New-onset headache age >50 years
  • Progressive worsening headache
  • Headache changing in character dramatically
  • Atypical aura (>60 min or with motor weakness)
  • First aura while using combined oral contraceptive
  • History of cancer or immunosuppression
  • Headache triggered by Valsalva/exertion

Primary Headache Disorders

Migraine Without Aura

STABLE Diagnostic Criteria (A-E)

  • A: ≥5 attacks
  • B: 4-72 hours duration (untreated)
  • C: ≥2 of: unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, aggravated by activity
  • D: ≥1 of: nausea/vomiting, photophobia AND phonophobia
  • E: Not better explained by another diagnosis

Migraine With Aura

STABLE Diagnostic Criteria

  • A: ≥2 attacks
  • B: ≥1 reversible aura symptom (visual, sensory, speech)
  • C: ≥3 of: gradual spread ≥5 min, each lasts 5-60 min, ≥1 unilateral, accompanied by headache within 60 min
  • D: Not better explained by another diagnosis

Tension-Type Headache

STABLE Diagnostic Criteria

  • A: 30 min to 7 days duration
  • B: ≥2 of: bilateral, pressing/tightening, mild-moderate, not aggravated by activity
  • C: No nausea/vomiting; ≤1 of photophobia or phonophobia
  • D: Not better explained by another diagnosis

Cluster Headache

STABLE Diagnostic Criteria

  • A: ≥5 attacks of severe unilateral orbital/supraorbital/temporal pain
  • B: 15-180 min duration (untreated)
  • C: Restlessness/agitation AND ≥1 ipsilateral autonomic feature
  • D: 1 every other day to 8 per day for >50% of active period
  • E: Not better explained by another diagnosis

Medication Overuse Headache

CAUTION Diagnostic Criteria

  • A: ≥15 days/month for >3 months
  • B: Regular overuse of headache medication >3 months:
    • Triptans/combination analgesics/opioids: ≥10 days/month
    • Simple analgesics: ≥15 days/month
  • C: Not better explained by another diagnosis

Treatment

Management based on headache type and severity

Emergency Management

Red Flag Present → Immediate Emergency Referral
Do NOT treat in primary care. Arrange urgent transfer to ED.

Migraine Acute Treatment

Step 1: Simple Analgesia

Paracetamol 1g or
NSAID (e.g., ibuprofen 400-600mg)

Step 2: Triptans

Sumatriptan 50-100mg at onset
May repeat after 2 hours

Step 3: Combination

Triptan + NSAID/paracetamol
More effective than monotherapy

Antiemetic

Metoclopramide or domperidone
For nausea/vomiting

Limit acute treatments to ≤10 days/month to avoid medication overuse headache

Tension-Type Headache Treatment

Simple Analgesia

Paracetamol or NSAID
Single dose often sufficient

Non-Pharmacological

Stress management, posture correction
Physical therapy, relaxation techniques

Cluster Headache Treatment

Acute Attack: High-flow oxygen (100%, 12-15 L/min) + subcutaneous sumatriptan 6mg

Preventive Treatment (Specialist Initiated)

  • Verapamil (first-line, require ECG monitoring)
  • Lithium carbonate (requires monitoring)
  • Topiramate or valproate as alternatives
  • Greater occipital nerve block (specialist procedure)

Medication Overuse Headache Management

Medication Withdrawal Required - Abrupt or tapered based on type

Withdrawal Strategy

  • Abrupt withdrawal: Simple analgesics, triptans
  • Tapered withdrawal: Opioids, benzodiazepines, barbiturates
  • Bridge therapy: NSAID or steroid bridge for withdrawal symptoms
  • Preventive: Start preventive medication during withdrawal
  • Follow-up: Review after 2-4 weeks, full recovery may take 2-6 months

Preventive Treatment Indications

Frequency

≥4 migraine days/month
or significant disability

Acute Treatment Failure

Ineffective, contraindicated
or causing adverse effects

Risk Factors

Hemiplegic migraine
Migraine with brainstem aura

First-Line Preventive Options

  • Propranolol 40-160mg daily
  • Topiramate 25-100mg daily
  • Amitriptyline 10-75mg at night
  • Candesartan 8-16mg daily

Follow-up

Monitoring and ongoing management strategies

Headache Diary

Essential for all patients with recurrent headaches
Minimum 8 weeks to establish patterns

Diary Should Include:

  • Date, time of onset, duration
  • Location and quality of pain
  • Severity (0-10 scale)
  • Associated symptoms
  • Possible triggers (stress, foods, menses, weather)
  • Medications taken and response
  • Impact on activities/function

Quality of Life Assessment

HIT-6 Questionnaire

Headache Impact Test
6-item screening tool

HRQOL

Health-related quality of life
measures

Disability Assessment

Days missed from work/school
Reduced productivity

Review Timeline

Recommended Review Schedule

  • After acute treatment trial: 2-4 weeks
  • After starting preventive: 4-6 weeks, then 3-6 monthly
  • During medication withdrawal: 2-4 weeks
  • Stable patients: Annually or as needed
  • Return promptly if: New red flags develop or pattern changes

When to Re-refer or Escalate

Consider specialist referral if:

Referral Indicated

  • Diagnosis remains uncertain after assessment
  • Atypical features or red flags emerge
  • Acute treatments ineffective or contraindicated
  • Preventive treatments have failed or not tolerated
  • Cluster headache (always refer to neurology)
  • Complicated migraine (hemiplegic, brainstem aura)
  • Suspected medication overuse headache requiring support
  • Significant disability despite optimal management
