RBD + Parkinsonism Signs = Screen for Synucleinopathy

REM Sleep Behaviour Disorder often precedes Parkinson's disease by years. Examine for rigidity, bradykinesia, tremor.

Red Flags & When to Refer
  • Violent behaviour with injury risk to patient/partner
  • New-onset RBD age >50 — investigate for neurodegeneration
  • Seizure-like features: incontinence, tongue biting, tonic-clonic
  • Atypical presentation — order PSG with extended EEG
  • Parkinsonian signs: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, shuffling gait
Reassurance Appropriate When
  • Childhood history with resolution in teens (NREM)
  • Isolated sleep paralysis — benign, common (up to 40%)
  • Exploding head syndrome — no treatment usually needed
  • Infrequent, non-injurious episodes with clear triggers
  • Sleep-related hallucinations — reassurance often sufficient

Confusional Arousals
Prevalence: 2.9-4.2% adults
Features: Confusion on arousal, mental slowing, disorientation, speech disturbance
PSG: Not required for diagnosis
Sleepwalking
Prevalence: 1-4% adults (higher in children)
Risk: HLA-DQB1 gene association; family history common
Presentation: Blank stare, aimless ambulation, unresponsive
Sleep Terrors
Prevalence: 2.3-2.6% adults
Features: Panicky scream, tachycardia, tachypnoea, mydriasis, sweating
Key: Autonomic hyperactivity, inconsolable during episode
Sleep-Related Eating Disorder
Association: History of eating disorders, sleepwalking
1st line Rx: Topiramate 25-150mg at bedtime
NREM Parasomnia Triggers to Address
  • Sleep deprivation
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea
  • Zolpidem use
  • Periodic limb movement disorder
  • Stress / forced awakenings
  • Environmental noise
Isolated RBD often precedes synucleinopathies (Parkinson's, Lewy Body Dementia)

Counsel patients about this association. Regular neurological follow-up recommended.

REM Sleep Behaviour Disorder (RBD)
Demographics: Older men (>60), 0.68% prevalence
Behaviour: Violent dream enactment — punching, kicking, shouting, jumping
PSG: Required — shows REM without atonia
Drug triggers: SSRIs, venlafaxine, mirtazapine, TCAs
RBD Immediate Safety Actions
1
Remove weapons and sharp objects from bedroom
2
Pad furniture, move bed away from windows
3
Consider separate sleeping or pillow barrier
4
Bed alarm if getting out of bed
5
Start melatonin or clonazepam
Recurrent Isolated Sleep Paralysis
Prevalence: 2.3-40% (varies by culture)
Management: Reassurance, avoid supine position, address sleep deprivation
Rule out: Narcolepsy (cataplexy, EDS, hypnagogic hallucinations)
Nightmare Disorder
Demographics: 5-8% adults, more common in women
Strong association: PTSD, anxiety, depression, bipolar
Treatment: Image rehearsal therapy, prazosin for PTSD-related
Treatment Algorithm Summary
NREM Parasomnias (Arousals/Sleepwalking/Terrors)
1
Safety modifications + correct triggers (sleep deprivation, OSA, stress)
2
Psychotherapy — CBT, hypnosis, relaxation
3
Benzodiazepine if refractory (clonazepam 0.5-2mg)
4
SSRI/TCA if benzos ineffective (sertraline, clomipramine)
REM Sleep Behaviour Disorder
1
Sleep environment safety — CRITICAL first step
2
Melatonin (favourable profile, esp. if fall risk/apnoea)
3
Clonazepam 0.5-2mg (highly effective)
4
Pramipexole or rivastigmine if refractory

Melatonin3–12 mg PO at bedtime

Pramipexole0.125–0.5 mg PO at bedtime
Imipramine (enuresis)10–50 mg PO at bedtime
Benzodiazepine Cautions
  • Daytime drowsiness
  • Falls risk in elderly
  • Avoid with alcohol/CNS depressants
  • Withdrawal = symptom recurrence
  • Use caution with comorbid OSA
Other Parasomnias
  • Exploding head syndrome: Reassurance, treat underlying sleep disorder
  • Sleep hallucinations: Reassurance, reduce triggers
  • Enuresis: Behavioural therapy → alarm → desmopressin/anticholinergics
Key Differential Diagnoses
ConditionDistinguishing FeaturesInvestigation
Sleep-Related Hypermotor Epilepsy Incontinence Tongue biting Tonic-clonic, drooling; can occur awake EEG — epileptiform activity
Narcolepsy Cataplexy, excessive daytime sleepiness, hypnagogic hallucinations MSLT — short sleep latency, SOREMPs
Nocturnal Dissociative Disorder Underlying psychopathology, bizarre behaviour (pelvic thrusting), self-harm marks PSG — behaviour emerges from wakefulness
Anti-IgLON5 Disease Gait disturbance, dysarthria, dysphagia, sleep apnoea + multiple parasomnias Anti-IgLON5 antibodies + HLA typing
When to Order PSG
PSG Required
  • Suspected RBD — essential for diagnosis
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Suspected comorbid OSA or PLMD
  • Atypical features suggesting seizures
  • Leg kicking reported by bed partner
PSG Not Required
  • Typical confusional arousals
  • Classic sleepwalking with clear history
  • Sleep terrors with typical presentation
  • Nightmare disorder
  • Exploding head syndrome
  • Sleep-related hallucinations
