Vertigo: Clinical Companion

Based on NICE CKS (Dec 2022)
For Healthcare Professionals
Vertigo: A false sensation of movement (spinning/rotation).
Not light-headedness, presyncope, or general disequilibrium.

Key History Questions

  • Timing: Duration? Onset? Frequency?
  • Triggers: Head movement? Positional? Spontaneous?
  • Associated Symptoms: Nausea/vomiting, hearing loss, tinnitus, fullness in ear.
  • Neurology: Headache, diplopia, dysarthria, weakness, visual disturbance.
  • Risk Factors: Recent URI, migraine history, head trauma, CV risk factors (stroke risk).

General Examination

  • Otoscopy: Look for discharge, vesicles (Ramsay Hunt), cholesteatoma.
  • Neurological: Cranial nerves, cerebellar function (heel-toe walking), limbs.
  • Eyes: Nystagmus (direction, suppression by fixation?), Fundoscopy.
  • Cardiovascular: BP, HR, Carotids.
  • Gait: Can they walk unaided? (Severe ataxia = Red Flag).

Specific Clinical Tests (HINTS Exam)

Head Impulse Test (VOR)

Indication: Detect unilateral vestibular hypofunction.

Procedure:

  1. Patient sits upright, fixes gaze on examiner's nose.
  2. Examiner rapidly turns head 10–20° to one side (unpredictably).
  3. Observe eyes: In normal vestibular function, eyes stay fixed on target.

Positive (Peripheral): Corrective saccade (eyes dragged off target then snap back).

Negative (Central): Eyes stay fixed (Normal VOR) despite vertigo symptoms.

Dix-Hallpike Manoeuvre (BPPV)

Indication: Positional vertigo.

Contraindication: Neck/back pathology, carotid sinus sensitivity.

Procedure:

  1. Head turned 45° to one side.
  2. Rapidly lie back (within 2 secs), head extended 20–30° over edge.
  3. Hold for 30+ secs. Observe eyes.

Positive for BPPV (Posterior canal): Torsional (rotatory) upbeating nystagmus after latency (5-20s). Fatigues.

Alternate Cover Test (Skew Deviation)

Indication: Check for brainstem involvement (Stroke).

Procedure:

  1. Patient looks at examiner's nose.
  2. Alternately cover right and left eye several times.
  3. Watch for vertical correction when eye is uncovered.

Abnormal (Central): Vertical correction (one eye up, one down). Highly suspicious for stroke.

Normal: No vertical correction.

Peripheral vs. Central Vertigo

Use clinical features to rapidly triage. Central causes require urgent imaging/admission.

Feature Peripheral (Common) Central (Stroke/Tumour)
Postural Stability Can walk (though unstable). Unable to stand/walk even with eyes open (Severe Ataxia).
Hearing/Tinnitus Common (Meniere's, Labrynthitis). Uncommon (but possible in stroke/tumour).
Other Neuro Signs No (Diplopia, dysarthria, weakness absent). Usually Present (Cranial nerve palsies, visual fields, limb weakness, sensory loss).
Nystagmus Horizontal or torsional. Unidirectional. Suppressed by fixation. Fatigues. Direction-changing (gaze-evoked), Purely Vertical, or Torsional. Not suppressed by fixation.
Head Impulse Test Positive (corrective saccade present). Negative (Normal VOR) despite vertigo.
Duration BPPV: Seconds. Meniere's: Hours. Neuronitis: Days. Constant, prolonged, progressive.

Common Peripheral Causes

  • BPPV: Short (<1 min), positional episodes. Triggered by turning in bed.
  • Vestibular Neuronitis: Acute, constant, severe vertigo for days. No hearing loss.
  • Meniere's Disease: Episodic (30 mins - hours), fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, ear fullness.
  • Labyrinthitis: Similar to neuronitis but WITH hearing loss/tinnitus.

Central Causes (High Risk)

  • Posterior Circulation Stroke: Brainstem or Cerebellum.
  • TIA: Transient ischemic attack.
  • Migraine: Vestibular migraine (common recurrent cause).
  • MS / Tumour: Acoustic neuroma, Cerebellar tumour.

Suspected Central Vertigo (Red Flags)

Immediate Action Required: If Central Vertigo is suspected, Admit or Urgently Refer.

Red Flags for Urgent Brain Imaging/Admission:

  • Isolated, persistent (>24 hours) vertigo of hyperacute onset.
  • Normal Head Impulse Test (implies central lesion if vertigo is severe).
  • New onset headache.
  • New onset unilateral deafness.
  • Cranial nerve weakness, limb weakness, or sensory loss.
  • Severe ataxia (inability to stand).
Admit to Hospital
Urgent Refer: Neurologist / Audiovestibular Physician

Peripheral Vertigo (Confirmed or Suspected)

BPPV

Posterior canal BPPV confirmed via Dix-Hallpike.

  • Treat with Epley Manoeuvre (Canalith Repositioning).
  • Refer to ENT/Audiovestibular if uncertain or refractory.

Vestibular Neuronitis / Labyrinthitis

Severe, prolonged vertigo.

  • Symptomatic drug treatment (e.g., Prochlorperazine, Cinnarizine) if severe.
  • Advice to rest initially, then encourage gradual movement.
  • Refer if persistent >3-4 weeks or hearing loss (Labyrinthitis).

Meniere's Disease

Triad of Vertigo, Tinnitus, Hearing Loss, Fullness.

  • Treat acute attacks symptomatically.
  • Refer to ENT/Audiovestibular for long-term management.
General Advice:
  • Advise caution regarding driving (inform DVLA if condition affects safety to drive).
  • Safety measures at home to prevent falls.
