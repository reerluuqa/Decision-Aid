Migraine Quick Reference

Clinical decision support tool for migraine diagnosis and management

Emergency Red Flags (SNOOP4)

Systemic symptoms: fever, weight loss
Neurological symptoms: confusion, impaired alertness
Onset: sudden, abrupt, thunderclap
Older: new-onset in patients >50 years
Pattern change: increased frequency or change in character
Papilloedema, Positional, Precipitated by Valsalva

Quick Diagnosis

Criterion Details
Attacks At least 5 attacks fulfilling criteria B-D
Duration 4-72 hours (untreated or unsuccessfully treated)
Characteristics At least 2 of: unilateral location, pulsating quality, moderate/severe intensity, aggravation by physical activity
Associated symptoms At least 1 of: nausea and/or vomiting, photophobia and phonophobia
Quick Fact

Aura occurs in only 15-30% of patients with migraine. Typical aura includes visual, sensory, or speech symptoms that develop gradually over 5-20 minutes and last less than 60 minutes.

Criterion Details
Frequency Headache on ≥15 days/month for >3 months
History Occurring in a patient with at least 5 attacks fulfilling criteria for migraine without aura or with aura
Migraine Features On ≥8 days/month for >3 months, fulfilling criteria for migraine or believed by patient to be migraine at onset and relieved by triptan/ergot
ID Migraine Screen

Answer "yes" to at least 2 of these 3 questions indicates migraine (93% sensitivity):

  1. Has a headache limited your activities for a day or more in the last 3 months?
  2. Do you feel nauseous or are you sick when you have a headache?
  3. Does light bother you when you have a headache?

Treatment Algorithm

Mild Symptoms
NSAIDs
First-line

Aspirin, ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen

Paracetamol
Alternative

Less effective than NSAIDs, but safer in pregnancy

Aspirin/Paracetamol/Caffeine
Combination

More effective than components alone

Moderate to Severe Symptoms
Triptans
First-line

5HT1 receptor agonists. Contraindicated in coronary artery disease.

Lasmiditan
Second-line

Safe for patients with cardiovascular disease

CGRP Antagonists (Gepants)
Second-line

Safe for patients with cardiovascular disease

Treatment Tips
  • Treat early in the attack for better response
  • Consider anti-emetics for nausea/vomiting
  • Maintain hydration with oral or IV fluids
  • If triptan alone is insufficient, add NSAID or paracetamol

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Tension-type Headache Bilateral pressure-like pain, not aggravated by physical activity, rarely has photophobia or phonophobia
Cluster Headache Severe unilateral pain around one eye, lasts up to 3 hours, causes restlessness and agitation, with autonomic signs
Medication Overuse Headache Occurs on ≥15 days/month in a patient with pre-existing headache disorder, characterized by regular medication overuse for ≥3 months
Subarachnoid Hemorrhage Sudden severe "thunderclap" headache, may have sentinel headache, physical exam may be normal initially

Special Considerations

Pregnancy
  • Paracetamol is preferred analgesic
  • NSAIDs should be avoided, especially in third trimester
  • Triptans generally avoided, but may be considered if benefits outweigh risks
Older Adults
  • Consider comorbid conditions and drug interactions
  • Be cautious with triptans if cardiovascular risk factors present
  • Consider CGRP antagonists which are safer in cardiovascular disease
