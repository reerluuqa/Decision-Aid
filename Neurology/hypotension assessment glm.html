Hypotension Assessment

Last updated: Oct 07, 2025

Emergency Conditions

Septic Shock

Sepsis with hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg

Cardiogenic Shock

Cardiac pump dysfunction with hypotension and signs of hypoperfusion

Major Hemorrhage

Acute blood loss with hypotension and signs of shock

Anaphylaxis

Severe allergic reaction with hypotension and airway compromise

Massive PE

Pulmonary embolism with hypoxemia and SBP <90 mmHg

Cardiac Tamponade

Beck's triad: hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds

Urgent Considerations

Initial
Assessment
ABC Assessment - Airway, Breathing, Circulation first priority in all critically ill patients
Supplemental Oxygen - Start with reservoir mask at 15 L/min for critically ill patients
ECG Monitoring - All patients require ECG, continuous monitoring in severe cases
IV Access - Secure reliable IV access for fluids and medications
Sign Interpretation
Tachycardia Early compensatory response to hypovolemia
Altered cognition Cerebral hypoperfusion, possible shock
Oliguria Renal hypoperfusion, inadequate cardiac output
Diaphoresis Sympathetic response, possible shock

Assessment Approach

Proper Measurement

Use appropriate cuff size. Under-cuffing may overestimate BP and mask hypotension.

Orthostatic Testing

Rest supine for 5 minutes. SBP drop ≥20 mmHg or DBP drop ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes is significant.

Arrhythmia Check

Palpate pulse before using automatic sphygmomanometer. Most overestimate BP in atrial fibrillation.

Key Point: Hypotension is any BP below normal expected for an individual in a given environment. No single numerical cut-off is universally accepted.

Types of Hypotension

Hypovolaemic

Decreased effective circulating volume (30.8% of shock cases)

  • Haemorrhage (overt/concealed)
  • GI losses (diarrhea, vomiting)
  • Renal causes (diuretics, dialysis)
  • Skin losses (burns, sweating)

Cardiogenic

Heart pump dysfunction (14% of shock cases)

  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Acute heart failure
  • Cardiac valvular lesions
  • Cardiac dysrhythmia

Obstructive

Obstruction to cardiac filling or flow (1% of shock cases)

  • Cardiac tamponade
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Pulmonary embolism

Distributive

Impaired peripheral vasoconstriction (50.6% of shock cases)

  • Sepsis (27.2%)
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Neurogenic causes
  • Medication effects

Fluid Management

Crystalloid Bolus - Initial fluid of choice for most hypotensive patients
Fluid Responsiveness Test - Passive leg raising or 300-500mL crystalloid bolus
Blood Products - For major hemorrhage, transfuse 2-4 units immediately
Antifibrinolytics - Tranexamic acid in acute severe hemorrhage
Caution: Aggressive fluid support should be avoided in pulmonary embolism as it may worsen right ventricle overload.

Medication Management

Antibiotics - Broad-spectrum within 1 hour for suspected sepsis
Vasopressors - Noradrenaline to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg in septic shock
Adrenaline - Intramuscular for anaphylaxis, repeat if needed
Hydrocortisone - For suspected adrenal crisis, even before lab confirmation
Remember: Stop antihypertensive medications in patients with hypotension, especially if associated with acute coronary syndrome.
