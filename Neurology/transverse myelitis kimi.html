Assessment
Key Clinical Features Acute Presentation
Motor Weakness Progressive paraparesis/quadriparesis following pyramidal pattern, with upper motor neuron signs
Sensory Impairment Ascending paresthesias with sensory level below lesion, loss of vibration/proprioception
Autonomic Dysfunction Urinary/bowel incontinence or retention (>60% of patients)
Pain Mid-line back pain at lesion level, radicular or segmental pain
History & Risk Factors Recent Events
Preceding Infection Systemic viral illness within 1-4 weeks prior to onset (>50% of patients)
Recent Vaccination Post-immunization myelitis reported with almost all vaccines
Demographics Bimodal age distribution (10-19 years, 30-39 years), female predominance
Critical First Step URGENT
Exclude Compressive Myelopathy MRI of entire spinal cord required to rule out compression, neoplasm, or abscess
Clinical Syndromes

Partial Cord Syndrome

  • Asymmetric lesions (1-2 segments)
  • Brown-Séquard pattern suggests MS
  • Mild to moderate severity

Complete Cord Syndrome

  • Longitudinally extensive (≥3 segments)
  • Bilateral, symmetric symptoms
  • Moderate to severe deficits
Diagnosis
Diagnostic Workup Essential Tests
1. MRI Spinal Cord with Gadolinium

First-line test to confirm lesion, exclude compression, and characterize pattern

2. MRI Brain

Detects disseminated lesions suggestive of MS or NMOSD

3. Serum Antibody Testing

AQP4-IgG (for NMOSD) and MOG-IgG (for MOGAD)

4. CSF Analysis

Pleocytosis, elevated protein, oligoclonal bands for inflammatory myelitis

MRI Interpretation Pattern Recognition

MS Pattern

  • Short-segment lesions (1-2 segments)
  • Peripheral location (<50% cord area)
  • Brain lesions typical of demyelination

NMOSD Pattern

  • Longitudinally extensive (≥3 segments)
  • Central location (>50% cord area)
  • Hiccups/nausea (area postrema)

MOGAD Pattern

  • Longitudinal lesion with "H-sign"
  • Conus involvement common
  • Optic neuritis often simultaneous

Infectious Pattern

  • Neutrophilic pleocytosis in CSF
  • Systemic infection signs
  • Specific PCR positive
Diagnostic Criteria
Transverse Myelitis Consortium Working Group (2002)
  • Inclusion: Bilateral sensory/motor/autonomic dysfunction, clear sensory level, progression 4hrs-21days
  • Exclusion: Compression, radiation, arterial thrombosis, connective tissue disease, infection
NMOSD Criteria (2015)

Core Clinical Characteristics:

  • Optic neuritis, acute myelitis, area postrema syndrome
  • Acute brainstem syndrome, diencephalic/cerebral syndrome
  • With AQP4-IgG: 1 core characteristic + positive test
  • Without AQP4-IgG: 2+ core characteristics + MRI requirements
MOGAD Criteria (2023)
  • Core demyelinating event + MOG-IgG positive
  • Supporting features: bilateral optic neuritis, longitudinally extensive myelitis, conus lesion
  • Exclusion of alternative diagnoses including MS
Differential Diagnosis Critical Distinctions
Compressive Myelopathy

Neoplasm, abscess, hematoma → Urgent surgical evaluation

Vascular Myelopathy

Anterior/posterior spinal artery occlusion → Sudden onset, no enhancement

Neoplastic Myelopathy

Intramedullary metastases → Persistent enhancement, CSF cytology

Paraneoplastic Myelopathy

Tract-specific changes → Antibody panel, tumor screening

Treatment
Acute Management 1st Line
IV Methylprednisolone 1000 mg IV daily for 3-5 consecutive days

First-line for acute neurological deficits. May follow with oral taper (5-14 days)

Rescue Therapy If No Response
Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis) 7 exchanges every other day

42% show meaningful improvement vs 6% with sham

IV Immunoglobulin (IVIG) Consult specialist for dose

Alternative if plasma exchange unavailable (limited evidence)

Supportive Care Essential

Respiratory Support

  • Monitor in cervical myelitis
  • Maximal respiratory pressures, FVC
  • Critical care involvement if ascending

Bladder Management

  • Catheterization for retention
  • Urology referral for neurogenic bladder
  • Anticholinergics or botulinum toxin

Spasticity Control

  • Stretching exercises
  • Baclofen, tizanidine
  • Botulinum toxin injections

DVT Prophylaxis

  • Subcutaneous heparin/enoxaparin
  • Compression stockings/devices
  • Early mobilization
Secondary Prevention Condition-Specific

Idiopathic TM

Observation - No preventive therapy if single event and no recurrence risk factors

High Risk for MS

Disease-Modifying Therapies for acute partial TM with typical brain MRI lesions

  • Interferon β, glatiramer acetate (for CIS)
  • Follow AAN 2018 guidelines for MS therapy

AQP4-IgG Positive (NMOSD)

Indefinite Immunosuppression - Targeted therapies preferred

Eculizumab 900 mg IV weekly ×4, then 1200 mg IV every 2 weeks

Complement C5 inhibitor - Requires meningococcal vaccination

Ravulizumab Dose by weight every 8 weeks

Longer-acting C5 inhibitor - 98.6% relapse risk reduction

Inebilizumab 300 mg IV day 1, day 15, then every 6 monthsAnti-CD19 B-cell depletion - Premedication required

Satralizumab 120 mg SC loading, then every 4 weeks

IL-6 receptor antagonist - Can be used as monotherapy

Rituximab (Alternative) 1000 mg IV every 2 weeks ×2, then every 6 months

Anti-CD20 - 80% reduction in attack rates

MOG-IgG Positive (MOGAD)

Consider Immunosuppression after relapse or persistent antibodies

Azathioprine 2.5-3 mg/kg/day orally
Mycophenolate Mofetil 1-2 g/day in 2 divided doses
IVIG Maintenance therapy reduces relapse risk
Tocilizumab IL-6 receptor blockade - 80% decrease in relapse rate
Vaccination Considerations Case-by-Case
Vaccine-Associated TM

Avoid new vaccinations during acute disease activity. Specific vaccine should be avoided if previously temporally associated with TM. Balance risks vs benefits.

Follow-up
Monitoring Ongoing Care

Immediate (1 week)

  • Respiratory function monitoring
  • Complications of immobility
  • Early rehabilitation initiation

Short-term (3 months)

  • DVT prophylaxis assessment
  • Bladder function evaluation
  • Spasticity management

Long-term (6-12 months)

  • Neurological recovery plateau
  • Symptom re-evaluation
  • Pain, depression screening

High-Risk Patients

  • Every 3-6 months if on immunotherapy
  • Monitor drug adverse effects
  • Relapse surveillance
Complications & Prognosis Important Outcomes

Multiple Sclerosis

High Risk: >80% develop MS within 10 years if brain MRI shows demyelinating lesions

Long-term Risk

Motor Deficits

Medium Risk: Residual weakness, spasticity requiring ongoing therapy

Variable Recovery

Bladder Dysfunction

Medium Risk: Neurogenic bladder, incontinence, retention

Manageable

Neuropathic Pain

Medium Risk: Chronic pain syndromes requiring multimodal therapy

Treatable
Recovery Patterns

Acute Partial TM

  • Good prognosis - most recover completely
  • Recovery over weeks to months
  • Majority of improvement in first 3 months
  • Slower improvement may continue up to 1 year

Complete/Longitudinally Extensive TM

  • Variable prognosis
  • ~33% complete/near-complete recovery
  • ~33% moderate residual deficits
  • ~33% severe disability
  • Recovery may continue up to 2 years
Patient Education Essential
Advise patients to contact neurologist immediately if experiencing:
  • New motor weakness
  • Pain or sensory disturbances
  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction
  • Changes in ambulatory ability
