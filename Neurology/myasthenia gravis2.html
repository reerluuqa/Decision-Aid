Clinical Assessment

Initial evaluation and clinical suspicion

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Muscle weakness with fatigability Core Feature
  • Ptosis and/or diplopia (ocular involvement) Common Presentation
  • Dysphagia or dysarthria (bulbar symptoms) Requires Evaluation
  • Proximal limb weakness Common in Generalized MG
  • Respiratory muscle weakness Myasthenic Crisis Risk
  • Diurnal variation (worse later in day) Supporting Feature

Clinical Assessment Checklist

Risk Factors & Red Flags

  • Age: Female <40 years, Male >60 years Typical Age Groups
  • Family history of autoimmune disorders Genetic Predisposition
  • Cancer immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) Drug-Induced MG
  • Thymoma or thymic hyperplasia on imaging Thymic Pathology

Diagnostic Investigations

Confirmatory tests and antibody analysis

Serological Testing

AChR Antibodies

Standard binding & modulating assay

80-90% positive in generalized MG

50% positive in ocular MG

99% specificity

MuSK Antibodies

Test if AChR negative

Up to 70% positive in seronegative generalized MG

Common in black women (US)

LRP4/Agrin Antibodies

Specialized testing

Mild to moderate phenotype

Research setting

Striational Antibodies

Titin & ryanodine

75-95% in thymoma-associated MG

Rare in non-thymoma cases

Electrophysiological Testing

  • Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS) First-line

    Sensitivity: 79% generalized, 50% ocular
    Specificity: 97%
    Positive: >10% decrement

  • Single-Fiber EMG (SFEMG) If RNS negative

    Sensitivity: 86-98%
    Specificity: 70-96%
    Shows increased jitter or blocking

Chronic Treatment Options

Symptomatic Treatment

Pyridostigmine (Cholinesterase Inhibitor)

First-line: Most patients with ocular or generalized MG

Dosing: Individualized (typically 60mg q4-6h)

Note: Limited efficacy in MuSK-MG

Side effects: Muscarinic symptoms (diarrhea, sweating)

Immunosuppressive Therapy

Corticosteroids

First-line immunotherapy

Low dose initiation, gradual titration

Avoid high starting doses

Azathioprine

First-line steroid-sparing

Check TPMT status

Monitor blood counts

Rituximab

MuSK-MG first-line

Refractory AChR-MG

B-cell depletion

Eculizumab

Complement C5 inhibitor

Follow-up & Monitoring

Long-term management and prognosis

Monitoring Schedule

Visit Frequency

Stable disease: Every 3-6 months

Active disease: Every 1-3 months

Crisis recovery: Weekly until stable

Assessment Items

• Muscle strength & fatigability

• Bulbar & respiratory function

• Functional status (MG-ADL)

• Treatment side effects

Laboratory Monitoring

• Liver function (steroids, azathioprine)

• Complete blood count

• Thiopurine methyltransferase

• Drug levels (tacrolimus, ciclosporin)

Outcome Measures

• MG-ADL score

• QMG score

• MG Composite

• Quality of life scales

Complications to Monitor

  • Myasthenic Crisis Emergency

    Respiratory failure requiring ventilation
    Monitor FVC <20 mL/kg or NIF <30 cm H2O

  • Treatment Side Effects Common

    Steroid-induced diabetes, osteoporosis
    Immunosuppression-related infections

  • Thymoma Recurrence Post-thymectomy

    Annual CT chest surveillance
    Titin/ryanodine antibodies monitoring

  • Pyridostigmine Overdose Cholinergic Crisis

    Increased secretions, bradycardia, diarrhea
    Differentiate from myasthenic crisis

Prognosis & Outcomes

Good Prognosis

• Early diagnosis & treatment

• AChR-positive MG

• Response to thymectomy

• Younger age at onset

Guarded Prognosis

• MuSK-MG (severe, frequent crises)

• Late-onset MG (>60 years)

• Thymoma-associated MG

• Refractory to treatment

Mortality Factors

• Myasthenic crisis

• Severe infection

• Cardiac complications

• Treatment complications

Quality of Life

• Most achieve good control

• Normal lifespan expected

• Functional limitations possible

• Psychosocial support needed

Patient Education Points

Patient Counseling Checklist

Special Populations

Pregnancy

• Plan pregnancy when stable

• Pyridostigmine: first-line

• Prednisolone: preferred immunosuppressant

• Avoid mycophenolate & methotrexate

• Monitor neonate for transient MG

Pediatric MG

• Pre-pubertal onset: different phenotype

• AChR antibodies: cell-based assays

• Rituximab: MuSK-MG effective

• Thymectomy: consider early

Elderly MG

• Late-onset: >60 years

• Higher risk of complications

• Striational antibodies common

• Comorbidities affect treatment

Familial MG

• Rare (3.5% of cases)

• Genetic counseling

• HLA associations

• Similar management
