Critical Warning: Rapidly Progressive Conditions

The following conditions require prompt recognition, diagnosis, and management due to risk of serious neurological and systemic complications:

  • Vasculitis: Sudden onset, severe neuropathic pain, rapid progression to mononeuropathy multiplex
  • Neoplastic compression: Consider in patients with known malignancy or unexplained rapid onset
  • Infections (HIV, CMV, Herpes viruses): Immunocompromised patients at highest risk
  • Diabetic amyotrophy: Rapid progression to weakness and muscle atrophy

Urgent Considerations

Requires Prompt Action

Vasculitis

Critical
Onset: Sudden, severe neuropathic pain
Pattern: Mononeuropathy multiplex (stepwise involvement)
  • Fever, malaise, weight loss
  • Vasculitic skin rash (purpura)
  • May progress rapidly to serious complications
  • Often not recognized until second mononeuropathy develops
Red Flags
Requires immediate corticosteroids + immunosuppressant. Can progress rapidly causing serious neurological and systemic complications.
First Test
EMG/NCS: Axon loss pattern, decreased sensory amplitudes

Neoplastic Compression

Critical
Types: Radiculopathy, Plexopathy, Nerve sheath tumors
Onset: Usually metastatic, can occur acutely
  • Consider in any patient with known primary malignancy
  • Lumbosacral plexopathy from intra-abdominal extension
  • Radiation-induced plexopathy (microvascular insufficiency)
  • Lymphoma, amyloidosis, nerve sheath tumors
Red Flags
Consider malignancy in all unexplained neuropathies. Unexplained weight loss + neuropathy = urgent oncological workup.
First Test
MRI of spine/pelvis: Identifies compressive lesions, tumors

Infectious Causes

Urgent
Key viruses: HIV, HSV, VZV, CMV, EBV, Lyme (bacterial)
  • HIV: Often asymptomatic initially; mononeuropathy multiplex or length-dependent polyneuropathy
  • Herpes Zoster: Burning/stabbing pain â†’ vesicular rash in dermatome
  • CMV: Pain/weakness in immunocompromised (AIDS patients)
  • Lyme disease: Mononeuropathy multiplex pattern (Borrelia infection)
  • Leprosy: Consider in patients from endemic areas
Red Flags
Immunocompromised patients at highest risk. High-risk sexual history or IV drug use = test for HIV.
First Test
Serology/PCR: HIV ELISA, VZV PCR, Lyme serology based on exposure history

Diabetic Amyotrophy

Urgent
Affects: ~1% of diabetic patients (or pre-diabetes)
Onset: Acute/subacute unilateral pelvic/thigh pain
  • Severe "boring" pelvic/thigh pain
  • Pain may resolve in 2-3 weeks before weakness develops
  • Progressive muscle atrophy (quadriceps)
  • Hip flexion, adduction, knee extension weakness
  • Loss of patellar reflex on affected side
Red Flags
Rapid progression to weakness/atrophy. Autoimmune microvasculitis component. Requires tight glucose control.
First Test
Fasting plasma glucose: >6.9 mmol/L (125 mg/dL) = diabetes. Confirm with HbA1c.

Common Conditions

Frequently Encountered

Lumbosacral Radiculopathy

Most Common
Presentation: Pain, weakness, numbness in nerve root distribution
  • Pain may originate in low back, radiate distally
  • Weakness/sensory loss usually mild
  • Reduced deep tendon reflexes
  • Straight leg raise may elicit symptoms
  • Common causes: disc herniation, osteophytes, tumor
First Test
EMG: Denervation in affected myotome. Best done 2-3 weeks after symptom onset.
Other Tests
MRI lumbar spine: Disc herniation, osteophytes, tumor, root avulsion

Fibular Neuropathy

Most Common
Key feature: Foot drop - most common lower extremity mononeuropathy
  • Injury at fibular neck (surgical positioning, crossing legs, trauma)
  • Partial/complete foot drop over days/weeks
  • Weakness of foot eversion, toe/ankle dorsiflexion
  • Sensory disturbance over lateral calf and dorsum of foot
  • Normal ankle inversion and toe flexion
First Test
EMG: Neurogenic changes in fibular-innervated muscles, sparing short head of biceps femoris

Meralgia Paresthetica

Common
Nerve: Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (L2/L3)
  • Compression under inguinal ligament at anterior iliac spine
  • Pain, paresthesias, numbness over anterolateral thigh
  • Risk factors: Obesity, tight clothes, heavy tool belts
  • Normal motor exam, no weakness
First Test
NCS: Reduced lateral femoral cutaneous sensory amplitude. EMG typically normal.

Morton's Neuroma

Common
Location: Base of 3rd and 4th toes (intermetatarsal space)
  • Perineural fibrosis of intermetatarsal nerve
  • Foot pain worse with weight-bearing and repetitive use
  • Palpable tenderness over affected area
  • Neurological examination normal (no weakness)
First Test
Clinical diagnosis: Based on history/exam. EMG/NCS normal. MRI if uncertain.

Less Common Conditions

Consider Based on Clinical Context

Sciatic Neuropathy

Uncommon
Origin: L4-S2 nerve roots, lumbosacral plexus
  • Weakness of knee flexion, foot drop
  • Sensory loss over anterior/posterior leg, foot
  • Causes: pelvic/thigh trauma, hip surgery, iatrogenic injury, vasculitis
First Test
EMG: Denervation in hamstrings + distal fibular/tibial muscles

Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome

Uncommon
Compression: Tibial nerve under flexor retinaculum at medial ankle
  • Perimalleolar pain, burning sensation
  • Pain worse with weight-bearing and at night
  • Possible Tinel's sign over ankle
  • True weakness/atrophy uncommon
First Test
EMG: Neurogenic changes in intrinsic foot muscles

Femoral Mononeuropathy

Uncommon
Origin: Lumbar plexus (L2-L4 posterior divisions)
  • Pain/weakness causing knee buckling and falls
  • Numbness over anterior thigh and/or medial calf
  • Causes: pelvic/hip trauma, hip surgery, compression, diabetes
  • Weakness of quadriceps, absent knee jerk
First Test
EMG: Denervation in femoral-innervated muscles

Obturator Neuropathy

Uncommon
Key feature: Thigh adduction weakness
  • Most common in females after obstetric/gynecological procedures
  • Pain, numbness, weakness in obturator distribution
  • Preservation of other L2-L4 muscles (e.g., quadriceps)
  • Numbness over proximal medial thigh
First Test
EMG: Neurogenic changes in thigh adductors

Tibial Neuropathy

Uncommon
Distribution: Distal lower extremity (ankle level)
  • Asymmetrical pain, weakness, numbness
  • Numbness over lateral foot and sole
  • Loss of Achilles reflex
  • Weakness of plantar flexion and inversion
First Test
EMG: Denervation in tibial-innervated muscles, sparing fibular muscles

HNPP

Rare
Type: Dominantly inherited (PMP22 deletion on chromosome 17)
  • Recurrent painless mononeuropathies at compression sites
  • Classic: Fibular neuropathy at fibular head
  • Normal exam between episodes
  • Relapsing and remitting course
First Test
NCS: Decreased conduction velocity/block at entrapment site
Confirmatory
Genetic testing: PMP22 deletion

Diagnostic Approach

Systematic Evaluation

Quick Assessment Checklist

Symptom Character Boring, burning, stabbing, shooting pains
Distribution Dermatomal vs peripheral nerve vs stocking-glove
Duration Constant vs relapsing-remitting
Progression Stable vs worsening over time
Associated Symptoms Weakness, gait changes, constitutional symptoms
Medical History Diabetes, cancer, infections, autoimmune disease
1

Neurological History

Sensory Symptoms
  • Boring, burning, stabbing
  • Pins and needles, prickling
  • Shooting pains (entrapment)
  • Painful paresthesias (vasculitis)
Motor Symptoms
  • Weakness patterns
  • Gait abnormalities
  • Foot drop
  • Muscle atrophy
Localization
  • Dermatome (root)
  • Multiple nerves (plexus)
  • Single nerve (peripheral)
  • Distal symmetric (polyneuropathy)
2

Physical Examination

Sensory Testing
  • Light touch (cotton)
  • Temperature (cold/warm)
  • Pinprick (sharp object)
  • Vibration (128 Hz tuning fork)
  • Joint position sense
Motor Testing
  • Muscle strength (0-5 scale)
  • Atrophy assessment
  • Deep tendon reflexes
  • Gait analysis (foot drop)
General Exam
  • Fever (infection)
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Skin changes/rash
  • Joint deformities
  • Funduscopy (diabetic retinopathy)
3

Initial Testing - Electrodiagnostic Studies

Electromyography (EMG)
  • Denervation/neuropathic changes
  • Localizes affected root(s)/nerve(s)
  • Assesses severity and chronicity
  • Best done 2-3 weeks after onset
Nerve Conduction Studies
  • Motor and sensory amplitudes
  • Conduction velocity
  • Conduction block at entrapment sites
  • Differentiates axonal vs demyelinating
Laboratory Tests
  • FBC, renal/liver function
  • Blood glucose/HbA1c
  • Thyroid function
  • ESR/CRP (inflammatory conditions)
  • Serologies as indicated
