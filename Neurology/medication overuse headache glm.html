Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

Quick Reference Clinical Tool

At a Glance

Chronic secondary headache attributable to overuse of acute medications by a person with a pre-existing primary headache disorder (almost always migraine or tension-type headache). Patients with MOH suffer from a headache on more days than not.

Red Flags

Consider alternative diagnosis if any red flags present
  • Abnormal neurological symptoms: brain tumour, abscess, intracranial hypotension, CVST
  • Headache aggravated by postural changes or Valsalva: intracranial hypertension/hypotension
  • Systemic symptoms: fever (infection), weight loss (malignancy)
  • History of cancer: potential brain metastasis
  • Age >50 years: consider giant cell arteritis
  • History of trauma: persistent headache from TBI
  • Pregnancy/puerperium: pre-eclampsia, cerebral sinus thrombosis

Diagnostic Criteria (ICHD-3)

All three criteria must be met:

  • Headache on ≥15 days/month on background of pre-existing primary headache disorder
  • Regular overuse for >3 months of acute treatments:
    • Simple analgesics on ≥15 days/month
    • Triptan, opioid, or ergot derivative on ≥10 days/month
    • Combination of analgesics on ≥10 days/month
  • No other ICHD-3 diagnosis better accounts for symptoms

Typical patterns:

  • Analgesic overuse: dull, diffuse, holocranial headache without migrainous symptoms
  • Triptan overuse: daily migrainous headache (unilateral pulsating with autonomic disturbances)

Triptans 1.7 years High
Ergot derivatives 2.7 years High
Simple analgesics 4.8 years Lower
NSAIDs Variable Lowest

Management Approach

1. Education & Advice

First step and may be sufficient alone to resolve condition

2. Medication Withdrawal

Abrupt or tapered depending on medication class

3. Preventative Regimen

Targets underlying headache disorder to facilitate withdrawal

Recommended options for underlying migraine:

  • Topiramate
  • Botulinum toxin type A
  • Calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) antagonists

Recommended for underlying tension-type headache:

  • Amitriptyline (most commonly used)

Inpatient treatment necessary for:

  • Patients withdrawing from opioids
  • Patients withdrawing from barbiturates
  • Patients withdrawing from benzodiazepines

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

  • High Migraine as underlying disorder (80% of cases)
  • High Female sex (2.8:1 ratio)
  • High Use of opioid, barbiturate, triptan, or ergot derivative
  • Medium Anxiety and/or depression

Weaker Risk Factors

  • Medium Chronic musculoskeletal disease
  • Low Chronic gastrointestinal disease
  • Low Low education level

Quick Assessment Tool

Diagnosis Checklist

  • Pre-existing primary headache disorder
  • Headache ≥15 days/month
  • Medication overuse ≥10-15 days/month
  • Duration >3 months
  • Normal neurological examination
  • No red flags present

Management Pathway

Uncomplicated MOH

Simple analgesics, triptans, ergot derivatives; no psychiatric comorbidity; no relapse history

Complicated MOH

Opioids, benzodiazepines, barbiturates; psychiatric comorbidity; history of relapse
