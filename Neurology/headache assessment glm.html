Headache Assessment

Target: Adults 16+
Source: NICE CKS (2022)

Immediate Action Required

Refer urgently or admit if ANY of the following are present:

Onset & Pattern

  • Thunderclap Headache: Reaches max intensity within 5 mins (SAH, bleed).
  • New onset > 50 years: Consider Giant Cell Arteritis or lesion.
  • Progressive/Persistent: Worsening over days/weeks.
  • Systemic illness: Fever, weight loss, malignancy history.

Neurological & Eyes

  • Papilloedema: Suggests raised ICP (tumor, thrombosis).
  • Focal Neuro Deficit: Weakness, vision loss, aphasia (Stroke, TIA, mass).
  • Altered Consciousness: Confusion, reduced GCS.
  • Meningism: Neck stiffness + photophobia (Meningitis/SAH).

Context & History

  • Head Trauma: Recent (within 3 months) history (Subdural).
  • Valsalva Trigger: Cough, sneeze, exertion triggers pain (Chiari/lesion).
  • Pregnancy: Consider Pre-eclampsia.
  • Immunocompromised: HIV, steroids (Abscess risk).

Symptom Specifics

  • Positional: Worse lying down (Mass), worse standing (CSF leak).
  • Atypical Aura: >60 mins, motor weakness, or onset on COC.
  • Contact History: Similar symptoms in household (CO poisoning).

Migraine (With or Without Aura)

Primary
Criteria Details
Duration 4 – 72 hours
Location Unilateral
Quality Pulsating / Throbbing
Severity Moderate or Severe
Aggravation Avoidance of routine physical activity
Associated Nausea/Vomiting OR Photophobia/Phonophobia

Tension-Type Headache

Primary
Duration 30 mins – 7 days
Location Bilateral (Band-like)
Quality Pressing / Tightening (Non-pulsating)
Severity Mild / Moderate
Activity Not aggravated by routine activity
Associated NO Nausea/Vomiting. Max ONE of (Photo/Phono).

Cluster Headache

Primary
Duration 15 – 180 mins
Location Strictly Unilateral (Orbital/Supraorbital/Temporal)
Severity Severe / Very Severe
Behaviour Restlessness / Agitation
Autonomic Signs Ipsilateral: Lacrimation, Conjunctival injection, Nasal congestion, Ptosis, Miosis.
Frequency 1 every other day to 8 per day.

History Taking (SOCRATES)

  • Onset: Sudden, gradual, frequency?
  • Character: Pulsating, tight, sharp?
  • Associated Symptoms: Aura (visual/sensory)? Nausea? Autonomic signs?
  • Triggers/Relief: Valsalva, posture, stress, sleep, medication?
  • Impact: Bedbound (Migraine)? Restless (Cluster)? No impact (Tension)?
  • Family/Social: Household symptoms (CO)? Family history?

Examination Steps

Vitals & General:
BP, Pulse, Temp, Sats. Check for rash (meningitis).
Head & Neck:
Palpate temporal arteries (GCA). Sinuses. TMJ. Neck stiffness/meningism.
Fundoscopy:
Check for Papilloedema (Raised ICP).
Neurology:
Cranial nerves, peripheral nerves, gait, limb strength.
Any new focal sign = Refer.

If Normal Exam + Primary Features

No imaging required. No increased risk of secondary precipitant. Manage in primary care.

If diagnosis uncertain: Use a Headache Diary for 8 weeks.

Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

A secondary headache caused by the regular use of acute headache treatments. It is a common cause of chronic daily headache.

Triptans / Ergotamines

≥ 10 days/month

for > 3 months

Includes:

  • Sumatriptan
  • Zolmitriptan
  • Ergotamine

Opioids / Combos

≥ 10 days/month

for > 3 months

Includes:

  • Codeine
  • Tramadol
  • Caffeine combos

Simple Analgesics

≥ 15 days/month

for > 3 months

Includes:

  • Paracetamol
  • Aspirin
  • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen)

Management Principle

Strategy: Withdraw the overused medication. Headache may worsen initially (withdrawal headache) before improving.

Note: MOH requires a pre-existing headache disorder (usually Migraine or Tension-type) to develop.
