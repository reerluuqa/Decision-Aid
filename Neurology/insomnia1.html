Definition (DSM-5-TR)

Difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep OR early awakening despite adequate opportunity.

  • Frequency: ‚â•3 nights/week
  • Duration: ‚â•3 months (Chronic)
  • Impact: Clinically significant distress/impairment
  • Note: Not explained by other mental/medical disorders or substance use alone.

Epidemiology & Theory

Prevalence: 30% report disruption; 10-15% meet criteria for Insomnia Disorder.

The '3P' Model

Predisposing

Genetics, age, female sex, comorbidities.

Precipitating

Stress, illness, life events, schedule change.

Perpetuating

Maladaptive sleep habits, conditioning, anxiety about sleep.

Diagnosis & Assessment

History & Key Questions

Subjective Complaints (Screen for):

  • Time in bed vs. time asleep.
  • Sleep Latency: >30 mins to fall asleep?
  • Wake After Sleep Onset: >30 mins awake?
  • Early Morning Awakening (>30 mins before intended)?

Daytime Symptoms:

  • Fatigue, poor concentration, irritability.
  • Work/Social impairment.

Risk Factors Profile

Strong Risk Factors

  • Strong Female sex
  • Strong Advanced Age
  • Strong Psychiatric illness (Anxiety/Depression)
  • Strong Chronic Medical Conditions (COPD, CHF, Cancer, Pain)
  • Strong Alcohol/Substance Misuse
  • Strong Stimulant Use (Caffeine, Nicotine)

Weak / Contributing

  • Weak Shift work / Night work
  • Weak Jet Lag
  • Weak Medications (SSRIs, Theophylline, Beta-agonists)
  • Weak Hyperthyroidism
  • Weak Poor Sleep Hygiene

Management Approach

1. First-Line Treatment

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)

Recommended for ALL patients (Acute & Chronic). Best balance of efficacy and safety.

  • Stimulus Control: Bed is for sleep only.
  • Sleep Restriction: Match time in bed to sleep time.
  • Cognitive Therapy: Address catastrophic thoughts about sleep.
  • Relaxation Training: Reduce hyperarousal.

2. Pharmacotherapy (Add-on)

Consider if CBT-I unavailable, insufficient, or patient prefers meds. Assess risks (falls, dependence).

Common Options:

  • Benzodiazepines: Effective but risk of tolerance/dependence. Avoid in elderly.
  • Z-Drugs (Zolpidem, Zopiclone): Short-acting. Watch for complex sleep behaviors.
  • Orexin Receptor Antagonists: Newer agents (e.g., Lemborexant).
  • Melatonin Receptor Agonists: (e.g., Ramelteon).
View Treatment Algorithm Overview
  1. Identify comorbidities (pain, apnea) & treat.
  2. Initiate CBT-I.
  3. If no improvement -> Add pharmacotherapy (short-term).
  4. Review & taper hypnotics regularly.

Addressing Comorbidities

Treating underlying conditions is necessary but often not sufficient for insomnia remission.

Pain: Optimize analgesia.
Mental Health: Treat Anxiety/Depression (Note: Treating insomnia can prevent depression).
Medical: Manage COPD, CHF, BPH (nocturia).

Key Differentials

Condition Differentiating Symptom Diagnostic Test
Restless Legs Syndrome Uncomfortable leg sensations, relieved by movement. Clinical History / Iron Studies (Ferritin)
Obstructive Sleep Apnoea Snoring, gasping, witnessed apneas, daytime sleepiness. Polysomnography (PSG)
Circadian Rhythm Disorders Timing issues (fall asleep too late/early) but sleep is normal if at right time. Sleep Diary / Actigraphy
Short Sleeper <6 hours sleep but no daytime impairment. Sleep History
Paradoxical Insomnia Patient reports severe insomnia, but objective measures show normal sleep. PSG (to show discrepancy)
