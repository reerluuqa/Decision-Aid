Quick Fact: Tics are brief, sudden, repetitive movements and/or sounds that increase with stress, anxiety, transitions, or excitement.

Definition

Tics are brief, sudden, repetitive movements and/or sounds. They are divided into motor tics (e.g., blinking, shrugging shoulders, grimacing, or jerking) and vocal tics (e.g., throat clearing, sniffing, grunting, or more complex utterances).

Patients describe an inner urge or a local premonitory sensation, which is then relieved by performing the tic. The tic can be voluntarily suppressed for short periods of time.

Epidemiology

  • More common in children than adults
  • More common in males than females (3:1 ratio)
  • Provisional tic disorders occur in up to 20% of primary school children
  • Chronic tic disorders have a prevalence of 1-3%
  • Tourette's syndrome prevalence: 0.3-0.9% in children

Pathophysiology

Tic disorders likely result from disturbances of the cortico-striatal-thalamo-circuitry. Neuroimaging studies suggest structural and functional abnormalities in these pathways.

The pathophysiology involves multiple neurotransmitters including dopamine, noradrenaline, serotonin, histamine, GABA, glutamine, and acetylcholine.

Comorbidities

  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)
  • Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Mood disorders
  • Sleep disorders

Types of Tics

Simple Motor Tics

  • Grimacing
  • Blinking
  • Head jerking
  • Shoulder shrugging
  • Abdominal tensing
  • Kicking

Simple Vocal Tics

  • Sniffing
  • Grunting
  • Throat clearing
  • Barking
  • Snorting
  • Coughing

Complex Motor Tics

  • Head shaking
  • Skipping
  • Hopping
  • Finger cracking
  • Touching
  • Echopraxia (imitating gestures)

Complex Vocal Tics

  • Whistling
  • Speech irregularities
  • Belching
  • Coprolalia (obscenities)
  • Echolalia (repeating words)
  • Palilalia (repeating own words)
Emergency: If there is an altered level of consciousness while the tic is occurring, or if movements cannot be controlled, consider focal motor or myoclonic seizures instead of tics.

Diagnostic Approach

The diagnosis of tic disorders is made on the basis of the history and an otherwise normal physical examination. No further investigations are necessary unless the presentation and clinical findings suggest other possible differential diagnoses.

Take Detailed History
Perform Physical Exam
Consider Investigations Only If Needed

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Presence of risk factors (age <18 years, male sex, family history)
  • Observed tics (or video documentation)
  • Premonitory sensation (inner urge)
  • Ability to suppress tics temporarily
  • Symptoms improve when distracted
  • Otherwise normal neurological examination
  • Symptoms worse with stress, anxiety, excitement
  • Variable severity over time (waxing and waning)

Investigations

First-line: Clinical diagnosis based on history and physical examination

Consider if needed:

  • MRI brain with and without contrast (if abnormal neurological exam)
  • EEG (if seizures are in differential)
  • Cerebrospinal fluid analysis (if encephalitis suspected)
  • Throat culture and ASO titres (if PANDAS/PANS suspected)

Risk Factors

Strong risk factors:

  • Onset at age <18 years
  • Male sex
  • Family history of tics
  • Family history of OCD, ADHD, or depression
  • Antenatal maternal smoking
  • Personal history of OCD or ADHD

Weak risk factors:

  • History of autism spectrum disorder

Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR)

Disorder Key Features Duration
Provisional Tic Disorder Single or multiple motor and/or vocal tics < 1 year

Remember: Most tics in children are mild and do not require treatment. Treatment is indicated only for patients whose tics interfere with activities of daily living or social interactions.

Initial Management Approach

Psychoeducation
Educate patient, parents, teachers, and peers about tics. Reassure about favorable prognosis. Advise not to focus on tics.
Assess for Comorbidities
Evaluate for ADHD, OCD, anxiety, and mood disorders.
Behavioral Therapy
Consider Comprehensive Behavioral Intervention for Tics (CBIT) as first-line treatment.
Pharmacotherapy
Consider medication if tics significantly interfere with daily life.

Behavioral Therapies

Comprehensive Behavioral Intervention for Tics (CBIT)

Includes habit reversal training, relaxation training, and functional intervention. Recommended as initial treatment option. Most effective when not combined with anti-tic medication.

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)

Can be used to treat tics if CBIT is not available.

Habit Reversal Training

Can be used as a stand-alone therapy for tics.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

May be helpful for managing tics and associated anxiety.

Pharmacotherapy

Alpha-2-adrenergic Agonists (First-line)

Options include clonidine and guanfacine. Preferred as first-line pharmacological agents. Monitor heart rate, blood pressure, and QTc interval.

Botulinum Toxin Injections

Can be used in adults and older adolescents with simple focal motor tics affecting the face or neck region, or with severely disabling vocal tics. Not suitable for complex tics involving multiple muscle groups.

Antipsychotics (Second-line)

Used if alpha-2-adrenergic agonists are ineffective or poorly tolerated. Atypical antipsychotics (aripiprazole, risperidone) are preferred to typical antipsychotics due to more favorable adverse-effect profile. Monitor for weight gain, sedation, metabolic syndrome, acute dystonic reactions, tardive dyskinesia, and neuroleptic malignant syndrome.

Follow-up and Monitoring

  • Regular follow-up appointments to assess tic severity and treatment response
  • Monitor for medication side effects
  • Assess for comorbid conditions and their management
  • Provide ongoing psychoeducation and support
  • Consider referral to specialists (neurology, psychiatry) as needed
Emergency Actions: Immediate referral to neurologist or emergency department required for red flag symptoms.

Red Flags in Tic Disorders

Abnormal neurological examination

Increased tone, spasticity, rigidity, or weakness may indicate secondary causes of tics.

Refer to Neurologist
Abrupt onset of tics

Sudden onset with other neurological symptoms may indicate underlying conditions.

MRI Brain
Unusual pattern of tics

Unilateral tics or tics associated with dystonia may indicate secondary causes.

Refer to Neurologist
Onset in adulthood

Adult-onset tic disorders require comprehensive evaluation to exclude underlying medical illness or toxicity.

Altered consciousness during tics

May indicate seizures rather than tics.

EEG
Fever and altered mental status

With abrupt onset of tic disorder may indicate encephalitis.

Secondary Causes of Tics

Infections

  • Encephalitis
  • Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease
  • Sydenham's chorea
  • Group A streptococcal infections (PANDAS/PANS)

Substance-related

  • Stimulant abuse (cocaine, methamphetamine)
  • Antipsychotics (tardive tics)

Neurological

  • Head trauma
  • Stroke
  • Huntington's disease
  • Wilson's disease
  • Neuroacanthocytosis
  • N-methyl D-aspartate encephalitis

Other

  • Carbon monoxide toxicity
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus

Emergency Action Plan

Identify Red Flags
Check for abnormal neurological examination, abrupt onset, unusual pattern, adult onset, altered consciousness, or fever with altered mental status.
Immediate Referral
Refer to neurologist or emergency department for comprehensive evaluation.
Diagnostic Testing
Order appropriate tests based on presentation (MRI, EEG, lumbar puncture, etc.).
Manage Underlying Condition
Treat the secondary cause of tics rather than the tics themselves.
