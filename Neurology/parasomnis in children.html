Parasomnias in Children

BMJ Best Practice Quick Reference
Key Clinical Concept
Summary

Parasomnias are undesirable events during sleep or transitions. They are most common in childhood and often resolve by adolescence.

NREM: Deep sleep, first half of night (Sleepwalking, Terrors).
REM: Dreaming sleep, second half (Nightmares, RBD).
NREM Parasomnias
First 1/3 to 1/2 of night
TypesConfusional Arousals, Sleepwalking, Sleep Terrors
RecallNone / Amnesia
BehavioursScreaming, Ambulation, Confusion
ConsolingDifficult/Rejected
REM Parasomnias
Second half of night
TypesNightmares, Sleep Paralysis, RBD
RecallVivid / Detailed
BehavioursFear, Muscle Atonia (loss of tone), Acting out dreams (RBD)
ConsolingEasy / Child orientates quickly
RED FLAGS: Do Not Miss

Nocturnal Seizures

  • Stereotypical, repetitive episodes
  • Occurs during wake AND sleep
  • Tongue biting, incontinence, drooling
  • Post-ictal confusion (prolonged)

RBD in Children

  • Extremely rare. Consider Narcolepsy.
  • Violent dream enactment
  • Loss of REM atonia
  • Link to antidepressants/SSRIs

Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)

  • Loud snoring, gasping, apnoeas
  • Neck hyperextension while sleeping
  • Daytime sleepiness
  • Treatment of OSA often resolves parasomnias
Primary Diagnosis Tool

Clinical History is Gold Standard.

  • Observer description (Parents/Siblings).
  • Home video recordings are highly recommended.
  • Timing of night (First vs Second half).
  • Family history of NREM parasomnias.
When to Order PSG?

Polysomnography is NOT routine. Order if:

  • Suspected Sleep Apnoea or PLMD.
  • Differentiating from Nocturnal Seizures.
  • Required for RBD Diagnosis.
  • Atypical presentation or frequent injury.
IMMEDIATE SAFETY MEASURES
Priority Action

Lock Doors & Windows
Prevent exiting home.

Sleep on Ground Floor
Avoid falls down stairs.

Install Door Alarms
Alert parents to wandering.

Remove Sharp Objects
Knives, keys, guns locked away.
General Management
  • Reassurance: Most resolve spontaneously by adolescence.
  • Sleep Hygiene: Fixed wake/sleep times. Avoid sleep deprivation.
  • Triggers: Treat fever, stress, and caffeine/alcohol (in adolescents).
  • Scheduled Awakenings: For frequent NREM parasomnias (wake 15-30min before usual episode).
During an Episode
  • DO NOT: Shake, shout, or try to forcefully wake the child (increases agitation/duration).
  • DO: Gently guide away from danger.
  • DO: Wait it out patiently.
Case 1: The Screamer
Sleep Terror

4-year-old girl. Awakes screaming, inconsolable, agitated. Episodes before 3am. No recall.

Key Features:
  • Autonomic hyperactivity (tachycardia, tachypnoea).
  • Difficult to console (pushes parents away).
  • Amnesia for event.
Action: Reassurance + Safety. Check for comorbid sleep apnoea if snoring present.
Case 2: The Monster
Nightmare

5-year-old boy. Screaming spell 4am. Hugs mom. Vividly describes monster under bed.

Key Features:
  • Occur in second half of night (REM).
  • Child is orientated quickly (< 1 min).
  • Vivid recall of dream content.
  • Autonomic activity less than terrors.
Action: Comfort child. Look for psychological stressors.
Case 3: The Wanderer
Sleepwalking

Child wakes in different location. Found by neighbor. No recollection.

Key Features:
  • Blank staring face.
  • Unresponsive to communication.
  • Ambulation out of bed.
Action: Environmental safety (locks, alarms) is critical here.
