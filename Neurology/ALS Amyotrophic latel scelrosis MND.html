ALS Clinical Assistant

Immediate Attention Required: Respiratory failure and aspiration are leading causes of mortality. Monitor for dyspnoea and choking.
Respiratory Failure

Symptoms: Dyspnoea, orthopnoea, frequent awakenings.

Caused by progressive diaphragmatic weakness.

Action:

  • Assess respiratory function regularly.
  • Discuss non-invasive ventilation (NIV) early.
  • Palliative care discussions.
Bulbar Crisis

Symptoms: Choking on liquids/food, coughing, sialorrhoea, dysarthria.

Risk of aspiration pneumonia & malnutrition.

Action:

  • Speech & Swallowing assessment.
  • Dietary modification (thickened fluids).
  • Consider PEG tube for nutrition.

Diagnostic Criteria & Findings

Key Diagnostic Factors
UMN Signs LMN Signs
  • Weakness: Asymmetrical limb weakness (70%) or bulbar (25%).
  • UMN Signs: Spasticity, hyper-reflexia, Babinski sign, Hoffmann's reflex.
  • LMN Signs: Atrophy, fasciculations, muscle cramps.
  • Progression: Without remission, spreading to contiguous areas.
Investigations
  • EMG: Evidence of diffuse denervation (fibrillation, fasciculation potentials).
  • Rule Out: MRI Brain/Spine to exclude compressive myelopathy.
  • Bloods: CK (elevated up to 1000 U/L), B12, HIV (if risk).
  • Genetic Testing: Offer panel (C9orf72, SOD1, FUS, TARDBP) to all patients.

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Features Key Test
Cervical Spondylosis Sensory symptoms, bladder/bowel disturbance, LMN signs at lesion, UMN below. MRI Cervical Spine (compression)
Multifocal Motor Neuropathy LMN-only signs, often minimal atrophy initially. Conduction block on NCS; Anti-GM1 antibodies
Myasthenia Gravis Fluctuating symptoms, ocular involvement common, no UMN signs. AChR/MuSK antibodies; Repetitive stimulation
Benign Fasciculations No weakness, atrophy, or UMN signs. Normal EMG (no motor unit potential changes)
Primary Lateral Sclerosis Isolated UMN disorder, slower progression. EMG (excludes LMN signs)
Disease-Modifying Therapy
Riluzole Standard of Care
  • Offer at diagnosis.
  • Mechanism: Anti-glutaminergic (reduces excitotoxicity).
  • Benefit: Prolongs survival (~9% annual benefit).
  • Monitoring: LFTs & FBC (monthly x3, then q3mo).
Edaravone Consider
  • Free radical scavenger (reduces oxidative stress).
  • Intensive regimen (IV or Oral).
  • Benefit: Slows progression in specific subgroups.
Multidisciplinary Care

Associated with improved survival and quality of life.

  • Respiratory: NIV, cough assist, early vaccinations (Flu, Pneumococcal, COVID).
  • Nutrition: Dietician support, PEG tube when needed.
  • Rehab: Physiotherapy (mobility), OT (adaptations), Speech (swallowing).
  • Palliative: Advance directives, discuss RRT/withdrawal early.
Emerging Therapies
  • Sodium phenylbutyrate/tauroursodeoxycholic acid: Modestly slowed progression in 6-month trial.
  • Gene-targeted therapies: Approved therapies exist for specific genetic causes (check ClinGen).
Definition

Progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterised by degeneration of motor neurons (cortical, brainstem, ventral cord). Results in progressive weakness and death (usually respiratory failure).

Epidemiology
  • Mean onset: 62 years.
  • Peak incidence: 60-75 years.
  • Sex ratio: Male > Female (1.25:1).
  • Incidence: ~2.1 per 100,000 (Europe).
Aetiology & Risk
  • Sporadic: 90% (Cause unknown, likely gene-time-environment).
  • Familial: 10% (Autosomal dominant).
  • Risk Factors: Age >40, Military service, Smoking, Agricultural chemicals, Lead exposure.
Pathophysiology
  • Glutamate Toxicity: Excess calcium entry -> cell death.
  • Protein Misfolding: TDP-43, FUS aggregation.
  • Oxidative Stress: Mitochondrial damage.
  • Cytoskeletal defects: Disrupted axonal transport.
