Multiple Sclerosis Clinical Reference

Last updated: Dec 18, 2025
Emergency Signs
Acute visual loss, severe motor weakness, respiratory compromise, or autonomic dysfunction require immediate evaluation
Red Flags
Acute vision loss in one eye with pain on movement
Severe motor weakness or paralysis
Respiratory compromise or dysphagia
Autonomic dysfunction (bladder/bowel)
Extensive spinal cord lesions (>3 vertebral segments)
Key Diagnostic Criteria
Two or more attacks with objective clinical evidence of 2+ lesions
Dissemination in space (periventricular, cortical/juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord)
Dissemination in time (simultaneous enhancing/non-enhancing lesions or new lesions on follow-up)
CSF oligoclonal bands can substitute for DIT in typical CIS with DIS
Quick Overview
Definition: Inflammatory demyelinating disease with episodic neurological dysfunction in at least two CNS areas separated in time and space
Demographics: White women, 20-40 years (but can affect any sex, age, ethnic group)
Prevalence: ~309 per 100,000 in US; female:male ratio ~3:1
Geographic gradient: Higher incidence at latitudes closer to poles
Course: 80-85% relapsing-remitting; 10-15% primary progressive
Genetics: First-degree relatives 20-40x higher risk; HLA region involved

Key Investigations
MRI Brain (3T preferred): Periventricular white matter lesions; corpus callosum involvement
MRI Spinal Cord: Cervical lesions; high specificity when present with brain lesions
CSF Analysis: Oligoclonal bands (80% of cases); elevated IgG index
Evoked Potentials: Visual evoked potentials most commonly abnormal
Key Examination Findings
Visual: Afferent pupillary defect; red desaturation; pale optic disc
Motor: Spasticity; increased deep tendon reflexes; clonus
Sensory: Band-like sensations; Lhermitte's sign; trigeminal neuropathy
Gait: Foot dragging; wide-based gait; imbalance
McDonald Criteria (2017) - MRI Findings
Criterion Requirement
Dissemination in Space (DIS) ≥1 T2-hyperintense lesions in ≥2 of 4 CNS areas: periventricular, cortical/juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord
Dissemination in Time (DIT) Simultaneous asymptomatic Gd-enhancing and non-enhancing lesions OR new T2/Gd-enhancing lesion on follow-up MRI
Cortical Lesions Can be used to fulfill DIS criteria (new in 2017 revision)
CSF Oligoclonal Bands Can substitute for DIT in typical CIS with DIS (new in 2017 revision)
Treatment Approach
Treatment of MS can be divided into three parts: treatment of acute attacks; prevention of future attacks; and symptomatic treatments
Acute Attack Management
High-dose IV corticosteroids: Methylprednisolone 500-1000mg daily for 3-5 days
Oral taper: Consider if severe symptoms or prolonged steroid use
Plasma exchange: For severe attacks unresponsive to steroids
Disease-Modifying Therapies
First-line: Interferon-beta, Glatiramer acetate, Dimethyl fumarate, Teriflunomide
Second-line: Fingolimod, Natalizumab, Alemtuzumab, Ocrelizumab
Considerations: Disease activity, comorbidities, patient preference, monitoring requirements
Symptom Management
Symptom Treatment Options
Spasticity Baclofen, Tizanidine, Diazepam, Dantrolene, Physical therapy
Pain Gabapentin, Pregabalin, Amitriptyline, Carbamazepine
Fatigue Amantadine, Modafinil, Energy conservation strategies
Bladder Dysfunction Oxybutynin, Tolterodine, Intermittent catheterization
Bowel Dysfunction Fiber supplements, Stool softeners, Bowel training program
Common Presentations
Optic Neuritis: Painful vision loss, decreased color vision, afferent pupillary defect
Transverse Myelitis: Sensory level, motor weakness, bladder/bowel dysfunction
Brainstem Syndrome: Diplopia, facial numbness, dysphagia, vertigo
Cerebellar Syndrome: Ataxia, tremor, dysmetria, gait instability
Sensory Syndrome: Paresthesias, numbness, Lhermitte's sign
Motor Syndrome: Weakness, spasticity, gait disturbance
Case Examples
Case #1: 28-year-old white woman with cloudy vision in one eye, pain on movement, and color discrimination issues. History of unilateral hemibody paraesthesias 6 years ago.
Case #2: 31-year-old woman, 6 months postnatal, with ascending numbness and weakness in both feet over 2 weeks, progressing to difficulty walking and urinary tract infection.
Risk Factors
Female Sex: 3:1 female to male ratio
Family History: First-degree relatives 20-40x higher risk
Northern Latitude: Higher incidence further from equator
Genetics: HLA region and interleukin receptor genes
Smoking: Increased risk and disability progression
Vitamin D Deficiency: Low childhood sunlight exposure
Epstein-Barr Virus: Strongest viral link to MS
Obesity in Youth: Particularly for girls
Differential Diagnosis
Pulmonary symptoms, elevated ACE level
Chest x-ray, gallium scans, ACE level
Condition Key Differentiating Features Diagnostic Tests
Neuromyelitis Optica Spectrum Disorders Severe optic neuritis, longitudinally extensive spinal cord lesions (>3 segments) Anti-AQP4 and anti-MOG antibodies
Myelopathy due to Cervical Spondylosis Symptoms below neck, gradual progression, older age MRI cervical spine shows compression
Vitamin B12 Deficiency Posterior column sensory loss, macrocytic anemia Low B12 level, high methylmalonic acid
Ischaemic Stroke Sudden onset, single vascular territory, risk factors CT/MRI diffusion-weighted imaging
