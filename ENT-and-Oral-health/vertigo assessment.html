Definition

True vertigo is a rotary sensation (patient or surroundings spinning). It is distinct from dizziness, presyncope, or disequilibrium.

Emergency: Do Not Miss Central Causes

Sudden onset vertigo with significant risk factors (HTN, DM, Smoking, CVD) requires urgent imaging. Central signs (nystagmus, ataxia) may be absent initially.

Clinical Conditions

Cerebrovascular (Stroke/TIA) Central

EMERGENCY

  • Symptoms: Sudden intense vertigo, nausea, vomiting.
  • Signs: Cannot stand without support (eyes open). Bilateral/Vertical nystagmus.
  • Key Exam: Head-impulse test is Negative (no corrective saccade).
  • Action: Urgent MRI requested for all risk factors.

Neoplastic Causes Central

URGENT REFERRAL

  • Symptoms: Vertigo, gait abnormality, signs of intracranial pressure (headache, vomiting).
  • Signs: Cranial nerve deficits, altered mental status.
  • Action: Neuroimaging (CT/MRI) essential.

BPPV Peripheral

Common & Benign

  • Trigger: Specific head movements (looking up, bending, rolling over).
  • Duration: < 30 seconds.
  • Signs: No associated neuro/oto symptoms. Normal neuro exam.
  • Key Test: Positive Dix-Hallpike (posterior) or Supine Lateral Head Turn.

Meniere's Disease Peripheral

Episodic

  • Triad: Vertigo + Hearing loss + Roaring tinnitus + Ear pressure.
  • Duration: Minutes to hours.
  • Progression: Hearing loss worsens, becomes constant over time.
  • Demographics: Onset usually 4th decade. Risk: Family history, viral infection.

Vestibular Neuritis Peripheral

Inflammation

  • Pathology: Disorder of vestibular apparatus/nerve.
  • Differentiation: Distinguish from syncope (loss of consciousness).
  • Key Feature: Unlike stroke, patient can usually stand (though unsteady).

Labyrinthitis Peripheral

Inflammatory

  • Cause: Affects cochlea & vestibular system. Often post-viral URI.
  • Symptoms: Severe room-spinning vertigo, nausea/vomiting.
  • Signs: Unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus.
  • Course: Most episodes are short-lived and self-limited.

Vestibular Migraine Peripheral/Neuro

Common Cause

  • Prevalence: Affects ~20% of migraine patients. Common cause of spontaneous episodic vertigo.
  • Symptoms: Spontaneous/positional vertigo, ataxia, head motion dizziness.
  • Duration: Seconds to days (independent of headache).
  • Key Signs: Photophobia, phonophobia, aura.

Dix-Hallpike Manoeuvre

Gold Standard for Posterior Canal BPPV.

  • Patient moves from sitting to supine with head turned 45¬∞ to affected side and extended 20¬∞.
  • Positive: Vertigo + torsional/upbeat nystagmus after latency (few secs), fatigable.

Head-Impulse Test (HINTS)

Differentiates Acute Vestibular Neuritis vs. Stroke.

  • Patient fixes vision on examiner's nose. Head turned rapidly to one side.
  • Peripheral: Eyes drift off target, then snap back (saccade) -> Positive.
  • Central: Eyes stay on target -> Negative.
