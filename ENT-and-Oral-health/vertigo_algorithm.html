Vertigo — One‑Page Clinical Algorithm

Start here

Confirm vertigo

True vertigo = false sense of motion (spinning/rotation). Distinguish from presyncope, disequilibrium, or light‑headedness.

  • Timing & triggers: seconds vs hours/days; positional vs spontaneous.
  • Nystagmus present? If yes, symptoms are likely vestibular.
Dx hint Ask what movements reliably trigger/relieve symptoms.
Classify

Peripheral vs central clues

  • Peripheral more likely: normal neuro exam; severe N/V; +/- hearing change.
  • Central suspicion: prolonged severe vertigo, new headache or trauma, vascular risk, focal neurology.
AVS: acute continuous vertigo with nystagmus ± gait unsteadiness; can be peripheral (e.g., neuritis) or central (e.g., stroke).
Immediate actions

Red flags

  • Hyperacute persistent vertigo >24h with normal head‑impulse.
  • New unilateral deafness or new severe headache.
  • Focal neurological deficit or severe truncal/gait ataxia.
  • Inability to tolerate fluids/drugs due to relentless vomiting.
Focused history

Ask quickly, document clearly

  • Onset, duration, frequency; positional triggers.
  • N/V; effect on walking/ADLs.
  • Otological: hearing loss, tinnitus, fullness, discharge.
  • Neurological: diplopia, dysarthria/dysphagia, paraesthesia, weakness, ataxia, migraine aura.
  • URTI/ear infection; head trauma; ear barotrauma.
  • Meds (aminoglycosides, loop diuretics, psychotropics) & alcohol.
  • Vascular risk (AF, HTN, DM, smoking, prior IHD).
  • FHx migraine/Ménière’s; anxiety/hyperventilation.
Examination

Look, listen, localise

  • Otoscopy: discharge, vesicles (zoster), cholesteatoma.
  • Eyes: direction & suppression of nystagmus; fundoscopy.
  • Cranial nerves & cerebellar tests; heel‑to‑toe.
  • Gait & stance (can they stand unaided?).
  • Peripheral neuropathy screen.
  • BP, pulse/rhythm, carotids.
Bedside

Dix‑Hallpike

For positional vertigo. Safety: avoid if unstable neck/back or carotid sinus syncope concerns.

  • Head 45° to side → brisk supine with neck extended 20–30°; hold & watch eyes.
  • BPPV: latent (5–20s) torsional up‑beating nystagmus that fatigues <1 min.
Bedside

Head‑impulse (VOR)

  • Ask patient to fixate; deliver small, rapid head turns.
  • Peripheral loss: corrective saccade (positive test). Central: usually normal.
Bedside

Test of skew & others

  • Alternate cover: vertical refixation → central concern.
  • Romberg: identifies instability (not localising).
  • Unterberger: rotation toward affected labyrinth.
Differentiate causes

Pattern recognition

Common peripheral

  • BPPV: brief (<1 min) episodes triggered by head position; Dix‑Hallpike positive.
  • Vestibular neuritis: sudden, severe vertigo lasting days; no hearing loss; positive head‑impulse.
  • Labyrinthitis: vertigo + hearing loss/tinnitus.
  • Ménière’s: spontaneous attacks 30 min–hours with fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness.

Scenario

Central vertigo — act now

  • Admit or urgent specialty referral (neurology/audiovestibular).
  • Urgent brain imaging if red flags present (see above).
  • Known vestibular migraine may be managed at home; suspected cases require assessment.
  • Short‑term symptom relief can be offered while arranging transfer — do not delay referral.
Scenario

Peripheral / cause unclear

  • Urgent admission/referral if: uncontrolled vomiting dehydration; sudden persistent vertigo not positional; central neuro signs; acute deafness without classic Ménière’s picture.
  • Otherwise: refer to balance specialist; urgency by severity and findings.
  • Symptom relief while awaiting review (≤1 week): see Drug quick reference.
Symptomatic therapy (short course)

Drug quick reference

Prochlorperazine

Oral short course for vertigo/N/V. Buccal tablets or IM/Deep IM options for rapid relief in severe vomiting.

Cyclizine

Antihistamine anti‑emetic; oral or IM for rapid symptom control.

Cinnarizine

Antihistamine/vestibular suppressant; short oral course for vertigo/nausea.

Promethazine teoclate

Sedating antihistamine for vertigo/N/V; short‑term use.

Important: Limit vestibular suppressants to ≤ 1 week while arranging assessment; longer use can impede compensation. Review interactions, cautions and pregnancy risks in your local formulary.
When to refer/admit

Escalation criteria

  • Any red flag or central features.
  • Acute unilateral hearing loss without typical Ménière’s features.
  • Persistent disabling vertigo or unclear diagnosis.
  • Severe vomiting preventing oral intake/medication.
Handy tips

Efficiency boosters

  • Pre‑prepare Epley instructions for BPPV discharges.
  • Document HINTS components (Head‑impulse, Nystagmus, Test‑of‑Skew) in AVS presentations.
  • Safety‑net: return immediately if new neuro symptoms, worsening gait, or hearing changes.
