Definition

Meniere's disease (MD) is an episodic auditory and vestibular disorder characterized by:

  • Sudden onset of vertigo (spinning sensation)
  • Sensorineural hearing loss (typically low-frequency)
  • Tinnitus (roaring/buzzing)
  • Sensation of aural fullness

Also called endolymphatic hydrops - thought to result from over-production or impaired absorption of endolymph in the inner ear.

Classic Presentation

40-year-old woman with 1-year history of recurrent vertigo episodes:

  • Room spinning sensation lasting 20 min to several hours
  • Associated nausea and vomiting
  • Aural fullness, tinnitus, and hearing loss in affected ear
  • Horizontal nystagmus during attacks
  • Positive Romberg's test; turns toward affected side on Fukuda test
Pathophysiology Overview

The Hydrops Theory

During acute attacks, excessive endolymphatic fluid pressure causes distension and rupture of Reissner's membrane. This releases potassium-rich endolymph into the perilymphatic space, causing injury to inner ear sensory and neural elements.

Between attacks, the membrane may reattach and chemical balance is restored, leading to symptom remission.

Key Factors

  • Over-production or impaired absorption of endolymph
  • Potential autoimmune mechanisms
  • Viral infection triggers (CMV, HSV, VZV)
  • Genetic predisposition in 10-50% of cases
  • Allergic responses (especially food allergies)

Exclude Other Causes First

Meniere's is a diagnosis of exclusion. Always rule out acoustic neuroma, vestibular migraine, BPPV, and other pathologies before diagnosing MD.

Diagnostic Criteria (AAO-HNS/Bárány Society)

Definite MD

  • ≥2 spontaneous episodes of vertigo, each lasting 20 min to 12 hours
  • Audiometrically documented low- to medium-frequency SNHL in one ear
  • Fluctuating aural symptoms (hearing, tinnitus, or fullness) in affected ear
  • Not better accounted for by another vestibular diagnosis

Probable MD

  • ≥2 episodes of vertigo or dizziness, each lasting 20 min to 24 hours
  • Fluctuating aural symptoms (hearing, tinnitus, or fullness) in affected ear
  • Not better accounted for by another vestibular diagnosis

Emergency Red Flags - Requires Urgent Evaluation

These findings warrant immediate investigation to rule out stroke, tumor, or other serious pathology.

Stroke Mimic - HINTS Exam Required

HINTS Exam is more sensitive than MRI for posterior stroke in acute vertigo. Perform in all acute vertigo presentations.

  • Head Impulse test abnormal (catch-up saccade) → Central
  • Nystagmus direction-changing or vertical → Central
  • Test of Skew present (vertical misalignment) → Central

Mnemonic: HINTS to INFARCT - Any abnormal finding suggests central etiology.

Tumarkin Drop Attacks

⚠️ Critical Warning

  • Sudden loss of balance WITHOUT loss of consciousness
  • No other autonomic or neurological symptoms
  • More common in end-stage disease and older patients
  • Incidence: 3-72% (varies by study)

Clinical significance: Indicates advanced endolymphatic hydrops affecting otolith function. Patients are at high risk of falls and injury.

Recommend fall precautions immediately
Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss

⚠️ Requires Urgent Treatment

  • ≥30 dB SNHL at 3 consecutive frequencies
  • Onset over ≤72 hours
  • Consider emergency ENT referral
  • Start oral corticosteroids within 14 days

Exclude: MRI to rule out acoustic neuroma, autoimmune workup if bilateral.

Bilateral Symptoms = Autoimmune Workup

⚠️ Consider Autoimmune Inner Ear Disease

  • Bilateral MD at presentation suggests autoimmune etiology
  • Check: ANA, ANCA, RF, TSH
  • Lyme disease and syphilis serology if indicated
  • Consider rheumatology referral

Up to 50% of MD patients have bilateral involvement during their lifetime.

Urgent Referral Criteria

To ENT/Audiology

  • Sudden SNHL (within 14 days)
  • Unilateral progressive hearing loss
  • Refractory vertigo despite treatment
  • Suspected bilateral disease
  • Consideration for intratympanic injections

To Neurology/Emergency

  • Positive HINTS exam (central signs)
  • Associated headache or neurological deficits
  • Vertigo with visual changes
  • Drop attacks (rule out cardiac/neurologic)
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)

Acute Attack Management

Focus on symptom control: vestibular suppression, antiemetics, and hearing preservation.

Vestibular Suppressants & Antiemetics
Meclozine (Antihistamine)
Dose: 12.5-25 mg PO q6h PRN Max: 100 mg/day
Dimenhydrinate (Dramamine)
Dose: 50 mg PO q4-6h PRN Max: 300 mg/day
Promethazine (Phenothiazine)
Dose: 12.5-25 mg PO/PR q4-6h Route: Oral or Rectal
Diazepam (Benzodiazepine) - 2nd line
Dose: 2-10 mg PO q4-6h PRN Warning: Risk of dependency

Note: Most effect is from sedative action. Avoid long-term use.

Corticosteroids (with acute hearing loss)
Prednisolone 20 mg PO TID x 2-3 weeks, then taper

Indications: Acute vertigo with sudden hearing loss or tinnitus.

No RCT evidence for efficacy in MD, but widely used for anti-inflammatory properties.

Contraindicated in uncontrolled diabetes, systemic infection, peptic ulcer disease.

Maintenance Phase Management

Focus on attack prevention through lifestyle modification, diuretics, and hearing preservation.

Lifestyle Modification (First-Line)
  • Low Salt Diet: 1500-2300 mg/day

    Prevents sodium-related water retention in endolymph

  • Limit Caffeine

    May trigger or worsen attacks

  • Reduce Alcohol

    Can trigger attacks

  • Smoking Cessation

    Smoking affects inner ear blood flow

  • Stress Management

    Stress is a known trigger

Diuretics (First-Line Medical Therapy)
HCTZ + Triamterene (Dyazide)
Dose: 37.5/125 mg PO daily Duration: 6 months, then reassess
Acetazolamide (Diamox)
Dose: 250 mg PO BID Note: Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor

May reduce endolymph volume. Evidence is controversial but widely used. Monitor electrolytes. Avoid in sulfonamide allergy.

Hearing Preservation Strategies

Amplification

  • Modern digital hearing aids work for MD
  • Directional mics help in noise
  • Regular reprogramming needed
  • Wireless technology beneficial

Audiology Support

  • Serial audiometry monitoring
  • Assistive listening devices
  • Tinnitus management
  • Communication strategies

Refractory Disease - Specialist Referral Required

Consider when symptoms persist despite maximal medical therapy for ≥6 months.

Intratympanic Injections
Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone/Methylprednisolone)
Indication: Hearing loss or vertigo Specialist: ENT consultation required

Used when systemic steroids contraindicated or ineffective.

Gentamicin (Chemical Labyrinthectomy)
Indication: Intractable vertigo Effect: 75-97% vertigo control

Risk of hearing loss (13-35%). Titrated dosing minimizes this risk.

Surgical Options

Endolymphatic Sac Surgery

  • Non-destructive, hearing-preserving
  • 90% report vertigo resolution (controversial)
  • Risk of hearing loss (~2%)

Vestibular Nerve Section

  • Selective cutting of vestibular portion of CN VIII
  • Up to 90% vertigo control
  • Contraindicated in bilateral MD

Labyrinthectomy

  • Complete removal of inner ear neuroepithelium
  • Up to 97% vertigo control
  • Reserved for: No serviceable hearing

Contraindications & Cautions
  • Diuretics: Avoid in sulfonamide allergy, renal impairment, gout
  • Diazepam: Avoid long-term use (dependency risk)
  • Corticosteroids: Contraindicated in diabetes, infection, PUD
  • Gentamicin: Risk of permanent hearing loss
  • Promethazine: Avoid in children <2 years, elderly (paradoxical excitation)
Monitoring Parameters
  • Diuretics: electrolytes (K+, Na+), renal function, uric acid
  • Corticosteroids: Blood glucose, BP, signs of infection
  • Gentamicin: Serial audiometry (critical!)
  • All patients: Serial hearing tests every 3-6 months
  • Vertigo diary: Track frequency, duration, severity

Differential Diagnosis

Meniere's is a diagnosis of exclusion. Rule out these conditions before confirming MD.

High Priority - Must Exclude
Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)
Key differentiators: Progressive unilateral SNHL, poor word discrimination (rollover sign), absent acoustic reflexes.
Test: MRI with gadolinium (gold standard)
Vestibular Migraine
Key differentiators: Much shorter vertigo episodes (<24h), history of migraine, visual auras, no hearing loss.
Note: Can coexist with MD
Posterior Circulation Stroke/TIA
Key differentiators: Positive HINTS exam, neurological deficits, headache, older vascular risk factors.
Test: MRI brain, MRA
Other Conditions to Consider
BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)
Brief vertigo (<1 min) triggered by head position changes. No hearing loss/tinnitus. Positive Dix-Hallpike test.
Vestibular Neuronitis
Acute onset vertigo, nausea, vomiting. No hearing loss/tinnitus. Often follows viral illness. Caloric weakness.
Viral Labyrinthitis
Similar to vestibular neuronitis but WITH hearing loss and tinnitus. Often post-viral.
Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency
Older patients with vascular risk factors. Vertigo lasting minutes, associated with other brainstem symptoms.
Autoimmune Inner Ear Disease
Rapidly progressive bilateral SNHL. Associated with systemic autoimmune conditions. Positive autoantibodies.

Patient Education Points

Dietary Recommendations

Low Salt Diet: Aim for 1500-2300 mg sodium per day. Read food labels carefully.

  • Avoid processed and canned foods
  • Limit restaurant meals
  • Reduce caffeine (coffee, tea, chocolate)
  • Minimize alcohol consumption
  • Avoid MSG and soy sauce

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Smoking: Stop smoking - affects inner ear blood flow
  • Stress: Practice relaxation techniques; stress triggers attacks
  • Sleep: Maintain regular sleep schedule
  • Exercise: Regular aerobic activity improves balance
  • Hydration: Stay well hydrated
Safety Precautions
  • Driving: Stop driving during active vertigo episodes; check local regulations
  • Operating machinery: Avoid during attacks
  • Falls: Use handrails, avoid slippery surfaces
  • Swimming: May need to avoid or have supervision due to orientation issues
  • Ladders/heights: Avoid during active symptoms

Drop attacks: Patients should sit or lie down immediately if they feel an attack coming.

During an Attack

What to Do

  • Sit or lie down immediately
  • Keep eyes open and fixed on a stationary object
  • Avoid bright lights
  • Take prescribed vestibular suppressant if available
  • Stay hydrated with small sips of water
  • Rest until vertigo subsides

When to Seek Help

  • First episode of vertigo
  • Associated chest pain or shortness of breath
  • Severe headache or stiff neck
  • Double vision or difficulty speaking
  • Weakness or numbness in face/limbs
  • Vertigo lasting more than 12 hours
