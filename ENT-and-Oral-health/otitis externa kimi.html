Otitis Externa Management Algorithm

Based on NICE CKS Guidelines • Last Revised August 2025

Assessment

Key History Questions

  • Onset, nature, and severity of symptoms (pain, itch, discharge)
  • Tenderness on moving ear (tragus/pinna) or jaw
  • Impact on daily functioning
  • Risk factors: recent trauma, water exposure, hearing aids, skin conditions
  • Diabetes mellitus or immunocompromise
  • Previous episodes and treatments
  • History of ear surgery or perforated tympanic membrane

Examination Findings

  • Inspect ear canal, pinna, and regional lymph nodes
  • Look for: redness, edema, discharge, debris, fungal signs
  • Assess tragus/pinna tenderness
  • Check for tympanic membrane perforation
  • Evaluate surrounding skin for dermatitis
Exercise caution if TM not visualized

Investigations

Consider ear swab for MC&S if:

  • Treatment failure
  • Severe, recurrent, or chronic infection
  • Ear canal occlusion preventing treatment
  • Suspected spread beyond ear canal
Routine for uncomplicated cases not required

Diagnosis

Acute Otitis Externa Common

Symptoms: Rapid onset (<48h), itch, pain, possible discharge

Signs: Tragus/pinna tenderness + red, edematous canal (≥2 signs)

Duration: ≤3 weeks

Chronic Otitis Externa

Symptoms: Persistent itch, mild discomfort

Signs: Dry scaly or red moist skin, canal stenosis, lack of wax

Duration: >3 months

Red Flag: Malignant Otitis Externa URGENT

High-risk patients: Diabetes, immunocompromised, elderly

  • Unremitting severe pain out of proportion
  • Purulent otorrhea with systemic illness
  • Granulation tissue in ear canal
  • Facial nerve palsy
  • Exposed bone in canal

Differential Diagnosis

  • Acute otitis media - TM perforation with discharge
  • Foreign body - Especially in children
  • Impacted earwax - Pain and hearing loss
  • Skin conditions - Dermatitis, eczema, psoriasis
  • Referred pain - Dental, sinus, throat
  • Cholesteatoma - Painless persistent discharge
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome - Herpes zoster with vesicles

Treatment

Initial Management

1. Self-Care Measures

  • Avoid ear trauma (no cotton buds)
  • Keep ear clean and dry for 7-10 days
  • Use ear plugs when bathing/swimming
  • Consider OTC acetic acid 2% drops (age 12+) for 7 days max
  • Use hair dryer on low setting to dry canal

2. Analgesia

Paracetamol or ibuprofen as needed for pain relief

3. Aural Toilet Enable effective treatment

  • Dry swabbing of secretions
  • Ear irrigation if TM intact and not immunocompromised

Topical Treatment

Prescribe topical antibiotic ± corticosteroid for 7-14 days

Available Preparations:

  • Astringent/Acidic: Acetic acid 2% spray (EarCalm®)
  • Antibiotic: Ciprofloxacin drops, Gentamicin 0.3% drops
  • Combined: Ciprofloxacin + dexamethasone, Betnesol-N® (neomycin + betamethasone)
  • Antifungal: Clotrimazole 1% for fungal infection
  • Steroid only: Prednisolone or betamethasone drops for inflammation

Administration: Fill ear canal, lie on side for 3-5 minutes, cotton plug optional

Aminoglycosides contraindicated if perforated TM

Oral Antibiotics

Consider if:

  • Patient is immunocompromised
  • Severe infection
  • Spread beyond external ear canal (cellulitis)
See Cellulitis CKS for guidance

Fungal Infection (Chronic)

  • Topical antifungal: Clotrimazole 1% 2-3 times daily
  • Continue for at least 14 days after resolution
  • Alternative: Clioquinol + corticosteroid

Follow-up & Referral

When to Reassess

  • No improvement within 48-72 hours of treatment
  • Symptoms not resolved after 2 weeks
  • Severe symptoms or spreading cellulitis
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Canal stenosis preventing TM visualization
65-90% resolve within 7-10 days

Urgent Referral Criteria EMERGENCY

Immediate ENT referral/admission if:

  • Suspected malignant otitis externa
  • Severe infection not responding to primary care management
  • Facial nerve palsy or other complications
  • Systemically unwell patient
  • Osteomyelitis suspected

Specialist Referral (Non-Urgent)

Refer to ENT if:

  • Persistent symptoms despite optimal management
  • Elderly/diabetic/immunocompromised with concerning features
  • Canal occlusion preventing topical treatment (needs microsuction/wick)
  • Cellulitis extending beyond canal
  • Requires topical treatment >2-3 months

Refer to Dermatology if:

  • Suspected contact sensitivity to neomycin/aminoglycosides
  • Persistent dermatitis reaction

Complications to Monitor

Local Complications

  • Chronic otitis externa
  • Canal stenosis/fibrosis
  • Perichondritis/chondritis
  • TM perforation

Serious Complications

  • Malignant otitis externa
  • Osteomyelitis
  • Facial nerve palsy
  • Meningitis/brain abscess
