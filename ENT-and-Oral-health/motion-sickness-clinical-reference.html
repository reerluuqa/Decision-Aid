Quick Overview

Definition

Motion sickness is characterized by stomach discomfort, nausea, and vomiting, accompanied by dizziness, vertigo, or autonomic features such as pallor, clamminess, flushing, or sweating.

Key Facts
Peak susceptibility: Puberty (~11-12 years)
Female:Male ratio: 5:3 for vomiting
Most provocative frequency: ~0.2 Hz
Immunity: Only with non-functional vestibular system

Classification (ICVD Criteria)

Normal Response

Motion Sickness

Acute episode from physical motion (vehicle, animal, amusement ride). Normal physiological response in most people.

Disorder

Motion Sickness Disorder (MSD)

≥5 episodes triggered by same/similar stimuli, no habituation with repeated exposure, behavioral/emotional response.

Normal Response

Visually Induced (VIMS)

Acute episode from visual motion (VR, simulators, screens). Separate diagnosis from physical motion sickness.

Any combination can co-occur

Motion sickness, MSD, VIMS, and VIMSD can coexist. Susceptibility to one type doesn't correlate with others.

Symptoms Checklist

Core Symptoms
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting / Retching
  • Epigastric discomfort
  • Dizziness / Light-headedness
  • Headache
  • Cold sweats
Associated Symptoms
  • Pallor
  • Dry mouth
  • Excessive salivation
  • Sweating
  • Sleepiness (Sopite syndrome)
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Increased odor sensitivity
  • Eye strain

Important Considerations

  • Headache may persist hours after motion exposure
  • Symptoms can continue after motion stops (avalanche phenomenon)
  • Rule out food poisoning, migraine, and inner ear disease

Treatment Algorithm

Acute Motion Sickness Management
1
Breathing + Behavioral Measures
Controlled breathing (regular, moderate depth), cool fresh air, minimize head movements, avoid unpleasant odors. ~50-67% efficacy vs hyoscine.
FIRST LINE
2
Pharmacotherapy - Anticipated Motion
Non-severe or sedation unacceptable: Hyoscine transdermal (apply 5-6 hrs before) or oral (150-300mcg 30 min before).
Sedation acceptable: Promethazine 25mg PO/IM 1 hr before.
ADJUNCT
3
Severe Disease with Repetitive Vomiting
Immediate IV hydration required. Promethazine IM if available. Monitor for dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
EMERGENCY
Known Susceptibility - Long Term
Habituation Training

Repeated brief exposures in desensitization regimen. Most effective for occupational exposure (navy, aircrew). Effects lost without maintenance.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
CBT + Anxiolytic Tactics

For polysymptomatic patients with headache, disorientation, severe fatigue. Systematic multidisciplinary approach.

Drug Safety Warnings

  • All drugs cause drowsiness - warn patients not to drive/operate machinery
  • Hyoscine contraindicated in children <10 years - can cause dangerous adverse effects
  • Antihistamines in young children - oversedation can be life-threatening
  • Drugs impede habituation - not for repeated/continuous exposure
  • Oral drugs ineffective if vomiting has started (gastric stasis)

Patient Advice - Prevention Strategies

Fix eyes on horizon

Look at stable reference point outside vehicle. Driver's seat recommended.

Minimize head movements

Keep head supported against headrest. Avoid reading or phone use.

Controlled breathing

Breathe regularly and moderately. Deliberately desynchronize from vehicle motion.

Cool fresh air

Open windows or ensure ventilation. Avoid unpleasant odors and smoke.

Optimal seating position

Car: front seat. Ship: middle. Train: front. Avoid rear seats.

Dietary measures

Stay hydrated. Small frequent meals. Limit alcohol and caffeine before travel.

Key Messages for Patients

Medication warning: Motion sickness medication causes drowsiness - do not drive or operate machinery.

Seek medical help: If repetitive vomiting occurs - risk of volume depletion.
