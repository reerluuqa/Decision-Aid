Acute Sore Throat - Clinical Reference

Quick reference for primary healthcare professionals (Last revised: September 2024)

EMERGENCY ALERT: Do not examine the throat of anyone with suspected epiglottitis as this may precipitate airway closure.

Red Flags & Emergency Conditions

IMMEDIATE REFERRAL REQUIRED
  • Epiglottitis: Severe acute onset, muffled voice, drooling, stridor
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy): Fever, neck pain, trismus, muffled voice, displaced uvula
  • Retropharyngeal abscess: Severe sore throat not resolving, trismus, neck swelling
  • Lemierre syndrome: Thrombophlebitis of jugular vein with SIRS/sepsis
  • Significant dehydration: Reduced fluid intake (50-75% normal), signs of clinical dehydration

Common Causes

Rhinovirus, coronavirus, parainfluenza virus - Most common cause
Influenza types A and B - Associated with systemic symptoms
Adenovirus - May cause pharyngoconjunctival fever
Herpes simplex virus - May cause vesicles/ulcers on palate
Epstein-Barr virus - Causes infectious mononucleosis
Group A beta-haemolytic streptococcus (GABHS) - Most common bacterial cause
Group C and G streptococci - May cause pharyngitis
Fusobacterium necrophorum - Rarely leads to Lemierre syndrome
Non-infectious: Physical irritation, hayfever, GERD, Kawasaki disease
Rare infections: Diphtheria, gonorrhoea, candidal pharyngitis

Management

NOTE: NICE does not recommend rapid antigen testing. Use clinical risk scoring instead.

Self-Care Advice

  • Regular analgesia (paracetamol, ibuprofen)
  • Adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration
  • Rest
  • Soothing measures (warm liquids, lozenges)

Antibiotic Choices

First-line

  • Phenoxymethylpenicillin (5-day course)
  • Amoxicillin (avoid if glandular fever possible)

Penicillin Allergy

  • Clarithromycin (5-day course)
  • Erythromycin (5-day course)
IMPORTANT: Concomitant treatment with clarithromycin and ivabradine is now contra-indicated.

Key Points

Definition

Sore throat occurs when an acute upper respiratory tract infection affects the mucosa of the throat. Includes:

  • Acute pharyngitis: inflammation of the oropharynx
  • Tonsillitis: inflammation of the tonsils

Prognosis

  • 40% resolve by 3 days
  • 85% resolve by 1 week
  • Generally self-limiting within two weeks
CLINICAL TIP: Rhinorrhoea, nasal congestion, and cough are usually present in viral pharyngitis, but not in bacterial pharyngitis.
