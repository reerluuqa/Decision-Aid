Vertigo Quick-Reference Guide

NHS CKS Dec 2022

1. Is it TRUE VERTIGO?

Spinning / rotatory illusion → YES → proceed.

Light-headedness, pre-syncope, unsteadiness → investigate other causes.

2. Red flags for CENTRAL cause → ADMIT / URGENT REFER

  • Persistent vertigo > 24 h + normal head-impulse test
  • New headache, deafness, diplopia, dysarthria, limb or gait ataxia, cranial nerve signs
  • Severe vomiting unable to take oral fluids

3. Bedside Differentiation

Feature Peripheral Central
Nystagmus Horizontal-torsional, fatigable, suppressed by fixation Vertical / direction-changing, non-fatigable
Head-Impulse Positive (corrective saccade) Negative (normal VOR)
Alternate-cover Normal Skew deviation
Gait Unsteady but able to walk Cannot stand / walk even with eyes open

4. Common Peripheral Patterns

  • BPPV – seconds, positional trigger → Dix-Hallpike
  • Vestibular neuronitis – hours-days, severe, no hearing loss, positive HIT
  • Labyrinthitis – as above + hearing loss / tinnitus
  • Ménière’s – 30 min–hours, fluctuating hearing, tinnitus, fullness

5. Symptom Relief (≤ 1 week)

  • Prochlorperazine 5 mg PO TDS or 3-6 mg buccal BD or 12.5 mg IM
  • Cinnarizine 30 mg PO TDS
  • Cyclizine 50 mg PO / IM TDS
  • Promethazine teoclate 25 mg nocte (max 100 mg/day)
Avoid prolonged use → delays compensation.

6. Referral Pathway

  • Central red flags → Emergency / Neurology / Audiovestibular physician
  • Peripheral, uncertain or recurrent → ENT / Neurology / Balance clinic
  • BPPV confirmed → Epley manoeuvre (see separate CKS)
Mnemonic: SPIN vs STEM
Stable gait Positive HIT Inhibits fixation Normal neuro → Peripheral
Severe ataxia Trauma/Headache Eye signs Malignant HTN → Central
