Oral Ulceration Assessment

Rapid clinical reference for triage and differential diagnosis. Use color-coded sections to quickly identify urgency levels and appropriate management pathways.

CRITICAL RULES - DO NOT MISS

2-Week Rule

Any ulcer > 2 weeks duration requires biopsy and urgent referral

Nikolsky Sign

Positive sign indicates autoimmune blistering disease - immediate referral

Immunocompromised

Unexplained ulcers in immunocompromised patients need urgent workup

Onset Pattern

Acute onset + fever + multiple ulcers = consider herpetic infection

EMERGENCY - Immediate Action Required

LIFE THREATENING

Oral Squamous Cell Carcinoma

6th most common malignancy worldwide

CRITICAL

Key Clinical Features

Solitary ulcer Indurated margins Verrucous/exophytic growth Persistent >2 weeks Bleeding Neck mass

High-Risk Locations

Ventrolateral tongue, floor of mouth, soft palate complex. Any site in immunocompromised patients.

Risk Factors

  • Tobacco use (smoking, chewing)
  • Alcohol consumption
  • HPV infection (especially type 16)
  • Betel quid chewing
  • Diet low in fruits/vegetables
  • Age >40 years, male predominance

IMMEDIATE ACTION

Urgent referral for biopsy and oncological assessment. Do not wait. 5-year survival < 60% when diagnosed late.

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome / TEN

Severe drug reactions with high mortality

CRITICAL

Key Clinical Features

Abrupt onset Mouth & lip ulcers Target skin lesions Ocular involvement Genital lesions Fever

Triggers

Allopurinol, antibiotics (sulfonamides, beta-lactams), anticonvulsants, NSAIDs, infections (Mycoplasma, HSV)

Severity Differentiation

  • SJS: < 10% BSA, better prognosis
  • SJS/TEN overlap: 10-30% BSA
  • TEN: >30% BSA, highest mortality

IMMEDIATE ACTION

Admit to burn unit or ICU. Stop all non-essential medications. Ophthalmology consult mandatory. Mortality: SJS 5-10%, TEN 25-35%.

Pemphigus Vulgaris

Potentially life-threatening autoimmune blistering

CRITICAL

Key Clinical Features

Chronic oral ulcers Fragile blisters Positive Nikolsky sign Painful erosions Buccal mucosa affected 90% present oral lesions

Epidemiology

Age 40-60, Ashkenazi Jewish/Mediterranean predisposition. 1-5 cases/million/year. Oral lesions FIRST manifestation in >50%.

Diagnostic Clues

  • Anti-desmoglein 3 antibodies (mucosal)
  • Anti-desmoglein 1 antibodies (cutaneous)
  • Flaccid bullae that rupture easily
  • Extends to pharynx/esophagus

IMMEDIATE ACTION

Urgent dermatology referral for biopsy (H&E + immunofluorescence). Without treatment, mortality >90% due to infection/fluid loss.

Invasive Fungal Infections

Rapidly progressive, potentially fatal

CRITICAL

Key Clinical Features

Deep-seated ulcers Necrotic base Rapid progression Immunocompromised host Sinus involvement Bone erosion

Common Pathogens

  • Aspergillus: Black/yellow lesions, palate/tongue
  • Zygomycosis: Sinusitis, facial cellulitis
  • Histoplasmosis: Chronic masses, bone erosion
  • Blastomycosis: Verrucous lesions

High-Risk Patients

Uncontrolled diabetes, hematologic malignancy, HIV/AIDS, prolonged neutropenia, stem cell transplant recipients

IMMEDIATE ACTION

Urgent infectious disease consultation. Start IV antifungals immediately. Surgical debridement often required. High mortality without prompt treatment.

URGENT - Same Week Referral

PROMPT EVALUATION

Malignant Salivary Tumors

Mucoepidermoid and adenoid cystic carcinoma

URGENT

Key Clinical Features

Rapid growth Firm, nodular Fixed to tissue Poorly defined margins Pain/nerve involvement Skin/mucosal ulceration

Typical Sites

Palate (most common), parotid, submandibular, minor salivary glands throughout oral cavity

URGENT ACTION

Urgent referral to head and neck surgery. Imaging (MRI/CT) required. Treatment: surgery + radiotherapy. No effective chemotherapy.

Behcet's Disease

Systemic vasculitis - classic triad

URGENT

Classic Triad

Oral ulcers Uveitis Genital ulcers

Associated Features

  • Joint symptoms (arthritis)
  • Skin lesions (erythema nodosum)
  • Pathergy reaction
  • Family history common
  • HLA-B51 association

Epidemiology

Highest prevalence along "Silk Road" - Turkey: 420/100,000. Onset 3rd-4th decade.

URGENT ACTION

Rheumatology referral. Ophthalmology assessment mandatory (uveitis can cause blindness). Manage with immunosuppressants.

Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis

Formerly Wegener's - necrotizing granulomatosis

URGENT

Classic Triad

Upper respiratory granulomas Lower respiratory granulomas Necrotizing vasculitis Glomerulonephritis

Oral Manifestations

  • Strawberry gingivitis (pathognomonic)
  • Hyperplastic, petechiae-laden gingiva
  • Destructive palatal ulcers
  • 13% present with oral lesions

Laboratory

c-ANCA (anti-PR3), elevated ESR/CRP, renal impairment

URGENT ACTION

Urgent rheumatology/nephrology referral. Can be rapidly fatal without treatment. Requires cyclophosphamide + steroids.

Lupus Erythematosus

Chronic inflammatory connective tissue disease

URGENT

Oral Manifestations

Ulcerated/atrophic lesions Central erythema White radiating striae White stippling Painful ulceration Concurrent skin lesions

Associated Features

  • Positive ANA (antinuclear antibodies)
  • Photosensitivity
  • Joint pain/swelling
  • Renal involvement
  • Malar rash

Complications

Risk of malignant transformation to squamous cell carcinoma. Long-term monitoring required.

URGENT ACTION

Rheumatology referral for autoantibody testing. Regular oral cancer screening essential. Manage with immunosuppressants.

ROUTINE - Common Benign Conditions

MANAGE IN PRACTICE

Recurrent Aphthous Stomatitis

Most common oral ulceration (5-25% prevalence)

ROUTINE

Three Types

Minor (80%): <1cm, 1-10 ulcers, 14 days Major (10-15%): >1cm, deeper, scarring Herpetiform (5-10%): crops of 1-3mm ulcers

Classic Features

  • Round/ovoid shallow ulcers
  • White-yellow pseudomembrane
  • Erythematous halo
  • On non-keratinized mucosa ONLY
  • Spares gingiva and hard palate

MANAGEMENT

Symptomatic treatment: topical steroids (triamcinolone), lidocaine rinses. Rule out celiac disease if severe. Reassure - benign, recurrent.

Traumatic Ulcers

Most common cause of acute oral ulcers

ROUTINE

Common Causes

Cheek/tongue biting Sharp tooth/ restoration Denture irritation Thermal burn Chemical burn Iatrogenic injury

Key Features

  • Acute onset
  • Correlates with trauma site
  • Heals within 2 weeks
  • Resolution with removal of cause

MANAGEMENT

Remove offending cause, smooth sharp edges, topical protectants. If >2 weeks, biopsy to rule out other causes.

Herpes Simplex Virus

Primary infection and reactivation

ROUTINE

Primary Infection (HSV-1)

Acute gingivostomatitis Fever, malaise Multiple vesicles â†’ ulcers Cervical lymphadenopathy Children/young adults

Recurrent HSV

  • Affects 20-40% of exposed individuals
  • On keratinized mucosa (hard palate, gingiva)
  • Prodrome: tingling, burning
  • Grouped vesicles â†’ shallow ulcers
  • Triggered by sun, stress, illness

MANAGEMENT

Primary: supportive care, hydration. Recurrent: oral antivirals (acyclovir, valacyclovir) if severe/frequent. Note: NOT related to herpetiform RAS.

Nutritional Deficiencies

Iron, folate, B12, vitamin C

ROUTINE

Key Associations

Chronic ulcerations Glossitis (beefy red tongue) Angular cheilitis Mucosal pallor Atrophic epithelium

Specific Deficiencies

  • Iron: Impaired immunity, epithelial changes
  • Folate/B12: Megaloblastic anemia, glossitis
  • Vitamin C: Gingival swelling, bleeding, loosening teeth

Red Flags in Males

Iron deficiency in males is UNCOMMON - suggests occult bleeding (GI malignancy, peptic ulcer).

MANAGEMENT

Laboratory workup: CBC, iron studies, B12, folate. Investigate underlying cause (malabsorption, blood loss). Refer for GI evaluation if indicated.

Oral Lichen Planus

Common T-cell mediated dermatological condition

ROUTINE

Clinical Forms

Reticular (Wickham's striae) Erosive/ulcerative Atrophic Plaque-like Bullous

Key Features

  • 2:1 female predominance
  • Most common on buccal mucosa
  • Bilateral, symmetrical
  • 15-35% have oral-only disease
  • Reticular often asymptomatic
  • Erosive forms are painful

MANAGEMENT

Biopsy to confirm diagnosis. Regular monitoring (malignant potential ~1-2%). Topical steroids for symptomatic erosive forms. Life-long follow-up recommended.

Contact Stomatitis

Allergic/toxic reaction to oral agents

ROUTINE

Common Triggers

Oral hygiene products Dental materials Foods/additives Medications Flavoring agents

Clinical Features

  • Single isolated ulcer
  • Side of tongue common
  • Erythematous halo
  • Resistant to usual treatments
  • Lichenoid variant near restorations

Common Culprits

Chlorhexidine, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, gold salts, sulfonamides, lidocaine, amalgam (lichenoid reaction)

MANAGEMENT

Remove offending agent. Patch testing if uncertain. Lesions resolve over weeks-months after elimination. Replace dental materials if related.

Red Flags Requiring Immediate Action

  • Any ulcer persisting >2 weeks
  • Positive Nikolsky sign
  • Indurated, fixed mass
  • Unexplained neck lymphadenopathy
  • Extensive oral involvement with skin lesions
  • Ulcer in immunocompromised host

Routine Findings Manage in Practice

  • Minor RAS with <1cm ulcers
  • Clear history of trauma
  • Typical recurrent HSV on keratinized mucosa
  • Asymptomatic reticular lichen planus
  • Single ulcer <2 weeks, non-indurated
  • Contact stomatitis with clear trigger
