Halitosis Management Guide

Comprehensive clinical reference for primary care professionals

Last Revised: September 2024
Target: NHS Primary Healthcare
Source: NICE CKS

Definition & Classification

Halitosis (bad breath/oral malodor) is an unpleasant odor in exhaled air, classified as:

  • Physiological: Transient (morning breath, fasting)
  • Pathological:
    • Intra-oral (85-90% of cases)
    • Extra-oral (uncommon)
  • Psychogenic:
    • Pseudo-halitosis (resolves with reassurance)
    • Halitophobia (persistent fear despite treatment)

Causes

Physiological Smoking Foods/Drinks
Tongue Coating Gingivitis Periodontitis Dry Mouth Poor Denture Hygiene
ENT Conditions Respiratory GI Disorders Systemic Diseases Medications

Prevalence & Impact

  • Estimated global prevalence: 31.8%
  • 8-50% perceive persistent oral malodor
  • No gender differences in prevalence
  • May increase with age
  • Often under-reported due to embarrassment
  • Severe cases: <5% of population
  • Psychogenic: 0.5-1% of adults

Complications: Social stigma, anxiety, depression, reduced quality of life

Management Algorithm

1

Assessment & Diagnosis

Organoleptic Testing: Assess breath from mouth vs. nose

  • Oral origin: Malodor from mouth only
  • Nasal origin: Malodor from nose only
  • Systemic: Equal malodor from both (rare)

History: Severity, timing, associated symptoms, lifestyle factors, oral hygiene routine, medical history, medications

Examination: Oral cavity, teeth, tongue coating, plaque, periodontal health

2

Initial Management

Reassurance: Physiological causes are normal and common

Lifestyle Changes: Avoid trigger foods, stop smoking, limit alcohol, stay hydrated

Oral Hygiene:

  • Clean between teeth daily (floss/interdental brushes)
  • Brush teeth twice daily with fluoride toothpaste (2 minutes)
  • Daily tongue cleaning (focus on posterior area)
  • Denture hygiene (remove at night, clean regularly)

Additional Measures: Antibacterial mouthwash (chlorhexidine), sugar-free gum for dry mouth

3

Specialist Referral

Urgent Referral

  • Unexplained oral ulceration >3 weeks
  • Lump on lip/oral cavity or red/white patch
  • Unexplained gingival enlargement
  • Suspected acute necrotizing ulcerative gingivitis

Routine Referral

  • Persistent halitosis despite adequate oral hygiene
  • Recurrent tonsillitis or suspected sinonasal disease
  • Suspected extra-oral cause requiring specialist assessment
  • Halitophobia causing significant distress

Dental Referral: Any suspected oral/dental cause, poor oral hygiene, poorly-fitting dentures

Management Goals

  • Confirm diagnosis of halitosis in primary care
  • Identify intra-oral or extra-oral causes
  • Manage underlying causes appropriately
  • Arrange specialist referral where necessary
  • Advise on oral hygiene and lifestyle measures
