Quick Differentiation: Labyrinthitis vs Vestibular Neuritis

Labyrinthitis (Inner Ear Inflammation)
  • Vertigo + Hearing Loss/Tinnitus
  • Inflammation of membranous labyrinth
  • Often follows URI (viral) or OM (bacterial)
  • Sensorineural hearing loss (often profound)
  • Vertigo lasts hours to days, imbalance weeks
KEY: Hearing loss + Vertigo
Vestibular Neuritis (Vestibular Nerve Inflammation)
  • Vertigo WITHOUT hearing loss
  • Inflammation of vestibular nerve only
  • Often follows viral illness (HSV-1 reactivation)
  • Normal hearing (cochlea not affected)
  • Vertigo improves over 48-72 hours, imbalance persists
KEY: Vertigo ONLY, normal hearing

HINTS Exam - Stroke Rule-Out in Acute Vestibular Syndrome

More sensitive than early MRI for posterior stroke

HINTS (Head-Impulse, Nystagmus, Test-of-Skew) can identify stroke with higher sensitivity than diffusion-weighted MRI in the first 24-48 hours.

Head-Impulse Test

Peripheral: Catch-up saccade (corrects VOR deficit)

Central: Normal impulse

Nystagmus Pattern

Peripheral: Direction-fixed, horizontal-torsional

Central: Direction-changing or purely vertical

Test of Skew

Peripheral: No vertical misalignment

Central: Vertical ocular misalignment present

Central Findings = ANY of the following:

• Direction-changing nystagmus • Pure vertical nystagmus • Skew deviation • Normal head-impulse with acute vestibular syndrome

IMMEDIATE MRI if:

Any central sign on HINTS, or patient cannot sit/walk, or new neurological deficits present

Key Symptoms Checklist

RED FLAGS - Consider Alternative Diagnosis:

• Headache, fever, neck stiffness (meningitis) • Focal neurological deficits • Bilateral symptoms • Progressive hearing loss over months • Recurrent episodes of vertigo (< 20 min each - think Meniere's)

SELECTED CASES

Imaging Decision Guide

◆ Patient with acute vertigo + vestibular syndrome?
◆ Any central signs on HINTS exam?
◆ Asymmetric hearing loss or suspicion for retrocochlear pathology?
◆ History of head trauma or temporal bone fracture suspected?
◆ Classic peripheral findings, no red flags, good follow-up available?

Imaging Modalities:

MRI brain with DWI: Gold standard for stroke (more sensitive than CT in first 24-48h)
CT head: Useful for trauma, bone detail; misses early stroke
Gadolinium-enhanced MRI: For asymmetric hearing loss, vestibular schwannoma evaluation

Medication Reference

Drug Adult Dose Duration Notes
Prednisolone 60 mg PO daily 10-14 days + 5-day taper First-line for both conditions; start early
Dexamethasone 10 mg PO/IV daily Similar taper Alternative if PO not tolerated
Promethazine 12.5-25 mg PO/IV q4-6h Max 5 days Avoid in elderly; watch for sedation
Dimenhydrinate 50-100 mg PO/IV q4-6h Max 5 days Sedating; caution with driving
Ondansetron 4-8 mg PO/IV q4-8h As needed Less sedating option for nausea
Metoclopramide 5-10 mg PO/IV q8h Max 5 days Risk of extrapyramidal effects; avoid in children

Important Reminders:

• Limit vestibular suppressants to 3-5 days - interferes with central compensation
• Warn patients about driving/operating machinery while on sedating medications
• Use only ONE anti-emetic/suppressant at a time
• Corticosteroids most effective when started within 48-72 hours of symptom onset

Risk Factors & Etiology

Strong Risk Factors

Recent upper respiratory infection
Acute otitis media
Chronic suppurative otitis media
Cholesteatoma
Bacterial meningitis
HSV-1 reactivation (VN)

Weak/Associated Risk Factors

Autoimmune inner ear disease
Syphilis (tertiary neurosyphilis)
Inner ear malformations
Vascular risk factors (VN)

Follow-up & Prognosis

Prognosis - Generally Favorable

• Most patients achieve substantial recovery within 6 weeks
• Vestibular neuritis: 50-80% achieve complete vestibular function recovery
• Labyrinthitis: Hearing loss may be permanent; vertigo typically resolves

Recovery Timeline

0-3 days Acute vertigo phase
3-7 days Vertigo improves
1-4 weeks Imbalance persists
4-6 weeks Compensation
6+ weeks Full recovery

Return Instructions (Red Flags)

Advise patients to return immediately if: worsening headache, fever, neck stiffness, new weakness/numbness, vision changes, worsening vomiting, or symptoms not improving after 1 week

Complications to Monitor

Persistent disequilibrium
Persistent hearing loss (labyrinthitis)
BPPV secondary to otolith debris
Chronic subjective dizziness
