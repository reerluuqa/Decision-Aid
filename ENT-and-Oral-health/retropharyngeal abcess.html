BMJ Best Practice Summary

Retropharyngeal Abscess (RPA)

Last Updated: Oct 18, 2022
Primary Triage: Airway Status
Is the airway compromised?

EMERGENCY PROTOCOL

Immediate Actions Required:

  • Admit Immediately to hospital.
  • Airway Management: Prepare for intubation or surgical airway (Fiber-optic preferred to prevent bursting).
  • Medical Stabilization: IV Corticosteroids + Nebulised Adrenaline (Epinephrine).
  • Surgery: Immediate Examination Under Anaesthetic (EUA) with drainage.
High Mortality Risk if Delayed Call Otolaryngology

STANDARD PROTOCOL

Management Steps:

  • Admit to Hospital for observation.
  • Start Empirical IV Antibiotics immediately (see treatment section).
  • Adjunct: IV Corticosteroids may be used.
  • Monitor: Re-evaluate in 24-48 hours. If no improvement or abscess >2cm, consider drainage.
Monitor Airway Closely CT Scan Required

Overview

Abscess between pre-vertebral fascia and constrictor muscles.

Peak Incidence: Ages 3-5 (Children) & increasing in Adults.
12-22% of deep neck infections Male predominance (53-62%)

Key Symptoms

Children may present non-specifically.

  • Neck Pain / Torticollis (Common)
  • Odynophagia / Dysphagia (Painful swallowing)
  • Spiking Fever
  • Drooling Alert
  • Stridor / Dyspnoea Emergency
  • Muffled Voice / "Hot Potato" voice

Risk Factors

  • Recent Upper Respiratory Infection (45%)
  • Foreign body ingestion / Trauma (27%)
  • Dental caries / Infection
  • Diabetes Mellitus (1/3 of adult cases)
  • Adenotonsillectomy history

Diagnosis & Imaging

Investigation Key Findings
CT Neck (Contrast)
Gold Standard		 Ring-enhancing lesion in retropharyngeal space.
Lateral Neck X-Ray Pre-vertebral space widening (>7mm at C2, >14mm at C6). Use only if CT unavailable.
Labs (FBC) Neutrophilia, raised ESR/CRP.
EUA Bulging posterior oropharyngeal wall; aspiration of pus.
Note: If airway concern, consider sedating/intubating prior to CT scan in children.

Medical Treatment

Empirical IV Antibiotics (cover Strep, Staph, Anaerobes)

Option A: Ampicillin/Sulbactam
Child: 100-200 mg/kg/day IV q6h
Adult: 1-2 g IV q6-8h
Option B: Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole
Child: Cef 50-80 mg/kg + Metro 22.5 mg/kg
Adult: Cef 1-2 g + Metro 500 mg
Option C: Clindamycin (+ Cefuroxime if needed)
Good for penicillin allergy.
Continue IV until afebrile, then switch to oral to complete 14-day course.

Differentials

Condition Discriminating Feature
Peritonsillar Abscess Medialized uvula; normal posterior pharyngeal wall.
Acute Epiglottitis More acute onset; thumb sign on x-ray; no retropharyngeal mass on CT.
Meningitis Headache, purpuric rash, positive lumbar puncture.
Retropharyngeal Lymphadenopathy Non-fluctuant; CT differentiates solid vs fluid.

Complications

Prognosis is good if caught early. Mortality 40-50% if complications develop.

  • Airway Obstruction
  • Aspiration Pneumonia
  • Mediastinitis (Spread to chest)
  • Necrotizing Fasciitis
  • Meningitis / Cervical osteomyelitis
  • Purulent pericarditis
