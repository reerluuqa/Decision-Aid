Overview

What is Otitis Externa?

Otitis externa describes diffuse inflammation of the skin and subdermis of the external ear canal, which may also involve the pinna or tympanic membrane. It is also known as 'swimmer's ear' or 'tropical ear'.

Chronic
Malignant
Acute Otitis Externa

Acute otitis externa is inflammation of less than 6 weeks duration, typically caused by bacterial infection with Pseudomonas aeruginosa or Staphylococcus aureus.

Risk Factors
Water exposure, trauma to the ear canal, use of hearing aids/ear plugs, underlying skin conditions
Chronic Otitis Externa

Chronic otitis externa is inflammation which has lasted longer than 3 months, and may be caused by fungal infection with Aspergillus species or Candida albicans.

Risk Factors
Diabetes mellitus, immunocompromise, prolonged topical antibiotic or corticosteroid use
Malignant Otitis Externa

Malignant otitis externa is a potentially life-threatening progressive infection of the external ear canal causing osteomyelitis of the temporal bone and adjacent structures.

Risk Factors
Diabetes mellitus, immunocompromise, older age, radiotherapy to the ear/head/neck, previous ear surgery

Diagnosis

Acute Otitis Externa

Symptoms:

  • Itch of the ear canal
  • Ear pain and tenderness of the tragus and/or pinna (often severe)
  • Ear discharge
  • Hearing loss due to ear canal occlusion

Signs:

  • Tenderness of the tragus and/or pinna
  • Red and oedematous ear canal
  • Debris and ear discharge contributing to swelling and canal occlusion
  • Tympanic membrane erythema
  • Cellulitis of the pinna and adjacent skin
Chronic Otitis Externa

Symptoms:

  • Constant itch in the ear
  • Mild discomfort or pain (rare)

Signs:

  • Lack of ear wax in the external ear canal
  • Dry scaly skin in the ear canal
  • Red, moist skin in the ear canal
  • Fluffy, cotton-like debris, hyphae, or dots of black debris (fungal infection)
  • Conductive hearing loss
Malignant Otitis Externa

Symptoms:

  • Unremitting disproportionate ear pain
  • Headache
  • Purulent otorrhoea
  • Fever or malaise
  • Vertigo
  • Profound conductive hearing loss

Signs:

  • Systemically unwell, high fever
  • Granulation tissue seen on the floor of the ear canal
  • Exposed bone in the ear canal
  • Ipsilateral facial nerve palsy
Assessment

History Taking:

  • Onset, nature, and severity of symptoms
  • Impact on daily functioning and quality of life
  • Possible causes or risk factors (ear trauma, hearing aids, radiotherapy)
  • Previous episodes and treatments used
  • Previous ear surgery or tympanic membrane perforation
  • History of allergic or irritant contact dermatitis
  • Comorbidities (diabetes, immunocompromise)

Examination:

  • Assess ear canal, tympanic membrane, pinna, and lymph nodes
  • Look for red, oedematous ear canal
  • Check for ear discharge (serous or purulent)
  • Look for signs of fungal infection
  • Assess for tympanic membrane perforation
  • Check surrounding skin for associated disorders
When to Take an Ear Swab
Consider arranging an ear swab for bacterial and fungal microscopy, culture, and sensitivity if there is treatment failure; severe, recurrent, or chronic infection; ear canal occlusion; or cellulitis beyond the ear canal.

Acute Otitis Externa Management

Initial Management
1
Provide Information and Support
Offer NHS patient leaflet on ear infections and explain the condition and treatment plan.
2
Advise on Self-Care Measures
  • Avoid damage to the external ear canal (no cotton buds)
  • Keep ears clean and dry
  • Avoid swimming and water sports for 7-10 days
  • Use ear plugs when swimming
  • Keep shampoo, soap, and water out of the ear
  • Consider using a hair dryer on low heat to dry the ear canal
3
Manage Underlying Causes
Address associated skin conditions or risk factors where possible.
4
Provide Analgesia
Recommend paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain relief as needed.
5
Consider Aural Toilet
Clean the external auditory canal to enable topical treatments to be applied effectively.
6
Prescribe Topical Treatment
Consider prescribing a topical antibiotic preparation with or without a topical corticosteroid for 7-14 days.
Over-the-Counter Option
Consider use of over-the-counter acetic acid 2% ear drops or spray (for people aged 12 years and older) morning, evening, and after swimming, showering, or bathing, for a maximum of 7 days.
Treatment Failure

If symptoms persist after initial management:

  • Assess for associated or alternative conditions
  • Reassess for underlying causes or risk factors
  • Ensure self-care measures are being followed
  • Ensure correct use of topical treatments
  • Consider switching from ear drops to ear spray (or vice versa)
  • If contact sensitivity suspected, switch to a preparation without aminoglycosides
  • Re-examine for ear canal occlusion, fungal infection, or other issues
  • Consider arranging an ear swab for culture and sensitivity
  • Consider oral antibiotics if infection has spread beyond the ear canal

Chronic Otitis Externa Management

Initial Management
1
Provide Information and Support
Offer NHS patient leaflet on ear infections and explain the condition and treatment plan.
2
Reinforce Self-Care Measures
Advise on measures to reduce risk of recurrent infection and avoid irritants.
3
Manage Underlying Causes
Address associated skin conditions or contact sensitivity where possible.
4
Provide Analgesia
Recommend paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain relief as needed.
5
Arrange Ear Swab
Consider arranging an ear swab for bacterial and fungal microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.
6
Consider Aural Toilet
Clean the external auditory canal to enable topical treatments to be applied effectively.
7
Prescribe Topical Treatment
  • If fungal infection: topical antifungal preparation (clotrimazole 1% solution)
  • If bacterial infection: manage as for acute otitis externa
  • If no obvious infection: topical corticosteroid preparation

When to Refer

Emergency Referral

Arrange emergency hospital admission or urgent referral to an ENT specialist if:

  • Malignant otitis externa is suspected
  • Serious complications are suspected
Routine Referral

Seek specialist advice or arrange referral to an ENT specialist if:

  • Symptoms persist despite optimal management in primary care
  • Severe infection not responding to management in primary care
  • The person is elderly, has poorly controlled diabetes, or is immunocompromised
  • External ear canal occlusion preventing effective topical treatment
  • Cellulitis extending beyond the external ear canal
  • Ongoing topical treatment needed beyond 2-3 months
Dermatology Referral

Consider referral to a dermatology specialist if:

  • Contact sensitivity to neomycin or another aminoglycoside ear preparation is suspected
  • Contact sensitivity to ear plugs, hearing aids, or earrings is suspected
  • Patch testing to confirm contact sensitivities may be needed

Important Safety Information

Topical aminoglycoside preparations are contraindicated in people with a perforated tympanic membrane due to the risk of ototoxicity. Prescribe a non-ototoxic preparation if the person has a known or suspected perforation of the tympanic membrane, including a tympanostomy tube in situ.

How to Administer Ear Drops
  1. If possible, ask the person to allow another person to administer the drops
  2. Warm the drops to body temperature by holding the bottle in hands for a few minutes
  3. The person should lie down with the ear to be treated uppermost
  4. The ear canal should be filled with ear drops
  5. Gently pulling and pushing the ear helps to let air out of, and liquid into, the ear canal
  6. The person should remain in this position for 3-5 minutes
  7. If unable to lie still, a small cotton plug covered with petroleum jelly can help retain the drops
  8. The ear canal should be left open to dry
