Halitosis Management Algorithm

A rapid reference guide based on NICE CKS, last revised September 2024.

1. Initial Assessment: Patient Reports Halitosis

  • History: Assess patient perception, lifestyle (diet, smoking), oral hygiene routine, medical history, and medications.
  • Objective Breath Test (Organoleptic): Smell exhaled breath from mouth (nose pinched) and then from nose (mouth closed).
  • Oral Examination: Inspect teeth, gums, oral cavity, and tongue for coating, caries, or lesions.

2. Diagnostic Pathway: Is halitosis objectively detected?

→ NO

(After 2-3 separate assessments)

Suspect Pseudo-halitosis or Halitophobia
  • Provide reassurance and explain that no malodour is present.
  • Advise on standard oral hygiene measures.
  • If distress persists, consider referral for psychological therapy.

→ YES

Determine the Origin

A) Odour from MOUTH: Suspect Intra-Oral Cause (85-90%). Proceed to Section 3.

B) Odour from NOSE: Suspect Nasal/Sinus Cause. Proceed to Section 4.

C) Equal odour MOUTH & NOSE: Suspect Systemic/Extra-Oral Cause (Rare). Proceed to Section 4.

3. Management of Intra-Oral Halitosis

Step 1: Advise on Core Oral Hygiene & Lifestyle Changes

  • ✔
    Mechanical Cleaning: Daily tongue scraping, twice-daily brushing (spit, don't rinse), and daily interdental cleaning.
  • ✔
    Lifestyle Modification: Stop smoking, limit alcohol, stay hydrated, and avoid causative foods.
  • ✔
    Dental Health: Advise routine dental appointment for assessment and cleaning. Ensure proper denture hygiene.

Step 2: If Halitosis Persists

  • ➡
    Strongly recommend a dental visit to rule out underlying dental disease.
  • ➡
    Consider a trial of an over-the-counter antibacterial mouthwash (e.g., chlorhexidine).

4. Management of Suspected Extra-Oral Halitosis & Referral

Identify potential cause based on symptoms and refer appropriately:

  • ➡
    ENT: For suspected chronic sinusitis, recurrent tonsillitis, or postnasal drip.
  • ➡
    Gastroenterology: For suspected GORD or other GI conditions.
  • ➡
    Respiratory: For suspected lower respiratory tract infection or bronchiectasis.
  • ➡
    Endocrinology: For suspected metabolic causes (e.g., uncontrolled diabetes).

URGENT REFERRAL & RED FLAGS

Arrange URGENT (2-week wait) referral to ENT / Oral Medicine specialist for:

  • ⚠Unexplained oral ulceration lasting more than 3 weeks.
  • ⚠A persistent lump in the oral cavity.
  • ⚠A red, or red and white, patch in the oral cavity.

Advise URGENT Dental Appointment for:

  • ⚠Suspected acute necrotizing ulcerative gingivitis (painful, bleeding gums, ulcers, fever).

Consider URGENT referral for suspected Haematological Malignancy if:

  • ⚠Unexplained or atypical enlargement of the gums.
