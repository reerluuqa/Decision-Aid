Recurrent Aphthous Stomatitis

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice (Aug 2022)

Treatment Overview

Stepwise Approach: 1st Line → 2nd Line → 3rd Line → Specialist Referral

Start with simple measures, escalate based on response and severity

Assessment & History

Key History Points

  • Age of Onset: Usually childhood/adolescence, 80% before 30 years
  • Pattern: Episodic/cyclical nature, frequency, duration of ulcer-free periods
  • Family History: 42% have first-degree relatives with RAS
  • Triggers: Trauma, foods (chocolate, peanuts, coffee, gluten), menstrual cycle
  • Smoking Status: Most patients are non-smokers; ulcers may start after cessation
  • Medications: NSAIDs, nicorandil, ACE inhibitors, anticonvulsants
  • Systemic Symptoms: Fever, weight loss, GI symptoms (suggests systemic cause)

Clinical Examination

Minor Ulcers (75-85%)

< 10mm diameter, non-keratinised mucosa, heal in 7-10 days without scarring

Major Ulcers (10-15%)

> 10mm diameter, any oral site, heal in 10-40 days, may scar

Herpetiform Ulcers (Rare)

5-100 pinhead ulcers, coalesce, extremely painful, frequent recurrences

Red Flags

Urgent Referral Indicators

  • Solitary ulcer >3 weeks duration (suspect malignancy)
  • Associated systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
  • Genital or ocular ulceration (suspect Behcet's)
  • Immunodeficiency (HIV, neutropenia)
  • Severe pain preventing intake

Investigations

  • FBC & Haematinics: Iron, ferritin, folate, B12 (if deficiency suspected)
  • Coeliac Screen: IgA-tissue transglutaminase
  • HIV Test: If risk factors or immunodeficiency signs
  • ESR/CRP: If autoinflammatory state suspected
  • Biopsy: If malignant ulcer or immunobullous disease suspected

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria for RAS

Diagnosis is clinical - based on history and examination after excluding systemic causes

Typical RAS Features
  • Recurrent round/ovoid ulcers with erythematous margins and greyish-yellow base
  • Onset in childhood/adolescence
  • Involvement of non-keratinised mucosa (minors)
  • No systemic symptoms (afebrile)
  • No genital or ocular ulceration
  • No immunodeficiency history

Aphthous-like Ulceration (ALU)

Consider when ulcers are associated with systemic disease:

Check for Systemic Associations
  • Haematinic Deficiencies: Iron, folate, B12 deficiency
  • IBD: Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, coeliac disease
  • Autoinflammatory: Behcet's syndrome, PFAPA, MAGIC syndrome
  • Immunodeficiency: HIV/AIDS, cyclic neutropenia
  • Medications: NSAIDs, nicorandil, beta-blockers

Differential Diagnosis

  • Herpes Simplex: Vesicles precede ulcers, communicable
  • Traumatic Ulcer: Single ulcer at site of trauma
  • Hand-Foot-Mouth: Children, rash on palms/soles
  • Syphilis: Chancre, mucous patches, gummas
  • Malignancy: Persistent, indurated, solitary ulcer
  • Pemphigus: Blistering disease with oral ulcers

Diagnostic Differentiation

Key Distinctions

RAS: Idiopathic, childhood onset, no systemic disease

ALU: Secondary to systemic condition, investigate accordingly

Major ulcers: Size >10mm, keratinised mucosa involvement

Herpetiform: Multiple pinhead ulcers, coalescing pattern

Treatment Algorithm

1st Line - Simple Measures

Lifestyle & Trigger Management

  • Change to SLS-free toothpaste
  • Avoid trigger foods (acidic, spicy, trauma)
  • Address dental trauma (sharp teeth, appliances)
  • Stop smoking if applicable

Symptom Relief

Lidocaine 2% Viscous Solution
Adults: 15mL swish/spit q3h PRN (max 8 doses/day)
Children: 1.25mL q3h PRN (max 4 doses/12h)
Benzydamine 0.15%
Adults & ≥13yr: 15mL mouthwash q1.5-3h PRN
≥12yr spray: 4-8 sprays q1.5-3h PRN

Adjunctive Therapies

Chlorhexidine 0.12% Mouthwash
10-15mL twice daily (brush teeth first to prevent staining)
Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin)
Adults: 1000mcg orally once daily (sublingual if available)
Effective even without deficiency
Iron (if deficient)
Ferrous sulfate: Adults 65-130mg elemental iron daily; Children 3mg/kg daily

2nd Line - Topical Corticosteroids

Mainstay Treatment

Indication

Use if simple measures fail or for moderate-severe disease

Triamcinolone Acetonide 0.1% Dental Paste
Adults: Apply to ulcers 2-3 times daily after meals & at bedtime
Children: Consult specialist
Betamethasone Soluble Tablets
≥12yr: 500mcg dissolved in 20mL water, rinse 4 times daily (spit out)
Hydrocortisone Buccal Tablets
≥12yr: 2.5mg dissolved slowly in mouth 4 times daily

Pediatric Considerations

Limited licensed options. Use off-label only if child can spit out medication (usually >6 years)

3rd Line - Systemic Therapy

Specialist Supervision Required
Prednisolone
Adults: 30-60mg daily for 7 days, then taper over 7 days
Children: 0.5-1mg/kg/day for 7 days, then taper
Colchicine
Adults: 0.5mg 2-3 times daily (first-line immunomodulator)
Weak evidence but used in Behcet's syndrome

4th Line - Specialist Referral

Specialist Only

Severe/Refractory Cases

  • Thalidomide: Most effective for severe RAS, extreme caution required
  • Azathioprine: Immunomodulator for refractory cases
  • Biologics: TNF-alpha antagonists (etanercept, adalimumab) - limited data

Thalidomide Requirements

Teratogenic - strict contraception required. Not used in children. Specialist prescription only with monitoring program.

Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring

  • No specific monitoring for most patients on topical therapy
  • Systemic therapy: Monitor according to drug (FBC, LFTs, etc.)
  • Biologics: Infection screening and monitoring
  • Thalidomide: Pregnancy testing, contraception compliance

Complications

Scarring

Long-term complication, mainly with major aphthae. Likelihood: Low

Oral Candidiasis

From prolonged topical corticosteroid use. Use for shortest term possible.

Tooth Staining

From chlorhexidine use. Brush teeth before use to prevent staining.

Prognosis

Natural History

Spontaneous resolution with age is common. Frequency and severity typically decrease over time.

  • Most patients benefit from topical therapy during flares
  • Avoidance of triggers reduces frequency
  • Small proportion require aggressive immunomodulatory treatment
  • Quality of life impact significant during active disease

Patient Education

  • Avoid oral trauma - seek dental treatment if needed
  • Identify and avoid personal trigger foods
  • Avoid acidic foods and drinks during active ulcers
  • Dilute chlorhexidine 50/50 if pain on rinsing
  • Return if ulcers persist >3 weeks or systemic symptoms develop
