Epistaxis (Nosebleeds)

Clinical Knowledge Summary • Last Revised: Oct 2024
IMMEDIATE A&E TRANSFER if signs of haemodynamic compromise (tachycardia, syncope, pallor), posterior bleed, or major haemorrhage.

Immediate Admit / Referral to A&E

  • Haemodynamic compromise: Tachycardia, syncope, pallor, lightheadedness.
  • Posterior Bleed: Profuse, bilateral, source unseen on speculum exam.
  • Patient Factors: Child < 2 years, frail/elderly, anticoagulant use with difficult bleed.
  • Failed Primary Care: Bleeding persists > 10-15 mins pressure & no facilities for cautery/packing.

First Aid (Do This First)

Sit forward, mouth open. Lean forward to drain blood out mouth, not stomach.
  1. Pinch soft part of nose (cartilage, not bone).
  2. Hold pressure continuously for 10–15 mins.
  3. Avoid lying flat (unless fainting).
  4. Avoid blowing/picking nose afterwards.
Success? Observe 30 mins. If discharged, avoid hot drinks/alcohol/strenuous activity for 24h.

Major Haemorrhage Update

Tranexamic Acid (New Oct 2024)

Consider 1 gram oral dose for major haemorrhage while awaiting transfer. Use according to local protocol.

Supported by UK guideline & expert opinion.

Nasal Cautery

Indication: Bleeding point visible & can be tolerated (usually >4 yrs).

  • Anesthetize (Lidocaine + Phenylephrine). Wait 3-4 mins.
  • Ask person to blow nose to clear clots (may restart bleed).
  • Lightly apply silver nitrate for 3-10s until grey-white.
  • CRITICAL: Only cauterize ONE side of septum to avoid perforation.

Nasal Packing

Indication: Cautery ineffective or point unseen.

  • Insert packs (Merocel/Rapid Rhino) according to instructions.
  • Admit to Hospital if pack inserted in primary care.
  • Avoid Ribbon Gauze unless specifically trained.
  • Check oropharynx for bleeding behind pack.

Stopping Recurrence

After bleeding stops:

  • Naseptin® (Chlorhexidine/Neomycin): Apply 4x daily for 10 days.
  • Contraindication: Neomycin/Peanut/Soya allergy.
  • Alternative: Mupirocin nasal ointment (2-3x daily, 5-7 days).
  • Note: Naspetin® reformulated (no arachis oil), but check supply.

Primary Assessment

History
  • Duration & estimated blood loss (Cups ≈ 250ml).
  • Side of bleed & history of previous episodes.
  • Trauma / Picking / Surgery?
  • Medications: Anticoagulants, Antiplatelets, Nasal sprays.
Examination
  • PPE: Gown, mask, gloves, eye protection.
  • Check ABCs if major bleed.
  • Examine with speculum & good light.
  • Look for bleeding point (small red dot <1mm).
  • Suspect posterior if profuse/bilateral.

Differential & Causes

Category Key Triggers
Local Trauma Picking, fracture, foreign body, septal perforation, surgery, oxygen prongs (drying).
Inflammation Sinusitis, allergic rhinitis, polyps.
Systemic Drugs Warfarin, DOACs, Aspirin, Clopidogrel, NSAIDs, Cocaine.
Haematological Leukaemia, Haemophilia, Von Willebrand, Thrombocytopenia, Liver disease.
Vascular/Tumour Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia, Nasal tumours (obstruction/pain/hearing loss).
Environment Dry air, cold temperature, altitude.

Investigations

Not routine. Only if underlying cause suspected:

  • FBC: If heavy/recurrent bleed or anemia suspected.
  • Coagulation/INR: If clotting disorder suspected or on Warfarin.
  • Group & Save: If major haemorrhage.

Serious Differentials

Posterior drainage can mimic:

  • Haemoptysis: (Cough, dyspnoea, wheeze).
  • Haematemesis: (Abdo pain, melaena).

Definition

Recurrent epistaxis is common in children (~9%) but less common in >15s.

Context: Often due to digital trauma (picking) or dry mucosa.

Management Strategy

1. Address Precipitants
  • Advise against picking nose.
  • Keep nails short.
  • Humidify home environment.
  • Use saline sprays/gels for dryness.
2. Treat Vestibulitis
  • Naseptin® cream 4x daily for 10 days.
  • Alternative: Mupirocin.
3. When to Refer
  • Frequent heavy bleeds causing anemia.
  • Suspected Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia (family history/telangiectasia).
  • Failed medical management.

Classification

Anterior (80-95%) Little's area (Kiesselbach plexus). Common in children/young adults. Usually resolves with pressure/cautery.
Posterior (5-10%) Sphenopalatine artery branches. Older people. Profuse, bilateral. Admit.

Prognosis

  • Self-limiting: Most episodes are harmless.
  • Mortality: Rare. Associated with hypovolaemia or comorbidities (coronary disease, severe HTN).

Complications

  • Of Bleeding: Anemia, Hypovolaemia, Death, Recalcitrant bleeding.
  • Of Packing: Sinusitis, Septal hematoma/abscess, Toxic shock syndrome, Apnoea/Hypoxia.
  • Of Cautery: Septal perforation (if both sides cauterized).
