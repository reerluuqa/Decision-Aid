Aphthous Ulcer Management Guide

A comprehensive clinical algorithm for diagnosis, assessment, and management of aphthous ulcers

Definition & Types

Aphthous Ulcers
Erythematous, small, round/oval oral ulcers with circumscribed margins, typically presenting in childhood/adolescence and not associated with systemic disease.
Types
  • Minor (85%): <1 cm diameter, heal in 7-10 days, minimal scarring
  • Major (10%): 1-3 cm diameter, heal in 10-40 days, often scar
  • Herpetiform (5%): 1-2 mm grouped lesions, extremely painful, may coalesce

Assessment

History
  • Duration of ulceration
  • Family history (40% have affected relatives)
  • Smoking status (more common in non-smokers)
  • Potential triggers (foods, stress, trauma)
Examination
  • Location, size, and appearance of ulcers
  • Check for local trauma (sharp teeth, dentures)
  • Assess for systemic signs (fever, lymphadenopathy)

Investigations

If Diagnosis Uncertain
  • FBC, ferritin, folate, B12
  • IgA-tTG (coeliac disease)
  • Viral serology (HIV, EBV)
  • ESR/CRP (systemic inflammation)

Differential Diagnosis

Urgent Referral
Solitary ulcer persisting >3 weeks (possible malignancy)
Systemic Conditions
  • Nutritional deficiencies (B12, folate, iron)
  • Coeliac disease, Crohn's, ulcerative colitis
  • Behçet's, Reiter's, immunodeficiency
Other Conditions
  • Herpes simplex
  • Drug reactions
  • Hand-foot-mouth disease

Management

1Conservative Measures
  • Avoid trigger factors
  • Soft toothbrush
  • Avoid sodium lauryl sulfate toothpaste
  • Dental treatment for local trauma
2Mild-Moderate Symptoms
  • Topical anesthetics (lidocaine)
  • Topical analgesics (benzydamine)
  • Topical antimicrobials (chlorhexidine, doxycycline rinses)
3Moderate-Severe Symptoms
  • Topical corticosteroids:
    • Hydrocortisone oromucosal tablets
    • Beclomethasone spray (off-license)
    • Betamethasone soluble tablets
4Severe Recurrent Ulcers
  • Short course systemic prednisolone
  • Vitamin B12 supplement

Referral Indications

  • Ulcer persisting >3 weeks (urgent, possible malignancy)
  • Severe ulceration unresponsive to topical/systemic corticosteroids
  • Suspected underlying systemic disease
  • Atypical presentation (onset later in life, atypical sites, extra-oral involvement)
