üìã Overview & Definition
What is it? Benign
A histologically benign, generally slow-growing tumour from the vestibular nerve (8th cranial nerve).
Common Presentation
  • Progressive unilateral sensorineural hearing loss (most common).
  • Tinnitus (ringing).
  • Intermittent dizziness/imbalance.
Epidemiology
  • Incidence: 1-4 per 100,000/year.
  • ~95% are sporadic (unilateral).
  • ~5% associated with Neurofibromatosis 2 (NF2).

Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)

Medical emergency. Do NOT assume recovery excludes VS. Immediate MRI required.

Large Tumor Signs
Indications of brainstem compression or obstructive hydrocephalus:
  • Headaches
  • Ataxia / Gait disturbances
  • Facial numbness/pain (Trigeminal involvement)
  • Diplopia / Nystagmus
Late Neurological Signs
Suggest significant mass effect:
  • Facial weakness (Usually late sign)
  • Dysphagia (Swallowing difficulties)
  • Papilloedema (signs of increased ICP)

üîç Differentials
Condition Key Differentiator Imaging Clue
Meningioma Hearing loss less prominent. Eccentric to IAC, dural tail, hyperostosis.
Epidermoid Cyst Congenital, less prominent hearing loss. Restricted diffusion on DWI MRI.
Facial Nerve Schwannoma Facial paresis/spasm is prominent (>50%). Enhancement extends into labyrinthine segment/geniculate ganglion.
Trigeminal Schwannoma Facial numbness prominent, less severe hearing loss. Dumbbell shape spanning posterior/middle fossa (Meckel's cave).
‚öñÔ∏è Management Algorithm

Decision depends on size, age, hearing status, and patient preference.

Small Tumor (< 1.5 cm) Observe
  • Initial: Observation (Active Surveillance) is most common.
  • Approx 50% show no growth in 3-5 years.
  • If Growth: Consider Radiosurgery or Surgery (esp. in younger patients).
  • Hearing Preservation: Attempted if baseline hearing is good.
Medium Tumor (1.5 - 3 cm) Treat
  • Primary: Up-front treatment usually recommended.
  • Options: Radiosurgery or Microsurgery.
  • Radiosurgery: Lower immediate risk, but hearing loss may still progress.
  • Surgery: Ensures complete resection.
Large Tumor (> 3 cm) Surgery
  • Primary: Surgery is standard of care.
  • Goal: Relieve mass effect on brainstem.
  • Hearing: Preservation rarely attempted.
  • Approaches: Translabyrinthine or Retrosigmoid.
Surveillance Schedule (Observation)
6 Months: First MRI
Year 1-5: Yearly MRI
After 5 Years: Every 2 Years
