Assessment
‚ñº
üìã History Taking

Key Diagnostic Factors:

  • Quality, duration, and frequency of tinnitus
  • Associated hearing loss or vertigo
  • Noise exposure history
  • Medication review (aspirin, NSAIDs, aminoglycosides)
  • Pulsatile vs non-pulsatile characteristics
  • Unilateral vs bilateral presentation
  • Impact on quality of life and sleep

Red Flag Features Urgent Referral

  • Unilateral persistent tinnitus (>6 months)
  • Pulsatile tinnitus with vascular symptoms
  • Associated focal neurological deficits
  • Sudden sensorineural hearing loss
  • Severe vertigo with neurological signs
üîç Physical Examination

Essential Components:

  • Complete head and neck examination
  • Auscultation for bruits (neck, mastoid, ear canal)
  • Otoscopy (cerumen, mass, perforation, effusion)
  • Tuning fork tests (Weber's & Rinne's)
  • Cranial nerve examination (V, VIII, IX, X, XI)

Special Findings:

  • Glomus tumor: Red mass behind intact TM (Brown's sign)
  • Otosclerosis: Red hue over promontory (Schwartze's sign)
  • Palatal myoclonus: Clicking sound with chewing
‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors:

  • Older age (40-70 years)
  • Hearing loss (11% increase per 10dB PTA increase)
  • Loud noise exposure (1.6x higher risk)
  • Vestibular schwannoma

Moderate Risk Factors:

  • Ototoxic medications
  • Cerebrovascular disease
  • Head trauma
  • Depression/anxiety

Diagnosis
‚ñº
üß™ Investigations

First-Line Tests:

üéµ Audiometry

Pure-tone thresholds, speech discrimination, tympanometry. Essential for all patients.

ü©∏ FBC

For pulsatile tinnitus to exclude anemia-induced hyperdynamic circulation.

ü¶ã Tympanometry

Assess middle ear function and Eustachian tube patency.

Secondary Tests When Indicated:

üß≤ MRI Head

Unilateral SNHL or pulsatile tinnitus. Gold standard for vestibular schwannoma detection.

üéØ CT Temporal Bones

Conductive hearing loss, suspected glomus tumor, or middle ear abnormalities.

üåä MR/CT Angiography

Pulsatile tinnitus to evaluate AVMs, aneurysms, or carotid stenosis.

üß† Auditory Brainstem Response

Unilateral/asymmetric symptoms when MRI contraindicated. False negative rate 22% for small schwannomas.

üéØ Key Differentials
üåÄ Meniere's Disease Episodic

Features: Tinnitus + vertigo + aural fullness + fluctuating hearing loss. Usually unilateral.

üé™ Acoustic Neuroma MRI Required

Features: Unilateral tinnitus + SNHL. May cause facial numbness if large.

üíé Glomus Tumor Pulsatile

Features: Red mass behind TM, pulsatile tinnitus in 60% of cases.

ü¶¥ Otosclerosis Conductive

Features: Conductive hearing loss, tinnitus in 75%, Schwartze's sign.

üìä Severity Classification

Tinnitus Severity Index (TSI):

  • Score 0-45 based on impact on daily life
  • Higher score = more bothersome tinnitus
  • Not correlated with loudness in decibels

Tinnitus Handicap Inventory (THI):

  • Categorizes: None, Mild, Moderate, Severe
  • Assesses functional, emotional, and catastrophic impact

Treatment
‚ñº
üéØ Management Approach
1

Treat Underlying Cause First Step

Address identifiable causes (medication, infection, vascular lesions, metabolic disorders) as this may resolve tinnitus without further treatment.

2

Education & Counseling All Patients

Explain pathophysiology, prognosis, and that goal is habituation, not cure. Discuss coping strategies and stress reduction techniques.

üß† Symptomatic Treatments

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) Evidence-Based

  • Reduces severity and impairment regardless of tinnitus severity
  • Improves quality of life and depression scores
  • Does not significantly decrease subjective loudness
  • 12-month follow-up shows sustained benefit

Hearing Aids With Hearing Loss

  • Success rate: ~50% partial or total relief
  • Amplifies external sounds, reducing tinnitus awareness
  • Can combine with sound enrichment
  • Advise wearing even during sleep

Sound Therapy & Masking Variable Response

  • Background noise reduces perception in silent environments
  • Tinnitus maskers: behind-ear devices generating white noise
  • Neuromonics: music + acoustic neural stimulus
  • 30-45% of patients have loudness intolerance

Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT) 1-2 Year Commitment

  • Counseling + low-level white noise devices
  • Promotes habituation to tinnitus
  • Requires 1-2 years for effectiveness
  • Statistically significant improvement in TSI scores
üíä Pharmacological Options

For Associated Depression/Anxiety Limited Evidence

Nortriptyline (TCA)

Dose: 25-50mg at bedtime. Treats underlying psychological issues rather than tinnitus directly.

Alprazolam

Dose: 0.5mg at bedtime, titrate up. 76% reduction in tinnitus intensity but risk of dependence.

Paroxetine (SSRI)

Dose: 10-50mg daily. Improves severity and aggravation scores.

‚ö†Ô∏è Poor evidence for use in tinnitus without associated depression/anxiety

üî¨ Emerging Therapies
üåø Ginkgo Biloba Controversial

Modest effect in some trials but Cochrane review shows no difference vs placebo. Low risk, may try for mild-moderate distress.

‚ö° Electrical Stimulation

Direct current to mastoid/zygoma shows 53-83% relief. May increase hearing thresholds. TMS shows mixed results.

ü¶ª Cochlear Implantation

Alleviates tinnitus in up to 80% of users with severe-profound SNHL. Consider for bilateral severe loss.

üíâ AM-101 (NMDA Antagonist)

Intratympanic injections blocking cochlear NMDA receptors. Phase 1/2 trials show safety and early efficacy.

üö´ Avoidance Strategies
  • ‚ùå Loud noise exposure (use ear protection)
  • ‚ùå Ototoxic medications (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides)
  • ‚ùå Caffeine, alcohol, simple sugars, MSG, aspartame
  • ‚ùå Tobacco use
  • ‚úÖ 6-8 week trial of dietary modification

Follow-up
‚ñº
üîç Monitoring

Annual Audiometry Routine

  • Evaluate for asymmetry or progression of hearing loss
  • If asymmetrical loss develops ‚Üí MRI to rule out retrocochlear pathology
  • Monitor effectiveness of interventions

Symptom Tracking:

  • Tinnitus Severity Index (TSI) scores
  • Tinnitus Handicap Inventory (THI) categories
  • Impact on daily functioning and quality of life
‚ö†Ô∏è Potential Complications
üò¥ Sleep Deprivation 5% of Patients

Assessment: Sleep diary, polysomnography, mood assessment. Treatment: CBT, biofeedback, background noise, tinnitus maskers at night.

üòû Depression Screen Regularly

Risk: Sleep deprivation, activity avoidance, concentration problems. Action: Counseling, therapy, pharmacotherapy. Hospitalize if suicidal ideation.

üò∞ Anxiety Common

Mechanism: Internal sound awareness ‚Üí worrying ‚Üí stress ‚Üí intensified perception. Management: Relaxation techniques, CBT, anxiolytics when appropriate.

üîÆ Prognosis
  • Treatable causes (vestibular schwannoma, otosclerosis, otitis) may resolve with appropriate treatment
  • Most cases require habituation and psychological support rather than cure
  • Goal is reducing disease impact and improving quality of life
  • Early intervention with CBT and sound therapy improves long-term outcomes
  • Regular follow-up essential for monitoring progression and complications

Patient Education Points:

  • Cure is unlikely; focus on habituation
  • Hyperacusis may benefit from desensitization or ear plugs
  • Background sound helpful in silent environments
  • Staying busy distracts from tinnitus perception
  • Support groups and information resources available
