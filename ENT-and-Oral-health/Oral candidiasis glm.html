Oral Candidiasis Clinical Reference

Comprehensive Guide for Healthcare Professionals

Definition

Fungal infection of the oral cavity caused by Candida species (most commonly C. albicans).

Common Types & Clinical Features

Type Key Features
Pseudomembranous (oral thrush) White/yellow plaques that can be removed, revealing red base
Erythematous (atrophic) Redness, soreness, especially on palate and tongue
Denture stomatitis Redness in denture-bearing area
Angular cheilitis Redness, fissuring at mouth corners
Hyperplastic White plaques not easily removed
Median rhomboid glossitis Central red area on tongue

Risk Factors

Extremes of age (infants, elderly)
Immunocompromise (HIV, cancer, chemotherapy)
Diabetes mellitus
Antibiotics and corticosteroids use
Dental prostheses
Poor dental hygiene
Smoking
Nutritional deficiencies

Assessment Process

Step 1: Patient History

  • Symptoms (pain, burning, dysphagia)
  • Duration and recurrence pattern
  • Risk factors (medications, medical conditions)

Step 2: Clinical Examination

  • Type and extent of oral lesions
  • Signs of systemic involvement
  • Assessment of dentures/oral hygiene

Step 3: Consider Underlying Conditions

  • Unexplained or recurrent infection may indicate:
  • Undiagnosed diabetes
  • Immunocompromise (HIV, hematologic malignancy)
  • Nutritional deficiencies

Management Decision Path

Initial Assessment

  • Treat with miconazole oral gel or nystatin suspension
  • Consider fluconazole if severe or no response to topical
  • Refer if no response after 2 weeks or recurrent episodes

Adult on Immunosuppressive Treatment

  • Consider systemic therapy (fluconazole) earlier
  • Seek specialist advice

Adult HIV Positive

  • Usually requires systemic therapy (fluconazole)
  • Seek specialist advice

Treatment Options

First-line: Miconazole Oral Gel

  • Dose: 2.5 mL (1/2 teaspoon) to affected areas 4 times daily
  • Continue for at least 7 days after lesions heal/symptoms resolve
  • Contraindicated in children <4 months (unlicensed)
  • CAUTION: Significant drug interactions (e.g., warfarin)

Alternative: Nystatin Suspension

  • Dose: 1 mL (100,000 units) 4 times daily
  • Continue for at least 7 days after lesions heal/symptoms resolve
  • Unlicensed in neonates

Second-line: Fluconazole

  • Dose: 50-100mg daily for 7-14 days
  • For severe cases or when topical treatment fails
  • Adjust dose if renal impairment

General Advice

Preventive Measures & Lifestyle Advice

  • Oral hygiene: Regular brushing, flossing, and dental check-ups
  • Inhaled corticosteroid users: Rinse mouth after use, consider spacer device
  • Denture wearers: Remove at night, clean regularly, ensure proper fit
  • Smoking cessation: Strongly encourage quitting
  • Diabetes management: Optimize glycemic control
  • Nutrition: Address nutritional deficiencies if present

Red Flags - Refer/Admit

Urgent Referral or Admission Indications

  • Systemic illness (candidaemia)
  • Difficulty/painful swallowing (suggests oesophageal involvement)
  • Failure to respond to 2 weeks of appropriate treatment
  • Recurrent episodes
  • Suspicion of immunocompromise
  • Infants <4 months with severe infection

Differential Diagnosis

Erythema migrans

Geographic tongue; central erythema with white borders

Hairy tongue

Elongated filiform papillae; white to black discoloration

Leukoplakia

White patch that cannot be rubbed off; may be premalignant

Lichen planus

White lacy striations or erosive lesions

Oral cancer

Persistent ulcer/lesion; may be erythroplakia or leukoplakia

Kaposi's sarcoma

Red/purple patches or nodules; associated with HIV

Chemical/thermal burns

History of exposure; pain and erythema

Amalgam tattoo

Localized pigmentation; iatrogenic from dental work
