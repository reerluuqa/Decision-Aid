Oral Herpes Simplex

Clinical Reference Algorithm
Last updated: May 2024

Diagnosis

Herpes Labialis (Cold Sores)

  • Prodrome: Pain, burning, tingling (6-48 hrs before lesions)
  • Vesicles at lip mucocutaneous junction (usually lower lip)
  • Vesicles → rupture → ulcers → crust → heal (usually no scarring)

Gingivostomatitis

  • Prodrome: Fever, malaise, sore throat, lymphadenopathy
  • Painful vesicles on oral/pharyngeal mucosa
  • Vesicles → ulcers (may have yellow/grey membranes)
  • More common in primary infection

Assessment

Key Evaluation Points

  • Location, frequency, duration of lesions
  • Associated symptoms (pain, fever)
  • Trigger factors (UV light, stress, fatigue, fever, immunosuppression)

Red Flags

  • Ulceration >3 weeks
  • Suspicious lump on lip or in oral cavity
  • Red or red-and-white patches in oral cavity

Referral Criteria

Admit If:

  • Unable to swallow (dehydration risk)
  • Immunocompromised with severe infection
  • Suspected serious complications

Urgent Referral (2 Week):

  • Red flags suggesting oral cancer

Specialist Advice/Referral:

  • Immunocompromised with recurrent infection
  • Pregnant (near term) with primary infection
  • Frequent (≥6/year), persistent, or severe episodes
  • Herpes-associated erythema multiforme
  • Lesions refractory to oral antivirals after 5-7 days
  • Atypical lesions or uncertain diagnosis

Treatment

Symptom Relief

  • Paracetamol/ibuprofen for pain/fever
  • Adequate hydration

Topical Treatments (OTC)

  • Antivirals (aciclovir/penciclovir) - may help if used early
  • Analgesics/anaesthetics - some not licensed for children

Oral Antivirals (Consider For)

Indication Recommendation
Primary infection Consider Start at prodrome, ≥5 days
Severe, frequent, or recurrent herpes labialis Consider Start at prodrome, ≥5 days
Recurrent gingivostomatitis Consider Start at prodrome, ≥5 days
Immunocompromised patients Strongly Consider Start at prodrome, ≥5 days
Drug Options Aciclovir or Valaciclovir (Famciclovir not licensed)

Self-Care Advice

  • Reassure: Usually self-limiting, heal without scarring
  • Pain relief: Regular paracetamol/ibuprofen
  • Maintain hydration
  • OTC topical preparations if helpful

Prevention

  • Avoid triggers where possible
  • Avoid direct contact with lesions
  • Avoid sharing utensils, towels
  • Hand hygiene after touching lesions
  • For immunocompromised or frequent recurrences: consider prophylactic antivirals (specialist advice)

This reference is intended for healthcare professionals working within the NHS in the UK. Always use clinical judgment and refer to full guidelines when making treatment decisions.
