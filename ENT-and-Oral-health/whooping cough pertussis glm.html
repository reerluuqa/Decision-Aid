Whooping Cough (Pertussis) - Quick Clinical Reference

Last updated: October 2024
EMERGENCY INDICATORS - Hospital Admission Required
Significant breathing difficulties (apnoea, severe paroxysms, cyanosis)
Seizures or encephalopathy
Pneumonia (secondary bacterial infection)
Children aged 6 months or younger (high risk group)
Severe dehydration or malnutrition
Arrange appropriate isolation before admission
Diagnosis Criteria
Suspect whooping cough if:
  • Acute cough lasting ≥14 days without apparent cause PLUS one or more:
    • Paroxysmal cough
    • Inspiratory 'whoop'
    • Post-tussive vomiting
    • Apnoeic attacks (in infants)
  • OR contact with confirmed case within 21 days
Confirmation Tests:
Test When to Use
Culture/PCR Cough <21 days (all ages)
Serology Adults >16y, cough >14d
Oral Fluid Test Children 2-16y, cough >14d
Clinical Phases
Catarrhal Phase (1-2 weeks)
  • Rhinorrhoea, malaise, mild cough
  • Low-grade fever (uncommon)
  • Most infectious stage
Paroxysmal Phase (1-6 weeks, up to 10)
  • Violent coughing fits with 'whoop'
  • Post-tussive vomiting
  • Night-time attacks (15/24h average)
Convalescent Phase (2-3 weeks)
  • Gradual improvement
  • Paroxysms may recur with subsequent infections
Management Protocol
Immediate Actions:
Notify local UKHSA health protection team within 3 days
Arrange lab confirmation based on age/symptom duration
Assess for hospital admission (see emergency indicators)
Self-Care Advice:
Rest and adequate fluid intake
Paracetamol/ibuprofen for symptomatic relief
Exclusion Requirements:
Children: Exclude until 48 hours after appropriate antibiotics OR 14 days from cough onset if untreated
Healthcare workers (infant care): Exclude until 48 hours after antibiotics OR 21 days from cough onset if untreated
Other staff: Exclude until 48 hours after antibiotics OR 14 days from cough onset if untreated
Antibiotic Treatment
Important: Prescribe antibiotics if cough onset within previous 14 days (or up to 21 days if household contact in priority group)
Patient Group First-line Treatment Alternative
Infants <1 month Clarithromycin Azithromycin (limited data)
Children ≥1 month Azithromycin or Clarithromycin Co-trimoxazole (if >6 weeks)
Non-pregnant adults Azithromycin or Clarithromycin Co-trimoxazole
Pregnant women Erythromycin Azithromycin (2nd line), Clarithromycin (3rd line)
Important Cautions:
Do NOT prescribe co-trimoxazole to pregnant women or infants <6 weeks
Concomitant treatment with clarithromycin and ivabradine is contra-indicated
Caution when co-administering clarithromycin with edoxaban
Infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis is a possible adverse effect of azithromycin in neonates
Prevention & Vaccination
Vaccination Schedule:
Pregnant women: 16-32 weeks (ideally 20 weeks)
Infants: 6-in-1 vaccine at 8, 12, 16 weeks
Pre-school: 4-in-1 booster at 3 years 4 months
Post-Exposure Prophylaxis:
Offer antibiotics to close household contacts within 21 days of exposure if:
  • Infants <1 year (especially <6 months)
  • Women in last month of pregnancy
  • Healthcare workers with infant contact
Key Facts:
Incubation period: 7-10 days (range 4-21 days)
Infectious period: 21 days from onset if untreated
Immunity wanes over time (no lifelong protection)
Special Considerations:
Infants <6 months: Highest risk of severe complications and death (case-fatality ratio ~1%)
Older children/adults: May present as persistent cough without classic symptoms
