Vertigo Assessment & Management Algorithm

A quick reference guide for busy clinicians

1

Confirm Vertigo

Vertigo: False sensation of movement (spinning/rotation)

Not vertigo: Light-headedness, presyncope, disequilibrium (no spinning sensation)

  • Ask about triggers: positional changes (BPPV), postural changes (orthostatic hypotension)
  • Presence of nystagmus suggests vertigo
2

History & Examination

History:

  • Duration, onset, frequency of symptoms
  • Aggravating factors (head movement?)
  • Severity and impact on daily activities
  • Associated symptoms: nausea/vomiting, otological (hearing loss, tinnitus), neurological (headache, visual disturbance)
  • Relevant history: recent URTI, migraine, head trauma, cardiovascular risk factors, medications

Examination:

  • Ear examination (discharge, vesicles, cholesteatoma)
  • Neurological exam (cranial nerves, cerebellar function)
  • Eye examination for nystagmus
  • Gait assessment
  • Specific tests: Dix-Hallpike, Head impulse test, Romberg's test, Alternate cover test
3

Differentiate Causes

Feature Peripheral Central
Postural stability Can walk (unstable) Unable to stand/walk
Hearing loss/tinnitus Common in some causes Uncommon
Neurological symptoms Absent Usually present
Nystagmus Horizontal-torsional, suppressed by fixation Direction-changing, vertical/torsional, not suppressed
Head impulse test May be positive Usually normal
Dix-Hallpike Positive in BPPV (latency, fatigue) Abnormal response if positive
4

Identify Specific Cause

Peripheral Causes

  • BPPV: Brief episodes (<1 min) triggered by head position changes; positive Dix-Hallpike
  • Vestibular neuronitis: Sudden severe vertigo lasting days, no hearing loss, positive head impulse test
  • Labyrinthitis: Similar to vestibular neuronitis but with hearing loss/tinnitus
  • Meniere's disease: Episodes lasting 30 min - hours, with hearing loss, tinnitus, ear fullness

Central Causes

  • Migrainous vertigo: Most common central cause; associated with migraine symptoms
  • Stroke/TIA: Consider with cardiovascular risk factors, neurological symptoms
  • Cerebellar tumor
  • Acoustic neuroma
  • Multiple sclerosis
5

Management

Central Vertigo

RED FLAGS: Urgent referral/admission for:

  • Isolated persistent vertigo (>24 hrs) with hyperacute onset
  • Normal head impulse test with acute vestibular syndrome
  • New onset headache
  • New unilateral deafness
  • Cranial nerve/limb weakness
  • Severe ataxia

Consider short-term symptomatic treatment while awaiting referral (buccal prochlorperazine or IM cyclizine)

Peripheral Vertigo

  • BPPV: Refer for Epley maneuver
  • Vestibular neuronitis/labyrinthitis: Symptomatic treatment, consider steroids
  • Meniere's disease: Refer to specialist
  • Symptomatic treatment: Prochlorperazine 5-10 mg TDS (oral/buccal), Cyclizine 50 mg TDS
  • Consider vestibular rehabilitation exercises
6

Follow-up & Safety

  • Advise on driving restrictions (DVLA guidelines)
  • Address fall risk
  • Consider anxiety/panic associated with vertigo
  • Arrange follow-up based on suspected cause
