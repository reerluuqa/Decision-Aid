Oral Candidiasis – One‑Page Algorithm

NICE CKS 2025 • Clinician quick reference for rapid decision‑making (primary care)

One‑pager NICE CKS 2025
Step 1

Confirm diagnosis

  • Thrush (pseudomembranous): white/yellow plaques that wipe off → erythematous base.
  • Erythematous: sore, red tongue/palate; smooth dorsal tongue.
  • Denture stomatitis: redness under dentures; usually not sore.
  • Angular cheilitis: sore fissures at mouth corners (Candida ± S. aureus).
  • Chronic plaque‑like: adherent white plaques; malignancy risk.
  • Median rhomboid glossitis: central red depapillated area.
Think differentials: leukoplakia, lichen planus, geographic tongue, hairy tongue, oral cancer, HIV.
Step 2

Red flags → admit / urgent refer

  • Systemic illness/candidaemia.
  • Oesophageal involvement: dysphagia, odynophagia, retrosternal pain.
  • Severe, extensive, recurrent, atypical, or non‑responsive disease.
  • Suspicion of malignancy (chronic plaque‑like).
Lower threshold for admission when markedly immunocompromised.
Step 3

Identify risk factors

  • Extremes of age; xerostomia.
  • Diabetes, HIV/AIDS; chemo/radiotherapy; systemic or inhaled steroids; DMARDs.
  • Recent broad‑spectrum antibiotics.
  • Dentures; poor oral hygiene; local trauma; smoking.
  • Nutritional/haematinic deficiency.
Step 4

Management by group

  • Admit if systemic / oesophageal disease; refer if severe/extensive.
  • Topical first‑line: Miconazole oral gel (≥4 months; unlicensed <4 m / preterm <5–6 m). Continue ≥7 days after resolution.
  • Alternative: Nystatin suspension (unlicensed in neonates).
  • Review/Refer: no adequate response after 2 weeks; recurrent episodes; consider underlying immunodeficiency / risk factors.
  • Mild/local: topical therapy.
    • 1st line: Miconazole oral gel.
    • Alt: Nystatin suspension (7 days, then 48 h after lesions resolve).
  • Extensive/severe: Fluconazole PO 200–400 mg day 1, then 100–200 mg OD for 7–21 d.
  • Advice: oral hygiene; smoking cessation; spacer/rinse with inhaled steroids; denture care (see below); review diabetes control.
  • Follow‑up: review at ~14 d; extend/adjust or refer if unresolved. Low threshold for early referral if severe.
  • Lower threshold to admit if systemic / oesophageal symptoms.
  • Seek specialist advice before Tx if on ciclosporin, tacrolimus, or receiving chemotherapy.
  • Mild/local: topical therapy — miconazole (1st) or nystatin.
  • Extensive/severe: Fluconazole PO 200–400 mg day 1, then 100–200 mg OD for 7–21+ d as needed.
  • Review: at 7–14 d; extend/switch or refer if not fully effective; swab if persistent; ensure DMARD monitoring.

  • First‑line: Fluconazole PO 200–400 mg day 1, then 100–200 mg OD for 7–14 d (if otherwise well and not on prophylaxis).
  • Seek specialist advice if severe/extensive or prior fluconazole failure.
  • Review: at ~7 d; extend another 7 d or refer/swap if unresolved; swab if persistent.
Quick safety

Key prescribing notes

  • Miconazole oral gel — avoid with: warfarin (contraindicated; ↑INR, bleeding risk), QT‑prolonging agents, simvastatin, quetiapine, triazolam/oral midazolam, ergotamine. Caution with sulfonylureas (↑hypoglycaemia), phenytoin, Ca‑channel blockers, ciclosporin, tacrolimus, sirolimus.
  • Nystatin suspension — fewer interactions; suitable alternative when miconazole contraindicated.
  • Fluconazole — adjust for renal impairment; interactions incl. warfarin and sulfonylureas; avoid in pregnancy. Manufacturers advise ~1‑week washout pre‑conception.
Infants: Do not apply miconazole to the back of the throat; divide doses; observe for choking. Avoid miconazole in <4 months (unlicensed), or <5–6 months if pre‑term.
Step 5

Supportive & preventive measures

  • Dentures: remove ≥6 h/day (longer if inflamed); brush then disinfect (e.g., chlorhexidine/hexetidine or baby‑bottle sterilizing solutions if no metal); air‑dry; brush mucosa; correct fit.
  • Inhaled steroids: check technique; rinse/brush after use; spacer; step down dose when appropriate.
  • Diabetes: review and optimize control (especially if recurrent).
  • Smoking: offer cessation support.
  • Oral hygiene: routine dental care; address xerostomia and local trauma.
Pocket checklist

Follow‑up & referral

  • Children: refer if severe/extensive or no response by 2 weeks.
  • Adults (non‑immunocompromised): review at ~14 d; extend fluconazole 7 d if partial; refer/specialist input if persistent.
  • Immunosuppressive Tx: review 7–14 d; early specialist advice for calcineurin inhibitors/chemo.
  • HIV: review ~7 d; extend 7 d if needed; refer if recurrent/breakthrough on prophylaxis.
  • Biopsy: chronic plaque‑like disease unresponsive to Tx (malignancy risk).
