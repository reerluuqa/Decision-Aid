Medical Emergency

Condition: Necrotising Otitis Externa (Malignant OE)

  • Cause: Involves bone/skull base (usually Pseudomonas aeruginosa).
  • Key Sign: Severe otalgia, granulation tissue at bony-cartilaginous junction.
  • Risks: Facial nerve palsy, osteomyelitis, intracranial spread.
ACTION REQUIRED:
CT/MRI Temporal Bone
Specialist ENT Referral
IV/Oral Systemic Antibiotics (Ciprofloxacin)

Otitis Externa

Overview Diagnosis & DDX Management Red Flag Check

Clinical Snapshot

Definition (AOE)

Diffuse inflammation of the external ear canal. Rapid onset (generally within 48 hours) of symptoms within the past 3 weeks.


Common Pathogens:
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa (20-60%)
  • Staphylococcus aureus (10-70%)
Typical Presentation
  • Ear Pain: Rapid onset, severe.
  • Tenderness: Tragus or pinna manipulation.
  • Itching / Fullness: Aural fullness common.
  • Otorrhoea: Discharge may be present.
  • Hearing Loss: Conductive (due to canal swelling).
Special Types
Fungal (Otomycosis)

Caused by Aspergillus or Candida. Look for hyphae/spores. Common after prolonged topical antibiotics or in humid climates.

Localised (Furunculosis)

Infection of hair follicle in cartilaginous canal. Severe localized pain. Confined to one spot.

Diagnosis & Differentials

Clinical Signs
Sign Observation
Canal Skin Erythematous, swollen (edematous)
Debris Variable white purulent debris/moist desquamation
Lymphadenopathy May be present
Tympanic Membrane May be erythematous (inflamed) but mobile (use pneumatic otoscopy to differentiate from AOM)
Key Differentials
Condition Key Differentiator
Acute Otitis Media TM not mobile on pneumatic otoscopy; less canal tenderness.
Herpes Zoster Oticus Vesicles in canal/auricle; facial nerve palsy.
Cholesteatoma Keratin debris in attic; usually painless until infected.
Temporomandibular Disorders Pain on jaw motion; normal ear exam.
Investigations (When to Order)
Routine:

Diagnosis is clinical. No routine labs.

If Refractory (>72h) or Complicated:
  • Ear Culture: Identify resistant bacteria/fungi.
  • CT Temporal Bone: Suspected Necrotising OE (pain > findings).
  • Microscopy: Visualize fungal hyphae.

Management Algorithm

UNCOMPLICATED AOE Healthy patient, no perforation, no DM/Immunocompromise
1
Aural Toilet
Clear debris via dry swabbing or microsuction.
Allows drops to penetrate; check TM status.
2
Topical Antibiotics (+/- Steroid)
Preferred: Ciprofloxacin/Dexamethasone or Ofloxacin.
Avoid Neomycin/Polymyxin if TM perforated (Ototoxic risk).
3
Analgesia
Manage pain (NSAIDs/Paracetamol).
Symptoms should improve in 48-72h.
Special Considerations
  • Perforated TM / Grommets: Use Fluoroquinolone only (Ofloxacin/Ciprofloxacin). NO aminoglycosides/alcohol.
  • Severe Canal Edema: Insert a wick (gauze/foam) to deliver drops.
  • Diabetic / Immunocompromised: Consider systemic antibiotics early to prevent necrotizing OE.
Systemic Therapy

Indications:

  • Cellulitis extending to pinna/face.
  • Patients with diabetes or immunocompromise.
  • Failure of topical therapy.

Drug Choice: Oral Ciprofloxacin (usually covers Pseudomonas). *Consult ID Specialist if unsure or resistance suspected.*
