At a glance

Key points

  • TMD is common and usually non‑progressive; symptoms fluctuate.
  • Biopsychosocial assessment is essential.
  • Reserve imaging/invasive therapies for refractory or severe cases.
  • Plan review at 6–8 weeks; refer if not improving.
Assessment (biopsychosocial)

History

  • Pain site/radiation, onset, character, intensity, duration; morning pain; triggers (chewing, yawning, prolonged opening).
  • Associated features: locking/clicking, otalgia, headaches, altered sensation.
  • Impact: sleep, mood, function; screen for anxiety/depression if relevant.
  • Comorbids: fibromyalgia, migraine, inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Parafunction (nail biting, clenching, gum), trauma/dental work.
  • Consider 3‑question TMD screener (any positive → TMD likely).

Examination

  • Inspect: facial asymmetry/swelling; oral cavity & dentition.
  • Palpate TMJ (anterior to tragus) for tenderness, noise, crepitus; measure ROM (typical 35–55 mm; often <35 mm in TMD).
  • Palpate masseter/temporalis for tenderness/trigger points.
  • Neurologic screen; cervical nodes.
  • ≥50y with new symptoms: palpate superficial temporal artery (consider GCA).
Red flags (click to expand)
  • History of malignancy, unexplained neck lump/lymphadenopathy.
  • Persistent/worsening pain; pain with exertion/cough/sneeze.
  • Jaw pain on bisphosphonates or meds linked to osteonecrosis.
  • Recent head/neck trauma; concurrent infection, fever, weight loss.
  • Neurologic symptoms/signs (CN deficits, unilateral hearing loss/tinnitus, vestibular symptoms).
  • Facial asymmetry/mass/profound trismus; recurrent epistaxis, purulent nasal discharge, persistent anosmia, ipsilateral hearing loss.
  • ≥50y with new unilateral headache or scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, malaise → consider GCA.
  • Occlusal changes suggesting neoplasia/inflammatory arthropathy/trauma.
  • Persistent hoarseness, dysphagia, or mouth ulcers ≥3 weeks.
Management pathway

A. Exclude serious / refer urgently

  • Trauma or fracture to TMJ complex.
  • Persistent closed lock (opening ≤25 mm) ⇒ suspect disc displacement without reduction.
  • Pain/reduced function with known rheumatic joint disease.
  • Recurrent dislocation (± hypermobility syndromes).
Same‑day or expedited referral as indicated

B. Conservative (first‑line for most)

Reassure & educate
Non‑progressive; symptoms fluctuate; goals & expectations.
Self‑care
Soft diet, jaw rest in acute phase; avoid parafunction (wide yawning, clenching, gum); local heat/ice; gentle massage; posture; relaxation; optimize sleep; keep active/social.
Pharmacologic
  • Paracetamol or NSAID short course at lowest effective dose.
  • Diazepam 2 mg up to TID for severe acute myogenous spasm; max 5 days.
  • Chronic pain: consider amitriptyline or gabapentin trial.
NSAIDs: consider gastroprotection and CV/GI risk; use minimal dose/duration

C. Adjuncts / allied health

  • Dentist: dental pathology, malocclusion, consider occlusal splint.
  • Physiotherapy: passive jaw stretching, posture training, massage, acupuncture (if available).
  • Psychology/CBT: distress, pain‑related anxiety; address stressors.
  • Provide reputable patient info/videos for self‑management.

Follow‑up & referral

  • Review at 6–8 weeks.
  • If improving → continue conservative care.
  • If persistent/worsening, unable to manage normal diet, severe pain/dysfunction, uncertain diagnosis, or complex comorbidity → refer to OMFS ± ENT, Neurology, or Pain Clinic.
What specialists may do
  • Imaging: panoramic X‑ray (dental/fracture/DJD), CT (DJD/sublux), MRI (disc displacement/arthrosis/synovitis).
  • Injections: intra‑articular hyaluronate or corticosteroid for DJD.
  • Procedures: arthrocentesis / arthroscopy; arthroplasty for advanced degeneration; eminectomy/eminoplasty for recurrent dislocation; prosthetic TMJ replacement for end‑stage disease.
  • Note: limited evidence for botulinum toxin in pain‑related TMD.
