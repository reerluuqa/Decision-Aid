Definition

Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated inflammatory disorder of the nose that occurs when the nasal mucosa becomes exposed and sensitized to allergens.

Classification
  • Intermittent: Symptoms < 4 days/week or < 4 weeks
  • Persistent: Symptoms ≥ 4 days/week and ≥ 4 weeks
  • Mild: No sleep disturbance or daily living impact
  • Moderate-severe: Sleep/daily activities affected
Prevalence
  • Affects up to 50% of population worldwide
  • 23-30% of population in Europe
  • 10% of 6-7 year olds in England
  • 15-19% of 13-14 year olds in England
Complications
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Impaired school/work performance
  • Asthma (15-38% of people with allergic rhinitis)
  • Sinusitis
  • Allergic conjunctivitis
Red Flags
  • Unilateral symptoms (suggests polyps, foreign body, carcinoma)
  • Discolored nasal discharge (suggests infection)
  • Recurrent nosebleeds
  • Facial or nasal pain
  • Loss of sense of smell
  • Children under 2 years with continuous symptoms
Common Allergens

Seasonal

  • Tree pollens (spring)
  • Grass pollens (late spring/early summer)
  • Weed pollens (early spring to early autumn)

Perennial

  • House dust mites
  • Animal dander
  • Moulds
When to Suspect Allergic Rhinitis

Suspect allergic rhinitis if a person presents with:

  • Classic symptoms: sneezing, nasal itching, nasal discharge, and nasal congestion
  • Bilateral symptoms that develop within minutes following allergen exposure
  • Additional symptoms: postnasal drip, itching of the palate, cough
  • Features of chronic nasal congestion: snoring, mouth breathing, halitosis
  • Associated eye symptoms: bilateral itching, redness, tearing
  • Personal or family history of atopy (asthma, eczema, or allergic rhinitis)
Assessment
History Taking
  • Type, timing, frequency, persistence, and location of symptoms
  • Severity and impact on quality of life (sleep, concentration, mood)
  • Housing conditions, pets, and occupation
  • Medication history and previous treatments
  • Family history of atopy
Examination
  • Nasal intonation of the voice
  • Mouth-breathing
  • Darkened eye shadows ('allergic shiners')
  • Horizontal nasal crease
  • Nasal mucosa swelling and greyish discolouration
  • Nasal polyps (rare in children)
  • Hypertrophic nasal turbinates
  • Purulent nasal discharge (suggests sinusitis)
  • Eye involvement (suggests allergic conjunctivitis)
Investigations

A therapeutic trial of first-line treatment may be considered as the first investigation when the history is typical.

Specialist allergy testing may involve:

  • Skin prick testing (high negative predictive value)
  • Serum-specific IgE testing (when skin prick testing not possible)
  • Nasal endoscopy (for diagnostic doubt or treatment failure)
  • Nasal allergen challenge
  • CT scans (when complications suspected)
Differential Diagnosis
  • Non-allergic rhinitis
  • Infective rhinitis
  • Nasal polyps
  • Foreign body (especially in children)
  • Cerebrospinal fluid leak
  • Nasal carcinoma
  • Sinusitis
  • Adenoidal hypertrophy (in children)
First Line Management

Intranasal Corticosteroids

Most effective treatment for persistent symptoms and nasal congestion. Examples:

  • Beclometasone (Beconase)
  • Budesonide (Rhinocort)
  • Fluticasone (Flixonase)
  • Mometasone (Nasonex) - from age 3 years

Regular use is important for optimal effect. Benefits may not be apparent for several days.

Oral Antihistamines

Effective for sneezing, itching, and eye symptoms, but less effective for nasal congestion. Examples:

  • Cetirizine
  • Loratadine
  • Fexofenadine
  • Desloratadine
  • Levocetirizine

Non-sedating antihistamines are preferred over sedating ones.

Additional Treatment Options

Intranasal Antihistamines

May be more effective than oral antihistamines for nasal symptoms. Examples:

  • Azelastine
  • Levocabastine

Intranasal Decongestants

Short-term use (≤7 days) for severe nasal congestion. Examples:

  • Oxymetazoline
  • Xylometazoline

Long-term use can cause rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa).

Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists

May be considered when symptoms persist despite optimal treatment with intranasal corticosteroids and antihistamines. Examples:

  • Montelukast

Particularly useful in patients with co-existing asthma.

Allergen Avoidance Measures
House Dust Mite Avoidance
  • Use allergen-impermeable bedding covers
  • Wash bedding weekly at 60°C
  • Remove carpets where possible
  • Reduce humidity with dehumidifiers
  • Regular vacuuming with HEPA filter
Pollen Avoidance
  • Keep windows closed during high pollen counts
  • Apply petroleum jelly to nostrils
  • Wear wraparound sunglasses outdoors
  • Shower and change clothes after being outdoors
  • Monitor pollen forecasts
Pet Dander Avoidance
  • Keep pets out of bedrooms
  • Regular bathing of pets
  • Use HEPA air filters
  • Consider rehoming pets if symptoms are severe
Follow-up
  • Review after 2-4 weeks to assess response to treatment
  • Consider stepping up treatment if inadequate response
  • Review inhaler technique for nasal sprays
  • Assess adherence to treatment
  • Consider referral if symptoms remain uncontrolled despite optimal treatment
Prescribing Information

Detailed information on medications for allergic rhinitis:

Intranasal Corticosteroids

Benefits
  • Most effective for all symptoms
  • Particularly effective for nasal congestion
  • Improves quality of life
  • Prevents recurrence of symptoms
Adverse Effects
  • Local irritation
  • Nasal dryness
  • Epistaxis
  • Headache
  • Unpleasant taste/smell
Prescribing Notes Regular use is important for optimal effect. Benefits may not be apparent for several days. Use the lowest effective dose.

Oral Antihistamines

Benefits
  • Effective for sneezing and itching
  • Beneficial for eye symptoms
  • Rapid onset of action
  • Convenient administration
Adverse Effects
  • Non-sedating: generally well tolerated
  • Sedating: drowsiness, impaired performance
  • Headache
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms
Prescribing Notes Non-sedating antihistamines are preferred over sedating ones. Dose adjustments may be required in renal impairment.

Intranasal Decongestants

Benefits
  • Rapid relief of nasal congestion
  • Useful before administering nasal sprays
  • Short-term symptomatic relief
Adverse Effects
  • Rebound congestion with prolonged use
  • Local irritation
  • Dryness
  • Sneezing
Prescribing Notes Limit use to maximum 7 days to avoid rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa).

Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists

Benefits
  • Useful for nasal congestion
  • Particularly beneficial in patients with asthma
  • May be combined with antihistamines
Adverse Effects
  • Generally well tolerated
  • Headache
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms
  • Rarely: neuropsychiatric reactions
Prescribing Notes Consider when symptoms persist despite optimal treatment with intranasal corticosteroids and antihistamines.
Special Considerations
Pregnancy
  • First generation antihistamines (chlorphenamine) have longest safety record
  • Intranasal corticosteroids (beclometasone, budesonide) have good safety profile
  • Avoid systemic decongestants in first trimester
  • Sodium cromoglicate eye drops can be used for allergic conjunctivitis
Children
  • Mometasone (Nasonex) is licensed from age 3 years
  • Most other intranasal corticosteroids are licensed from age 6 years
  • Dose adjustments may be required for some antihistamines
  • Monitor growth in children on long-term corticosteroids
Elderly
  • Caution with sedating antihistamines due to increased risk of falls
  • Renal impairment may require dose adjustment for some medications
  • Consider drug interactions with polypharmacy
When to Refer

Consider referral to an immunology or ENT specialist if:

  • Symptoms remain poorly controlled despite optimal treatment
  • Symptoms suggest complications (e.g., sinusitis, nasal polyps)
  • There is diagnostic uncertainty
  • There are atypical features (e.g., unilateral symptoms, facial pain)
  • There is suspicion of structural abnormalities
  • Immunotherapy is being considered
  • Occupational rhinitis is suspected
Immunotherapy

Consider referral for immunotherapy if:

  • Symptoms are severe and not controlled by conventional treatment
  • Allergen avoidance is not practical
  • Medication side effects are problematic
  • The patient prefers to avoid long-term medication

Immunotherapy options include:

  • Subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT)
  • Sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT)

Immunotherapy is most effective for:

  • Grass pollen allergy
  • House dust mite allergy
  • Birch pollen allergy
Occupational Rhinitis

Consider referral if occupational rhinitis is suspected:

  • Symptoms improve when away from work (weekends, holidays)
  • There is a clear temporal relationship between exposure and symptoms
  • Specific occupational allergens are identified

Common occupational allergens include:

  • Latex gloves
  • Chlorine
  • Flour
  • Wood dust
  • Laboratory animals
Initial Assessment

Take detailed history and perform examination. Consider therapeutic trial of first-line treatment.

Review (2-4 weeks)

Assess response to treatment. Consider stepping up treatment if inadequate response.

Second Review (4-6 weeks)

If symptoms remain uncontrolled despite optimal treatment, consider referral to specialist.

Specialist Assessment

Further investigations may be performed. Consider immunotherapy if appropriate.
