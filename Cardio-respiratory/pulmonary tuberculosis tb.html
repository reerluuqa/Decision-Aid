Pulmonary Tuberculosis

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment & Risk Stratification

Key Diagnostic Factors

Cough >2-3 weeks Low-grade fever Weight loss / anorexia Malaise Night sweats Haemoptysis (advanced)

Risk Factors - STRONG

Exposure to infection
Household contacts: 30% acquire latent TB, 1-2% active TB
Birth in endemic country
High-risk regions: Asia, Latin America, Africa
HIV infection
10% annual risk vs 10% lifetime risk in HIV-negative
Immunosuppressive medicines
Steroids >15mg/day, TNF-alpha antagonists
Silicosis
30x increased risk
Apical fibrosis
Prior untreated TB on chest x-ray

Risk Factors - WEAK

Malignancy
Hematological, head & neck cancers
End-stage renal disease
Hemodialysis patients
IV drug use
Independent of HIV status
Diabetes
High global prevalence with TB
Tobacco smoking
Active or passive exposure
Age extremes
<5 years and elderly
Immediate Actions
  • Isolate patient (home or negative-pressure room)
  • Obtain chest x-ray
  • Collect 3 sputum samples for AFB smear & culture
  • Perform NAAT on at least one respiratory specimen
  • Report to public health authorities
Diagnostic Investigations

1st Line Tests

Chest X-ray Sputum AFB smear (3 samples) NAAT Sputum culture Full blood count
Chest X-ray

Findings: Upper lobe fibronodular opacities ± cavitation (typical)

Atypical: Middle/lower lobe, lymphadenopathy, pleural effusion

HIV: Lymphadenopathy, effusion, lower zone, miliary pattern

Sputum AFB Smear

3 samples: Minimum 8 hours apart, include early morning

Sensitivity: 50-60% in US, higher infectiousness if positive

Grading: 1+ to 4+ indicates bacterial load

NAAT

Speed: Faster diagnosis in smear-negative cases

Resistance: Detects rifampicin resistance (Xpert MTB/RIF)

WHO: Initial test in children (Xpert Ultra)

Culture

Gold standard: Most sensitive and specific

Time: Solid media 4-8 weeks, liquid 1-3 weeks

Essential: Required for drug susceptibility testing

Additional Tests

Gastric Aspirate

For children unable to produce sputum

Bronchoscopy

When sputum induction unsuccessful

Stool Testing

Alternative in children, sensitivity 79%

HIV Test

ALL TB patients within 2 months

Diagnostic Criteria

Confirmed: Culture positive Confirmed: NAAT positive Clinical: Compatible symptoms + response to therapy
Negative Results
  • 40-50% of cases are AFB smear negative
  • 15-20% have negative cultures
  • Strong clinical suspicion may warrant empirical therapy
  • NAAT helpful in smear-negative cases
Treatment Protocols

Latent TB Infection

WHO Recommended Regimens
Isoniazid 6-9 months Rifapentine + Isoniazid 3 months Isoniazid + Rifampicin 3 months Rifampicin 4 months

MDR-TB contacts: Fluoroquinolone 6-12 months

Pregnancy: Isoniazid/Rifampicin safe Pyridoxine with Isoniazid

Drug-Susceptible Active TB

Standard 6-Month Regimen
Isoniazid (H) Rifampicin (R) Pyrazinamide (Z) Ethambutol (E)

Intensive Phase: 2 months HRZE

Continuation: 4 months HR

4-Month Regimen (age ≥12)
Isoniazid Rifapentine Moxifloxacin Pyrazinamide
Children 3 months-16 years (non-severe)
Isoniazid Rifampicin Pyrazinamide ±Ethambutol

Total duration: 4 months

Isoniazid-Resistant TB

6-Month Regimen
Rifampicin Ethambutol Pyrazinamide Levofloxacin
Confirm rifampicin susceptibility

Multidrug-Resistant TB

6-Month All-Oral BPaLM Regimen
Bedaquiline Pretomanid Linezolid Moxifloxacin

Age: ≥14 years, HIV any CD4, <1 month prior exposure

9-Month Regimen

Bedaquiline + Fluoroquinolone + Ethionamide/Linezolid + Clofazimine + Isoniazid + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol

Longer Regimens (≥18 months)

Individualized based on susceptibility - consult specialist

Special Populations

HIV: Start ART within 2 weeks Pregnancy: Avoid Pyrazinamide Hepatic dysfunction: Monitor LFTs Renal impairment: Adjust doses if CrCl <30
CRITICAL WARNING - Fluoroquinolones
  • Serious, disabling, potentially irreversible adverse events
  • Tendinopathy/tendon rupture, peripheral neuropathy
  • Aortic aneurysm, heart valve regurgitation
  • Use only for serious, life-threatening infections
  • Consult local guidelines for restrictions
Monitoring & Follow-up

Monitoring Schedule

Liver Function

Baseline: All patients

Monthly: If abnormal, liver disease, HIV, pregnancy

Action: Stop hepatotoxic drugs if ALT >5x ULN

Vision

Ethambutol: Monthly checks if >2 months

Test: Color vision, visual acuity

Sputum Culture

Monthly: Until 2 consecutive negative

Failure: Positive at ≥5 months

Complications

Transmission to contacts IRIS (Immune Reconstitution) ARDS / Respiratory failure Bronchiectasis / Lung destruction Pneumothorax / Empyema Haemoptysis

Infection Control

Home Isolation
  • Consider infectious until: 3 negative sputum smears + 2 weeks therapy + clinical improvement
  • Avoid new contacts, especially children/immunocompromised
  • Household members require evaluation
Air Travel Restrictions
  • All infectious patients: No commercial flights until sputum smear-negative x2
  • MDR/XDR-TB: No travel until 2 consecutive negative cultures
  • Physician responsibility: Report to public health if patient intends to travel

Prognosis & Recurrence

Treated TB: Excellent prognosis Recurrence: 2-5% at 6-12 months Untreated: >50% mortality
Risk Factors for Death
  • Increased age
  • Delayed diagnosis
  • Extensive radiographic involvement
  • Need for mechanical ventilation
  • ESRD, diabetes, immunosuppression
Recurrence Management
  • Verify adherence
  • Drug susceptibility testing
  • Expand regimen with ≥2 new drugs
  • Consult specialist
