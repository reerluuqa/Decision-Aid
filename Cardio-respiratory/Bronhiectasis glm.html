Bronchiectasis Clinical Guidance

Evidence-based guidance for primary care management

What is Bronchiectasis?

Bronchiectasis is a persistent or progressive, chronic, debilitating lung condition, characterized by permanent dilation of the bronchi due to irreversible damage to the elastic and muscular components of the bronchial wall.

It can affect both children and adults, with symptoms ranging from intermittent expectoration and infection to persistent daily expectoration of large volumes of purulent sputum.

Red Flags Urgent

Massive haemoptysis (>250mL) - requires immediate hospital assessment
Severe dyspnoea - consider urgent referral
High fever with systemic symptoms
Rapid deterioration in clinical condition

Key Clinical Features

  • Daily expectoration of purulent sputum (75% of people)
  • Dyspnoea (60% of people)
  • Fever
  • Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance
  • Haemoptysis (26-51.2% of people)
  • Rhinosinusitis (≈60% of adults)
  • Weight loss
  • Chest pain (non-pleuritic)

When to Suspect Bronchiectasis

In Adults
  • Persistent production of mucopurulent or purulent sputum
  • Cough persisting >8 weeks, especially with sputum production
  • Rheumatoid arthritis with chronic productive cough or recurrent chest infections
  • COPD with frequent exacerbations (≥2 annually) and/or positive sputum culture for Pseudomonas
  • Severe or poorly controlled asthma
  • Inflammatory bowel disease with chronic productive cough
In Children
  • Chronic moist or productive cough unresponsive to 4 weeks of antibiotics
  • Recurrent or persistent wet cough (>6 weeks duration)
  • Asthma that does not respond to treatment
  • Severe pneumonia with incomplete resolution
  • Recurrent pneumonia
  • Persistent and unexplained physical signs or chest radiographic abnormalities

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis of bronchiectasis requires a combination of clinical assessment and investigations. All suspected cases should be referred to a respiratory specialist for confirmation.

Initial Investigations

  • Sputum culture - to identify colonizing pathogens
  • Chest X-ray - to exclude other pathology
  • Spirometry - to assess airflow obstruction
  • Oxygen saturation levels
  • Full blood count including differential white cell count

Secondary Care Investigations

  • High-resolution CT (HRCT) - gold standard for diagnosis
  • Testing for cystic fibrosis
  • Screening for antibody deficiency
  • Serum IgE and specific IgE for Aspergillus
  • Test for primary ciliary dyskinesia

Differential Diagnosis

Key Conditions to Consider
  • Asthma - may co-exist with bronchiectasis
  • COPD - may co-exist with bronchiectasis
  • Chronic sinusitis
  • Pneumonia - symptom duration is shorter
  • Lung cancer - consider with persistent cough, haemoptysis, weight loss
  • Interstitial lung disease - usually dry cough with fine lung crepitations
  • Tuberculosis - persistent productive cough with breathlessness and haemoptysis

Management Approach

Initial Management

When bronchiectasis is suspected:

  • Take a thorough history and examine the person
  • Arrange initial investigations (sputum culture, chest X-ray, spirometry)
  • Assess for anxiety or depression
  • Document smoking history and offer cessation support
  • Measure weight and calculate BMI
  • Refer to respiratory specialist for confirmation and management

Follow-up and Monitoring

Review people with bronchiectasis at least annually:

  • Assess symptoms and impact on daily life
  • Measure BMI (seek specialist advice if <20)
  • Document exacerbation frequency
  • Take sputum sample for bacterial culture
  • Assess using Medical Research Council dyspnoea scale
  • Perform spirometry
  • Check oxygen saturation levels
  • Review compliance with airway clearance exercises

Preventative Measures

  • Offer immunization against Streptococcus pneumoniae, COVID-19, and seasonal influenza
  • Ensure the person understands their condition and how to recognize exacerbations
  • Confirm they have been taught an airway clearance technique by a physiotherapist

Treatment Considerations

Antibiotic Therapy

Antibiotics are used for:

  • Treatment of acute infective exacerbations
  • Long-term prophylactic therapy in people with frequent exacerbations
  • Eradication of initial Pseudomonas colonization

Choice of antibiotic should be guided by sputum culture results and local resistance patterns.

Airway Clearance Techniques

All people with bronchiectasis should be taught an airway clearance technique by a physiotherapist. Techniques include:

  • Active cycle of breathing techniques
  • Autogenic drainage
  • Positive expiratory pressure devices
  • Oscillating positive expiratory pressure devices
  • High-frequency chest wall oscillation
Pulmonary Rehabilitation

Offer pulmonary rehabilitation to people with breathlessness that affects their daily life. This includes:

  • Supervised exercise program
  • Breathing control techniques
  • Education on managing their condition
  • Nutritional advice
  • Psychological support

Recognizing an Infective Exacerbation

When to Suspect an Exacerbation

Suspect an infective exacerbation in people with bronchiectasis if there is:

  • A change in one or more of the common symptoms: increased sputum volume or purulence, worsening dyspnoea, increased cough, or increased fatigue/malaise
  • Development of new symptoms, such as fever, pleuritic pain, or haemoptysis

Community Management

For mild to moderate exacerbations:

  • Start appropriate antibiotics (guided by previous sputum culture results if available)
  • Increase airway clearance techniques
  • Consider short-term bronchodilator therapy
  • Ensure adequate hydration
  • Review within 48-72 hours
Prescribe Antibiotics

When to Admit

Consider hospital admission for:

  • Severe exacerbations not responding to oral antibiotics
  • Marked increase in breathlessness
  • Confusion or drowsiness
  • Unable to maintain oral intake
  • Social circumstances that preclude safe management at home
  • Rapid clinical deterioration
Arrange Admission

Antibiotic Choices for Exacerbations

First-line Options (if no previous sputum culture results)
  • Amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 10-14 days
  • Clarithromycin 500mg BD for 10-14 days (if penicillin allergic)
  • Doxycycline 200mg on day 1, then 100mg daily for 10-14 days

Consider local resistance patterns when choosing antibiotics.

For Known Pseudomonas Colonization
  • Ciprofloxacin 500-750mg BD for 10-14 days
  • Consider IV therapy if severe exacerbation or oral therapy fails

Refer to respiratory specialist for long-term management of Pseudomonas colonization.

Referral Criteria

When to Refer to Secondary Care

  • All people with suspected bronchiectasis for confirmation of diagnosis
  • People with three or more exacerbations in a year
  • Chronic colonization with Pseudomonas aeruginosa, MRSA, or non-tuberculous mycobacteria
  • Deteriorating bronchiectasis with declining lung function
  • Suspected allergic pulmonary aspergillosis
  • Consideration for long-term antibiotic therapy
  • Associated rheumatoid arthritis, immune deficiency, inflammatory bowel disease, or primary ciliary dyskinesia
  • Advanced disease and those considering lung transplantation

Information to Include in Referral

  • Full history of presenting complaint
  • Previous sputum culture results
  • Results of initial investigations (CXR, spirometry)
  • Frequency and severity of exacerbations
  • Current medications
  • Smoking history
  • Comorbidities

Urgency of Referral

Routine

Most suspected cases of bronchiectasis without red flags

Urgent
  • Rapidly declining lung function
  • Frequent severe exacerbations
  • Systemic symptoms (weight loss, fever)
Immediate
  • Massive haemoptysis
  • Severe respiratory distress
  • Confusion or drowsiness

Multidisciplinary Team Involvement

Key Specialists

  • Respiratory physician - overall management and medication
  • Physiotherapist - airway clearance techniques and pulmonary rehabilitation
  • Specialist nurse - education, support, and coordination of care
  • Dietitian - nutritional support (especially if BMI <20)
  • Microbiologist - advice on antibiotic therapy
  • Psychologist - support for anxiety or depression
