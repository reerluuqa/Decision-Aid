Acute Bronchitis Management

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | Based on BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Clinical Presentation

Acute bronchitis is a self-limiting lower respiratory tract infection characterized by:

  • Cough as the predominant symptom
  • Acute illness lasting <21 days (cough may persist 2-4 weeks)
  • At least one other lower respiratory symptom (sputum, wheezing, chest pain)
  • No alternative explanation for symptoms

Key Diagnostic Factors

Duration of cough <30 days Productive cough No chronic respiratory illness Exclude other respiratory/cardiac illness

Risk Factors

Strong Viral/Atypical Bacterial Exposure

Seasonal increase in winter months, close contact with ill individuals

Weak Cigarette Smoking

May increase severity of episodes and likelihood of seeking care

Weak Household Pollution

Domestic solid fuel use - limited evidence for association

Investigations

Test Indication Interpretation

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Features Tests
Pneumonia Higher fever, appears more ill, rales on examination Chest X-ray shows infiltrate
Asthma Recurrent/progressive bronchospasm, bilateral wheezing PFTs between bouts show obstructive pattern
COVID-19 Exposure history, specific symptoms RT-PCR for SARS-CoV-2
Pertussis Characteristic whoop (in children), persistent cough PCR or culture for Bordetella pertussis
CHF Dyspnea on exertion, orthopnea, peripheral edema Chest X-ray shows vascular congestion
Reflux Esophagitis Burning chest pain, chronic non-productive cough Endoscopy, pH monitoring

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria

MacFarlane Criteria for Acute Bronchitis

  • An acute illness of <21 days duration
  • Cough as the predominant symptom
  • At least one other lower respiratory tract symptom (sputum production, wheezing, chest pain)
  • No alternative explanation for symptoms

Note: Cough may persist for >30 days in about 25% of patients (post-bronchitis syndrome).

Clinical Assessment

Diagnosis is primarily clinical based on:

  • Acute onset cough with recent respiratory infection symptoms
  • Physical exam: wheezing, rhonchi, prolonged expiratory phase
  • Absence of rales (suggests pneumonia or CHF)
  • Exclusion of other causes through targeted investigations
Rule out pneumonia if rales present Persistent cough >30 days warrants further evaluation

Red Flags Requiring Further Investigation

Urgent Hemoptysis

May indicate malignancy, TB, or severe infection

Urgent Systemic Symptoms

Weight loss, night sweats, persistent fever

Caution Underlying Lung Disease

COPD, asthma, bronchiectasis may complicate presentation

Treatment

Treatment Algorithm Overview

Cough ≤4 weeks (Acute Phase)

  • 1st: Observation + patient education
  • Adjunct: Antipyretics for fever
  • Adjunct: Short-acting beta-agonist for wheezing
  • Adjunct: Antitussive for severe cough
  • Plus: Consider antibiotics if systemically unwell or high risk

Cough >4 weeks (Ongoing)

  • 1st: Evaluate for other causes
  • Adjunct: Short-acting beta-agonist
  • Adjunct: Consider antibiotics if systemically unwell or high risk

Symptomatic Treatment

Antipyretics

Paracetamol: 500-1000 mg PO q4-6h PRN

Max: 4000 mg/day

For fever and comfort

Bronchodilator

Salbutamol: 100-200 mcg (1-2 puffs) inhaled q4-6h PRN

OR 2.5 mg nebulized q4-6h PRN

For wheezing symptoms

Antitussive

Dextromethorphan: 20 mg PO q4h PRN or 30 mg q6-8h PRN

Codeine: 15-30 mg PO q6-8h PRN (adults ≥18 years)

Max: 120 mg/day

NICE: No bronchodilator unless underlying airways disease Opioid cough meds: Adults ≥18 years only

Antibiotic Therapy

Routine antibiotics NOT recommended Most cases are viral

When to Consider Antibiotics (NICE Guidelines):

Immediate Systemically unwell OR

High risk of complications:

  • Pre-existing comorbidity
  • Age ≥80 + 1 risk factor
  • Age ≥65 + 2 risk factors:
    • Hospitalization in past year
    • Current oral steroids
    • Diabetes mellitus
    • History of CHF

Delayed CRP 20-100 mg/L

Consider delayed prescription with patient education

No Antibiotics CRP <20 mg/L

Symptoms present >24 hours, no high-risk features

Antibiotic Strategy Benefits:

  • Delayed prescribing reduces antibiotic use (31% vs 93% immediate)
  • Similar patient satisfaction rates
  • Reduced repeat consultations for worsening illness
  • Limited evidence for benefit in acute bronchitis (≈0.5 day faster recovery)

Alternative/Herbal Medicines

Pelargonium sidoides (EPs 7630)

Small studies show reduced symptom duration and intensity

Eastern European studies

Ivy Leaf Preparations

Safe to use but minimal efficacy

Cineole (Eucalyptol)

Improves bronchitis symptom scores, decreases cough

Evidence: Limited, more trials needed to assess effectiveness and safety.

Ineffective Treatments

Corticosteroids: No benefit for post-bronchitic cough Mucolytics: Not recommended Routine antibiotics: Not indicated

Follow-up

Monitoring

Long-term monitoring is rarely necessary for acute bronchitis:

  • Symptoms resolve within a few weeks in most patients
  • Post-bronchitis syndrome may cause cough for 4-6 weeks
  • Further evaluation needed if cough persists >4 weeks
Nearly all patients recover within 6 weeks

Complications

Low Risk Chronic Cough

25% of patients cough >4 weeks

Post-bronchitis syndrome may last up to 6 months

Treatment: Salbutamol PRN until resolution

Rare Pneumonia

Possible complication, especially in older people

Consider if clinical deterioration or persistent fever

Counseling Opportunities

Smoking Cessation

  • Acute bronchitis episode is an opportunity to discuss quitting
  • Direct evidence linking smoking to acute bronchitis is limited
  • Recurrent infections more common in smokers
  • Most effective approach to prevention

Patient Education

  • Acute bronchitis is self-limited, usually resolves in up to 4 weeks
  • Antibiotics are not routinely needed
  • Importance of completing full course if antibiotics prescribed
  • Proper inhaler technique if bronchodilators used

Recurrence

Recurrence is common in subsequent viral infection seasons, especially in:

  • Smokers
  • People with chronic respiratory conditions
  • Immunocompromised individuals
  • Those with frequent viral exposures (e.g., healthcare workers, teachers)
Use recurrence as motivation for smoking cessation

When to Re-evaluate

Cough >4 weeks without improvement Persistent or recurrent fever Hemoptysis or worsening symptoms Development of new rales or consolidation

Persistent cough evaluation should include assessment for:

  • Asthma/eosinophilic bronchitis
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease
  • Postnasal drip/upper airway cough syndrome
  • ACE-inhibitor use
  • Chronic infections (TB)
  • Malignancy
