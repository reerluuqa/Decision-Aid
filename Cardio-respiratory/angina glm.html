Angina Quick Reference

Last revised: May 2025

Emergency Action Required

Refer immediately to hospital if patient presents with new onset angina, abrupt deterioration in previously stable angina, or angina at rest. This may indicate unstable angina requiring urgent assessment.

Assessment of Chest Pain

Typical Angina Features

  • Precipitated by physical exertion
  • Constricting discomfort in chest, neck, shoulders, jaw or arms
  • Relieved by rest or GTN within ~5 minutes

Atypical Angina Features

Presents with two of the typical features or includes:

  • Gastrointestinal discomfort
  • Breathlessness
  • Nausea

Factors Making Angina Less Likely

  • Pain that is continuous or prolonged
  • Pain unrelated to activity
  • Pain brought on by breathing
  • Associated with dizziness, palpitations, tingling, or difficulty swallowing

Diagnostic Pathway

Clinical Assessment
12-lead ECG
CT Coronary Angiography

ECG Findings Suggesting Ischaemia

  • Pathological Q waves
  • Left bundle branch block (LBBB)
  • ST-segment and T-wave abnormalities

Note: A normal ECG does not rule out stable angina.

Do NOT Use

  • Exercise ECG to diagnose or exclude stable angina in people without known CAD

Management While Awaiting Diagnostic Testing

Provide

  • Sublingual GTN for symptom relief
  • Clear instructions on when to use GTN
  • Consider aspirin 75mg daily if angina is likely

Emergency Instructions

Instruct patient to:

  • Stop and rest if chest pain occurs
  • Use GTN as instructed
  • Take a second dose after 5 minutes if pain persists
  • Call 999 if pain persists 5 minutes after second dose

Documentation

  • Include detailed description of anginal chest pain in referral
  • Record cardiovascular risk factors
  • Note any atypical features

Management Goals

Symptom Management

  • Prevent symptoms of angina
  • Increase exercise tolerance
  • Improve quality of life

Risk Management

  • Manage cardiovascular risk factors
  • Reduce risk of cardiovascular events
  • Reduce associated mortality

Referral

  • Arrange appropriate specialist assessment
  • Consider revascularisation when indicated

New Diagnosis

First-line Treatment

  • Short-acting nitrate (GTN)
  • Either a beta-blocker OR calcium-channel blocker

Patient Information

  • Explain diagnosis clearly
  • Discuss triggers (exertion, stress, cold, large meals)
  • Address misconceptions about prognosis
  • Explain treatment benefits and side effects

Red Flag Advice

Seek immediate medical help if:

  • Sudden worsening in frequency or severity of angina
  • Angina at rest
  • Angina not responding to GTN

Poor Control on Treatment

Medication Review

  • Confirm adherence to medication
  • Consider adding second anti-anginal drug
  • Consider switching to alternative anti-anginal

When to Refer

  • Symptoms not responding to optimal medical therapy
  • Symptoms worsening despite treatment
  • Patient experiencing significant side effects

Re-evaluation

  • Confirm diagnosis is still correct
  • Review for new comorbidities
  • Consider psychological factors

Anti-anginal Medications

Short-acting Nitrates (GTN)

For immediate relief of angina symptoms. Use sublingual spray or tablets.

Administration Instruct patient to use at onset of symptoms, repeat after 5 minutes if needed, call emergency services if no relief after second dose.

Beta-blockers

First-line option for symptom prevention. Examples: atenolol, bisoprolol, metoprolol.

Cautions Use with caution in asthma, COPD, peripheral arterial disease, and diabetes. Avoid in severe bradycardia, heart block, or decompensated heart failure.

Calcium-channel Blockers

Alternative first-line option or add-on therapy. Examples: amlodipine, diltiazem, verapamil.

Cautions Verapamil and diltiazem are contraindicated with beta-blockers. Use with caution in heart failure and hepatic impairment.

Long-acting Nitrates

Second-line option if first-line treatments not tolerated or ineffective. Examples: isosorbide mononitrate.

Administration Use a nitrate-free interval of 10-14 hours to prevent tolerance. Consider asymmetric dosing schedule.

Ivabradine

For patients who cannot take beta-blockers or as add-on therapy. Reduces heart rate.

Indications Consider for patients with sinus rhythm and heart rate >70 bpm despite beta-blocker therapy. Contraindicated in atrial fibrillation.

Nicorandil

Third-line option. Potassium channel opener with nitrate-like properties.

Safety Alert Associated with serious ulcer complications. Discontinue if ulcers develop and treat appropriately.

Ranolazine

Third-line option as add-on therapy when other treatments not effective.

Monitoring Monitor ECG for QT-interval prolongation. Can cause constipation, nausea, and dizziness.

Secondary Prevention

Recommended for All Patients

  • Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin 75mg daily or clopidogrel if aspirin intolerant)
  • Statin therapy (atorvastatin 80mg if high cardiovascular risk)
  • ACE inhibitor or ARB if comorbid hypertension, diabetes, or CKD

Lifestyle Interventions

  • Smoking cessation
  • Regular physical activity (cardiac rehabilitation)
  • Healthy diet (Mediterranean style)
  • Weight management
  • Alcohol in moderation

Medication Interactions & Cautions

Significant Interactions

  • Verapamil/diltiazem with beta-blockers (risk of bradycardia, heart block)
  • Nitrates with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) - severe hypotension
  • Diltiazem with DOACs (increased bleeding risk)

Special Cautions

  • Nitrates with severe anaemia (risk of hypoxaemia)
  • Beta-blockers in diabetes (may mask hypoglycaemia symptoms)
  • Calcium-channel blockers in heart failure
  • Ivabradine in atrial fibrillation (ineffective)

When to Refer Urgently

Immediate Referral Required

Refer immediately to hospital if patient presents with any of the following:

Symptom Patterns

  • New onset angina
  • Angina at rest
  • Increasing frequency or severity of angina
  • Angina occurring with minimal exertion

Associated Symptoms

  • Angina with syncope or presyncope
  • Angina with acute breathlessness
  • Angina with arrhythmias
  • Angina with heart failure signs

ECG Changes

  • New ST-segment depression
  • New T-wave inversion
  • New left bundle branch block
  • Dynamic ECG changes

Routine Referral Indications

Diagnostic Confirmation

  • Typical or atypical angina requiring specialist assessment
  • When non-invasive testing is required
  • For consideration of coronary angiography

Management Review

  • Symptoms not responding to optimal medical therapy
  • Recurrent hospital admissions despite treatment
  • Significant limitation of daily activities

Revascularisation Considerations

When to Consider

  • Left main stem disease
  • Anatomically complex three-vessel disease
  • Proximal LAD disease with extensive ischemia
  • Failed medical therapy

Decision Process

  • Multidisciplinary team discussion
  • Consideration of patient preferences
  • Assessment of surgical risk
  • Discussion of benefits/risks with patient

Driving & Activity Advice

Private Driving

  • Can continue driving if symptoms are well-controlled
  • Must stop driving and inform DVLA if symptoms occur at rest or with emotion
  • Must stop driving if advised by healthcare professional

Professional Driving

  • More stringent requirements
  • Generally requires specialist assessment
  • May be restricted depending on severity

Physical Activity

  • Regular moderate exercise recommended
  • Warm-up and cool-down periods important
  • Avoid extreme temperatures
  • Use GTN prophylactically before exertion if needed
