Heart Failure - Chronic Clinical Reference

Emergency: Acute Decompensation

Immediate referral required for patients with worsening symptoms, pulmonary edema, or hypotension. Monitor for signs of acute kidney injury.

Definition

Clinical syndrome with typical symptoms (breathlessness, ankle swelling, fatigue) and signs (elevated JVP, basal crepitations, peripheral edema) caused by structural/functional cardiac abnormalities.

Classification

  • HFrEF: LVEF ≤40%
  • HFmrEF: LVEF 41-49%
  • HFpEF: LVEF ≥50%

Red Flags

  • Rapid weight gain (>2kg/week)
  • Increasing dyspnea at rest
  • Orthopnea or PND
  • New palpitations or chest pain
  • Dizziness or syncope

Prevalence

  • 1 in 35 people aged 65-74
  • 1 in 15 people aged 75-84
  • 1 in 7 people aged 85+
  • Average age at diagnosis: 76 years

Management Goals

  • Confirm diagnosis
  • Manage symptoms
  • Improve quality of life
  • Reduce hospitalizations
  • Prolong survival

Common Causes of Heart Failure

Myocardial Disease

  • Coronary artery disease (most common)
  • Hypertension
  • Cardiomyopathies (familial, infective, immune-mediated)
  • Toxins (alcohol, cocaine)
  • Infiltrative diseases (sarcoidosis, amyloidosis)

Other Causes

  • Valvular heart disease
  • Arrhythmias (especially AF)
  • High output states (anemia, thyrotoxicosis)
  • Volume overload (CKD, nephrotic syndrome)
  • Drugs (NSAIDs, beta-blockers)

Urgent Referral Required

Refer urgently if NT-proBNP >2000 ng/L or if patient has symptoms at rest or with minimal exertion.

Symptoms

  • Breathlessness
  • Ankle swelling
  • Fatigue
  • Reduced exercise tolerance
  • Nocturnal cough

Signs

  • Elevated JVP
  • Basal crepitations
  • Peripheral edema
  • Third heart sound
  • Tachycardia

NT-proBNP Levels and Referral Timeline

NT-proBNP Level Interpretation Referral Timeline Action
< 400 ng/L Heart failure unlikely - Consider alternative diagnosis
400-2000 ng/L Heart failure possible Within 6 weeks Refer for specialist assessment
> 2000 ng/L Heart failure likely Within 2 weeks Urgent referral

Diagnostic Investigations

First-line Tests

  • NT-proBNP
  • ECG
  • Chest X-ray
  • Blood tests (FBC, U&E, LFT, TFT)

Specialist Tests

  • Echocardiogram
  • Cardiac MRI (if needed)
  • Exercise testing
  • Coronary angiography

Medication Review Required

Review medication within 2 weeks of any change in dose or type of heart failure medication.

Initial Assessment

Confirm diagnosis, classify heart failure, assess severity

Pharmacological Therapy

Initiate evidence-based medications at optimal doses

Monitoring & Review

Regular review with dose optimization and monitoring

Lifestyle Interventions

  • Regular exercise as tolerated
  • Sodium restriction (<2g/day)
  • Fluid restriction (if severe HF)
  • Smoking cessation
  • Alcohol moderation
  • Weight management

Monitoring

  • Regular weight monitoring
  • Blood pressure
  • Renal function
  • Electrolytes
  • Medication adherence

Device Therapy

Device Indication Benefit
ICD LVEF ≤35% despite optimal medical therapy Prevents sudden cardiac death
CRT LVEF ≤35%, QRS duration ≥130ms, NYHA II-IV Improves symptoms, reduces mortality
CRT-D Meets criteria for both ICD and CRT Combined benefits

Medications to Avoid

  • NSAIDs
  • Thiazolidinediones
  • Class I antiarrhythmics
  • Calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem)

Vaccinations

  • Annual influenza vaccine
  • Pneumococcal vaccine
  • COVID-19 vaccine

Renal Function Monitoring

Monitor eGFR and creatinine within 1-2 weeks after starting or titrating ACE inhibitors, ARBs, MRAs, or ARNIs.

First-line Therapy (HFrEF)

  • ACE inhibitor or ARB
  • Beta-blocker (licensed for HF)
  • Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist

Second-line Therapy (HFrEF)

  • SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin)
  • ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan)
  • Ivabradine (if HR ≥70 bpm)

Symptom Control

  • Loop diuretics for fluid overload
  • Oxygen if hypoxic
  • Anticoagulation if AF

Medication Dosing & Monitoring

Medication Starting Dose Target Dose Monitoring
ACE Inhibitor Low dose (e.g., ramipril 1.25mg daily) Titrated up to max tolerated U&E, BP, renal function
Beta-blocker Low dose (e.g., bisoprolol 1.25mg daily) Titrated up to max tolerated HR, BP, symptoms
MRA Low dose (e.g., spironolactone 12.5mg daily) Titrated up to max tolerated K+, renal function
SGLT2 Inhibitor Dapagliflozin 10mg daily 10mg daily Renal function, signs of UTI

Contraindications

ACE Inhibitors/ARBs

  • Pregnancy
  • Bilateral renal artery stenosis
  • Angioedema history

MRAs

  • K+ >5.0 mmol/L
  • eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²

Adverse Effects

Common

  • ACE inhibitors: Cough, hypotension
  • ARBs: Dizziness, hyperkalaemia
  • Beta-blockers: Fatigue, bradycardia
  • MRAs: Hyperkalaemia, gynecomastia

Prognosis Overview

  • ~50% die within 5 years of diagnosis
  • 1-year survival: 80.8%
  • 5-year survival: 48.2%
  • 10-year survival: 26.2%

Poor Prognostic Indicators

  • Increased age
  • Reduced ejection fraction
  • Comorbidities (AF, CKD, COPD, diabetes)
  • NYHA Class III-IV
  • Raised JVP, third heart sound
  • Low systolic BP, tachycardia

Complications

  • Cardiac arrhythmias (AF common)
  • Depression (up to 20%)
  • Cachexia (10-15%)
  • Anaemia
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Sudden cardiac death (30-40% of deaths)

Improving Prognosis

  • Optimize medical therapy
  • Device therapy when indicated
  • Cardiac rehabilitation
  • Comorbidity management
  • Regular review and monitoring
  • Patient education

Palliative Care Considerations

End-of-Life Care

  • Discuss prognosis and goals of care early
  • Advance care planning
  • Symptom management (dyspnea, pain)
  • Consider deprescribing
  • Psychological and spiritual support
  • Support for caregivers
