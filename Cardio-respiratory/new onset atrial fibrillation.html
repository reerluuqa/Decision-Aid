Immediate Emergency Assessment

EMERGENCY DC CARDIOVERSION REQUIRED IF:
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90 mmHg, syncope, shock)
  • Acute pulmonary edema
  • Ongoing myocardial ischemia
  • Pre-excitation syndrome (WPW)
  • Signs/symptoms of acute stroke

Diagnosis

Confirm AF and risk stratification

ECG Confirmation

Gold Standard: 12-lead ECG - Do NOT trust computer interpretation alone. Look for:
  • No distinct P waves (chaotic baseline)
  • Irregularly irregular R-R intervals
  • QRS complexes narrow (unless underlying block)

Complete Heart Block Warning: Regular ventricular rhythm with AF = risk of unreliable escape mechanism → likely needs pacemaker

Initial Investigations

FBC

All patients - detect anemia/infection

Clotting Profile

All patients - baseline for anticoagulation

U&E, Creatinine

All patients - kidney function, electrolytes

Calculate Cockroft-Gault for DOAC dosing

Thyroid Function

All patients - rule out thyrotoxicosis

CXR

All cardiac presentations

Troponin

Selected patients

May be elevated with prolonged tachycardia

Echocardiography

TTE Indicated For:
  • Baseline for long-term management
  • Rhythm control strategy planned
  • Suspected structural/functional heart disease
  • Refinement of antithrombotic risk stratification

TOE Consider: If TTE inadequate or to exclude left atrial appendage thrombus pre-cardioversion

Risk Stratification Tools

CHA₂DS₂-VASc Score (Stroke Risk)

Score: 0 | Low risk

HAS-BLED Score (Bleeding Risk)

Score: 0 | Low bleeding risk
Anticoagulation Threshold (ESC/NICE):
  • Men: CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥ 2 (ESC) or ≥ 1 (NICE)
  • Women: CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥ 3 (ESC) or ≥ 2 (NICE)
  • DOAC preferred over warfarin
  • High HAS-BLED = review at 4 weeks, not contraindication

Inpatient Monitoring

Consider telemetry/24-hour Holter if:

  • Heart rate control assessment needed
  • Bradycardia/conduction disturbances suspected
  • Titrating rate/rhythm control drugs

Emergency Management (Hemodynamically Unstable)

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS:
  1. Urgent admission to acute medical unit
  2. Call anesthesia for sedation
  3. Synchronized DC cardioversion (DO NOT DELAY)
  4. Start anticoagulation pre-cardioversion (LMWH/UFH)
  5. Post-conversion: Continue OAC ≥4 weeks
  6. Continuous monitoring for bradycardia
Duration Considerations:
  • AF >24 hours: OAC mandatory before & after cardioversion
  • AF 24-48 hours: OAC optional if <24 hours, mandatory if 24-48 hours
  • AF ≥48 hours or uncertain: 3 weeks OAC before, 4 weeks after cardioversion

Rate Control Strategy

Start in all patients initially while assessing, unless hemodynamically unstable

First-Line Options (AV Nodal Blocking Drugs):

Beta-blockers (Preferred)

Bisoprolol 1.25-20 mg OD

Metoprolol 25-100 mg BD

Rapid onset, effective for high sympathetic tone

Rate-limiting CCBs

Verapamil 40-120 mg TDS

Diltiazem 60-360 mg daily

Avoid in HFrEF

Digoxin (Second-line)

0.75-1.5 mg loading, then 0.125-0.25 mg OD

Sedentary patients, good for older adults

CONTRAINDICATIONS: Do NOT use AV nodal blockers in WPW syndrome (risk of VF/sudden death). Seek specialist advice.

Combination Therapy:

If monotherapy fails, use 2 of: beta-blocker, diltiazem, digoxin

Rhythm Control Strategy

Consider if persistent symptoms despite rate control or as initial strategy if onset <48 hours

Cardioversion Options:
  • Electrical: Quicker, more effective, shorter stays. Requires fasting, sedation
  • Pharmacological: Flecainide (no structural disease) or Amiodarone (structural disease). Requires monitoring

Pharmacological Cardioversion:

Flecainide

200-300 mg PO single dose

IV 2 mg/kg over 10 min (UK unavailable)

Co-prescribe AV nodal blocker. NO structural/ischaemic HD

Amiodarone

300 mg IV over 30-60 min, then 900-1200 mg/24h

5-7 mg/kg IV over 1-2h, then 50 mg/h infusion

For structural heart disease

Anticoagulation Required: Before and after cardioversion. See sidebar calculator.
  • <24 hours: OAC optional
  • 24-48 hours: OAC mandatory ≥4 weeks
  • ≥48 hours or uncertain: 3 weeks before, 4 weeks after

Anticoagulation Strategy

DOAC Preferred over Warfarin: Non-inferior efficacy, safer (less major bleeding), no monitoring

DOAC Options:

Apixaban

2.5-5 mg BD

Reduce if ≥80y, weight <61kg, Cr ≥133 μmol/L

Rivaroxaban

20 mg OD

15 mg if CrCl 15-49 mL/min

Dabigatran

110-150 mg BD (age-dependent)

Reduce dose with verapamil/amiodarone

Edoxaban

30-60 mg OD (weight-dependent)

60 mg if ≥60kg, 30 mg if <60kg

Warfarin Alternative: If warfarin used, maintain TTR >65% (NICE) or >70% (ESC). Start with LMWH/UFH bridge.
CONTRAINDICATED: Aspirin monotherapy for stroke prevention

WPW Syndrome Management

CRITICAL: Avoid AV nodal blockers (beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin) → accelerate conduction via accessory pathway → VF risk

Preferred: Procainamide or urgent DC cardioversion

Follow-up & Prevention

Long-term management and monitoring

Specialist Referral

Refer all hospitalized AF patients for follow-up:

  • Primary care (AF-trained nurses)
  • Cardiologist/Arrhythmia specialist
  • Within 4-6 weeks of discharge
Urgent Referral Indicated: Young patients, structural heart disease, valvular disease, WPW, uncontrolled rate, recurrent episodes

Complication Monitoring

Acute Stroke

Low risk if anticoagulated. Consult neurologist if stroke occurs on OAC

Myocardial Infarction

Low risk. Consider if rapid rate in CAD patients

Heart Failure

Monitor for decompensation, especially with pre-existing LV dysfunction

Tachycardiomyopathy

High index of suspicion in unexplained HF with recent 'silent' AF

Lifestyle & Risk Factor Modification

ABC pathway continues long-term: Comprehensive risk factor management reduces AF burden and recurrence

Physical Activity

Moderate exercise encouraged

Avoid excessive endurance if >50y

Weight Management

Weight loss if BMI >27

Reduces BP, DM, dyslipidemia

Alcohol Reduction

<14 units/week

Abstinence reduces recurrence

Smoking Cessation

Eliminate tobacco use

Reduces CV risk

Comorbidity Management:

Hypertension

Strict BP control target <130/80

Diabetes Mellitus

Optimize glycemic control

Obstructive Sleep Apnea

Screen and treat (CPAP)

Coronary Artery Disease

Secondary prevention

Prognosis

Prognosis depends on: Precipitating event, underlying cardiac status, thromboembolic risk, AF pattern (paroxysmal/persistent/permanent)
  • Excellent: Young, no structural disease, alcohol-induced (with abstinence)
  • Poor: HF post-MI, persistent AF with multiple comorbidities
  • Increased mortality: AF in setting of MI (regardless of timing)
