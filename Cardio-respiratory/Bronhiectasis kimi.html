Clinical Assessment

Identify patients requiring further investigation for bronchiectasis

Adult Suspicion Criteria
  • Persistent production of mucopurulent or purulent sputum Caution
  • Cough persisting >8 weeks with sputum production Caution
  • Rheumatoid arthritis with chronic productive cough Caution
  • COPD with ≥2 exacerbations/year or Pseudomonas culture Urgent Referral
  • Severe or poorly controlled asthma Caution
  • Inflammatory bowel disease with chronic productive cough Caution
Pediatric Suspicion Criteria
  • Chronic moist/productive cough unresponsive to 4 weeks antibiotics Caution
  • Recurrent/persistent wet cough >6 weeks Caution
  • Asthma not responding to treatment Caution
  • Severe pneumonia with incomplete resolution Urgent Referral
  • Recurrent pneumonia Urgent Referral
  • Unexplained haemoptysis Urgent Referral

Red Flag Features
  • Cyanosis, confusion, marked breathlessness Emergency
  • Temperature ≥38°C with systemic symptoms Emergency
  • Massive haemoptysis (>235mL) Emergency
  • Finger clubbing in children Urgent Referral

Confirmatory Diagnosis

Specialist investigations to confirm bronchiectasis and identify underlying cause

Diagnostic Imaging
High-resolution CT (HRCT)
Gold standard test - refer to respiratory consultant for definitive diagnosis Required
Investigations for Underlying Cause
  • Cystic fibrosis testing - All children, adults <40y, or >40y with CF features
  • Serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) - All confirmed cases
  • Aspergillus IgE/Skin prick test - Exclude allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia test - If continuous rhinitis, neonatal RDS, or dextrocardia
  • Rheumatoid factor & ANCA - Clinically indicated
  • HIV serology - Risk factor assessment
Differential Diagnosis
Asthma
No inspiratory squeaks, family history, atopic disease Consider
COPD
Diminishing breath sounds, smoking history Consider
Pneumonia
Shorter duration, systemic features Rule out
Lung cancer
Hoarse voice, weight loss, persistent cough Exclude

Treatment Management

Evidence-based treatment for stable disease and acute exacerbations

Stable Disease Management
  • Airway clearance technique taught by physiotherapist Essential
  • Smoking cessation support if applicable Offer
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation for breathlessness Recommended
  • Nutritional support if BMI <20 Refer
  • Vaccinations: Pneumococcal, COVID-19, Influenza Annual
Acute Exacerbation: Where to Manage?
Hospital Admission
Comorbidities, need IV antibiotics, sepsis signs Required
Community Management
No red flags, can tolerate oral therapy Suitable
Antibiotic Treatment Principles
  • Send sputum for culture BEFORE starting antibiotics (do NOT delay)
  • Duration: 7-14 days for acute exacerbations
  • If on long-term prophylaxis: STOP and switch class during exacerbation
  • Review response when culture results available
  • Ensure airway clearance maintained during treatment
Antibiotic Prophylaxis
Specialist advice required - Consider if ≥3 exacerbations/year
  • Azithromycin 500mg three times weekly OR 250mg daily
  • Erythromycin ethyl succinate 400mg twice daily
  • Inhaled colistin if Pseudomonas colonized
  • Duration: Minimum 6 months, up to 1 year

Long-term Follow-up

Monitoring schedule and prevention strategies

Review Schedule
  • All patients: Annual review minimum Routine
  • High-risk patients: Secondary care review
    • ≥3 exacerbations/year
    • Chronic Pseudomonas, MRSA, or NTM colonization
    • Deteriorating lung function
    • Associated conditions (ABPA, RA, immunodeficiency, IBD, PCD)

Re-referral Indicators
  • Three or more infective exacerbations per year Refer
  • Significant morbidity despite fewer exacerbations Consider
  • New colonization with Pseudomonas, NTM, or MRSA Specialist
  • Rapid decline in lung function Urgent
  • Massive haemoptysis (>235mL) Emergency
