Definition

Snoring is the noise produced by vibration of the upper airway walls as a result of partial upper airway obstruction during sleep. It ranges from:

  • Simple intermittent snoring
  • Chronic heavy simple snoring
  • Upper airway resistance syndrome (UARS)
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA)
Critical Principle

⚠️ Always rule out obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) in patients who snore

History and examination predict OSA in only ~50% of patients. Male sex, snoring, and increased BMI are useful predictors.

Clinical Classification

Simple Snoring

Generally considered benign. No daytime sleepiness complaints.

  • No obstructive apnoeas
  • No frequent arousals
  • No gas exchange abnormalities
  • AHI < 5 episodes/hour

UARS

Upper Airway Resistance Syndrome - increased respiratory effort with sleep fragmentation.

  • Increased inspiratory efforts
  • Frequent arousals (EEG-confirmed)
  • No overt apnoeas/hypopnoeas
  • Subjective daytime sleepiness
  • Est. prevalence: 10-15%

Obstructive Sleep Apnoea

Episodes of complete or partial upper airway obstruction during sleep with desaturations.

  • AHI ≥ 15 episodes/hour
  • OR AHI ≥ 5 with symptoms/comorbidities
  • Oxyhaemoglobin desaturations
  • Associated arousals
  • Chronic snoring, gasping, daytime sleepiness

Red Flags & Emergency Actions

⚠️

IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

These clinical features suggest OSA or serious pathology requiring urgent evaluation and possible referral.

Witnessed Apnoeas

Patient stops breathing for 10 seconds or more during sleep

Action: Order overnight sleep study immediately. High suspicion for OSA.

Choking/Gasping Episodes

Patient wakes gasping or choking during night

Action: High priority for polysomnography. Strong indicator of OSA.

Excessive Daytime Sleepiness

Epworth Sleepiness Score > 10/24, or patient falls asleep inappropriately

Action: Calculate ESS, arrange sleep study. Risk for accidents.

Morning Headaches

Regular morning headaches on waking

Action: Consider OSA. Evaluate with overnight oximetry or polysomnography.

Comorbidities in Young Patients

Diabetes, hypertension, or ischaemic heart disease in relatively young patients

Action: Screen for OSA. Comorbidities may be secondary to untreated sleep disorders.

Unrefreshing Sleep

Patient reports sleeping through night but still tired on waking

Action: Screen for OSA/UARS. Correlate with ESS score.

⚠️ Children: Special Red Flags (Refer Immediately)
Heavy snoring with apnoeas
Daytime sleepiness (children)
Hyperactivity/behavior problems
Enuresis (bedwetting)
Night terrors
Snort arousals

Referral: Any child with snoring + above features requires urgent referral to paediatric sleep specialist or ENT for adenotonsillectomy evaluation.

Treatment Overview

💡

General Measures First

All patients should be advised on lifestyle modifications regardless of severity or treatment choice.

Lifestyle Measures (ALL Patients)
Weight loss if BMI > 25
Stop smoking
Avoid alcohol (especially before bed)
Avoid sedating medications
Avoid back-sleeping (tennis ball method)
First-Line: CPAP (UARS/OSA)

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure

Sleeping with sealed mask over face with positive airway pressure prevents upper airway collapse. First-line for UARS or OSA.

Efficacy: Highly effective but ~20% of children have difficulty tolerating it. Consider when surgery contraindicated or failed.

Mandibular Advancement Splint (MAS)

Dental splint worn during sleep

Holds lower jaw forwards, drawing tongue base forwards to enlarge oropharyngeal airway. For tongue-base level snoring.

Success: 76% improve snoring frequency, 84% improve severity. Requires sufficient teeth for anchoring.

Surgical Options (After Conservative Measures Failed)
Procedure Indication Success Rate Notes
Radiofrequency (RFTVR) Soft palate/tongue base 82% (BMI <25), 31% (BMI >30) Day case, local anaesthetic
Palatal implants Palatal flutter 79-88% Clinic-based, 3 implants
UPPP Large tonsils, palatal obstruction 65-100% initially, ~45% long-term General anaesthetic, higher morbidity
LAUP/RAUP Palatal level snoring 74-79% initially, 55% long-term Laser or radiofrequency

Treatment Algorithms

Adult Treatment Algorithm

1️⃣ Rule out UARS/OSA first

Order sleep study if: witnessed apnoeas, gasping, daytime sleepiness, ESS >10, comorbidities (DM, HTN, IHD)

If OSA/UARS confirmed → CPAP is FIRST-LINE

Add lifestyle measures + consider MAS/nasal surgery as adjuncts

2️⃣ If NO UARS/OSA (Simple Snoring)

Start with lifestyle measures: weight loss, avoid alcohol/smoking/sedatives, positional therapy

3️⃣ Nasal symptoms present?

If allergic rhinitis/chronic inflammation → Add intranasal corticosteroids. If anatomical obstruction → Consider nasal surgery.

4️⃣ Site of snoring identified?

Tongue base level → MAS or radiofrequency to tongue. Uvulopalatal level → Upper airway surgery (RFTVR, implants, UPPP).

5️⃣ Surgery considerations

Only for non-OSA severe snorers, not overweight, failed conservative measures, single well-defined obstruction site. Best results with tonsils size 3-4.

Child Treatment Algorithm

1️⃣ Assess for OSA red flags

Snoring + witnessed apnoeas/gasping/EDS/hyperactivity/enuresis/night terrors → Refer urgently

If OSA features present → Refer to specialist for adenotonsillectomy evaluation

First-line treatment for childhood OSA is adenotonsillectomy (if no contraindication)

2️⃣ Simple snoring (no OSA features)

Usually benign and self-limiting. Treat allergic rhinitis if present. Encourage weight loss if obese.

3️⃣ Intranasal corticosteroids

May reduce adenoid size and improve snoring symptoms in children with allergic rhinitis

4️⃣ Surgery only if other indications

Adenoidectomy/tonsillectomy if: recurrent tonsillitis, nasal obstruction from adenoidal hypertrophy, or OSA confirmed

Quick Decision Tree
Adult → OSA/UARS suspected? → YES → CPAP + lifestyle
Adult → OSA/UARS suspected? → NO → Lifestyle first → Consider MAS/surgery
Child → OSA features? → YES → Refer for adenotonsillectomy
Child → OSA features? → NO → Reassure + monitor → Treat allergic rhinitis

Medications & Dosages

Beclometasone (Nasal)

Intranasal corticosteroid for allergic rhinitis

Adults: 50-100 mcg (1-2 sprays) in each nostril twice daily

Budesonide (Nasal)

Intranasal corticosteroid for allergic rhinitis

Adults: 32-64 mcg (1-2 sprays) in each nostril once daily

Flunisolide (Nasal)

Intranasal corticosteroid for allergic rhinitis

Adults: 50 mcg (2 sprays) in each nostril twice daily

Fluticasone Propionate (Nasal)

Intranasal corticosteroid for allergic rhinitis

Adults: 50-100 mcg (1-2 sprays) in each nostril once daily

Mometasone (Nasal)

Intranasal corticosteroid for allergic rhinitis

Adults: 100 mcg (2 sprays) in each nostril once daily

Oxymetazoline (Nasal)

Nasal decongestant for URTI-associated snoring

Adults: 2-3 sprays in each nostril every 10-12 hours, max 6 sprays/nostril/24hrs. Short-term only!
Medications to AVOID (Worsen Snoring)
Sleeping tablets
Tranquillisers
Sedating antihistamines

These medications lower upper airway muscle tone and predispose to snoring.

Paediatric Considerations

Key Differences from Adults

Normal

  • ✓ Snoring common ages 18mo-6y
  • ✓ Usually due to physiological adenotonsillar hyperplasia
  • ✓ Often resolves with age
  • ✓ No treatment needed if no OSA features

Refer if:

  • ✗ Witnessed apnoeas
  • ✗ Gasping/choking episodes
  • ✗ Daytime sleepiness
  • ✗ Hyperactivity/behavior problems
  • ✗ Enuresis/night terrors
First-Line Treatments
  • ✓ Adenotonsillectomy - first-line for confirmed OSA
  • ✓ Weight management - for obese children
  • ✓ Intranasal corticosteroids - may reduce adenoid size
  • ✓ CPAP - if surgery contraindicated or failed
Do NOT Recommend
  • ✗ Surgery for simple snoring alone (without other indications)
  • ✗ UPPP or other adult-type palate surgery
  • ✗ Mandibular advancement splints (generally unsuitable)

Quick Reference

Always Rule Out OSA

Ask about:

  • Witnessed apnoeas
  • Gasping/choking
  • Daytime sleepiness (ESS >10?)
  • Morning headaches
  • Unrefreshing sleep
  • Comorbidities (DM, HTN, IHD) in young
First-Line for Everyone

Advise ALL patients:

  • Weight loss if BMI >25
  • Stop smoking
  • Avoid alcohol (esp. before bed)
  • Avoid sedating medications
  • Avoid back-sleeping
Risk Factors by Severity

STRONG RISK FACTORS

  • • Increasing age
  • • Male sex
  • • Obesity (BMI >30)
  • • Neck >40cm
  • • Alcohol consumption
  • • Smoking
  • • Sedating medications
  • • Adenotonsillar hypertrophy
  • • Long soft palate/uvula

MODERATE RISK FACTORS

  • • Cranio-facial abnormalities
  • • Retro-/micrognathia
  • • Hypothyroidism
  • • Acromegaly
  • • Down's syndrome
  • • Rhinitis/nasal obstruction

WEAK RISK FACTORS

  • • Gastro-oesophageal reflux
  • • Abnormal epiglottis
  • • Pharyngeal cysts/tumours
  • • Asthma
