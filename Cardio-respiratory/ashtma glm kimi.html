Asthma Management Algorithm

Primary Care Guidelines - NICE CKS (May 2025)

Clinical Assessment
Key Symptoms to Assess
Suspect asthma if patient presents with:
  • Variable wheeze, cough, breathlessness, chest tightness
  • Symptoms worse at night/early morning or seasonal
  • Triggers: exercise, viral infections, allergens, NSAIDs, beta-blockers
  • Expiratory polyphonic wheeze on examination
  • Note: Normal exam doesn't exclude asthma
Occupational Asthma Red Flags
Suspect occupational asthma if:
  • Working age with new or worsening symptoms
  • High-risk occupation (baking, painting, healthcare, etc.)
  • Symptoms improve away from work/holidays
Urgent Referral to occupational lung service
Atypical Features - Consider Alternative Diagnosis
  • Persistent cough without lung function abnormalities
  • Prominent systemic features (fever, weight loss, hemoptysis)
  • Finger clubbing, cyanosis, focal chest signs
  • Onset >35 years with >30 pack-year smoking history
  • Unexplained restrictive spirometry
  • Persistent chest X-ray changes
Objective Tests by Age Group
Adults & Young People (>16 years):
  • Blood eosinophils > lab reference range OR FeNO ≥50 ppb
  • If negative: Bronchodilator reversibility (FEV1 ↑≥12% & ≥200mL)
  • If still uncertain: Peak flow variability ≥20% over 2 weeks
  • If still suspected: Bronchial challenge test
Children (5-16 years):
  • FeNO ≥35 ppb
  • If negative: Bronchodilator reversibility (FEV1 ↑≥12%)
  • If uncertain: Peak flow variability ≥20%
  • If still suspected: Skin prick test or IgE/eosinophil count
Children under 5 years:
  • Objective testing difficult
  • Trial of low-dose ICS for 8-12 weeks
  • Review response - if ineffective, reassess diagnosis
Confirming Diagnosis
Diagnostic Criteria
Do NOT diagnose without:
  • Suggestive clinical history AND
  • Supporting objective test result
Code as 'suspected asthma' until confirmed
Differential Diagnoses to Consider
  • COPD: Age >35, smoking history >30 pack-years
  • Bronchiectasis: Persistent productive cough, recurrent infections
  • Heart failure: Orthopnea, PND, peripheral edema
  • Pulmonary embolism: Acute dyspnea, pleuritic pain
  • Vocal cord dysfunction: Recurrent 'asthma' without objective evidence
  • Gastroesophageal reflux: Postural/food-related cough
Red Flags for Specialist Referral
Urgent Referral if:
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Suspected occupational asthma
  • Symptoms despite optimized therapy for 3-6 months
  • Severe/difficult-to-treat asthma
  • History of near-fatal asthma
  • Asthma-COPD overlap with uncertainty
Initial Treatment by Age
Age 12+ Years:
  • As-needed AIR therapy: Low-dose ICS/formoterol combination inhaler for symptom relief
  • Highly symptomatic: Low-dose MART (maintenance & reliever therapy)
  • Not controlled: Step up to moderate-dose MART
  • NEVER prescribe SABA alone
Children 5-11 Years:
  • Step 1: Twice-daily low-dose ICS + SABA as needed
  • Uncontrolled: Consider low-dose MART (off-label) OR add LTRA
  • Continue stepping up: Low-dose ICS/LABA → Moderate-dose ICS/LABA
Children under 5:
  • Trial period: 8-12 weeks low-dose ICS twice daily
  • If ineffective: Check technique, adherence, triggers
  • If symptoms resolve: Consider stopping treatment and review after 3 months
  • If symptoms recur: Restart ICS; consider moderate-dose
Additional Therapies
Step-Up Options
If uncontrolled on moderate-dose MART:
  • Check FeNO and blood eosinophils
  • If raised: Refer to respiratory specialist
  • If normal: Trial LTRA or LAMA for 8-12 weeks
  • If still uncontrolled: Refer to specialist
Montelukast (LTRA):
  • Adults: 10mg daily in evening
  • 6-14 years: 5mg daily
  • 6 months-5 years: 4mg daily
  • Watch for neuropsychiatric effects
Tiotropium (LAMA):
  • Age 12+: 5 micrograms once daily
  • Add-on for uncontrolled asthma
  • Caution: Cardiac disease, bladder obstruction
Follow-up & Monitoring
Review Frequency
  • At least annually when stable
  • Within 2 working days after any exacerbation
  • Early pregnancy & post-partum
  • Whenever control deteriorates
Review Components
  • Symptoms: Day/night symptoms, activity limitation
  • Inhaler technique: Check at every review
  • Adherence: Prescription records, SABA overuse (>2 inhalers/year)
  • Exacerbations: OCS courses (≥2/year indicates poor control)
  • Risk factors: Smoking, occupational exposures, psychosocial issues
  • Comorbidities: Rhinitis, GERD, obesity, anxiety/depression
  • FeNO monitoring: In adults at regular review
  • Growth: Monitor children annually
Asthma Action Plans
Essential for all patients
  • Based on symptoms (children) or PEF
  • Include triggers and avoidance strategies
  • Clear instructions for recognizing deterioration
  • Step-by-step medication changes
  • When to seek urgent medical attention
  • Review and update at every asthma attendance
Step-Down Criteria
Consider reducing therapy if:
  • Well controlled for 8-12 weeks
  • At annual review
  • Discuss risks/benefits with patient
  • Reduce gradually, monitoring response
  • Allow 8-12 weeks between changes
Specialist advice if uncertain
Acute Exacerbation Management

Severity Assessment

LIFE-THREATENING
  • SpO2 <92% on air
  • PEF <33% best/predicted
  • Silent chest, cyanosis, poor respiratory effort
  • Hypotension, exhaustion, confusion, drowsiness
ACUTE SEVERE
  • Adults: Can't complete sentences, RR >25, HR >110, SpO2 <92%
  • Children: Too breathless to talk/feed, accessory muscle use
  • PEF 33-50% best/predicted
MODERATE
  • Talking in sentences, no accessory muscle use
  • SpO2 ≥92%, PEF >50%
  • Can be managed at home if responds to treatment
Emergency Treatment
Immediate Actions:
  • Oxygen: Aim SpO2 94-98% (don't delay if no pulse oximetry)
  • Bronchodilator: Salbutamol 5mg nebulized (or 10 puffs via spacer)
  • Add ipratropium: 0.5mg if severe (0.25mg children 2-12 years)
  • Oral prednisolone: 40-50mg adults/children 16+, 30-40mg 6-15 years, 20mg 2-5 years
  • Immediate hospital referral if life-threatening features
Home Management of Moderate Exacerbation
  • On MART/AIR: Continue maintenance dose, increase reliever doses to max licensed
  • On ICS maintenance: Quadruplicate ICS dose for up to 7 days (adults)
  • SABA: 2 puffs every 30-60 seconds up to 10 puffs, repeat after 10 min if needed
  • Prednisolone: Continue 5 days minimum (adults) or 3-5 days (children)
  • Call 999 if no improvement
Post-Exacerbation Follow-up
  • Review within 2 working days
  • Reassess symptoms and signs
  • Check inhaler technique and adherence
  • Identify and address triggers
  • Update personalized asthma action plan
  • Consider home rescue prednisolone
  • Ensure regular follow-up arranged
