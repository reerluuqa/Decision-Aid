Interactive Chest Infections Algorithm

Evidence-based Management for Adult Chest Infections (Age 12+)

Assessment & History
1
Initial Assessment - Key Questions
Start with: "Do you have a cough?"
  • Onset and duration of symptoms
  • Type of cough (dry or productive)
  • Additional symptoms:
    • Breathlessness
    • Wheeze
    • Pleuritic pain
    • Fever
  • Smoking status
  • Check for sepsis signs
2
Vital Signs & Examination
Systematic Assessment
Record Vital Signs:
  • Temperature
  • Pulse rate
  • Blood pressure
  • Respiratory rate
  • Oxygen saturation (SpO2)
  • Assess for confusion
Respiratory Examination:
  • Wheeze or rhonchi
  • Focal chest signs
  • Assess hydration status
3
Special Considerations - Elderly
Atypical Presentation in >65 years
  • Altered consciousness
  • Gastrointestinal discomfort
  • Absence of typical symptoms (cough, fever, difficulty breathing)
  • Higher risk of complications
Diagnosis & Severity Assessment
1
Differential Diagnosis
Key Distinction

Clinical Features Comparison:

Feature Acute Bronchitis Community-Acquired Pneumonia
Primary Symptom Cough (predominant) Cough with systemic symptoms
Chest Signs Wheeze, rhonchi Focal signs (dullness, crackles)
Systemic Illness Mild constitutional symptoms Moderate to severe
Chest X-ray Normal Abnormal (infiltrate)
2
CRB-65 Severity Score
Mortality Risk Assessment Tool
C
Confusion
(New disorientation)
R
RR ≥30/min
B
BP <90/60 mmHg
65
Age ≥65 years
Interpretation:
  • Score 0: Low risk (<1% mortality) → Manage at home
  • Score 1-2: Intermediate risk (1-10% mortality) → Consider hospital assessment
  • Score 3-4: High risk (>10% mortality) → Urgent hospital admission
3
Point-of-Care Investigations
When to Investigate
  • Pulse Oximetry:
    • SpO2 <92%: Consider hospital admission
    • SpO2 <90%: Urgent referral
  • CRP Testing:
    • <20 mg/L: Consider no antibiotics
    • 20-100 mg/L: Delayed antibiotic prescription
    • >100 mg/L: Offer antibiotics
  • Chest X-ray: If suspected CAP and risk of underlying lung pathology
Treatment Options
1
Acute Bronchitis Management
Self-limiting Condition

Non-Antibiotic Management:

  • Adequate fluid intake and rest
  • Paracetamol or ibuprofen for symptomatic relief
  • Honey for cough (if not contraindicated)
  • Pelargonium (herbal medicine)
  • OTC cough medicines with guaifenesin
  • Smoking cessation advice
  • Expected duration: 2-3 weeks

Antibiotic Strategy:

Immediate antibiotics if systemically very unwell
High-risk groups (consider immediate or backup prescription):
  • Pre-existing comorbidities (heart, lung, kidney, liver, neuromuscular)
  • Immunosuppression or cystic fibrosis
  • Age >65 with hospital admission in previous year
  • Diabetes mellitus or congestive heart failure
  • Current oral corticosteroid use
2
Community-Acquired Pneumonia Treatment
Antibiotic Therapy Required

Low Severity (CRB-65 = 0):

First Choice:
Amoxicillin 500mg three times daily for 5 days

Penicillin Allergy or Atypical Pathogens:

Alternatives:
Doxycycline 200mg day 1, then 100mg once daily for 4 days
OR Clarithromycin 500mg twice daily for 5 days
OR Erythromycin 500mg four times daily for 5 days (pregnancy)

Moderate Severity (CRB-65 = 1-2):

Community Management:
Amoxicillin 500mg three times daily for 5 days
PLUS (if atypical pathogens suspected):
Clarithromycin 500mg twice daily for 5 days
3
Paediatric Dosing (12-17 years)
Age-appropriate Antibiotics
First Choice:
Amoxicillin 500mg three times daily for 5 days
Alternatives:
Clarithromycin 250-500mg twice daily for 5 days
OR Erythromycin 250-500mg four times daily for 5 days
OR Doxycycline 200mg day 1, then 100mg once daily for 4 days
Avoid doxycycline in pregnancy
Follow-up & Monitoring
1
Acute Bronchitis Follow-up
No Routine Follow-up Required
  • Reassess if symptoms worsen rapidly
  • Review if cough persists beyond 3-4 weeks
  • Consider pneumonia or other causes if not improving
  • Check for antibiotic resistance if prior antibiotics used
2
Pneumonia Recovery Timeline
Expected Recovery Milestones
  • 1 week: Fever should resolve
  • 4 weeks: Chest pain and sputum substantially reduced
  • 6 weeks: Cough and breathlessness substantially reduced
  • 3 months: Most symptoms resolved (fatigue may persist)
  • 6 months: Full recovery expected
3
When to Reassess Urgently
Red Flag Indicators
  • Symptoms not improving after 3 days of antibiotics
  • Symptoms worsen rapidly at any time
  • Patient becomes systemically very unwell
  • Persistent fever beyond 5 days
  • Development of new symptoms (pleuritic pain, breathlessness)
4
Chest X-ray Follow-up
6-Week Review

Arrange chest X-ray after 6 weeks for:

  • Persistent symptoms despite treatment
  • Smokers aged >50 years
  • Patients at higher risk of underlying malignancy
  • Any patient with concerning or atypical features
5
Prevention & Vaccination
Post-Illness Prevention
  • Assess need for pneumococcal vaccination
  • Consider influenza vaccination
  • Smoking cessation support
  • Lifestyle modification advice
  • Consider referral for pulmonary rehabilitation if indicated
