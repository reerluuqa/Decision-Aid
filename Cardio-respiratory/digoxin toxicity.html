Key Diagnostic Factors

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors (Common)

  • Age >55 years: Decreased clearance, increased bioavailability
  • Decreased renal clearance: Digoxin clearance ‚Üì in renal impairment
  • Hyperkalaemia (>5.0 mmol/L): Marker of severe toxicity & poor prognosis
  • Hypokalaemia (<3.0-3.5 mmol/L): Exacerbates toxicity effects
  • Concomitant drugs: P-glycoprotein inhibitors (verapamil, diltiazem, amiodarone, quinidine, macrolides)
  • Hypomagnesaemia & Hypercalcaemia: Increases susceptibility

üìã History & Exposure

  • Confirm digoxin exposure history
  • Dose and time of ingestion
  • Thorough drug history (including supplements & herbal remedies)
  • Assess for intentional overdose/self-harm
  • Cardiac conditions & renal impairment
  • Other illnesses affecting susceptibility

üö® Clinical Symptoms

Gastrointestinal:
  • Nausea & vomiting (most common)
  • Anorexia
  • Abdominal pain
  • Diarrhea
Cardiovascular:
  • Palpitations
  • Syncope
  • Bradycardia
  • Arrhythmias
Neurological:
  • Lethargy & weakness
  • Confusion
  • Visual disturbances (xanthopsia)
  • Blurred vision/diplopia
üö®
Chronic vs Acute Toxicity: Chronic toxicity is more common and presents indolently over days-weeks, often in elderly patients with risk factors. Acute toxicity has rapid onset (minutes-hours) and can deteriorate quickly.

Initial Investigations

ü©∫ 12-Lead ECG (ALL patients)

  • Acute OD: Marked bradycardia, PR & QRS prolongation
  • Toxicity features: Increased automaticity + AV blockade
  • Common: Premature ventricular contractions (most frequent), bigeminy/trigeminy
  • Specific: Bidirectional VT, atrial tachycardia with block, accelerated junctional rhythms
  • NOT associated: SVT with rapid ventricular response
  • Repeat ECGs in symptomatic patients or mixed overdoses

Classification of Toxicity

üî¥ Acute Toxicity

No prior digoxin use; accidental/intentional overdose

  • Rapid onset (1-2 hours)
  • Initially asymptomatic, then rapid deterioration
  • Life-threatening effects may take >12 hours

üü° Acute-on-Chronic

On digoxin therapy + overdose

  • More toxic than acute alone
  • Existing cardiac disease
  • Higher risk complications

üü¢ Chronic Toxicity

Drug accumulation without overdose

  • Most common presentation
  • Indolent course over days-weeks
  • Subtle presentation, often elderly

Serum Digoxin Concentration

‚è∞ Timing & Interpretation

  • Wait ‚â•6 hours after ingestion before measurement
  • Urgent sample: If severe toxicity suspected & Fab fragments considered
  • Therapeutic range: 0.6-1.2 nmol/L (0.5-0.9 ng/mL)
  • Severe toxicity: >4.0 mcg/L (5.2 nmol/L)
  • Children: Tolerate higher peak concentrations
  • Key point: No exact level predicts toxicity; interpret clinically
  • Half-life: 30-40 hours (normal renal), up to 100 hours (impaired renal)
  • Post-Fab: Assays measure free + Fab-bound digoxin ‚Üí falsely elevated levels

  • Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure assessment
  • Alert: Cardiac arrest possibility - continue resuscitation ‚â•1 hour
  • Get senior help before stopping resuscitation
  • Continuous monitoring: Cardiac rhythm, vitals, BP, capillary glucose

  • Discontinue digoxin and rate control agents
‚ö†Ô∏è
Hemodynamic Instability: If hypotension or altered consciousness, give digoxin-specific antibody (Fab) fragments as PRIMARY management

Electrolyte Abnormality Management

üß™ Hyperkalaemia Management

üö®
Severe hyperkalaemia (‚â•6.5 mmol/L) resistant to conventional treatment = INDICATION for Fab fragments

Adult Treatment (severe hyperkalaemia):

  • IV calcium: 10% calcium chloride OR 10% calcium gluconate (slow IV with cardiac monitoring)
  • Repeat: After 5-10 min if no ECG improvement (max 30 mL)
  • Nebulised salbutamol
  • Insulin-dextrose: 10 units short-acting insulin + 100 mL 20% dextrose IV over 5 min
  • Sodium bicarbonate: For metabolic acidosis
  • Haemodialysis: For renal failure with severe hyperkalaemia/acidosis
  • Monitor potassium repeatedly if Fab fragments administered (risk of hypokalaemia)
üë∂
Children (<12 years): Discuss rapidly with paediatric team and refer for specialist input

üß™ Hypokalaemia Management

  • Use oral or IV potassium
  • Target high end of normal range
  • Correct before/during Fab therapy

üß™ Hypomagnesaemia Management

  • Give additional magnesium
  • Careful monitoring to restore normal levels
  • Correct before/during Fab therapy

Digoxin-Specific Antibody (Fab) Fragments

üéØ Indications for Urgent Administration

Cardiac arrest Life-threatening ventricular arrhythmia Severe bradyarrhythmia Severe hyperkalaemia (>6.5)

Dosing Strategy:

  • Full neutralisation: Calculated to neutralise all digoxin load
  • Half neutralisation (adequate for most): Calculated using weight & serum digoxin concentration
  • Consult TOXBASE/NPIS for dose calculation
  • Cardiac arrest: Empirical dosing per toxicology resource
  • Repeated doses may be required if unstable
‚è±Ô∏è
Onset & Monitoring: Therapeutic effect seen within 15-30 minutes. Monitor ‚â•24-48 hours for rebound toxicity (12-24 hours, up to 10 days in renal impairment)
‚ö°
Key Interaction: Fab fragments cause intracellular potassium shift ‚Üí monitor for profound hypokalaemia, especially if treated for hyperkalaemia

Arrhythmia Management

üíì Bradyarrhythmias & AV Block

  • First-line (if Fab not immediately available): Atropine for symptomatic bradycardia/AV block
  • Second-line: Temporary pacing wire if haemodynamic compromise & Fab unavailable
  • Primary: Fab fragments for severe cases
  • Consult cardiology/toxicology senior specialist
  • Paediatric: Lower atropine doses

üíì Tachyarrhythmias

  • Ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation: See Fab indications above
  • DO NOT USE: Class Ia antiarrhythmics (procainamide, quinidine)
  • AVOID: DC cardioversion (risk of severe arrhythmias)

Gastrointestinal Decontamination

‚ö´ Activated Charcoal

Ingestion >20 mcg/kg Symptomatic patients Toxic plant ingestion
  • Indication: Symptomatic or ingested ‚â•20 mcg/kg digoxin OR any toxic plant amount
  • Precautions: Only if airway protected and gut motility normal
  • Timing: Ideally within 2 hours; later administration may still help
  • Dosing: Adults: 50g every 4 hours; Children: 1g/kg every 4 hours (max 50g/dose)
  • If vomiting: Smaller doses every 2 hours + antiemetic
  • Stop when: Severe features resolved OR plasma concentration reduced OR ileus develops
  • Review: After 4 doses

üß† Intentional Overdose Protocol

  • Urgent mental health assessment at earliest opportunity (do not delay unless life-saving treatment needed)
  • Establish emotional/mental state concurrent with physical assessment
  • Safe environment: Supportive setting with regular supervision
  • Refer to specialist: Self-harm assessment per NICE guidelines
  • See: Suicide risk mitigation protocol

üè† Safe Discharge (after ‚â•6 hours observation)

  • Asymptomatic with normal ECG

  • Patient educated on return precautions
  • Psychosocial assessment completed (if intentional)
  • Follow-up: Arrange primary care review within 1 week

Acute Phase Monitoring

üìä Parameters to Monitor

  • Cardiac rhythm: Continuous telemetry until stable
  • Vital signs: Hourly initially, then as clinically indicated
  • Capillary blood glucose: Especially if insulin-dextrose given
  • Pupil size: Neurological assessment
  • Blood pressure: Watch for hypotension
  • Serum potassium: Every 4-6 hours initially, then daily
  • Digoxin concentration: Every 24 hours until stable
  • Renal function: Daily during acute phase
‚è∞
Minimum monitoring period: 24-48 hours after Fab fragment administration, longer if renal impairment present

Poor Prognostic Factors:

  • Male sex
  • Underlying heart disease
  • High-degree AV block
  • Severe hyperkalaemia
  • Delay in Fab fragment administration

Long-term Management

üîÑ Ongoing Care (if digoxin continued)

  • Discontinue digoxin if possible and prescribe alternative therapy
  • If must continue: Adjust dose for medication profile and renal function
  • Monitor: GFR and serum digoxin every 2-4 weeks initially
  • Measure digoxin: 2-3 weeks after any therapy change or new medication
  • Note: Toxicity can occur in therapeutic range (rare)
  • High-risk patients: Those with renal impairment, hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypothyroidism

üö´ Contraindications to Digoxin

  • Intermittent complete heart block
  • Myocarditis
  • Second-degree AV block
  • Supraventricular arrhythmias with accessory pathways
  • Ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation

Patient Education & Prevention

üì¢ Key Messages

  • Overdose: Seek urgent medical attention immediately
  • Herbal remedies: Beware of cardiac glycosides (foxglove, oleander, lily of the valley)
  • Drug interactions: Consult doctor before starting new medications
  • Renal function: Monitor kidney function regularly
  • Symptoms: Report nausea, vomiting, palpitations, visual changes promptly
