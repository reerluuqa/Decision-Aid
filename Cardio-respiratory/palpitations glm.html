Palpitations Quick Reference
Last revised: April 2020

Emergency Admission Required

Arrange emergency admission for a person with current palpitations and any of the following:

Cardiac Red Flags

  • Ventricular tachycardia (VT)
  • Persistent supraventricular tachycardia (SVT)
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension and/or tachycardia)
  • High risk structural heart disease including ischemic heart disease
  • High degree atrioventricular block on ECG

Symptom Red Flags

  • Significant breathlessness
  • Chest pain
  • Syncope or near syncope
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death under 40 years
  • Onset of palpitations precipitated by exercise

Consider Admission

Consider admission for a person with current palpitations and evidence of a serious or life-threatening systemic cause, such as thyrotoxicosis, severe anaemia, or sepsis.

Urgent Referral

Refer to cardiology, the urgency depending on clinical judgement, people with:

  • Atrial flutter
  • SVT terminated by Valsalva
  • Pre-excitation/WPW syndrome
  • Ventricular extrasystoles with suspected heart disease
  • Frequent extrasystoles
  • Suspected ventricular tachycardia

History Assessment

Key Questions

  • Circumstances prior to and during the episode
  • Onset associated with exercise (red flag)
  • Duration and frequency of palpitations
  • Nature of palpitations (ask person to tap out rhythm)

Associated Symptoms

  • Breathlessness
  • Chest pain
  • Syncope or near syncope

Physical Examination

  • Check heart rate and rhythm
  • Measure blood pressure
  • Check for murmurs suggestive of valvular disease
  • Assess for signs of heart failure (raised JVP, lung crepitations, peripheral oedema)
  • Assess for signs of thyrotoxicosis, anaemia, or other co-morbidity

ECG Assessment

For Current Palpitations

Take an ECG immediately, including a long rhythm strip if possible, to identify:

  • Ventricular tachycardia (VT) or supraventricular tachycardia (SVT)
  • Long or short QT interval
  • Short PR interval (pre-excitation/WPW)
  • Atrial fibrillation or flutter
  • Extrasystoles (atrial and ventricular)
  • Sinus tachycardia

For History of Palpitations

Arrange an ECG (urgency depending on clinical judgement) to identify:

  • Evidence of ischaemic heart disease or previous MI
  • Left or right ventricular hypertrophy
  • P-wave abnormalities
  • Evidence of pre-excitation/WPW
  • Long QT syndrome

Definition

The term palpitations is used to describe an 'abnormally perceived heartbeat' and/or an 'uncomfortable awareness of the heart rhythm'.

This can include a feeling of the heart racing, an irregularity, or an unusual pounding sensation. Some people describe feeling like they have to cough or that their breath is being taken away.

Causes of Palpitations

Cardiac Arrhythmias

  • Supraventricular/ventricular extrasystoles
  • Tachycardias (can be life-threatening)
  • Atrial fibrillation/flutter
  • Pacemaker/ICD anomalies

Structural Heart Disease

  • Mitral valve prolapse
  • Severe mitral/aortic regurgitation
  • Mechanical prosthetic valves
  • Congenital heart disease
  • Cardiomegaly/hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
  • Heart failure

Psychosomatic Disorders

  • Anxiety and panic attacks
  • Depression
  • Somatization disorders

Systemic Causes

  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Hypoglycaemia
  • Menopause
  • Pregnancy
  • Fever
  • Anaemia
  • Hypovolaemia
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Phaeochromocytoma

Drugs

  • Sympathomimetic agents
  • Beta-2 agonists
  • Antimuscarinics
  • Recent beta-blocker withdrawal
  • QT interval prolonging drugs
  • Antihistamines
  • Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine
  • Cocaine, heroin, amphetamines
  • Ecstasy, cannabis

Management Scenarios

Current Palpitations

For people with current palpitations who do not require admission:

  • Arrange blood tests (FBC, U&E, TFT, LFT, HbA1c)
  • Carry out cardiovascular risk assessment
  • Give lifestyle advice (reduce stress, caffeine, alcohol, smoking)
  • For atrial fibrillation, see specific CKS topic
  • For extrasystoles without heart disease, reassure
  • Give advice about driving and work

History of Palpitations

For people with a history of palpitations who do not require referral:

  • Arrange blood tests (FBC, U&E, TFT, LFT, HbA1c)
  • Carry out cardiovascular risk assessment
  • If symptoms infrequent (<1/week) but last >1 hour, advise to attend A&E for ECG during episode
  • If symptoms short-lived but frequent, arrange 24-48 hour Holter monitor
  • If symptoms short-lived and infrequent, arrange self-activated recorder
  • Arrange echocardiogram if murmur found or heart failure suspected

Driving Advice

Group 1 Entitlement

Driving must cease if an arrhythmia has caused or is likely to cause incapacity. Driving may be permitted when the underlying cause has been identified and controlled for at least 4 weeks.

Group 2 Entitlement

The driver is disqualified from driving if an arrhythmia has caused or is likely to cause incapacity. Driving may be permitted when the underlying cause has been identified and the arrhythmia is controlled for at least 3 months.

People with certain occupations (e.g., working at height or with dangerous machinery) will need to stop work until diagnosis is confirmed or condition is treated.

When Referral is Not Indicated

Referral is generally not indicated if palpitations are isolated, the 12 lead ECG is normal, and if symptoms are:

  • Not provoked by exercise
  • Not associated with lightheadedness, syncope, persistent breathlessness, or chest pain
  • Not associated with a history or signs of structural heart disease, heart failure, or hypertension
  • Not associated with a family history of sudden cardiac death

ECG Features of Common Arrhythmias

Arrhythmia Key ECG Features Clinical Significance
Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) No obvious P-waves, Broad QRS complex (>120ms), Rate >160 bpm Life-threatening, assume VT unless proven otherwise
Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) No identifiable P-waves, Regular narrow QRS (<120ms), Rate 140-280 bpm May require urgent treatment if persistent
Long QT Interval QT interval >450ms (varies with heart rate) May lead to ventricular tachycardia
Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Short PR interval (<120ms), Delta wave on QRS Can cause paroxysmal tachycardia
Atrial Fibrillation No distinct P-waves, Irregular baseline, Irregular QRS rate Common, requires specific management
Atrial Flutter P-wave rate 300 bpm (saw-tooth pattern), Variable block Requires specific management
Ventricular Extrasystoles Early QRS, No P-wave, Wide QRS (>120ms), Abnormal T-wave Usually benign unless frequent or with heart disease
Atrial Extrasystoles No P-wave or abnormal P-wave, Early QRS Usually benign
Sinus Tachycardia Rate >100 bpm, Normal sinus rhythm Usually physiological response to underlying cause

ECG Features with Increased Risk of Arrhythmia

Previous Myocardial Infarction

Pathological Q-waves
Q waves >0.04s or >25% of QRS amplitude indicate previous MI
Inversion of T-waves
T wave inversion in leads with dominant R waves suggests ischemia
Loss of R-wave progression
Following an anterior MI
Left bundle branch block
Following a previous MI

Ventricular Hypertrophy

Left Ventricular Hypertrophy

R-wave in V6 >25mm or R-wave in V6 + S-wave in V1 >35mm

Right Ventricular Hypertrophy

Tall R-wave in V1, T-wave inversion in V1-V3 or V4

P-wave Abnormalities

Peaked P-waves

Right atrial overload (pulmonary/tricuspid stenosis, pulmonary hypertension)

Broad and bifid P-waves

Left atrial overload (hypertension, mitral valve disease)
