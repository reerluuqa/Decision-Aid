Quick Reference Algorithm

NAFLD in Primary Care

Age ≥16 years • Focused on rapid decision‑making in consultations • Last reviewed Oct 2023

Reduce disease progression Lower cardio‑metabolic risk Primary care first

1) When to Suspect NAFLD

  • Incidental raised ALT (typically ≤3× ULN, > AST) persisting >3 months.
  • Risk factors: obesity, T2DM, dyslipidaemia, hypertension, metabolic syndrome.
  • Ultrasound with increased hepatic echogenicity (fatty liver).
  • Exclude excess alcohol and secondary causes (viral hepatitis, autoimmune, haemochromatosis, Wilson’s, α1‑antitrypsin deficiency).

Ultrasound is sensitive for moderate–severe steatosis; normal LFTs/US do not exclude NAFLD.

2) Initial Assessment

A History
Symptoms (fatigue/RUQ pain), alcohol intake (<20g/day women, <30g/day men), comorbidities, drugs.
B Examination
BMI, waist circumference, BP, and signs of advanced liver disease (jaundice, spider naevi, palmar erythema, ascites, hepatosplenomegaly, encephalopathy).
C Investigations
LFTs, FBC (platelets), clotting; hepatitis B/C; autoantibodies; ferritin ± transferrin saturation; caeruloplasmin (<40y); α1‑antitrypsin (if indicated); HbA1c; lipids; U&E; TFTs; coeliac serology.

3) Assess Risk of Advanced Fibrosis

Use non‑invasive tools first. Scores suggesting advanced fibrosis:

If low risk ➜ manage in primary care. If high / indeterminate / discordant ➜ refer to hepatology.

4) Management in Primary Care (if low fibrosis risk)

Lifestyle – cornerstone

  • Weight loss 5–10% over ~6 months (≈ 600 kcal/day deficit).
  • Mediterranean diet; prefer water; avoid sugar‑sweetened drinks.
  • Physical activity: 150–200 min/week moderate intensity in 3–5 sessions; combine aerobic + resistance.

Risk reduction & pharmacology

  • Keep alcohol within national limits.
  • Optimise hypertension, diabetes, lipids. Statins are safe and may improve steatosis/inflammation/fibrosis.
  • No licensed drugs in primary care for NAFLD. Pioglitazone and Vitamin E for NASH are specialist‑initiated (off‑label; discuss weight gain, bladder cancer risk, osteoporosis in women).
Patient info: British Liver Trust NICE patient leaflet
Clinical pearl: Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in NAFLD—address cardio‑metabolic risk every visit.

5) When to Refer to Hepatology

  • High risk for advanced fibrosis by FIB‑4/NFS/ELF.
  • Signs of advanced liver disease on examination.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty.

6) Specialist Investigations & Management

  • Transient elastography (FibroScan) for stiffness/fibrosis.
  • Liver biopsy if needed (confirm NASH/cirrhosis; discordant tests).
  • Therapies (specialist): pioglitazone, Vitamin E; emerging agents (e.g., obeticholic acid). Consider bariatric surgery when appropriate.
  • HCC surveillance for advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis; transplant assessment for decompensation.

7) Follow‑Up in Primary Care (if low risk)

  • Annual review: BMI/weight, BP, exam for advanced signs.
  • Labs: HbA1c, lipids, U&E; manage abnormalities.
  • Reassess CVD risk each year.
  • Reassess fibrosis every 3 years (FIB‑4/NFS/ELF). Escalate to hepatology if risk increases.
