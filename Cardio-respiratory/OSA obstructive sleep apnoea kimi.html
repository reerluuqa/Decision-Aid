Obstructive Sleep Apnoea Syndrome

Clinical Decision Algorithm for Primary Care Management

Last revised: November 2021 | NICE CKS Guidelines

Assessment & Screening

Initial Suspicion

Suspect OSAS in adults with:

  • Excessive daytime sleepiness + snoring + fatigue
  • Witnessed breathing pauses (apnoeas), gasping, or choking while sleeping
  • Unrefreshing sleep or impaired concentration
Primary Care Assessment

Clinical Assessment

Ask about:

  • Duration and severity of symptoms (snoring, apnoeas, daytime sleepiness)
  • Collateral history from partner (if possible)
  • Impact on quality of life, relationships, mood, and social activities
  • Driving and work-related safety issues
  • Risk factors and associated conditions

Examine for:

  • Jaw abnormalities (micrognathia, retrognathia)
  • Nasopharyngeal obstruction signs
  • Blood pressure, BMI, and neck circumference
  • Signs of COPD, respiratory failure, or pulmonary hypertension

Screening Tools

STOP-Bang Questionnaire

8-item tool assessing snoring, sleepiness, apnoeas, hypertension, obesity, neck circumference, age, and sex

Epworth Sleepiness Scale

Self-administered 8-item tool assessing likelihood of daytime sleepiness

Note: Do not use ESS alone to determine referral

Differential Diagnosis

Consider other conditions:

  • Snoring: Simple snoring, nasal obstruction
  • Daytime sleepiness: Sleep deprivation, insomnia, periodic limb movements, narcolepsy
  • Neurological: Previous head injury, Parkinson's disease
  • Other: Hypothyroidism, depression, medication effects

Diagnosis & Severity Assessment

Sleep Studies

Polysomnography (PSG) or limited sleep study measures:

  • Sleep-wake state and apnoea/hypopnoea episodes
  • Can be done at home or in specialist sleep laboratory
  • Diagnosis requires ≥5 episodes/hour lasting ≥10 seconds

Mild OSAS

AHI: 5-14 episodes/hour

Monitor

Moderate OSAS

AHI: 15-30 episodes/hour

Consider Treatment

Severe OSAS

AHI: >30 episodes/hour

Treat Promptly

Alternative Measures

Oxygen Desaturation Index (ODI): May be more reliable predictor of cardiovascular outcomes

Home Oximetry: Can be used for screening if polygraphy access limited

Pediatric Considerations

Suspect OSAS in children with:

  • Witnessed snoring and breathing pauses while sleeping
  • Restlessness, sudden arousals, laboured breathing
  • Daytime symptoms: behavioral problems, irritability, hyperactivity
  • Mouth breathing, faltering growth (rare)
Peak incidence: 2-8 years due to adenotonsillar hypertrophy

Treatment Options

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP)

First-line treatment for moderate-to-severe OSAS

  • Airway pressure delivered through nasal or face mask
  • Splints upper airways to prevent collapse during inspiration
  • Improves daytime sleepiness, fatigue, and quality of life
  • May lower blood pressure, especially in resistant hypertension
CPAP Adherence Critical

Adherence rates improve with early patient education and support

Intra-oral Devices

Alternative or adjunct to CPAP

  • Mandibular advancement devices for mild OSAS or snoring
  • Forward advancement of mandible and tongue during sleep
  • Custom-made titratable appliances most effective
  • Can be used if CPAP not tolerated or preferred by patient
Dental Specialist Required

Surgical Options

Selected cases only

  • Adenotonsillectomy for children with adenotonsillar hypertrophy
  • Upper airway surgery occasionally in adults
  • Alternative surgery if other treatments fail

Children

Adenotonsillectomy usually curative

Adults

Surgery rarely first-line option

Lifestyle & Risk Factor Management

Essential components of treatment:

  • Weight loss: Diet, exercise, and/or weight loss surgery if appropriate
  • Smoking cessation: Reduces airway inflammation
  • Alcohol reduction: Decreases airway collapse during sleep
  • Sleep positioning: Avoid supine position, use positioning devices

Pediatric Treatment

Children with OSAS:

  • Adenotonsillectomy first-line for adenotonsillar hypertrophy
  • CPAP if surgery contraindicated or unsuccessful
  • Weight management if obesity present
ENT Referral for Tonsil Assessment

Follow-up & Monitoring

Driving Advice & DVLA Regulations

Professional Drivers

Group 2 (Bus/Lorry):

  • Suspected/confirmed OSAS with sleepiness = DO NOT DRIVE
  • Notify DVLA if symptoms persist >3 months
  • Moderate/severe OSAS requires annual review

Private Drivers

Group 1 (Car/Motorcycle):

  • Avoid driving if excessively sleepy
  • Driving may resume after symptom control
  • DVLA notification if symptoms >3 months
  • Moderate/severe OSAS requires 3-yearly review

Long-term Management

Provide ongoing support:

  • Encourage CPAP adherence with regular monitoring
  • Manage associated conditions (hypertension, diabetes, depression)
  • Monitor for treatment complications
  • Regular follow-up with sleep clinic
  • Reassess driving fitness periodically
Lifelong Condition Management

Risk Factors & Associated Conditions

Adult Risk Factors

Demographics

  • Obesity
  • Increasing age
  • Male sex (2-3:1 ratio)
  • Neck circumference >40.6cm
  • Family history

Lifestyle

  • Smoking
  • Alcohol consumption
  • Supine sleeping position

Medical Conditions

  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Hypertension
  • Heart failure
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Stroke/TIA
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Down's syndrome
  • Acromegaly
  • COPD

Pediatric Risk Factors

  • Adenotonsillar hypertrophy (most common)
  • Obesity
  • Craniofacial abnormalities
  • Neuromuscular disease (e.g., cerebral palsy)
  • Down's syndrome
  • Prader-Willi syndrome

Associated Conditions

Cardiometabolic associations:

  • Hypertension (including non-dipping nocturnal BP)
  • Stroke risk increased 2.24-fold (independent of other risk factors)
  • Cardiovascular disease and atrial fibrillation
  • Heart failure and pulmonary hypertension
  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus
  • Depression

Safety risks:

  • 2.5-fold increased risk of road traffic collisions
  • Double risk of workplace accidents

Urgent Referral Criteria

Urgent referral if:

  • Professional driver with excessive sleepiness
  • COPD, arrhythmia, heart failure, pulmonary hypertension
  • Pregnancy
  • Pre-operative assessment
  • Non-arteritic anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy

Referral Timeline:

  • Urgent: Within 4 weeks
  • Routine: Based on severity
