Shock Assessment

State of reduced end-organ oxygenation (Imbalance of O2 delivery/demand)
Clinical Priority: Immediate resuscitation required. Mortality varies 23%-70% depending on type and duration. Untreated shock leads to irreversible multi-organ failure (>6-12 hrs).

Pathophysiology

Shock is characterised by systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) mediated by tissue hypoxia.

  • Oxygen Delivery (DO2): Cardiac Output x Oxygen Content.
  • Cardiac Output: Stroke Volume x Heart Rate.
  • Stroke Volume: Affected by Preload, Contractility, Afterload.

Resuscitation Goals

Regardless of type, the goals are the same:

  • Restore oxygen delivery.
  • Reverse tissue oxygen debt.
  • Normalize mixed venous oxygen levels (SvO₂).
  • Address underlying cause.

Classification of Shock

1. Hypovolaemic Low Preload

Decreased vascular volume.

Causes: Haemorrhage, GI losses, Burns, Dehydration.

2. Cardiogenic Pump Failure

Heart dysfunction (often left-sided).

Causes: MI, Cardiomyopathy, Valve lesions, Beta-blocker toxicity.

3. Obstructive Blockage

Obstruction to flow or filling.

Causes: PE, Tamponade, Tension Pneumothorax.

4. Distributive Vasodilation

Relative hypovolaemia, decreased SVR.

Causes: Sepsis, Anaphylaxis, Neurogenic shock, Adrenal insufficiency.
EMERGENCY: Bleeding requires immediate control. Massive trauma needs coagulation support.

Key Features

  • Physiology: ↓ Preload → ↓ Cardiac Output.
  • Compensation: ↑ SVR (vasoconstriction) to divert blood to vital organs.
  • Clinical Signs: Tachycardia, weak pulse, pale skin, ↓ urine output.

Immediate Actions

  • Fluid Resuscitation: Balanced crystalloid (Lactated Ringer's) or Normal Saline. Colloids have no proven superiority.
  • Bleeding Control: Tourniquets, local haemostatic measures.
  • Tranexamic Acid: Give within 3 hours of injury (Benefit decreases 10% every 15 mins delay).
  • Surgery/IR: Immediate intervention for haemostasis if indicated.

Common Causes

Haemorrhage

Overt (GI bleed, trauma) or Concealed (ruptured aneurysm, retroperitoneal, long-bone fractures).

Fluid Loss

Diarrhoea, vomiting, insensible losses (burns), third-spacing (pancreatitis, obstruction).

Excessive Loss

Diuretics, Diabetes insipidus, heat exposure without replacement.

CRITICAL: Pump dysfunction. Early aggressive management improves outcome.

Key Features

  • Physiology: ↓ Contractility → ↓ Cardiac Output.
  • Compensation: ↑ Catecholamines → ↑ SVR (Usually, though can be ↓ in ACS).
  • Risks: Patients on beta-blockers/CCB/Flecainide are at risk.

Management

  • Revascularisation: Primary Angioplasty (preferred) or Thrombolysis.
  • Medication: Drug-eluting stents preferred over bare metal.
  • Respiratory Support: Non-invasive/invasive ventilation, diuresis, nitrates.
  • Mechanical Support: Consider Intra-aortic balloon pump (IABP) or ECMO.

Differential Diagnoses

Condition Key Clinical Signs Key Tests
Myocardial Infarction Chest pain, radiation to arm, S3/S4 gallop Troponin (High sensitivity), ECG, Angiography
Heart Failure Dyspnoea, orthopnoea, ankle swelling, crackles Echo (↓EF), BNP/NT-proBNP, CXR
Drug Toxicity (BB/CCB) Syncope, weakness, bradycardia, weakness ECG (Bradycardia), Urine drug screen
TIME SENSITIVE: Requires immediate intervention (Needle decompression / Pericardiocentesis).

Pathophysiology

Secondary to obstruction to cardiac flow or filling.

  • Flow Restriction: Pulmonary Embolism (PE), severe Pulmonary HTN.
  • Filling Restriction: Cardiac Tamponade, Tension Pneumothorax.

Emergency Interventions

Tension Pneumothorax: Immediate Needle Thoracostomy followed by chest drain.

Signs: Unilaterally decreased breath sounds, hyper-resonance, tracheal deviation.

Cardiac Tamponade: Urgent Echo & Pericardiocentesis.

Signs: Beck's Triad (Muffled sounds, JVD, Bradycardia), Pulsus paradoxus.

Common Obstructive Causes

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Risk Factors: Surgery, immobilization, malignancy.

Treatment: Anticoagulation, Thrombolysis (if no contraindications), surgical clot removal.

Tension Pneumothorax

Risk Factors: Chest trauma, young people with history of pneumothorax.

Treatment: Decompression.

Septic Shock

Definition (Sepsis-3):

  • Persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg.
  • Serum lactate >2 mmol/L.

Anaphylactic Shock

Triggers: Foods, drugs, blood, stings.

Signs: Bronchospasm, hypotension.

Neurogenic Shock

Cause: Spinal cord injury.

Signs: Hypotension without reflex tachycardia, warm skin.

Emergency Management Protocols

Septic Shock Actions

  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum + MRSA coverage within 1 HOUR (3 hrs if no shock).
  • Fluids: Rapid crystalloid administration.
  • Vasopressors: Noradrenaline is first choice. Start peripherally if needed (MAP ≥65).
  • O2: High-flow nasal oxygen for hypoxaemic respiratory failure.
  • Steroids: Consider low-dose corticosteroids only if ongoing vasopressor requirement (≥0.25 mcg/kg/min for >4 hrs).

Anaphylaxis Actions

  • Adrenaline: IM Adrenaline Immediately for hypotension/shock.
  • Stop Exposure: Stop offending agents (drugs, IV fluids).

Neurogenic Shock Actions

  • Fluids: Resuscitation to maintain volume.
  • Imaging: Immediate imaging for spinal injury.

Approach to Assessment

Is the patient in shock?

Check: Oxygen delivery vs. cellular demand.

Fluid Responsive?

Check: Preload (Right ventricle filling).

Tone & Pump?

Check: Arterial tone (Afterload) & Contractility.

Scoring & Markers

Marker Significance
Serum Lactate >2 mmol/L is abnormal.
>4 mmol/L indicates higher mortality.
SOFA Score Score ≥2 suggests Sepsis (with infection).
Score ≥7 = significant shock risk.
Base Deficit <-2 mEq/L is abnormal.
<-10 mEq/L = severe metabolic acidosis.
Shock Index HR / Systolic BP.
>1 indicates variable response to fluid.

Hemodynamic Monitoring

  • MAP: Maintain ≥65 mmHg (typically).
  • Cardiac Index: Normal 2.2-2.5 L/min/m². Cardiogenic shock typically <1.8-2.2.
  • Ultrasonography:
    • ↓ IVC filling/diameter → Hypovolaemic.
    • FAST → Free fluid/Pneumothorax.
    • Echo → Wall motion, Valve issues, Effusion.
  • Mixed Venous O2 (SvO₂):
    • Normal: 65-75%.
    • <70% = Independent predictor of mortality.
Note on Urine Output: Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour is a key indicator of decreased organ perfusion.
