Initial Assessment

Perform a rapid, focused assessment to identify stroke/TIA and rule out mimics.

TIME IS CRITICAL: For suspected ACUTE STROKE, arrange immediate emergency admission. For suspected TIA within 1 week, URGENT referral & immediate treatment is needed.
1. Use FAST Screen Core Test
  • Facial weakness: Can they smile? Is there asymmetry?
  • Arm/leg weakness: Can they raise both arms?
  • Speech disturbance: Is speech slurred? Can they understand/find words?
  • Time: Note exact time of onset/last known well.
If positive, treat as ACUTE STROKE
2. Focused History & Exam Key Details
  • Time of onset (critical for treatment eligibility)
  • Speed & evolution of symptoms
  • Stroke risk factors (AF, HTN, diabetes, smoking)
  • Medications (especially anticoagulants)
  • Comorbidities
3. Immediate Bedside Investigaions Rule Out Mimic
  • Check capillary blood glucose to rule out hypoglycemia (<3.3 mmol/L)
  • Record vital signs (BP, HR, O2 sats, Temp)
  • Perform ECG to check for AF
Distinguish TIA vs Stroke

Differentiate based on symptom duration and imaging. Be aware of common mimics.

Key Differentiator: Symptom Duration Critical

Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

Focal neuro dysfunction caused by ischemia, WITHOUT evidence of acute infarction.

  • Symptoms completely resolve within 24 hours (usually <1 hour)
  • No persistent neurological deficit

Stroke

Rapidly developing signs of focal or global cerebral disturbance, lasting >24 hours or leading to death.

  • Persistent neurological deficit
  • Symptoms last >24 hours or cause death
Beware Stroke Mimics 20-25% of cases
  • Seizure/PES (Todd's paresis)
  • Migraine aura
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Vestibular disorders (vertigo)
  • Functional/psychogenic
  • Space-occupying lesion (tumor, SDH)
Always check glucose
Management Pathways

Treatment depends on whether it's an acute stroke, TIA, or secondary prevention phase.

Acute Management Time-Sensitive

Suspected ACUTE STROKE Emergency

  • IMMEDIATE emergency admission to acute stroke facility.
  • DO NOT DELAY transfer for any investigation in primary care.
  • Give advanced warning to hospital: onset time, current state, medications (esp. anticoagulants).
  • Do NOT start antiplatelet/anticoagulant until ICH excluded by imaging.
  • While awaiting transfer:
    • Monitor ABCs.
    • Give O2 if sats <95% (no contraindications)

Suspected TIA (Symptoms Resolved) Urgent Action

  • IF within 1 week of onset:
    • Give Aspirin 300 mg immediately (unless contraindicated) Start Now
    • Add PPI if dyspepsia risk.
    • URGENT referral to stroke specialist (to be seen WITHIN 24 HOURS) <24hrs
  • IF more than 1 week ago:
    • Refer for specialist assessment as soon as possible (within 7 days).
  • Advise not to drive until seen by specialist.
Secondary Prevention (Initiated in Secondary Care, Managed in Primary Care) Long-term

Key Components:

  • Antiplatelet Therapy (if no AF):
    • Clopidogrel 75 mg daily (standard)
    • Aspirin 75mg + MR-dipyridamole 200mg BD (if clopidogrel CI)
    • Aspirin 75 mg (if both above CI)
  • Lipid Modification
    • High-intensity statin (e.g., Atorvastatin 80mg)
    • Aim LDL-C ≤2.0 mmol/L or non-HDL-C ≤2.6 mmol/L
    • Add ezetimibe if targets not met
  • Anticoagulation (if AF/flutter):
    • Start 14 days post-disabling ischemic stroke (sooner post-TIA/non-disabling stroke)
    • Warfarin (INR 2.5) or DOAC
  • Blood Pressure Control: Optimize to standard targets (<140/90 for <79yrs, <150/90 for 80+)
Lifestyle & Risk Factor Management Prevent
  • Smoking cessation (NHS Stop Smoking Service)
  • Diet: 5 portions fruit/veg, 2 portions oily fish/week, low saturated fat, low salt
  • Alcohol: ≤14 units/week, spread over ≥3 days
  • Physical activity tailored to ability
  • No routine supplementation with vitamins/folate
  • Annual influenza vaccination
  • Contraception advice: Avoid combined oral contraceptive pill in pre-menopausal women
Follow-up & Complications

Regular review ensures secondary prevention measures are continued and complications are managed.

Review Schedule Routine
  • At 6 weeks post-discharge (or at 6 months if no early review)
  • At 6 months post-stroke
  • Then at least annually
Screen for & Manage Complications Common
  • Swallowing/Nutrition: Screen for dysphagia, ensure adequate intake, refer to dietitian if needed.
  • Continence: Community continence service follow-up. Manage UTI, constipation.
  • Communication: SLT support for aphasia/dysarthria.
  • Mood & Cognition: Screen for depression/anxiety. Brief psychological interventions first, then antidepressants if needed.
  • Pain: Central post-stroke pain (amitriptyline, gabapentin, pregabalin) or musculoskeletal pain (paracetamol, topical NSAIDs).
  • Fatigue: Assess for reversible causes, provide energy conservation strategies.
  • Spasticity: Physio, botulinum toxin (focal), muscle relaxants (generalized).
  • Oral Health: Mouth care at least twice daily.
  • Hearing/Vision: Screen and refer as needed.
Driving & Work Advice Legal Duty
  • Single TIA: Do not drive for 1 month. No need to notify DVLA.
  • Multiple TIAs: Do not drive for 3 months. Must notify DVLA.
  • Stroke: Do not drive for 1 month. Notify DVLA only if residual neuro deficit persists at 1 month (e.g., visual field defects, cognitive impairment, limb weakness).
  • Returning to work: Discuss with rehab team. Refer to specialist employment/disability services if needed.
