Assessment & History

Clinical evaluation and risk factor identification

Urgent if airway compromise

Key Risk Factors

  • • Smoking history - Strong relationship to lung cancer (most common cause)
  • • Multiple pacemaker leads - Increasingly frequent benign cause
  • • Central venous catheters/ports - Iatrogenic thrombosis risk
  • • Age >50 years - Malignancy more likely
  • • Prior radiation - Mediastinal fibrosis risk

Critical Symptoms

  • • Sudden-onset dyspnea from laryngeal edema
  • • Cyanosis and stridor (4% of cases)
  • • Mental changes, stupor, or coma (4% of cases)
  • • Severe cerebral edema with papilledema

These require IMMEDIATE intervention

Common Clinical Features

Facial/Upper Extremity

  • • Facial swelling (80%)
  • • Upper extremity edema
  • • Facial plethora

Respiratory

  • • Dyspnea (60%)
  • • Cough (54%)
  • • Orthopnea

Venous Congestion

  • • Distended neck veins (63%)
  • • Chest wall veins (53%)
  • • Prominent collateral vessels

Symptoms worsen with bending forward or lying down. Engorged veins worsen with these maneuvers.

Investigations & Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis and identify underlying etiology

CT Chest is test of choice

First-Line Investigations

Chest CT with IV Contrast

Most useful test - establishes diagnosis, shows location/severity, identifies pathology (malignancy vs thrombosis). Can guide biopsy.

Chest X-ray

May show widened mediastinum or lung mass. Initial screening tool.

Chest MRI

Alternative if contrast allergy or renal failure. Avoid gadolinium in severe renal insufficiency.

Upper Extremity Ultrasound

Non-invasive screening. Monophasic flow in SVC or loss of respiratory variation suggests obstruction.

Definitive/Tissue Diagnosis

Biopsy Options

  • • Supraclavicular lymph node: Least invasive, examine for cervical lymphadenopathy
  • • Bronchoscopy: 50-70% yield
  • • Transthoracic needle aspiration: ~75% yield
  • • Mediastinoscopy: >90% yield

Sputum Analysis

Cytology for malignancy, culture for TB/fungal infections

Thoracentesis

If pleural effusion present - cytological analysis

Key Differentials

Cardiac Tamponade

No facial/upper extremity edema, pulsus paradoxus

Constrictive Pericarditis

Elevated JVP with Kussmaul's sign

Right-sided Heart Failure

Preserved respiratory JVP variation

Pulmonary Embolism

Usually absent upper extremity edema

COPD Exacerbation

Wheezing, obstructive PFTs

Cardiac Tumor

Usually absent upper extremity edema

Treatment Management

Interventional & therapeutic strategies

Airway = Emergency

ACUTE: Life-Threatening Airway Obstruction

Option 1: Secure Airway + Radiotherapy + Steroids

• Airway: Intubation or surgical airway

• Dexamethasone: 10mg IV bolus → 4mg q6h

• Radiotherapy: Urgent (only if life-threatening)

⚠️ May interfere with histopathological diagnosis

Option 2: Endovascular Stenting

• Preferred: Can be done before tissue diagnosis

• Access: Femoral vein, conscious sedation

• Success: High technical/clinical success rates

• Patency: 80-94%, 20% require repeat

Malignant Obstruction Management

1. Treat Underlying Malignancy

  • • Chemotherapy: Effective for lung cancer, lymphomas, germ cell tumors
  • • Radiotherapy: Most malignant tumors are sensitive
  • • Surgery: Thymomas resistant to chemo/radiation may need SVC reconstruction

2. Percutaneous Endovascular Stenting

Recommended for ALL patients - achieves rapid symptom improvement with fewer side effects than radiotherapy

• Self-expanding or balloon-expandable stents (usually bare metal)

• Complications: 3-7% (thrombosis, migration, perforation, bleeding)

• Anticoagulation: Aspirin, clopidogrel, warfarin (1-14% bleeding risk)

Benign Obstruction Management

Infectious Causes

• Treat infection with appropriate antimicrobials (aspergillosis, TB, histoplasmosis, etc.)

• If obstruction persists: Consider stents or bypass surgery

Catheter Thrombosis

  • • Remove catheter(s)
  • • Local thrombolysis ± short-course anticoagulation
  • • Warfarin 1 year (INR 2-3) if chronic therapy needed
  • • Thrombolysis most effective within 5 days

Pacemaker Leads

  • • Percutaneous balloon dilatation/stenting preferred
  • • Lead removal: 1-3% mortality risk
  • • Check for infection (blood cultures, TEE)
  • • Long-term warfarin (INR 2-3)

Supportive Measures

  • • Diuretics: For symptom relief (dose per renal function)
  • • Low-salt diet: Reduce fluid retention
  • • Avoid upper extremity lines: Prevent worsening obstruction
  • • Head elevation: Reduce venous pressure
  • • Oxygen: For dyspnea

Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring and long-term outcomes

Regular surveillance needed

Monitoring

  • Regular follow-up by treating physician (oncologist/surgeon/cardiologist/radiotherapist)
  • Frequency depends on underlying etiology and treatment
  • Monitor for symptom recurrence: upper extremity swelling, engorged neck veins, facial edema/p plethora
  • ED referral if dyspnea or airway compromise develops

Potential Complications

Stent-related (3-7%)

  • • Thrombosis or migration
  • • Dissection, perforation
  • • Bleeding, infection
  • • Cardiac tamponade

Anticoagulation-related

  • • Bleeding (1-14% risk)
  • • Volume overload/heart failure exacerbation

Prognosis by Etiology

Malignant Causes

Treatment-Responsive Malignancies

SVC syndrome does NOT necessarily signify adverse outcomes

Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (Resistant)

Poor prognosis, median survival <6 months

General Malignant Prognosis

Depends on tumor type, staging, and histopathology

Benign Causes

Stenting or Surgery

~90% patency rates, may need repeat stenting

Anticoagulation Duration

1-3 months post-stenting (no clear guidelines)

Long-term Outcomes

Generally excellent with appropriate intervention

Patient Education Points

  • • Monitor for recurrence symptoms (swelling, engorged veins, facial changes)
  • • Report immediately for dyspnea or airway symptoms
  • • Understand underlying cause determines prognosis
  • • Compliance with anticoagulation if prescribed
  • • Regular follow-up appointments are critical
