Varicose Veins Clinical Reference
Last updated: September 2024
EMERGENCY: If varicose veins are bleeding, offer first aid and admit to a vascular service immediately

Emergency Actions

  • Bleeding varicose veins: Offer first aid and admit to vascular service
  • Deep vein thrombosis: Immediate anticoagulation and referral
  • Severe pain with swelling: Consider DVT, urgent referral
NICE CKS Guidelines

Assessment

History
Examination
Ask about symptoms: pain, aching, itching, swelling
Ask about red flags: weight loss, bleeding, pelvic pain
Identify risk factors: family history, pregnancy, prolonged standing
Ask about previous interventions: surgery, trauma, DVT
Examine legs standing and lying for varicose veins
Assess for complications: skin changes, ulceration
Check for superficial vein thrombosis
Perform abdominal examination for masses
NICE CKS Guidelines

Red Flags

  • Weight loss
  • Rectal or vaginal bleeding
  • Changes in bowel habits
  • Urinary symptoms
  • Pelvic pain
  • Night sweats
  • Sudden onset of severe pain
  • Unilateral leg swelling
Added in August 2024 review

Complications

Bleeding

Especially from large, traumatized veins over bony prominences

Superficial vein thrombosis

Hard, painful veins

Deep vein thrombosis

Requires immediate anticoagulation

Skin changes

Hyperpigmentation, venous eczema, lipodermatosclerosis

Ulceration

Occurs in 3-6% of people with varicose veins

NICE, 2013a; BMJ Best Practice, 2023

Referral Criteria

Condition Urgency
Symptomatic varicose veins Routine
Skin changes (pigmentation, eczema) Routine
Superficial vein thrombosis Urgent
Active venous leg ulcer Urgent
Healed venous leg ulcer Routine
Bleeding varicose veins Emergency
NICE, 2013a

Management

Self-Care Advice

  • Lose weight if overweight
  • Light to moderate physical activity
  • Avoid prolonged standing/sitting
  • Elevate legs when possible

Compression Therapy

  • Class 2 stockings more effective
  • Exclude arterial insufficiency first
  • Consider patient preference
NICE CKS Guidelines

Treatment Options

Treatment hierarchy (NICE recommended):

1. Endothermal Ablation

Radiofrequency or laser to seal veins

2. Foam Sclerotherapy

Inject irritant foam to close veins

3. Surgery

Ligation and stripping of affected vein

4. Compression Hosiery

Class 2 stockings recommended

Note: All patients seen by vascular service should be assessed with duplex ultrasound Duplex ultrasound provides both anatomical and haemodynamic data on the functioning of the superficial and deep venous systems.
NICE, 2013a; NICE Quality Standards

Risk Factors

Increasing age

Prevalence increases with age (22% at 40, 41% at 60)

Family history

90% risk if both parents affected

Female sex

20-25% prevalence in women vs 10-15% in men

Pregnancy

38% with one pregnancy, 59% with four or more

Obesity

More significant impact in women than men

Prolonged standing/sitting

Increases venous pressure

NICE, 2013a; BMJ Best Practice, 2023

Pregnancy Considerations

  • Reassure that varicose veins are common in pregnancy
  • Not harmful to the baby
  • Often improve considerably after pregnancy
  • Offer compression stockings after excluding arterial insufficiency
  • Interventional treatments unlikely during pregnancy
