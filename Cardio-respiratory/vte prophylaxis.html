Venous Thromboembolism (VTE) Prophylaxis

Evidence-based algorithm for VTE prevention in hospitalized patients

Last updated: Dec 02, 2025 | BMJ Best Practice

Assessment Critical First Step

Evaluate VTE risk, bleeding risk & contraindications in all patients

Baseline Investigations

Before initiating thromboprophylaxis, all patients should have:

  • Renal function: Creatinine & creatinine clearance (agents like LMWH, fondaparinux eliminated through kidney)
  • Full blood count: Rule out acute drop in Hb or severe thrombocytopenia
  • Coagulation profile: If coagulation disorder suspected
  • Serum anti-PF4 antibodies: If clinical suspicion of HIT

VTE Risk Stratification

Thrombotic risk varies with admission reason & patient characteristics. Key risk factors:

Strong Risk Factors
  • Previous VTE 8x risk
  • Thrombophilia
  • Active malignancy 4-7x risk
  • Postoperative setting 4-22x risk
  • Recent trauma 13x risk
  • Indwelling central catheter
  • Immobility
Additional Risk Factors
  • Increasing age (>40 years)
  • Obesity (BMI >30)
  • Estrogen therapy/OCPs/HRT
  • Varicose veins
  • Pregnancy/postnatal
  • Extended travel
  • CHF, COPD, IBD

Bleeding Risk Assessment

Pharmacological agents are contraindicated if:

  • Active bleeding present
  • Severe thrombocytopenia
  • Coagulation disorder
  • High bleeding risk procedure
Neuraxial anesthesia considerations: Spinal hematoma is a rare but catastrophic complication. Avoid anticoagulants close to catheter insertion/removal.

Risk Assessment Tools

Medical Patients

1
Padua Prediction Score: Identify low vs high VTE risk Score ≥4 = High Risk
2
IMPROVE Score: Assess bleeding risk to guide decisions

Surgical Patients

1
Caprini Score: Very low (0) to high (≥5) risk categories
2
Rogers Score: Very low (<7) to moderate (>10) risk

Diagnosis Risk Stratification

Apply validated risk scores & identify patient category

Medical Inpatients

High Risk Criteria Requires Prophylaxis

  • Acutely ill with reduced mobility
  • AND one or more additional VTE risk factors
  • Examples: respiratory/cardiovascular decompensation, acute infection, inflammatory conditions

Low Risk Early Ambulation

  • Mobile patients with no additional risk factors
  • Short hospital stay expected

Surgical Patients

Risk varies by procedure type:

High Risk
  • Major orthopaedic surgery (THA, TKA, hip fracture)
  • Major abdominal/pelvic cancer surgery
  • Neurosurgery (high bleeding risk)
  • Trauma surgery
Moderate Risk
  • General/gynae/urological surgery
  • Thoracic surgery
  • Cardiac surgery
  • Bariatric surgery
Low Risk
  • Minor surgery (<30 min)
  • Laparoscopic procedures
  • Early ambulation feasible

Special Populations

Cancer Patients

Hospitalized cancer patients should receive thromboprophylaxis. Ambulatory cancer patients: consider prophylaxis if Khorana score ≥2 and low bleeding risk.

Pregnancy

LMWH preferred (does not cross placenta). Avoid pharmacological prophylaxis during active labor.

ICU Patients

Most critical care patients should receive thromboprophylaxis with UFH or LMWH.

Treatment Action Required

Pharmacological & non-pharmacological prophylaxis strategies

Pharmacological Options

First-Line Agents

Enoxaparin
Tinzaparin
Dalteparin
Fondaparinux
UFH

LMWH preferred over UFH due to lower HIT risk. Start postoperatively for surgical patients.

Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)

Rivaroxaban
Apixaban
Dabigatran

Indications: Orthopaedic surgery (THA, TKA) Not for hip fracture

Contraindications: Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min), pregnancy, mechanical heart valves

Aspirin

Controversial for primary prophylaxis. May be used for extended prophylaxis after orthopaedic surgery or as alternative in high-risk cancer patients.

Non-Pharmacological Options

Graduated Compression Stockings (GCS)

Generally safe but adherence 30-65% (pain, discomfort, heat). Use alone only if pharmacological agents contraindicated.

Intermittent Pneumatic Compression (IPC)

Significantly decreases DVT vs placebo. Preferred mechanical method. Add to pharmacological prophylaxis in high-risk patients.

Early Ambulation

Encourage all patients when safe. Most important non-pharmacological measure.

Patient-Specific Recommendations

Medical Patients LMWH or Fondaparinux

1
High-risk (Padua ≥4): LMWH, fondaparinux, or UFH until mobile or discharge Preferred: LMWH
2
Low-risk: Early ambulation, no routine pharmacological prophylaxis
Extended prophylaxis beyond hospitalization NOT recommended for medical patients.

Major Orthopaedic Surgery Pharmacological + Mechanical

1
THA/TKA: LMWH, DOACs, or aspirin for minimum 10-14 days, extend to 35 days
2
Hip fracture: LMWH (preferred), fondaparinux, UFH, or aspirin for minimum 10-14 days, up to 35 days
3
Add IPC devices during hospital stay

Neurosurgery IPC First

1
Routine: IPC devices (start on admission, continue 30 days or until mobile)
2
If low bleeding risk: Add LMWH or UFH 24-48h post-op for minimum 7 days

Cardiac/Thoracic Surgery Mechanical + Pharmacological

1
All patients: IPC devices (start on admission)
2
Moderate-high VTE risk: Add LMWH or UFH for minimum 7 days if bleeding risk acceptable

Contraindications & Special Considerations

Absolute Contraindications

  • Active bleeding
  • Severe thrombocytopenia
  • Acute HIT (use alternative agents)
  • Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min) - adjust dose or use UFH
Obesity (BMI >30)

Weight-based dosing may be preferable for LMWH, though evidence is mixed. Consider higher doses for bariatric surgery patients.

Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT)

History of HIT is contraindication to UFH/LMWH. Consult thrombosis specialist for alternatives (fondaparinux, DOACs).

Follow-up Ongoing Monitoring

Monitoring, complications, patient education & secondary prevention

Monitoring Requirements

Bleeding Surveillance

1
Daily clinical assessment for bleeding (surgical site, GI tract, injection sites)
2
Monitor hemoglobin if bleeding suspected

Platelet Count Monitoring

1
Post-surgical patients on UFH: Every other day for 4-14 days
2
Medical patients on LMWH: Routine monitoring not required (low HIT risk)
3
If thrombosis or necrotic reaction: Check platelets immediately

Complications to Monitor

Pulmonary Embolism (PE) Medium Risk
  • Pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, hypoxemia
  • Systolic BP <90 mmHg suggests massive PE
  • Action: O2/ALS protocol, immediate anticoagulation
  • Consider thrombolysis if high-risk
Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) Medium Risk
  • Leg swelling, pain, venous distension
  • 75% of postoperative VTE occurs after discharge
  • Action: Diagnostic ultrasound, initiate treatment
Anticoagulant-Related Bleeding Low Risk
  • Risk varies by agent and patient factors
  • Prophylactic doses cause less bleeding
  • Management: Stop anticoagulant, antidote if available, supportive care
Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia Low Risk
  • 50% platelet count drop 5-14 days after heparin start
  • More common with UFH than LMWH
  • Action: Stop all heparin, consult hematology

Duration of Prophylaxis

Standard Duration

•
Medical patients: Until fully mobile or discharged
•
General surgery: Minimum 7 days, consider up to 28 days if high-risk cancer surgery
•
THA/TKA: Minimum 10-14 days, extend to 35 days
Extended Prophylaxis: NOT recommended for medical patients beyond hospitalization. Consider for high-risk orthopaedic surgery and cancer patients.

Patient Education

Key Teaching Points

1
Signs/Symptoms of VTE: Leg swelling/pain, chest pain, shortness of breath
2
When to seek help: Immediately if any VTE symptoms or injection site necrosis
3
Allergic reactions: Breathing difficulty, wheezing, facial swelling = Emergency
4
Medication adherence: Importance of completing course, proper injection technique
Discharge Planning

75% of postoperative VTE occurs after discharge. Ensure patients understand:

  • Duration of prophylaxis prescribed
  • How to self-administer injections if required
  • Arrangements for community support
  • Follow-up appointment scheduling

Secondary Prevention

After First VTE Episode

Recurrence risk after stopping anticoagulation:

  • 10% at 1 year
  • 16% at 2 years
  • 25% at 5 years
  • 36% at 10 years

Long-term anticoagulation: Consider in patients with active cancer or thrombophilia.

Post-Thrombotic Syndrome: 20-50% of DVT patients develop long-term complications. Appropriate anticoagulation reduces this risk.

Key Points

Remember:

Red Flag

  • Massive bleeding on anticoagulation
  • Severe allergic reaction
