Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) Clinical Reference

Last updated: July 2025
IMMEDIATE REFerral REQUIRED: Pregnant women or postpartum (within 6 weeks) with suspected DVT need same-day assessment.

DVT Wells Score Assessment

+1
Active cancer
+1
Paralysis or recent plaster immobilization
+1
Recently bedridden >3 days or major surgery
+1
Localized tenderness along deep venous system
+1
Entire leg swollen
+1
Calf swelling >3 cm than asymptomatic leg
+1
Pitting edema confined to symptomatic leg
+1
Collateral superficial veins
+1
Previously documented DVT
-2
Alternative diagnosis at least as likely as DVT
Interpretation: DVT likely if score ≥2 points, unlikely if score ≤1 point.

Key Symptoms & Signs

Common Symptoms Examination Findings
Pain or tenderness (often throbbing/cramping) Unilateral swelling (usually in calf)
Pain while walking or weight-bearing Pitting edema
Skin redness and warmth Visible superficial veins
Can range from severe to subtle/asymptomatic Calf circumference >3 cm difference

Management Pathway

High Probability (Wells ≥2): Proximal leg vein ultrasound within 4 hours. If not possible, D-dimer + interim anticoagulation + scan within 24 hours.
Low Probability (Wells ≤1): D-dimer test within 4 hours. If positive, proximal leg vein ultrasound within 4 hours.
If D-dimer negative: Stop interim anticoagulation, consider alternative diagnosis, advise on warning signs.

Anticoagulation Options

1
First-line
Apixaban or Rivaroxaban (consider comorbidities, contraindications, preferences)
2
Alternative
LMWH (at least 5 days) followed by Dabigatran or Edoxaban
3
Alternative
LMWH alongside Vitamin K antagonist (warfarin) for at least 5 days

Risk Factors

Continuing/Intrinsic Risk Factors

Previous DVT
Cancer (known or undiagnosed)
Age over 60 years
Overweight or obesity
Male sex
Heart failure

Temporary Risk Factors

Recent major surgery
Recent hospitalization
Recent trauma
Chemotherapy
Significant immobility
Prolonged travel (>4 hours)

Complications

Pulmonary Embolism: Life-threatening condition where emboli obstruct pulmonary arterial system, causing severe respiratory dysfunction.
Recurrent DVT: Previous clot formation affects venous valves and blood flow, increasing risk of new clots.
Post-thrombotic Syndrome (PTS): Chronic obstruction of venous outflow causing leg pain, swelling, dermatitis, and ulcers (up to 50% within 2 years).
Venous Insufficiency: Poor circulation caused by venous valve incompetence or impaired calf muscle pump.

Post-DVT Management

Monitoring & Follow-up

Regular Review
Review at 3 months, then at least annually if continuing anticoagulation
Blood Tests
For warfarin: monitor INR regularly. For DOACs: periodic assessment of renal function

Patient Advice

Anticoagulant Alert Card
Always carry an anticoagulant alert card
Travel Precautions
Special precautions for long-haul travel
Do NOT offer graduated elastic compression stockings (ECS) to prevent recurrence of post-thrombotic syndrome or venous thromboembolism after a proximal DVT.

Investigations for Unprovoked DVT

Cancer Screening

Review medical history and baseline blood tests, offer physical examination. No further investigations unless relevant symptoms/signs.

Thrombophilia Testing

Consider antiphospholipid antibodies if stopping anticoagulation. Consider hereditary thrombophilia if first-degree relative with DVT/PE.
