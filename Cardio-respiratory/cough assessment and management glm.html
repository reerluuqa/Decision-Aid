Initial Assessment

Key components of a comprehensive cough evaluation

History

  • Onset and duration of cough
  • Frequency and pattern
  • Type (dry or productive)
  • Precipitating/exacerbating factors
  • Associated symptoms
  • Smoking history
  • Environmental/occupational factors

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Haemoptysis
  • Hoarseness
  • Peripheral oedema with weight gain
  • Prominent dyspnoea (especially at rest/night)
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
  • Trouble swallowing
  • Vomiting

Examination

  • Chest examination
  • Cardiovascular assessment
  • Ears, nose, throat examination
  • Vital signs
  • Oxygen saturation
  • Peak expiratory flow (if asthma suspected)

Investigations

  • Chest X-ray (for chronic cough)
  • Full blood count
  • Diagnostic spirometry
  • Fractional exhaled nitric oxide (if available)
  • C-reactive protein (if pneumonia suspected)
  • Pertussis serology (if whooping cough suspected)

Comorbid Conditions to Consider

Respiratory

  • Asthma
  • COPD
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Interstitial lung disease
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea

Other Systems

  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease
  • Heart failure
  • Allergic rhinitis
  • Medication-induced (ACE inhibitors, sitagliptin)

Emergency Admission Criteria

Immediate hospital referral required
Pulmonary Embolism or Pneumothorax

Clinical features of suspected pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax require immediate emergency admission.

Signs of Serious Illness

Any signs or symptoms suggesting serious underlying illness warrant urgent assessment.

CRB65 Score ≥ 2

For community-acquired pneumonia, use CRB65 score to assess admission need:

  • Confusion (abbreviated Mental Test score ≤ 8)
  • Respiratory rate ≥ 30 breaths/minute
  • Blood pressure (diastolic ≤ 60 mmHg or systolic < 90 mmHg)
  • 65 Age ≥ 65 years

Consider admission if score is 2 or greater.

Other Concerning Signs
  • Tachycardia > 130 beats per minute
  • Oxygen saturation < 92% (or central cyanosis if no history of chronic hypoxia)
  • Peak expiratory flow rate < 33% of predicted
  • Use of accessory muscles of respiration (especially if becoming exhausted)
  • Clinical features of foreign body aspiration

Acute Cough Management

Duration: Less than 3 weeks

Upper Respiratory Tract Infection

Recommended Management
  • Advise that acute cough usually persists for up to 3-4 weeks
  • Recommend self-care (paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain)
  • Honey, pelargonium, or OTC medicines with guaifenesin may be tried
  • Smoking cessation advice if applicable
  • Seek medical advice if symptoms worsen, don't improve in 3-4 weeks, or become systemically unwell
Avoid
  • Oral or inhaled bronchodilators (unless underlying airways disease)
  • Oral or inhaled corticosteroids (unless underlying airways disease)
  • Mucolytics (e.g., acetylcysteine or carbocisteine)
  • Antibiotics (unless systemically very unwell or at higher risk of complications)

Systemically Very Unwell

Immediate Antibiotic

First-line: Doxycycline 200 mg on first day, then 100 mg once daily for 4 days (avoid in pregnancy)

Alternatives:

  • Amoxicillin 500 mg three times daily for 5 days
  • Clarithromycin 250-500 mg twice daily for 5 days
  • Erythromycin 250-500 mg four times daily for 5 days

Advise about possible adverse effects, particularly diarrhoea and nausea.

Higher Risk of Complications

Consider
  • Immediate antibiotic
  • Back-up antibiotic prescription (advise to use if symptoms worsen rapidly or significantly)

Specific Conditions

Refer to Specific Guidelines
  • COVID-19: Follow Public Health England advice
  • Acute bronchitis or pneumonia: See CKS topic on Chest infections - adult
  • Acute exacerbation of asthma/COPD/bronchiectasis: See respective CKS topics

Sub-acute Cough Management

Duration: 3-8 weeks

Post-infectious Cough

Most common cause of sub-acute cough
Clinical Features
  • Persistent dry cough following obvious respiratory tract infection
  • Systemically well with normal respiratory examination
  • Usually self-limiting, lasting no longer than 8 weeks
Management Options
  • Explain natural course and that it's usually self-limiting
  • Advise re-attendance if cough doesn't improve after 2 months
  • Consider trial of inhaled ipratropium
  • Inhaled corticosteroids if quality of life affected and cough persists despite ipratropium
  • Oral prednisolone 30-40 mg daily for short period for severe paroxysms (after ruling out other causes)
  • Centrally acting antitussives (codeine, dextromethorphan) if other treatments fail

Chronic Cough Management

Duration: More than 8 weeks

Common Causes

Upper Airway Cough Syndrome

Clinical Features
  • Frequent throat clearing
  • Unpleasant sensation in throat
  • Nasal congestion or discharge
  • Sneezing
  • Visible mucus and cobblestone appearance to posterior oropharyngeal wall
Management
  • Antihistamine (e.g., chlorphenamine)
  • Decongestant (e.g., pseudoephedrine)
  • Avoid allergic/environmental triggers
  • Treat coexisting sinusitis/rhinitis

Asthma

Clinical Features
  • Wheeze, breathlessness
  • Worsening at night, morning, or with exercise
  • Exposure to allergens
  • Reduced peak expiratory flow rate during episodes
Management

See CKS topic on Asthma for detailed management.

Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease

Clinical Features
  • Cough worse during/after eating
  • Worse with talking or bending
Management

See CKS topic on Dyspepsia - proven GORD for detailed management.

Medication-Related Causes

ACE Inhibitor-Induced Cough

Clinical Features
  • Bouts of coughing with airway irritation
  • Onset within hours to months of first dose
Management
  • Stop ACE inhibitor treatment
  • Prescribe alternative antihypertensive
  • Cough usually resolves within 1 month (may persist for several months)

Other Causes

Consider
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
  • Lung cancer
  • Interstitial lung disease
  • Environmental/occupational causes
  • Heart failure
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea
  • Pertussis
  • Pulmonary tuberculosis
  • Sitagliptin-induced cough
  • Somatic cough syndrome

Initial Investigations

Recommended Tests
  • Chest X-ray
  • Full blood count
  • Diagnostic spirometry
  • Fractional exhaled nitric oxide (if available)

Referral Criteria

When to refer to specialist care
Urgent Referral
  • Suspected lung cancer (haemoptysis, persistent chest pain, weight loss, hoarseness, finger clubbing)
  • Suspected pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax
  • Suspected foreign body aspiration
  • Suspected interstitial lung disease
  • Suspected pulmonary tuberculosis
Routine Referral
  • Chronic cough (>8 weeks) with no clear diagnosis after initial assessment and investigation
  • Chronic cough not responding to sequential treatment trials
  • Suspected bronchiectasis (daily sputum production, haemoptysis)
  • Suspected cough-variant asthma or eosinophilic bronchitis requiring specialist confirmation
  • Environmental or occupational causes requiring specialist assessment
Consider Specialist Input
  • Recurrent acute coughs that may represent an undiagnosed chronic condition
  • Cough with significant impact on quality of life despite initial management
  • Somatic cough syndrome suspected after extensive evaluation
