Stable Angina: Primary Care Algorithm

A Quick Reference Guide for Clinicians (based on NICE CKS, May 2025)

1. Initial Presentation & Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment

Classify pain based on 3 key features:

  • Constricting discomfort (chest, neck, jaw, arms).
  • Precipitated by physical exertion.
  • Relieved by rest or GTN in ~5 mins.

Classification & Action

  • Typical (3/3) or Atypical (2/3) Angina: Refer to specialist chest pain service & organize a resting 12-lead ECG.
  • Non-Anginal Pain (0-1/3): Exclude diagnosis unless other factors raise high suspicion.

Management While Awaiting Diagnosis

  • Prescribe sublingual GTN for symptom relief.
  • Provide strict "999" instructions.
  • Consider Aspirin 75mg daily if highly likely.

2. Managing a New Diagnosis

Immediate Symptom Relief (All Patients)

Prescribe sublingual GTN (spray or tablets).

Patient "999" Instructions:
  1. Rest & use 1 dose of GTN.
  2. If no relief after 5 mins, take a 2nd dose.
  3. If pain persists 5 mins after 2nd dose, call 999.

First-Line Symptom Prevention

Offer monotherapy with EITHER a Beta-Blocker OR a Calcium-Channel Blocker.

Secondary Prevention (CV Protection)

  • Aspirin: 75mg once daily.
  • Statin: High-intensity (e.g., Atorvastatin 80mg).
  • ACE Inhibitor: Strongly consider.
  • Lifestyle Advice: Smoking, diet, exercise.
Patient Advice Highlight: Contraindication of Nitrates/Nicorandil with PDE-5 inhibitors (e.g., Sildenafil). Advise patient to call 999 if chest pain occurs post-PDE5i use.

3. Ongoing Management & Escalation

Treatment Steps for Uncontrolled Symptoms

Optimize Monotherapy: Ensure max tolerated dose of BB or CCB.
Dual Therapy: If on BB, add dihydropyridine CCB (e.g., Amlodipine). If on CCB, add a BB.
!! SAFETY WARNING !!
NEVER combine a Beta-Blocker with a rate-limiting CCB (Verapamil or Diltiazem).
Third-line Options: While awaiting specialist review, consider adding: Long-acting Nitrate, Ivabradine, Nicorandil, or Ranolazine.

Routine Review (6-12 months)

Assess symptoms, adherence, BP/HR, and review medications and risk factors.

4. Red Flags: Refer or Admit

Immediate Hospital Admission

Suspect unstable angina if patient has:

  • Pain at rest.
  • Pain on minimal exertion.
  • Rapidly progressing symptoms despite treatment.

Early Cardiologist Referral

  • Symptoms persist on optimal dual therapy.
  • Diagnosis is in doubt.
  • Significant ECG abnormalities (LBBB, Q waves).
  • Co-existing heart failure, AF, or suspected aortic stenosis (murmur).

This is a quick reference tool and not a substitute for clinical judgment. Always consult full, up-to-date clinical guidelines.
