Key Symptoms

Physical Examination Findings

Click to view key examination findings
  • Systolic ejection murmur at left sternal border
    • Accentuated by: standing, Valsalva, exercise
    • Decreased by: squatting, handgrip
  • LV lift/heave at apex
  • Double apical impulse (systolic anterior motion)
  • S4 gallop (stiff ventricle)
  • Irregularly irregular pulse (atrial fibrillation)

Red Flag Features

Syncope with exertion → Urgent cardiology referral
Family history of sudden death → Immediate screening
Diagnosis & Investigations

Initial Diagnostic Tests

12-lead ECG: Required for all patients
Normal in 5-10% Abnormal in 90-95%
Transthoracic Echo: Diagnostic imaging modality
Diagnostic Criteria: LV wall thickness ≥15mm
  • Borderline: 13-14mm + family history/genetic mutation
  • Asymmetric septal hypertrophy typical
  • Assess: LVOTO, systolic/diastolic function, SAM

Laboratory Tests

European Guidelines Recommend: CK, LFTs, renal function, NT-proBNP, troponin, urinalysis

US guidelines do not require routine labs for initial evaluation

Genetic Testing

60% sensitivity for common sarcomeric mutations
Cascade screening recommended for families
When to consider genetic testing
  • Confirm diagnosis when unclear
  • Screen first-degree relatives
  • Reproductive counseling
  • Identify genotype-positive/phenotype-negative individuals

Treatment & Management

Asymptomatic Patients

Observation & Monitoring
  • Clinical assessment every 1-2 years (children/adolescents)
  • Every 3-5 years in adults
  • Annual follow-up if high-risk features
  • Genotype-positive/phenotype-negative: competitive sports reasonable
No therapy required unless high-risk features develop

Symptomatic LVOTO (EF ≥50%)

First-Line: Negative inotropic therapy

Beta-Blockers (First-Line)

Atenolol
50-100 mg orally once daily
Metoprolol
100-450 mg/day in 2-3 divided doses
Propranolol (SR)
80-160 mg orally once daily

Non-DHP Calcium Channel Blockers (Alternative)

Verapamil
Consult specialist for dosing
⚠️ Avoid in severe LVOTO (>100mmHg) or hypotension
Diltiazem
Consult specialist for dosing
Advanced therapy for persistent symptoms
Mavacamten (Cardiac Myosin Inhibitor)
2.5-15 mg orally once daily
EF must be ≥55% to initiate
  • Add-on to beta-blocker/CCB
  • Monitor with serial echocardiograms
  • Interrupt if EF drops <50%
Disopyramide (Type Ia Antiarrhythmic)
200-250 mg twice daily (controlled-release)
Use with AV nodal blocking agent (beta-blocker/CCB)

Septal Reduction Therapy

Indicated for refractory severe symptoms
Criteria: NYHA III/IV symptoms + LVOT gradient ≥50mmHg
Decision Point: Surgical myectomy vs. Alcohol septal ablation

Surgical Myectomy

  • Gold standard treatment
  • Abolishes gradient in >90% cases
  • Long-term symptomatic benefit in >80%
  • Consider if: younger age (<50), need for concomitant surgery
Complications: Complete heart block (3-10%), LBBB (40-56%)

Alcohol Septal Ablation (ASA)

  • Alternative to surgery in selected patients
  • Similar symptom improvement
  • Gradual remodelling over months
Complications: Need for permanent pacemaker (>10%)

ICD Indications

Secondary Prevention (Class I)
  • Survived cardiac arrest
  • Sustained VT causing syncope/hemodynamic compromise
Primary Prevention (Consider if ≥1 major risk factor)
  • Max LV wall thickness ≥30mm
  • Unexplained syncope (suspected arrhythmic)
  • NSVT + other risk factors
  • LV apical aneurysm
  • Extensive late gadolinium enhancement (>15%)
  • LVEF <50%
Follow-up & Monitoring

Monitoring Intervals

At Diagnosis: Comprehensive assessment
Low Risk: Every 1-2 years (clinical, ECG, echo)
High Risk: Every 6-12 months
Change in Status: Re-evaluate promptly

Complications to Monitor

Exercise & Lifestyle

Encourage moderate exercise (150 min/week)
Avoid high-intensity competitive sports (unless specialist clearance)
Recent Evidence: Vigorous exercise in experienced centers does not increase mortality for selected patients

Family Screening

First-degree relatives: ECG + Echo at diagnosis
Children/Adolescents: Repeat every 12-24 months
Adults: Repeat every 3-5 years (lifelong)
Genetic testing: Cascade screening if mutation identified

Prognosis

Benign prognosis in majority
  • 90% asymptomatic at presentation
  • Annual mortality: 0.9% (asymptomatic) vs 1.9% (symptomatic)
  • Annual SCD: 0.1% (asymptomatic) vs 1.4% (symptomatic)
End-stage HCM: Poor prognosis
  • Develops in 2-15% of patients
  • Mean time to transplant/death: 2.7 years
  • LVEF <50% indicates high risk
