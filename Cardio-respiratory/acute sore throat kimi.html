Acute Sore Throat Management

Evidence-based algorithm for primary care management of acute sore throat in patients from birth onwards, based on NICE Clinical Knowledge Summaries (September 2024)

Initial Assessment

Red Flag Assessment

Immediate Emergency Referral
Arrange 999 ambulance transfer for suspected epiglottitis. Do NOT examine the throat as this may precipitate airway closure.
1
Airway Assessment
Check for breathing difficulty, stridor, drooling, or muffled voice
2
Systemic Illness
Assess for signs of sepsis, dehydration, or marked systemic illness

Clinical Examination

Throat Examination
Look for pharyngeal/tonsillar exudate, erythema, and swelling
Neck Examination
Check for cervical lymphadenopathy or lymphadenitis
Temperature
Document fever >38°C or >38.5°C
Hydration Status
Assess fluid intake, especially in children

Key Questions

•
Symptom Duration
When did symptoms start? Rapid onset suggests bacterial cause
•
Cough Presence
Absence of cough increases likelihood of streptococcal infection
•
Recent Contacts
Any known exposure to streptococcal infection?

Risk Factors

Age Group
Streptococcal infection more common in ages 5-15 years
Season
Bacterial pharyngitis more common in winter/early spring
Immunocompromise
Check for DMARDs, chemotherapy, HIV, asplenia
Drug History
Carbimazole (neutropenia risk), immunosuppressants

Diagnostic Evaluation

Clinical Prediction Scores

Use either FeverPAIN or Centor criteria to determine likelihood of streptococcal infection:

FeverPAIN Score
1 point each (max 5):
• Fever (previous 24h)
• Purulence (exudate)
• Attend rapidly (<3 days)
• Severely Inflamed tonsils
• No cough/coryza
Centor Criteria
1 point each (max 4):
• Tonsillar exudate
• Tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy
• History of fever (>38°C)
• Absence of cough

Differential Diagnosis

Viral Pharyngitis
Rhinorrhoea, nasal congestion, cough, hoarseness
Infectious Mononucleosis
Prolonged duration, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly
Scarlet Fever
Characteristic rash, erythrogenic toxin-producing strain
Gonococcal Pharyngitis
Consider in sexually active individuals with negative GAS cultures

Investigations

•
Rapid Antigen Testing
Not routinely recommended by NICE Use clinical prediction scores instead
•
Throat Swab
Consider for suspected gonococcal, chlamydial, or diphtheria infection
•
Monospot Test
For suspected infectious mononucleosis in patients >4 years
•
Full Blood Count
If immunocompromised or suspected hematological disorder

Severity Indicators

High Likelihood of Streptococcus
FeverPAIN 4-5 or Centor 3-4 → Consider immediate or backup antibiotic prescription
Moderate Likelihood
FeverPAIN 2-3 → Consider no antibiotic or backup prescription
Low Likelihood
FeverPAIN 0-1 or Centor 0-2 → Do not offer antibiotic prescription

Treatment Strategy

Antibiotic Prescribing Strategy

1
Systemically Very Unwell
Immediate Prescription Offer immediate antibiotic if high-risk or systemically unwell
2
FeverPAIN 4-5 or Centor 3-4
Consider Options Immediate OR backup antibiotic prescription
3
FeverPAIN 2-3
Consider Options No antibiotic OR backup prescription
4
FeverPAIN 0-1 or Centor 0-2
No Antibiotic Do not offer antibiotic prescription

Antibiotic Choices

First-line: Phenoxymethylpenicillin
Adults: 500mg QDS or 1g BD (5-10 days)
Children: Weight-based dosing
5-day course for symptomatic cure, 10-day for microbiological cure
Penicillin Allergy: Clarithromycin
Adults: 250-500mg BD (5 days)
Children: Weight-based dosing
Contra-indicated with ivabradine, caution with QT prolongation risk
Pregnancy: Erythromycin
Adults: 250-500mg QDS or 500mg-1g BD (5 days)
Preferred macrolide in pregnancy
Specific Infections
• Gonococcal: Cefixime + azithromycin
• Chlamydial: Doxycycline or azithromycin
• Candidal: Nystatin or fluconazole

Backup Prescribing

•
When to Use Backup Prescription
Advise use if symptoms don't improve within 3-5 days, or worsen rapidly at any time
•
Patient Education
Explain that antibiotics are not needed immediately and why

Supportive Care

Analgesia
• Paracetamol: first-line for pain/fever
• Ibuprofen: NSAID option if appropriate
Medicated Lozenges
Containing local anaesthetic, NSAID, or antiseptic for temporary relief
Fluids & Rest
Adequate fluid intake and rest during illness
Avoid Hot Drinks
Hot drinks may exacerbate pain

Hospital Admission Criteria

Immediate Hospital Referral
• Breathing difficulty or airway compromise
• Clinical dehydration
• Peri/retro-pharyngeal abscess or cellulitis
• Signs of sepsis or marked systemic illness
• Suspected rare causes (Kawasaki disease, diphtheria, etc.)
•
Vulnerable Groups
Use clinical judgment for infants, elderly, immunocompromised patients

Follow-up & Monitoring

Expected Course

3 Days
40% of cases spontaneously resolve
1 Week
85% of people are symptom-free
Infectious Mononucleosis
Symptoms resolve in 1-2 weeks, but lethargy may persist
Recurrent Tonsillitis
>7 episodes/year (1 year), >5/year (2 years), or >3/year (3 years)

Red Flags for Review

Seek Urgent Medical Help
• Difficulty swallowing saliva or liquids
• Breathing difficulty develops
• One-sided neck or throat swelling
• Symptoms worsen rapidly or significantly
• No improvement after 1 week

Antibiotic Review

•
No Improvement
If no improvement after 3-4 days of antibiotics → review diagnosis
•
Persistent Fever
If fever >38.3°C persists after 3 days → review treatment

Return to Normal Activities

•
Children
Return to school/day care when fever resolved and feeling well, OR after 24 hours of antibiotics
•
Adults
Return to work when feeling well and fever-free

Surgical Referral

•
Tonsillectomy Criteria
Frequency Thresholds >7 episodes in 1 year, OR
>5 episodes/year for 2 years, OR
>3 episodes/year for 3 years
•
ENT Referral
Refer to ENT specialist if meeting above criteria and no other explanation

Patient Education

Self-care Advice
Adequate fluids, rest, analgesia use, and expected duration
Antibiotic Awareness
Explain why antibiotics may not be needed and resistance concerns
Warning Signs
When to seek urgent medical attention
Follow-up Plan
When and how to seek further medical advice

No Routine Follow-up

Self-limiting Condition
Routine follow-up is not required for uncomplicated acute sore throat. Advise patient to seek medical help if red flag symptoms develop.
