Quick Overview

A varicocele is a scrotal swelling consisting of a collection of dilated veins of the pampiniform plexus in the spermatic cord.

Grading system:

Sub-clinical: Detected only by Doppler ultrasound Grade I: Palpable only with Valsalva manoeuvre Grade II: Palpable without Valsalva manoeuvre Grade III: Visible through the scrotal skin

Varicoceles occur in 14% to 20% of males from adolescence onwards.

Approximately 40% of men with infertility have a varicocele.

Primary causes: Incompetent or absent valves in the testicular vein

Why left side predominates (90%):

  • Left internal spermatic vein drains vertically into left renal vein at a right angle
  • Left internal spermatic vein is 8-10 cm longer, increasing hydrostatic pressure

Secondary causes (rare): Tumors or other pathological processes obstructing the spermatic vein

Complication Details
Pain Uncommon (<3% of cases)
Testicular growth issues 70% of adolescent males with grade II-III varicocele show left testicular volume loss
Fertility problems 20% of males with varicocele will have fertility problems
Clinical Features & Assessment
Typical Presentation
Painless scrotal swelling (90% on left side)
'Bag of worms' appearance above testis
Scrotum hangs lower on affected side
Dilation increases on standing, decreases when lying down
Valsalva manoeuvre increases dilation
Assessment Approach
Clinical diagnosis is usually sufficient
Ultrasound with Doppler if diagnostic uncertainty
Examine adolescents in supine and standing positions
Consider semen analysis and FSH/testosterone for men
Management Guidelines
Sub-clinical or Grade I

Provide advice and reassurance

Grade II or III with Symmetrical Testes

Primary indication for surgery is testicular growth arrest

Grade II or III with Asymmetrical Testes
Sub-clinical or Grade I

Offer semen analysis if fertility is a concern

Grade II or III Asymptomatic with Normal Semen

Semen analysis every 1-2 years if clinically appropriate

Grade II or III Symptomatic or with Abnormal Semen
Referral Guidelines
Urgent Referral
Sudden onset painful varicocele
Varicocele that doesn't drain when lying down
Routine Referral
Pain or discomfort
Adolescents with testicular volume concerns
Symptomatic varicocele or abnormal semen parameters
Uncertainty about nature of scrotal swelling
Special Note

Do not routinely refer the male partner of an infertile couple for varicocele surgery solely as a form of fertility treatment. However, consider seeking specialist advice for men with clinical varicocele, abnormal semen parameters, and otherwise unexplained infertility.

Patient Advice
When No Treatment is Required
Reassure that varicocele is unlikely to cause symptoms or long-term complications
Explain that nearly two-thirds of men with varicocele have no difficulty fathering children
Managing Discomfort
Supportive underwear
Simple analgesia
