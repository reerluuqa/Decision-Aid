Anticoagulants Overview

Oral anticoagulants are used to prevent thrombus formation or extension of existing thrombus in the venous circulation. Two main types:

  • Vitamin K antagonists (VKAs): Warfarin
  • Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs): Apixaban, Dabigatran, Edoxaban, Rivaroxaban

Red Flags

  • Active clinically significant bleeding
  • CrCl < 15 mL/min (for DOACs)
  • Severe hepatic impairment with coagulopathy
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome (DOACs)
  • Prosthetic heart valves (DOACs)

Safe Practices

  • Regular monitoring of renal function
  • Review for drug interactions
  • Assess bleeding risk regularly
  • Patient education on signs of bleeding
  • Consider dose adjustments in elderly/frail

Common Indications

Atrial Fibrillation

Prevention of stroke and systemic embolism in NVAF with CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥ 2

Pulmonary Embolism

Treatment and prevention of recurrent PE

Deep Vein Thrombosis

Treatment and prevention of recurrent DVT

Orthopedic Surgery

VTE prophylaxis after hip/knee replacement

Monitoring Requirements

DOACs

4-6 monthly for ≥75 years or frail patients; 6-12 monthly for others

Warfarin

Regular INR monitoring (target 2-3 for most indications)

Renal Function

Check at least annually, more frequently in elderly or if deteriorating

Liver Function

Check if clinically indicated, especially with DOACs

Key Drug Interactions

Strong Interactions (Avoid or Use with Caution)

  • NSAIDs: Increased bleeding risk with all anticoagulants
  • Strong CYP3A4/P-gp Inhibitors: Increase DOAC levels (e.g., ketoconazole, ritonavir)
  • Strong CYP3A4/P-gp Inducers: Decrease DOAC levels (e.g., rifampicin, carbamazepine)
  • Antiplatelets: Increased bleeding risk

Special Considerations

  • Antibiotics: May affect warfarin metabolism
  • SSRIs/SNRIs: Possible increased bleeding risk
  • Amiodarone: Increases apixaban levels (no dose adjustment needed)
  • Verapamil/Diltiazem: Increase apixaban levels (no dose adjustment needed)
CONTRAINDICATED: CrCl < 15 mL/min, significant liver disease with coagulopathy, prosthetic heart valves, antiphospholipid syndrome, active clinically significant bleeding.

Adverse Effects

  • Common: Bleeding, anemia, bruising, nausea, skin reactions
  • Uncommon: Hypersensitivity, thrombocytopenia, deranged LFTs
  • Rare: Angioedema, cutaneous vasculitis, anticoagulant-related nephropathy
REVERSAL: Andexanet alfa (Ondexxya®) for life-threatening or uncontrolled bleeding.

Avoid With

  • Other anticoagulants (except when switching)

Use With Caution

  • NSAIDs (increased bleeding risk)

  • SSRIs/SNRIs (possible increased bleeding)

Switching Anticoagulants

From Warfarin to Apixaban

  • Stop warfarin and measure INR
  • If INR < 2: Start apixaban
  • If INR 2-2.5: Start apixaban immediately or next day
  • If INR > 2.5: Wait until INR < 2

From Apixaban to Warfarin

  • Start warfarin but continue apixaban
  • After ≥2 days of concurrent therapy, measure INR before next apixaban dose
  • If INR in target range: Stop apixaban
  • If INR not in target range: Continue both until INR in range
CONTRAINDICATED: CrCl < 30 mL/min, significant liver disease with coagulopathy, prosthetic heart valves, antiphospholipid syndrome, active clinically significant bleeding.

INITIATION: Usually 5mg daily for 2-3 days, then adjusted based on INR. Elderly or frail may require lower starting dose.

Adverse Effects

  • Common: Bleeding, skin necrosis (rare, usually within first week)
  • Uncommon: Purple toe syndrome, calciphylaxis
  • Rare: Hepatotoxicity, hypersensitivity
REVERSAL: Vitamin K (phytonadione) orally or IV for severe bleeding; Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (PCC) for life-threatening bleeding.

Key Drug Interactions

Increase INR (Bleeding Risk)

  • Antibiotics (especially macrolides, quinolones)
  • Amiodarone, statins, fibrates
  • SSRIs, SNRIs
  • NSAIDs, aspirin
  • Allopurinol, cimetidine

Decrease INR (Thrombosis Risk)

  • Carbamazepine, phenytoin, rifampicin
  • St. John's Wort
  • Oral contraceptives
  • High vitamin K intake (leafy greens)
  • Chronic alcohol use

INR Management

High INR Without Bleeding

  • INR 5.0-8.0: Omit 1-2 doses, consider vitamin K 1-2.5mg orally
  • INR >8.0: Omit doses, vitamin K 2.5-5mg orally
  • If high bleeding risk: Consider vitamin K even at lower INR levels

High INR With Bleeding

  • Minor bleeding: Hold warfarin, vitamin K 2.5-5mg orally/IV
  • Major bleeding: Hold warfarin, vitamin K 5-10mg IV slow infusion, PCC
  • Life-threatening: PCC, vitamin K 10mg IV, FFP if PCC unavailable
EMERGENCY SITUATION: Life-threatening bleeding requires immediate reversal of anticoagulation.

Emergency Red Flags

Neurological

  • Sudden severe headache
  • Confusion or altered consciousness
  • Focal neurological deficits
  • Seizures

Respiratory

  • Hemoptysis
  • Dyspnea
  • Chest pain

Gastrointestinal

  • Hematemesis
  • Malena
  • Severe abdominal pain

Genitourinary

  • Macroscopic hematuria
  • Severe flank pain
PATIENT ADVICE: All patients on anticoagulants should carry an alert card and seek immediate medical attention for any uncontrolled bleeding.
