EMERGENCY: Severe hypertension (≥160/110 mmHg) or symptoms of pre-eclampsia requires immediate assessment and hospital admission.
Red Flag Symptoms
  • Severe headache (unrelieved by analgesics)
  • Visual disturbances (blurring, flashing lights)
  • Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Sudden swelling of face, hands, or feet
  • Breathlessness (pulmonary edema)
Critical BP Readings
≥160/110 mmHg
Severe Hypertension

Action: Admit immediately for full assessment

140-159/90-109 mmHg
Hypertension

Action: Refer to specialist if symptoms present

Immediate Actions

For severe hypertension or pre-eclampsia symptoms, arrange immediate assessment by healthcare professional trained in managing hypertension in pregnancy.

Definitions
Condition Definition Prevalence
Chronic Hypertension Hypertension present at booking visit or before 20 weeks gestation 0.6-2.7% of pregnancies
Gestational Hypertension New hypertension after 20 weeks without significant proteinuria 4.2-7.9% of pregnancies
Pre-eclampsia New hypertension after 20 weeks with proteinuria or organ dysfunction 1.5-7.7% of pregnancies
HELLP Syndrome Haemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets 0.5-7.6 per 1000 deliveries
Eclampsia Seizures in a woman with pre-eclampsia 5 per 10,000 births
Pre-eclampsia Diagnostic Criteria

New hypertension after 20 weeks AND at least one of:

  • Proteinuria (≥30 mg/mmol protein:creatinine ratio)
  • Renal insufficiency (creatinine ≥90 μmol/L)
  • Liver involvement (elevated transaminases)
  • Neurological complications
  • Haematological complications
  • Uteroplacental dysfunction
Key Points
  • Blood pressure tends to fall in first and second trimesters
  • A diastolic BP of 90 mmHg or greater on two occasions more than 4 hours apart requires increased surveillance
  • A single diastolic BP reading greater than 110 mmHg requires immediate action
  • Do not use first morning urine void to quantify proteinuria
High Risk Factors for Pre-eclampsia

Women with ONE of the following are at high risk:

  • Previous hypertensive disease in pregnancy
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)
  • Type 1 or type 2 diabetes
  • Chronic hypertension
Moderate Risk Factors

Women with TWO or MORE of the following are at increased risk:

  • First pregnancy
  • Age 40 years or older
  • Pregnancy interval >10 years
  • BMI ≥35 kg/m²
  • Family history of pre-eclampsia
  • Multiple pregnancy

Management Goals
  • Diagnose raised blood pressure during pregnancy and postpartum
  • Provide appropriate management to control blood pressure
  • Refer to secondary care appropriately
  • Detect pre-eclampsia early and admit for assessment
Blood Pressure Targets

For pregnant women taking antihypertensive medication:

Target: ≤135/85 mmHg

For severe hypertension:

≥160/110 mmHg
Admit for assessment
Antihypertensive Treatment

First-line options:

  • Labetalol
  • Nifedipine (modified-release)
  • Methyldopa (if other options not tolerated)

Avoid:

  • ACE inhibitors
  • Angiotensin II receptor blockers
Monitoring Recommendations

For All Pregnant Women:

  • Measure blood pressure at each antenatal visit
  • Dipstick urine for protein at each visit
  • If dipstick positive [1+ or more], quantify proteinuria

For Women with Hypertension:

  • Increased surveillance based on severity
  • More frequent monitoring if pre-eclampsia develops
  • Specialist input for management decisions
Postpartum Follow-up

At 6-8 week postnatal medical check:

  • Discuss future pregnancy and lifetime cardiovascular risks
  • Review need for ongoing antihypertensive treatment
  • Ensure postnatal care plan includes antihypertensive management
  • Communicate plan to GP when transferred to community care
Long-term Health Implications
Condition Long-term Cardiovascular Risk
Gestational Hypertension 2-4× increased risk of hypertension
1.5-3× increased risk of major cardiovascular event
Chronic Hypertension 1.8-4× increased risk of stroke
1.7-2.2× increased risk of major cardiovascular event
Pre-eclampsia 2-5× increased risk of hypertension
2-3× increased risk of stroke
2× increased risk of cardiovascular mortality

Additional risks: Type 2 diabetes, hyperlipidaemia, heart failure, atrial fibrillation, vascular dementia, and venous thromboembolism.

Breastfeeding and Antihypertensives

Compatible with breastfeeding:

  • Labetalol
  • Nifedipine
  • Methyldopa
  • Enalapril
  • Captopril

Use with caution:

  • Atenolol (may accumulate in infant)
  • ACE inhibitors (monitor infant for hypotension)
