Definition

Autosomal codominant genetic disorder caused by mutations in the SERPINA1 gene. Results in decreased circulating plasma levels of alpha-1 antitrypsin, leading to unopposed protease activity causing lung damage and protein polymerization causing liver injury.

AAT = alpha-1 antitrypsin = alpha-1 proteinase inhibitor

Key Manifestations

  • Panacinar emphysema (basilar predominant)

    Most common pulmonary manifestation

  • COPD with early onset (32-41 years)

    ~60% develop severe pulmonary disease

  • Liver disease (cirrhosis, HCC)

    Second most common cause of liver transplant in children

  • Rare: Panniculitis, Granulomatosis with polyangiitis

Major Risk Factors

  • Smoking — Greatest risk factor for emphysema in PI*ZZ

    Affects both lung function and survival

  • Occupational exposures — Gas, fumes, dust, passive smoking
  • Alcohol consumption — Increases liver disease risk in PI*ZZ
  • Family history of AAT deficiency

Epidemiology

PI*ZZ prevalence (US) 1 in 4,455
Z allele carriers ~59,000 in US
Highest risk Northern European ancestry
Mean age of presentation 32-41 years (smokers)
COPD + PI*ZZ 1 in 408 (Northern Europe)

WHEN TO TEST FOR AAT DEFICIENCY

Test ALL patients with COPD or adult-onset asthma. Consider testing in unexplained liver disease, bronchiectasis, or panniculitis.

Indications for Testing

  • All patients with COPD
  • All patients with adult-onset asthma
  • Airflow obstruction (partially/irreversible with bronchodilators)
  • Liver disease of unknown etiology
  • Bronchiectasis of unknown etiology (especially with panacinar emphysema)
  • Panniculitis of unknown etiology
  • c-ANCA vasculitis (granulomatosis with polyangiitis)
  • Sibling of AAT homozygous patient

Additional Workup

System Tests
Pulmonary PFTs, CXR, CT chest (panacinar emphysema, lower lobes)
Hepatic LFTs, AFP, ultrasound annually, CT/MRI if indicated
Exercise ABG analysis if dyspnea out of proportion

Essential Interventions

  • SMOKING CESSATION — CRITICAL

    Worst FEV1 decline in smokers; no difference between ex-smokers and never-smokers

  • Hepatitis A & B vaccination

    For PI*ZZ, PI*Mmalton, PI*Siiyama phenotypes

  • Alcohol avoidance

    <60g/day if liver disease present

  • Pollution avoidance

    Occupational exposures, passive smoke

Standard COPD Management

  • Short and long-acting bronchodilators
  • Inhaled corticosteroids
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation
  • Oxygen therapy if indicated
  • Antibiotics for exacerbations
  • Oral corticosteroids (acute)
  • Vaccinations (influenza, pneumococcal)

AAT Augmentation Therapy

Criteria Recommendation
Indication AAT <11 μmol/L + airflow obstruction
FEV1 range 25-65% predicted (some guidelines)
Smoking status Never-smoker or ex-smoker
Dose 60 mg/kg IV weekly
Duration Indefinite
Check IgA Before starting (anaphylaxis risk if IgA near zero)

⚠️ NICE (UK) does NOT recommend AAT replacement therapy.

End-Stage Disease

Intervention Criteria
Lung Transplant FEV1 <25% or chronic CO2 retention
5-year survival: ~50%
Liver Transplant End-stage liver disease
5-year survival: 79-80%

Patient Counseling Points

  • Family screening recommended — autosomal codominant inheritance
  • Genetic counseling for siblings, children, and partners
  • Avoid smoking and secondhand smoke
  • Limit alcohol intake if liver involvement
  • Report respiratory infections early

Monitoring

  • Pulmonary: PFTs annually (FEV1 decline >120mL/year = consider augmentation)
  • Hepatic: LFTs, AFP annually; liver ultrasound annually for at-risk phenotypes
  • Screen family members — siblings, children, parents

RED FLAGS — REQUIRES IMMEDIATE ACTION

Critical clinical scenarios requiring urgent intervention or escalation.

Respiratory Emergencies

  • Severe COPD exacerbation with respiratory failure

    PaO2 <60mmHg, PaCO2 rising, pH <7.35

  • FEV1 <25% predicted

    Consider lung transplant evaluation

  • Chronic CO2 retention

    Sign of end-stage lung disease

  • Rapid FEV1 decline >120 mL/year

    Consider augmentation therapy if not already on it

Hepatic Emergencies

  • Hepatic encephalopathy

    Confusion, asterixis, altered mental status

  • Variceal bleeding

    Hematemesis, melena, hemodynamic instability

  • Ascites with complications

    Refractory ascites, SBP

  • Suspected hepatocellular carcinoma

    Rising AFP, abnormal liver imaging

  • Acute liver failure

    INR >1.5, encephalopathy, jaundice

Testing Urgency — DON'T MISS

  • Any COPD patient NOT yet tested

    Testing all COPD patients is a quality measure

  • Adult-onset asthma with fixed airflow obstruction

    WHO recommends testing all adult-onset asthma

  • Unexplained liver disease in young patient

    Consider AAT deficiency especially with family history

  • Panacinar emphysema on CT

    Classic finding — lower lobe predominant

Life-Saving Interventions

  • IMMEDIATE smoking cessation

    Single most impactful intervention

  • Vaccinate against Hep A & B

    For PI*ZZ, PI*Mmalton, PI*Siiyama

  • Refer for transplant evaluation early

    FEV1 <25% or decompensated liver disease

  • Avoid alcohol completely

    If any liver involvement

Inheritance Pattern

PI*ZZ + PI*ZZ = 100% PI*ZZ
PI*ZZ + PI*MM = 100% PI*MZ
PI*MZ + PI*MM = 50% PI*MM, 50% PI*MZ
PI*MZ + PI*MZ = 25% PI*MM, 50% PI*MZ, 25% PI*ZZ

Family Screening Recommendations

  • First-degree relatives — Offer genetic counseling and screening
  • Siblings — Highest priority for screening
  • Children — Discuss screening; parental decision
  • Partners — Discuss carrier testing
  • Reproductive counseling — Important for family planning

Diagnostic Algorithm

1

Clinical Suspicion

COPD, adult-onset asthma, unexplained liver disease, bronchiectasis, panniculitis, or family history of AAT deficiency

↓
2

Order Serum AAT Level

Quantitative test (nephelometry). Remember: AAT is acute-phase reactant — inflammation can normalize levels

↓
3

Interpret Results

<20 μmol/L = Deficient | <11 μmol/L = Inadequate protection

↓
4

Confirmatory Testing

Phenotyping (quick) or genotyping (definitive) to identify specific allele variants

↓
5

Full Assessment

PFTs, CXR/CT chest, LFTs, AFP, liver ultrasound. Screen family members if diagnosis confirmed.

Treatment Algorithm

1

Essential for ALL

Smoking cessation, pollution avoidance, Hep A&B vaccination (if PI*ZZ, Mmalton, Siiyama)

↓
2

Manage Manifestations

Standard COPD treatment OR standard liver disease treatment based on organ involvement

↓
3

Evaluate for Augmentation

AAT <11 μmol/L + FEV1 25-65% predicted + never/ex-smoker

↓
4

End-Stage Disease

Consider lung transplant (FEV1 <25%) or liver transplant

Key Decision Points

Question Action
Does patient smoke? URGENT — Smoking cessation is #1 priority
PI*ZZ phenotype? URGENT — Vaccinate Hep A&B, liver monitoring
FEV1 25-65% + AAT <11? Consider — AAT augmentation therapy
FEV1 <25% or decompensated liver? URGENT — Transplant evaluation
Family member with AAT deficiency? Screen — Offer genetic counseling
Heterozygote (MZ, MS)? Reassure — Augmentation not indicated; smoking cessation key

AAT Level Thresholds

Protective Threshold <11 μmol/L = Inadequate
Deficient 11-20 μmol/L
Normal >20 μmol/L
