Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)

Straight to the point of care. Last updated: Jun 10, 2025

Suspected Rupture / Symptomatic

Presentation

Suspect rupture in ANY patient with:

  • New abdominal and/or back pain
  • Cardiovascular collapse
  • Loss of consciousness
High Risk if: Age >60, Male, Smoker, Family History, Hypertension.

ACT NOW Immediate Actions

  1. Get help: Senior colleague & Vascular service.
  2. Imaging: Bedside Aortic Ultrasound OR CTA.
    (Do not delay for results if clinical certainty is high).
  3. Resuscitation: Permissive hypotension.
70-90 mmHg

Target Systolic BP (ESVS)

(Aim for 90-120mmHg if awaiting transfer)

The Classic Triad (Present in ~50% of cases)

Diagnostic Pathway
Modality Indication Notes
Aortic Ultrasound First-line for screening and initial diagnosis. Bedside US for suspected rupture.
Limitation: Cannot rule out rupture definitively if aorta looks normal in hypotensive patient.
CT Angiography (CTA) Definitive imaging. Used for anatomical mapping (pre-op planning) and confirming rupture.
Essential for surgical planning (EVAR vs Open).
PET-CT Specialized use for inflammatory aneurysm or infection (mycotic).

Definition

Diameter >3.0 cm or >1.5 times the expected AP diameter.

>90% originate below renal arteries.

Symptoms (Unruptured)

Usually asymptomatic/incidental.

If symptomatic: Abdominal, flank, or back pain.

Management Overview

Repair Indications

  • Ruptured / Symptomatic: Urgent repair.
  • Asymptomatic: Surgery preferred when rupture risk > operative mortality risk.
  • Size Threshold: Usually ‚â•5.5 cm.
  • Rapid Growth: >4.0 cm and growing >1 cm/year.
