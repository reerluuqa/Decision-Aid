Hypertension Classification
1
Stage 1 Hypertension: Clinic BP 140/90-159/99 mmHg; ABPM/HBPM 135/85-149/94 mmHg
2
Stage 2 Hypertension: Clinic BP 160/100-179/119 mmHg; ABPM/HBPM ≥150/95 mmHg
3
Stage 3/Severe: Clinic SBP ≥180 or DBP ≥120 mmHg
4
Accelerated/Malignant: BP ≥180/120 mmHg + retinal hemorrhage/papilledema
Note: White-coat hypertension is defined by a discrepancy of >20/10 mmHg between clinic and average daytime ABPM/HBPM. Masked hypertension occurs when clinic BP is normal but home/ambulatory readings are elevated.
Diagnostic Approach
1
Clinic BP ≥140/90 mmHg
2
Confirm with ABPM or HBPM
3
Exclude white-coat effect (discrepancy >20/10 mmHg)
4
Consider secondary causes if indicated
When to Suspect Secondary Hypertension
  • Resistant to treatment
  • Age <30 or sudden onset
  • Significant difference in BP between arms
  • Associated symptoms: abdominal/loin mass, hematuria, sweating attacks, palpitations, Cushingoid features
  • Femoral pulse delay, abdominal bruit
Treatment Targets
  • Age <80 years: Clinic BP <140/90 mmHg
  • Age ≥80 years: Clinic BP <150/90 mmHg
Management Approach
1
Assess CV risk + lifestyle modification
2
Stage 1 with ↑ CV risk or Stage 2-3
3
Consider pharmacological treatment
4
Annual review of CV risk factors
Red Flags Requiring Immediate Attention
  • BP ≥180/120 mmHg with signs of end-organ damage
  • Acute symptoms: chest pain, severe headache, visual disturbances
  • Suspected accelerated/malignant hypertension

Complications to Screen For
  • Heart failure
  • Coronary artery disease
  • Stroke/TIA
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Peripheral arterial disease
  • Vascular dementia
Prognosis: Every 10 mmHg reduction in BP results in 17% reduction in coronary heart disease, 27% reduction in stroke, and 28% reduction in heart failure.

This reference does not cover hypertension in pregnancy, chronic kidney disease, type 1 diabetes, or children.

Refer to specific NICE CKS topics for these conditions.
