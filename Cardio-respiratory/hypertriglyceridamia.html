Hypertriglyceridaemia

Interactive Clinical Management Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Key Diagnostic Factors

Hypertriglyceridaemia is typically clinically silent and detected on routine screening.

Clinical Features by Severity
1
Mild-to-Moderate HTG

No symptoms or physical findings unless ASCVD has developed

2
Severe HTG

Characteristic features of chylomicronaemia may be present

Physical Findings (Severe HTG):

  • Eruptive xanthomas: Small yellowish papules on extensor surfaces Uncommon
  • Lipaemia retinalis: White/pink retinal vessels on fundoscopy Uncommon
  • Hepatosplenomegaly: From fat infiltration
  • Abdominal pain: May indicate pancreatitis Urgent
Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors:

Family History Obesity Diabetes Excess Alcohol High Fat Diet Sedentary Lifestyle HIV Infection Hypothyroidism

Secondary Causes:

  • Medical Conditions: Diabetes, metabolic syndrome, CKD, nephrotic syndrome, hypothyroidism, pregnancy, liver disease, Cushing syndrome
  • Medications: Glucocorticoids, oral oestrogens, thiazide/loop diuretics, beta-blockers, antipsychotics, protease inhibitors, immunosuppressants
Initial Investigations

Essential Tests:

Test Purpose Interpretation
Fasting Lipid Profile Confirm TG elevation 12-14 hour fast recommended. Non-fasting acceptable for screening
Glucose/HbA1c Screen for diabetes Essential as insulin resistance is major cause
TSH Rule out hypothyroidism Common secondary cause
LFTs Assess liver disease Check for MASLD/MASH
Renal Function Rule out CKD/nephrotic syndrome Urea, creatinine, urine protein

Note: LDL-cholesterol calculation inaccurate if TG >4.6 mmol/L (>400 mg/dL)

Consider: Apolipoprotein B measurement for comprehensive risk assessment

Diagnosis

Classification Criteria

Major Society Guidelines:

American Heart Association/ACC:
Category Triglyceride Level Badge
Normal < 2.0 mmol/L (< 175 mg/dL) Normal
Moderate HTG 2.0-5.6 mmol/L (175-499 mg/dL) Moderate
Severe HTG ≥ 5.6 mmol/L (≥ 500 mg/dL) Severe
Endocrine Society:
  • Mild: 1.7-2.3 mmol/L (150-199 mg/dL)
  • Moderate: 2.3-11.2 mmol/L (200-999 mg/dL)
  • Severe: 11.2-22.4 mmol/L (1000-1999 mg/dL)
  • Very severe: >22.4 mmol/L (>2000 mg/dL)
Clinical Significance
1
Mild-to-Moderate HTG ASCVD Risk

Associated with increased atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease risk

2
Severe HTG Pancreatitis Risk

TG >10 mmol/L (>885 mg/dL) increases acute pancreatitis risk exponentially

Pancreatitis Risk Assessment

Risk Stratification:

Low
TG < 10 mmol/L (< 885 mg/dL)

Low to moderate risk of acute pancreatitis

High
TG > 10 mmol/L (> 885 mg/dL)

3-5% annual incidence of pancreatitis

Critical
TG > 20 mmol/L (> 1770 mg/dL)

Exponential increase in risk URGENT

When to Consider HTG as Cause:

  • All patients with acute pancreatitis should have TG measured
  • Consider even when primary cause is obvious (e.g., alcohol)
  • Severe HTG may be contributory factor
Differential Diagnosis

Conditions to Exclude:

Condition Differentiating Features Key Test
Diabetes Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss Fasting glucose ≥7 mmol/L, HbA1c ≥6.5%
Hypothyroidism Weight gain, sluggishness, dry skin Elevated TSH
Nephrotic Syndrome Oedema, proteinuria, hypoalbuminaemia Urine protein, serum albumin
Liver Disease Jaundice, spider angiomas, ascites LFTs, coagulation profile
Lipodystrophy Altered fat distribution, insulin resistance Clinical features, genetic testing

Treatment

Lifestyle Modifications
Core Lifestyle Interventions
1
Alcohol Restriction Critical

Abstinence or severe limitation. >1-2 units/day worsens HTG

2
Dietary Changes

Low-fat diet (<30% calories). If TG >11.3 mmol/L: very low-fat (<15%)

3
Weight Management

Weight loss reduces TG levels significantly

4
Glycaemic Control If Diabetic

Strict control essential for TG reduction

Specific Dietary Recommendations:

  • Avoid simple carbohydrates and high glycaemic index foods
  • Replace saturated fats with monounsaturated fats (olive/canola oil)
  • Consider medium-chain fatty acids for severe HTG/FCS
  • Supplement essential fatty acids and fat-soluble vitamins if on very low-fat diet
Pharmacological Management

Management Algorithm by Severity:

1. Mild-to-Moderate HTG TG 2.0-5.6 mmol/L
First-line: Statins
Based on ASCVD risk and LDL-C levels
Evidence-Based
Add-on: Icosapent Ethyl
2g twice daily
Consider if TG ≥1.7 mmol/L despite statin
2. Severe HTG TG ≥5.6 mmol/L
First-line: Fibrates
Fenofibrate (preferred) or Gemfibrozil
Most Effective for TG Reduction
Omega-3 Fatty Acids
4g/day (icosapent ethyl or omega-3 acid ethyl esters)
30% TG reduction possible
Statins
Initiate once TG <5 mmol/L
For ASCVD prevention

Statin Intensity Options:

Intensity Drug & Dose Indication
High Atorvastatin 40-80mg or Rosuvastatin 20-40mg ASCVD risk ≥7.5%
Moderate Atorvastatin 10-20mg, Rosuvastatin 5-10mg, or Simvastatin 20-40mg ASCVD risk 5-7.5%
Important Safety Notes
DO NOT combine Gemfibrozil with Statins

Risk of severe myopathy. Use Fenofibrate if combination needed.

Monitor for Atrial Fibrillation

Omega-3 fatty acids associated with dose-dependent increased AF risk

Acute Pancreatitis Management
Emergency Management
1
Hospital Admission MANDATORY

All patients with acute pancreatitis due to chylomicronaemia

2
Supportive Care

IV hydration, bowel rest (NPO for 48-72 hours)

3
TG Reduction

TG levels fall with half-life 24-30 hours with cessation of oral intake

4
Insulin Infusion If Diabetic

Helps reduce TG levels in poorly controlled diabetes

5
Plasmapheresis Rarely Needed

Not routinely recommended. Consider only in specific situations (e.g., pregnancy with TG >20 mmol/L)

Transition to Oral Intake:

  1. Clear fluids when stable
  2. Low-fat diet with lifestyle modification advice
  3. Once TG <5 mmol/L: start statin for ASCVD prevention
  4. Fibrate therapy for pancreatitis prophylaxis

Clinical Context
Familial Chylomicronaemia Syndrome (FCS)

These agents specifically indicated for genetically confirmed FCS with inadequate response to diet/traditional therapy

Not for Routine Severe HTG

Standard severe HTG or mild-to-moderate HTG should use conventional therapies

Follow-up

Monitoring Schedule
Recommended Monitoring
1
During Treatment Initiation

Lipid profile every 6 weeks until stable

2
Maintenance Phase

Every 6-12 months once stable

3
Liver Function

Before starting statins/fibrates, at dose changes, then every 6-12 months

4
Lifestyle Reinforcement

Emphasize diet and exercise at each visit

Complications & Prognosis

Long-term Complications:

1
Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease High Risk

Leading cause of morbidity/mortality. TG-rich remnants are highly atherogenic

2
Acute Pancreatitis Medium Risk

Variable risk depending on TG level. Can lead to chronic pancreatitis, pseudocysts, increased mortality

3
Aortic Stenosis Medium Risk

Associated with increased triglyceride-rich remnant lipoproteins

Prognosis:

Excellent with Optimal Control

Significant improvement in ASCVD and pancreatitis risk with TG lowering to recommended ranges

Target: TG <1.7 mmol/L (<150 mg/dL) where achievable

Prevention Strategies:

  • Adherence to low-fat diet and lifestyle modifications
  • Regular physical activity and weight management
  • Medication compliance
  • Avoidance of trigger drugs and excessive alcohol
  • Screening first-degree relatives
Key Guidelines Summary

International Guidelines:

United Kingdom:
  • NICE (2023): Cardiovascular disease risk assessment and reduction
  • NICE (2022): Icosapent ethyl with statin therapy for elevated TG
  • SIGN (2017): Risk estimation and prevention of CVD
Europe:
  • ESC/EAS (2019): Management of dyslipidaemias
  • Target TG: <1.7 mmol/L for lower ASCVD risk
  • Recommendation: Statins first-line, icosapent ethyl for high-risk patients
North America:
  • AHA/ACC (2018): Blood cholesterol management
  • ACC (2021): Persistent hypertriglyceridaemia pathway
  • Endocrine Society (2020): Hypertriglyceridaemia guideline
