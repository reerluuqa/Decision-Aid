Assessment

‚ñº
1
Initial Risk Assessment
Screening

Identify patients at high risk for TB infection:

  • Recent exposure to active TB cases
  • Immunocompromised status (HIV, transplant, biologics)
  • Recent travel to high-prevalence areas
  • Symptoms suggestive of active TB
  • Residence in congregate settings
üîç Key Questions to Ask:
  • Have you had a cough for more than 2 weeks?
  • Have you had fever, night sweats, or weight loss?
  • Have you been in contact with anyone diagnosed with TB?
  • Have you recently traveled or lived outside the UK?
  • Are you taking any immunosuppressive medications?
2
Clinical Evaluation
Examination

Perform comprehensive physical examination looking for:

  • Pulmonary signs: Persistent cough, hemoptysis, crackles
  • Extrapulmonary signs: Lymphadenopathy, organomegaly, neurological deficits
  • Systemic signs: Fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • Risk factors: Malnutrition, substance use, social vulnerabilities
Urgent referral if severely ill
3
Initial Investigations
Primary Care

Arrange baseline investigations:

ü´Å For suspected pulmonary TB:
  • Chest X-ray (arrange urgently)
  • Three sputum samples for AFB microscopy and culture
  • NAAT testing if available
ü¶¥ For suspected extrapulmonary TB:
  • Site-specific imaging (ultrasound, CT, MRI)
  • Tissue/fluid samples for histology and culture
  • Consider lymph node biopsy
4
Risk Stratification
Priority

Classify patients based on urgency:

üî¥ High Priority (Urgent referral):
  • Severely ill or systemically unwell
  • Suspected TB meningitis
  • Disseminated TB
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Pregnant women with suspected TB
üü° Medium Priority (Routine referral):
  • Stable patients with suspected pulmonary TB
  • Extrapulmonary TB without CNS involvement
  • Latent TB infection

Diagnosis

‚ñº
1
Diagnostic Testing
Laboratory

Confirm TB infection with appropriate tests:

üß¨ Molecular Testing (NAAT):
  • Rapid detection of M. tuberculosis complex
  • Results within 24-48 hours
  • High sensitivity and specificity
  • Can detect rifampicin resistance
üß´ Culture Methods:
  • Gold standard for diagnosis
  • Allows drug susceptibility testing
  • Solid media: 4-8 weeks
  • Liquid media: 1-3 weeks
üî¨ Microscopy:
  • AFB smear microscopy
  • Rapid results (same day)
  • Limited sensitivity
  • Cannot differentiate species
2
Differentiating Active vs Latent TB
Important

Key differences in diagnosis:

Feature Active TB Latent TB
Symptoms Present (cough, fever, weight loss) Absent
Radiology Abnormal (cavities, infiltrates) Normal
Microbiology Positive cultures/smears Negative
Infectious Yes (if pulmonary) No
3
Drug Resistance Testing
Critical

Assess for drug resistance in all confirmed TB cases:

  • Initial testing: Rifampicin resistance (Xpert MTB/RIF)
  • Full susceptibility: Isoniazid, rifampicin, ethambutol, pyrazinamide
  • Second-line drugs: For MDR-TB cases
  • Turnaround time: 1-4 weeks depending on method
Urgent referral if MDR-TB suspected
4
Latent TB Infection (LTBI) Diagnosis
Screening

Tests for LTBI diagnosis:

üíâ Mantoux Test (TST):
  • Intradermal PPD injection
  • Read at 48-72 hours
  • Positive if induration ‚â•5mm (high risk) or ‚â•10mm (low risk)
  • Not affected by BCG after 10 years
ü©∏ Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA):
  • Blood test measuring T-cell response
  • Not affected by BCG vaccination
  • Single visit required
  • Preferred for BCG-vaccinated individuals

Treatment

‚ñº
1
Active TB Treatment Initiation
Immediate

Start treatment promptly after diagnosis:

üö® Standard Regimen (6 months):
  • Initial phase (2 months): Isoniazid + Rifampicin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol
  • Continuation phase (4 months): Isoniazid + Rifampicin
  • Monitoring: Liver function, visual acuity, uric acid
  • Adherence support: Directly observed therapy (DOT) if needed
Notify public health within 3 days
‚ö†Ô∏è Special Populations:
  • Pregnancy: Avoid pyrazinamide, extend treatment to 9 months
  • Liver disease: Dose adjustments required
  • Renal impairment: Adjust ethambutol and pyrazinamide
  • Children: Adjust doses by weight
2
Latent TB Infection (LTBI) Treatment
Preventive

Treat LTBI to prevent progression to active disease:

üíä Preferred Regimens:
  • 3HR: 3 months isoniazid + rifampicin (preferred)
  • 6H: 6 months isoniazid (alternative)
  • 4R: 4 months rifampicin (if interactions concern)
  • Weekly therapy: 3 months isoniazid + rifapentine (DOT)
üë• Priority Groups for LTBI Treatment:
  • People living with HIV
  • Recent close contacts of active TB cases
  • Children under 5 years
  • Patients starting anti-TNF therapy
  • Transplant recipients
3
Drug-Resistant TB Management
MDR/XDR-TB

Specialized management for drug-resistant TB:

üö® Multidrug-Resistant TB (MDR-TB):
  • Definition: Resistant to isoniazid and rifampicin
  • Treatment duration: 18-24 months
  • Regimen: At least 5 effective drugs including:
    • Fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin/moxifloxacin)
    • Second-line injectable (if susceptible)
    • Bedaquiline or linezolid
    • Other Group C and D drugs
  • Monitoring: Monthly cultures until negative
üÜï Newer Approaches:
  • Bedaquiline: First new TB drug in 40 years
  • Pretomanid: Part of BPaL regimen
  • Shorter regimens: 9-11 months for selected MDR-TB
  • Surgical options: For localized disease
4
Supportive Care & Monitoring
Essential

Comprehensive care package:

  • Baseline assessment: Weight, LFTs, visual acuity, pregnancy test
  • Monthly monitoring: Symptoms, weight, medication adherence
  • Toxicity monitoring: Liver function, vision (ethambutol), uric acid (pyrazinamide)
  • Support services: DOT, social support, mental health
  • Nutritional support: High-calorie diet, supplements if needed
  • Comorbidity management: HIV, diabetes, substance use
Monitor for drug interactions Pyridoxine with isoniazid

Follow-up

‚ñº
1
Treatment Response Monitoring
Monthly

Regular assessment of treatment response:

  • Clinical assessment: Symptoms, weight gain, energy levels
  • Microbiological response: Sputum conversion to negative
  • Radiological improvement: Repeat chest X-ray at 2-3 months
  • Adherence monitoring: Pill counts, pharmacy records, DOT
  • Tolerance assessment: Side effects, toxicity monitoring
Culture conversion by 2 months If not, assess for resistance
2
Contact Tracing & Public Health
Mandatory

Public health responsibilities:

üë• Contact Investigation:
  • Household contacts: Screen all members
  • Close contacts: Work, school, social contacts
  • High-risk contacts: Children, immunocompromised
  • Screening tests: TST or IGRA, chest X-ray
  • Window period prophylaxis: For children <5 years
üè• Infection Control:
  • Respiratory isolation: Until 2 weeks of effective therapy
  • Mask wearing: For infectious patients in public
  • Ventilation: Ensure good air circulation
  • Environmental controls: UV germicidal irradiation if available
3
Completion of Therapy
Cure

Ensure successful treatment completion:

  • Standard treatment: 6 months total therapy
  • Confirmation: At least 4 months of negative cultures
  • Clinical criteria: Symptom resolution, weight gain
  • Radiological: Improvement or stable CXR
  • Documentation: Complete treatment card, notify outcome
Cure rate: >90% with full adherence
4
Long-term Follow-up & Complications
Surveillance

Monitor for relapse and complications:

üîç Post-treatment Monitoring:
  • Relapse risk: Highest in first 12 months
  • Symptom review: Persistent or recurrent symptoms
  • Risk factors: Drug resistance, poor adherence, HIV
  • Re-infection: Consider if new exposure
‚ö†Ô∏è Potential Complications:
  • Post-TB lung disease: Bronchiectasis, fibrosis
  • Chronic respiratory impairment: COPD, restrictive defects
  • Mental health: Depression, anxiety
  • Social impact: Stigma, employment issues
  • Drug toxicity: Long-term sequelae
5
Cure Verification & Discharge
Complete

Final assessment before discharge:

  • Treatment completion: All doses taken as prescribed
  • Clinical recovery: Symptom-free, weight normalized
  • Microbiological cure: Negative cultures confirmed
  • Radiological stability: No active disease signs
  • Follow-up plan: Clear instructions for seeking care if symptoms recur
  • Contact tracing completion: All contacts evaluated
  • Public health notification: Treatment outcome reported
Patient cured and discharged Provide education on TB recurrence
