Atrial Fibrillation Quick Reference

Last updated: May 2025
NHS Clinical Reference

EMERGENCY ACTIONS

  • Arrange immediate hospital admission if haemodynamically unstable
  • Refer to acute medical unit for immediate cardioversion if onset <48 hours
  • Consider emergency rate control if severe symptoms or life-threatening complications

Red Flags & When to Refer

  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, altered mental status)
  • Acute heart failure or pulmonary oedema
  • Angina or myocardial ischaemia
  • Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome
  • Prolonged QT interval on ECG
  • Valve disease or left ventricular systolic dysfunction
  • Contraindication to rate-control or anticoagulation
  • Failed rate-control (refer within 4 weeks)

Management Priorities

  • Assess stroke risk using CHA₂DS₂-VASc score
  • Assess bleeding risk using ORBIT score
  • Offer anticoagulation if CHA₂DS₂-VASc score ≥2 (men) or ≥3 (women)
  • Consider anticoagulation for men with score of 1
  • Rate control is first-line for most patients
  • Consider rhythm control if symptoms persist despite rate control

Definition & Classification

Supraventricular tachyarrhythmia with uncoordinated atrial electrical activation and ineffective atrial contraction.

Classification:
  • Paroxysmal: Episodes >30s, terminate spontaneously within 7 days
  • Persistent: Episodes >7 days or requiring cardioversion
  • Long-standing persistent: Continuous AF ≥12 months
  • Permanent: Accepted rhythm, no attempts to restore sinus rhythm

Key Risk Factors

Cardiac

  • Hypertension (most common)
  • Ischaemic heart disease
  • Heart failure
  • Valvular heart disease
  • Cardiomyopathy

Non-Cardiac

  • Increasing age
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Obesity
  • Alcohol excess
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea

CHA₂DS₂-VASc Score for Stroke Risk

Risk Factor Points
Congestive heart failure/LV dysfunction 1
Hypertension 1
Age ≥75 years 2
Diabetes mellitus 1
Stroke/TIA/Thromboembolism 2
Vascular disease (prior MI, PAD, aortic plaque) 1
Age 65-74 years 1
Sex category (female) 1

ORBIT Score for Bleeding Risk

Risk Factor Points
Older age (≥75 years = 2 points, 65-74 = 1 point) 1-2
Reduced haemoglobin/haematocrit or history of anemia 1
Bleeding history 1
Insufficient kidney function (eGFR <60 ml/min/1.73m²) 1
Treatment with antiplatelet (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel) 1
Interpretation: ORBIT score ≥3 indicates high bleeding risk. Consider modifiable risk factors and discuss with patient.
Anticoagulation Recommendation:
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc score ≥2 (men) or ≥3 (women): Offer anticoagulation
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc score = 1 (men): Consider anticoagulation
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc score = 0 (men) or 1 (women): Do not offer anticoagulation

Anticoagulation Options

Medication Dose Key Considerations
Apixaban First-line 5mg twice daily (2.5mg twice daily if ≥80 years, weight ≤60kg, or serum creatinine ≥133µmol/L) Lower risk of intracranial bleeding compared to warfarin. No routine monitoring required.
Dabigatran First-line 150mg twice daily (110mg twice daily if ≥80 years, high bleeding risk) Higher risk of GI bleeding compared to warfarin. Avoid in severe renal impairment.
Edoxaban First-line 60mg once daily (30mg if ≥80 years, weight ≤60kg, or concomitant verapamil) Lower risk of bleeding compared to warfarin. Consider dose reduction in elderly.
Rivaroxaban First-line 20mg once daily (15mg if ≥80 years or creatinine clearance 15-49ml/min) Take with food. Higher risk of GI bleeding compared to apixaban.
Warfarin Dose adjusted to maintain INR 2.0-3.0 Regular monitoring required. Multiple food and drug interactions. Consider when DOACs contraindicated.
Contraindications to Anticoagulation:
  • Active bleeding or high risk of bleeding that cannot be corrected
  • Severe uncontrolled hypertension
  • Recent intracranial hemorrhage
  • Esophageal varices with high bleeding risk
When to Consider Left Atrial Appendage Closure:
  • Contraindicated for both DOACs and warfarin
  • High stroke risk but also high bleeding risk
  • Refer to cardiology for consideration

Rate Control Options

Medication Dose Key Considerations
Beta-blockers First-line Bisoprolol 1.25-10mg once daily Caution: In asthma/COPD, consider cardioselective agents. Avoid in severe bradycardia.
Calcium channel blockers First-line Diltiazem 60-300mg daily in divided doses Contraindicated: In heart failure with reduced ejection fraction. Avoid with grapefruit juice.
Digoxin 62.5-250µg once daily Consider for sedentary patients or if beta-blockers/calcium channel blockers contraindicated. Monitor renal function.
Rate Control Targets:
  • Resting heart rate <90 bpm (lenient control)
  • Resting heart rate <80 bpm (strict control) if symptomatic
  • Consider stricter control in patients with heart failure or tachycardiomyopathy

Rhythm Control Options

Electrical Cardioversion

Indications:
  • AF with reversible cause
  • Heart failure caused/worsened by AF
  • New-onset AF
  • Patient preference after discussion
Anticoagulation Requirements:
  • If onset <48 hours: Cardioversion without anticoagulation
  • If onset >48 hours or unknown: Anticoagulate for ≥3 weeks before and ≥4 weeks after
  • Or perform TOE to exclude left atrial thrombus

Pharmacological Cardioversion

Medication Considerations
Flecainide Contraindicated: In structural heart disease, CAD, heart failure
Propafenone Contraindicated: In structural heart disease, CAD, heart failure
Amiodarone Can be used in structural heart disease. Monitor for thyroid, liver, lung toxicity
Vernakalant Intravenous only. Contraindicated: In hypotension, recent MI, heart failure
Long-term Rhythm Control:
  • Consider if symptoms persist despite adequate rate control
  • Refer to cardiology for consideration of anti-arrhythmic drugs or ablation
  • Continue anticoagulation based on stroke risk, not rhythm

Diagnosis & Assessment

When to Suspect AF

  • Irregular pulse on examination
  • Palpitations
  • Breathlessness/reduced exercise tolerance
  • Chest discomfort
  • Dizziness or syncope
  • Detected incidentally on ECG or device

Diagnostic Confirmation

  • 12-lead ECG showing irregularly irregular rhythm
  • No discernible P waves
  • Absolutely irregular RR intervals
  • Ambulatory ECG monitoring if paroxysmal AF suspected
  • Consider patient-initiated mobile/wearable devices
Further Assessment:
  • Echocardiography to assess for structural heart disease
  • Blood tests: FBC, U&E, TFT, LFT
  • Risk factor assessment and management
