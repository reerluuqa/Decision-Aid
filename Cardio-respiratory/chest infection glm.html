Chest Infections in Adults

Interactive Clinical Management Algorithm

Based on NICE CKS Guidelines (Last revised January 2025)

Assessment

Initial Assessment

Clinical judgement must always be used to assess people with suspected community-acquired pneumonia as no combination of symptoms or signs is definitively diagnostic.

Clinicians should be alert for the signs and symptoms of sepsis in people presenting with acute respiratory infection.

Symptom Assessment

Cough is the predominant symptom in both acute bronchitis and community-acquired pneumonia. Ask about:

  • Onset and duration of symptoms
  • Type of cough (dry or productive)
  • Additional symptoms such as breathlessness, wheeze, pleuritic pain, and fever
  • Smoking status

Physical Examination

Examine the person, including the respiratory system. Measure:

  • Temperature
  • Pulse rate
  • Blood pressure
  • Respiratory rate
  • Assess for signs of confusion

Be aware that in elderly people with pneumonia, symptoms may vary (for example altered consciousness and gastrointestinal discomfort) and typical symptoms (cough, fever, and difficulty breathing) may be absent.

Severity Assessment (CRB-65 Score)

If an adult has clinical symptoms and signs suggestive of community-acquired pneumonia, assess the severity of the illness using clinical judgement and the CRB-65 score for mortality risk.

Score: 0 - Low Risk

The score is calculated by giving 1 point for each of the above prognostic features:

  • Score of 0: Low risk of death (less than 1% mortality risk)
  • Score of 1 or 2: Intermediate risk (1-10% mortality risk)
  • Score of 3 or 4: High risk (more than 10% mortality risk)

Additional Considerations

In young people, use clinical judgement to assess the severity of symptoms and signs, taking into account features suggesting severe community-acquired pneumonia, including:

  • Difficulty breathing
  • Oxygen saturation less than 90%
  • Raised heart rate
  • Grunting and very severe chest indrawing
  • Inability to drink fluids
  • Lethargy or reduced level of consciousness

Investigations

Consider investigations including:

  • Pulse oximetry — arrange urgent hospital admission for people who require supplemental oxygen
  • Point-of-care C-reactive protein test — if the person has symptoms of a lower respiratory tract infection, a clinical diagnosis of pneumonia has not been made, and there is uncertainty about whether antibiotics are indicated
  • Chest X-ray — if the person has suspected community-acquired pneumonia and they are at risk of underlying lung pathology (for example lung cancer) or the cause is uncertain

Do not routinely carry out microbiological tests for people with low-severity community-acquired pneumonia.

Take blood and sputum samples for microbiological testing, for a person with moderate severity community-acquired pneumonia being managed in the community.

Diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis

The main conditions to differentiate are:

  • Acute bronchitis — defined as a self-limiting lower respiratory tract infection which causes inflammation in the bronchial airways
  • Community-acquired pneumonia — an infection of the lung tissue in which the air sacs in the lungs become filled with microorganisms, fluid and inflammatory cells

Key Differentiating Features

General Appearance Mildly ill Moderately to severely ill
Symptoms Cough, may or may not have sputum, wheeze, or breathlessness. Substernal or chest wall pain may be present when coughing. Sometimes mild constitutional symptoms. Dyspnoea, sputum production, pleural pain, sweating, fever, shivers, aches, and pains.
Examination Findings Wheeze often present; rhonchi that improve with coughing may be present. Focal chest signs such as decreased or asymmetric breath sounds, bronchial breath sounds, dullness to percussion, course crepitations, and vocal fremitus. Atypical Presentations

Unusual presentations can indicate certain atypical pathogens:

  • Legionella pneumonia — dry cough, no fever, headache, confusion, diarrhoea, and hyponatraemia
  • Mycoplasma pneumonia — upper respiratory involvement, skin changes, encephalitis, uveitis, myocarditis, and haemolytic anaemia

Red Flag Symptoms

Refer or seek specialist advice on further investigation and management if a person with acute cough has any symptoms or signs suggesting a more serious condition:

  • Sepsis
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Lung cancer

Treatment

Treatment for Acute Bronchitis

Acute bronchitis is usually a self-limiting illness and the cough usually lasts about 3-4 weeks.

Self-Care Advice

  • Adequate fluid intake
  • Paracetamol or ibuprofen for symptomatic relief
  • Honey
  • Pelargonium (a herbal medicine)
  • Over-the-counter cough medicines containing guaifenesin (an expectorant)
  • Over-the-counter cough medicines containing cough suppressants (except codeine) if the person does not have a persistent cough or excessive secretions

Advise the person to seek medical help if symptoms worsen rapidly or significantly, do not improve after 3-4 weeks, or they become systemically very unwell.

Antibiotic Prescribing

Do not routinely offer an antibiotic to treat an acute cough associated with acute bronchitis in people who are not systemically very unwell or at higher risk of complications.

Inform the person that:

  • Acute bronchitis is usually a self-limiting illness and the cough usually lasts about 3-4 weeks
  • Antibiotics do not make a large difference to the duration of symptoms, only shortening cough duration by about half a day on average
  • Adverse effects, including diarrhoea and nausea are possible with antibiotic treatment
  • Unnecessary antibiotic prescriptions may result in antibiotic resistance

When to Consider Antibiotics

Offer an immediate antibiotic prescription if the person is systemically very unwell.

Consider an immediate antibiotic prescription or a backup antibiotic prescription for a person at higher risk of complications, for example:

  • A pre-existing comorbid condition such as heart, lung, kidney, liver, or neuromuscular disease; immunosuppression; or cystic fibrosis
  • Older than 65 years of age with two or more of the following, or older than 80 years with one or more of the following:
    • Hospital admission in the previous year
    • Type 1 or type 2 diabetes mellitus
    • History of congestive heart failure
    • Current use of oral corticosteroids

CRP-Guided Antibiotic Prescribing

If a C-reactive protein (CRP) test has been carried out, use the results to guide antibiotic prescribing as follows:

  • CRP less than 20 mg/L — do not routinely offer antibiotics
  • CRP 20-100 mg/L — consider a delayed antibiotic prescription
  • CRP greater than 100 mg/L — offer antibiotic therapy

Antibiotic Choices for Acute Bronchitis

If antibiotics are indicated, for adults 18 years of age and older:

  • First-line choice is oral doxycycline: 200 mg on the first day, then 100 mg once a day for 4 days (5-day course in total). Note doxycycline is not the first-choice antibiotic for pregnant women.
  • Alternative first choices are oral:
    • Amoxicillin (preferred in pregnant women) 500 mg three times a day for 5 days
    • Clarithromycin 250 mg to 500 mg twice a day for 5 days
    • Erythromycin (preferred in pregnant women) 250 mg to 500 mg four times a day or 500 mg to 1000 mg twice a day for 5 days

If antibiotics are indicated, for young people aged 12-17 years:

  • First-line choice is oral amoxicillin (preferred in young women who are pregnant): 500 mg three times a day for 5 days
  • Alternative first choices are oral:
    • Clarithromycin 250 mg to 500 mg twice a day for 5 days
    • Erythromycin (preferred in young women who are pregnant) 250 mg to 500 mg four times a day or 500 mg to 1000 mg twice a day for 5 days
    • Doxycycline 200 mg on first day, then 100 mg once a day for 4 days (5-day course in total). Note doxycycline should not be given to young women who are pregnant.

Treatments to Avoid

  • Do not offer an oral or inhaled bronchodilator (for example salbutamol) or an oral or inhaled corticosteroid to a person with an acute cough associated with acute bronchitis unless they have an underlying airway disease such as asthma
  • Do not offer a mucolytic (for example acetylcysteine or carbocisteine) to treat an acute cough associated with acute bronchitis

Treatment for Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Severity-Based Management

Treatment depends on the severity of pneumonia as assessed by the CRB-65 score:

  • Low severity (CRB-65 score 0) — can be managed in the community with oral antibiotics
  • Intermediate severity (CRB-65 score 1-2) — consider hospital referral, especially if score 2 or if other risk factors present
  • High severity (CRB-65 score 3-4) — requires urgent hospital admission

Antibiotic Treatment for Low-Severity Pneumonia

For adults with low-severity community-acquired pneumonia:

  • Prescribe a 5-day course of a single antibiotic
  • First-line options include:
    • Amoxicillin 500 mg three times a day for 5 days
    • Doxycycline 200 mg on first day, then 100 mg once a day for 4 days (5-day course in total)
    • Clarithromycin 500 mg twice a day for 5 days

Antibiotic Treatment for Intermediate-Severity Pneumonia

For adults with intermediate-severity community-acquired pneumonia being managed in the community:

  • Consider higher-dose amoxicillin (1 g three times a day for 5 days) or combination therapy
  • Consider adding a macrolide (clarithromycin 500 mg twice a day) if atypical pathogens suspected
  • Arrange follow-up within 48 hours

Management of High-Severity Pneumonia

For adults with high-severity community-acquired pneumonia:

  • Arrange urgent hospital admission
  • Consider intravenous antibiotics
  • Consider oxygen therapy
  • Consider fluid management

Additional Management

  • Advise adequate fluid intake
  • Provide analgesia for pleuritic pain if needed
  • Consider VTE prophylaxis in hospitalized patients
  • Advise smoking cessation if applicable

Follow-up

Follow-up for Acute Bronchitis

Routine follow-up is not necessary for a person with suspected acute bronchitis.

When to Reassess

Reassess a person with an acute cough if their symptoms get rapidly or significantly worse.

Consider:

  • Other diagnoses
  • Ruling out serious causes for symptoms (such as pneumonia or sepsis)
  • Previous antibiotic use and the potential for resistant bacteria

If the person has already received a course of antibiotics, seek specialist advice on further management.

Expected Course

  • The cough usually lasts about 2-3 weeks
  • Most people recover fully with no residual symptoms after an episode of acute bronchitis
  • Around a quarter of people will have a cough for more than 4 weeks
  • In some, cough will persist for up to 6 months (post-bronchitis syndrome)

Follow-up for Community-Acquired Pneumonia

For Low-Severity Pneumonia Managed in the Community

  • Arrange follow-up within 48 hours to ensure clinical improvement
  • Consider a chest X-ray 6 weeks after diagnosis for people who:
    • Are smokers
    • Have persistent symptoms
    • Have recurrent chest infections
  • Consider referral to a respiratory specialist if there is no improvement after 48 hours of appropriate antibiotic therapy

For Intermediate-Severity Pneumonia

  • Arrange follow-up within 24-48 hours
  • Consider hospital referral if no improvement or worsening symptoms
  • Consider a chest X-ray 6 weeks after diagnosis, especially for smokers or those with persistent symptoms

Complications to Monitor For

  • Pleural effusion and empyema
  • Lung abscess
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome
  • Sepsis
  • Disseminated infection

Prevention

  • Advise pneumococcal vaccination for at-risk individuals
  • Advise annual influenza vaccination for at-risk individuals
  • Advise smoking cessation
