UNSTABLE BRADYCARDIA

If patient shows signs of instability → Immediate treatment required. Do not delay for tests.

Definition: Heart Rate < 50 bpm

Bradycardia is any heart rhythm slower than 50 bpm, even if transient. While <60 bpm is sometimes used, most clinicians consider <50 bpm significant. Common in athletes and during sleep. Treatment depends on symptoms and hemodynamic stability.

Signs of Instability - ACT NOW

Critical Signs - Requires Immediate Intervention
  • Hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg) with bradycardia
  • Acute altered mental status (confusion, lethargy)
  • Signs of shock (pallor, diaphoresis, cool extremities)
  • Chest pain with bradycardia (possible ischemia)
  • Acute heart failure symptoms
  • Syncope/near-syncope attributed to bradycardia
ABCDE Assessment Approach

Immediate Assessment

  • Airway - Is airway patent?
  • Breathing - Rate, effort, O2 sat
  • Circulation - Pulse, BP, perfusion
  • Disability - GCS, pupil response
  • Exposure - Rash, temperature

Key Questions

  • What is the heart rate?
  • Is the patient symptomatic?
  • Are symptoms related to bradycardia?
  • Is there hemodynamic compromise?
  • What medications is patient taking?

Causes & Risk Factors

Intrinsic Causes
  • Age-related fibrosis (most common in elderly)
  • Sinus node dysfunction (sick sinus syndrome)
  • AV nodal disease (Lev-Lenegre disease)
  • Myocardial infarction (especially inferior)
  • Infiltrative diseases (amyloidosis, sarcoidosis)
  • Post-cardiac surgery
Extrinsic/Reversible Causes
  • Beta-blockers (most common culprit)
  • Calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil)
  • Digoxin toxicity
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Electrolyte disorders (hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia)
  • Increased vagal tone (neurogenic)
Medications That Cause Bradycardia
Category Medications Action
Antiarrhythmics Amiodarone, Sotalol, Digoxin, Flecainide Review and hold if possible
Antihypertensives Beta-blockers, Clonidine, Methyldopa, Reserpine Common culprits
Calcium Channel Blockers Verapamil, Diltiazem (non-DHP) Hold or reduce dose
Psychoactive Tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs, Lithium Consider alternatives
Other Ivabradine, Opioids, Chemotherapy (Paclitaxel) Monitor closely

ECG Patterns & Heart Block Types

First-Degree AV Block

PR Interval > 0.20 seconds

Fixed prolonged PR interval with 1:1 conduction. Usually benign but may indicate underlying conduction disease.

1° Block Usually Benign

Location: Usually AV nodal (more benign)
Significance: May progress to higher block; assess for reversible causes.

Second-Degree AV Block

Type I (Wenckebach)

Progressive PR prolongation until dropped beat. Grouped beating pattern.

AV Nodal Usually Benign

Type II (Mobitz II)

Constant PR interval with intermittent dropped beats. No PR prolongation.

Infranodal HIGH RISK
Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block

No AV Conduction

Atria and ventricles beat independently. Regular P waves, regular QRS complexes at slower rate.

When to Consult Cardiology

Urgent Consult
  • Symptomatic bradycardia unresponsive to initial treatment
  • Second-degree Mobitz II or third-degree AV block
  • Complete heart block with symptoms
  • Bradycardia with acute MI
  • Persistent hemodynamic instability
Elective Consult
  • Symptomatic sinus node dysfunction
  • Asymptomatic bradycardia with documented pauses >3 sec
  • Chronotropic incompetence
  • Post-TAVR with new conduction abnormalities
  • Consideration for permanent pacemaker

Quick Reference Summary

STABLE - Observe
  • No symptoms or mild symptoms only
  • Hemodynamically stable (BP normal)
  • Review and hold offending medications
  • Consider reversible causes (5 H's, 5 T's)
  • ECG and cardiac monitoring
UNSTABLE - Treat Immediately
  • Atropine 0.5 mg IV q3-5min (max 3mg)
  • If no response: Start TCP or dopamine/epinephrine
  • Prepare for transvenous pacing
  • Consult cardiology emergently
  • Treat reversible causes simultaneously
