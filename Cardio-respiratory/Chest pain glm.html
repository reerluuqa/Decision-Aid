Red Flags - Immediate Action Required

CRITICAL Acute Coronary Syndrome
Symptoms: Central chest pain lasting >15 minutes, dull/crushing, radiating to arms/jaw/back, associated with nausea/vomiting/sweating/breathlessness.
Action: Immediate emergency admission. Do not delay for investigations in primary care.
CRITICAL Aortic Dissection
Symptoms: Sudden tearing chest pain radiating to back and interscapular region.
Signs: High blood pressure, blood pressure differentials between arms, inequality in pulses, new diastolic murmur.
Action: Immediate emergency admission.
CRITICAL Tension Pneumothorax
Symptoms: Sudden-onset pleuritic pain and severe breathlessness.
Signs: Tracheal deviation away from pneumothorax, tachycardia, hypotension.
Action: Immediate emergency admission.
CRITICAL Massive Pulmonary Embolism
Symptoms: Acute-onset severe breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain, syncope.
Signs: Tachypnoea >20 breaths/min, tachycardia, signs of DVT, haemodynamic instability.
Action: Immediate emergency admission.

Assessment Approach

History Taking
  • Check if person currently has chest pain
  • Ask about nature, onset, duration, site, radiation
  • Identify exacerbating/relieving factors
  • Ask about associated symptoms
  • History of previous chest pain/investigations
  • Cardiovascular risk factors
  • History of respiratory, GI, musculoskeletal issues
  • Consider anxiety/depression
Physical Examination
  • Cardiovascular examination (heart sounds, murmurs)
  • Blood pressure in both arms
  • Pulse rate and rhythm
  • Jugular venous pressure
  • Chest wall examination (tenderness)
  • Lung fields assessment
  • Respiratory rate and pulse oximetry
  • General appearance (pallor, sweating)

Differential Diagnosis

HIGH RISK Cardiac Causes
Acute Coronary Syndrome ▼

Symptoms: Central chest pain lasting >15 minutes, dull/crushing, radiating to arms/jaw/back, associated with nausea/vomiting/sweating/breathlessness.

Signs: May have haemodynamic instability, abnormal ECG changes.

Stable Angina ▼

Symptoms: Central chest pain triggered by exertion, relieved by rest, lasting <10 minutes.

Signs: May have normal examination between episodes.

Aortic Dissection ▼

Symptoms: Sudden tearing chest pain radiating to back and interscapular region.

Signs: High blood pressure, blood pressure differentials between arms, inequality in pulses.

Pericarditis ▼

Symptoms: Sharp, constant sternal pain relieved by sitting forward, worse when lying down.

Signs: Pericardial friction rub.

MEDIUM RISK Pulmonary Causes
Pulmonary Embolism ▼

Symptoms: Acute-onset breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain, cough, haemoptysis, syncope.

Signs: Tachypnoea >20 breaths/min, tachycardia, signs of DVT.

Pneumothorax ▼

Symptoms: Sudden-onset pleuritic pain and breathlessness.

Signs: Reduced chest wall movements, reduced breath sounds, increased resonance on affected side.

Pneumonia ▼

Symptoms: Cough and at least one other symptom of sputum, wheeze, dyspnoea, or pleuritic chest pain.

Signs: Focal chest signs plus at least one systemic feature.

LOWER RISK Other Causes
Gastrointestinal ▼

GORD/Oesophagitis: Sub-sternal pain after meals, worse when lying flat.

Acute Pancreatitis: Severe epigastric pain radiating to back.

Acute Cholecystitis: Right upper quadrant pain, Murphy's sign.

Musculoskeletal ▼

Costochondritis: Localized tenderness over costochondral junctions.

Rib Fracture: History of trauma, localized pain worse with inspiration.

Cervical Spine Issues: Pain aggravated by neck movements.

Psychogenic ▼

Non-specific chest pain: May be associated with anxiety, depression.

Features: Often described differently, may have associated psychological symptoms.

Investigations

Initial Investigations
Note: If admission or referral to hospital is required, do not delay management to arrange investigations in primary care.

Essential for All Patients

  • 12-lead ECG (electrocardiography)
  • Blood pressure measurement
  • Pulse oximetry
  • Respiratory rate

Consider Based on Clinical Suspicion

  • High-sensitivity troponin I or T
  • Chest X-ray
  • Blood glucose
  • Full blood count

Important: A normal ECG does not exclude ACS, especially in the early stages.

However, a normal ECG makes the diagnosis of coronary artery disease less likely.

ST-elevation MI: ST elevation ≥1mm in two contiguous leads.
NSTEMI/Unstable Angina: ST depression, T-wave inversion, or transient ST elevation.
Other findings: Pathological Q waves, left bundle branch block (LBBB).
Sinus tachycardia is the most common finding.
Other findings: S1Q3T3 pattern, right bundle branch block, T-wave inversion in V1-V4.

ECG may be normal in small PEs.

Pericarditis: Widespread concave ST elevation and PR depression.
Aortic Dissection: May show left ventricular hypertrophy, nonspecific ST-T changes.
Pneumothorax: May show tachycardia, reduced voltage, rightward axis.
Further Investigations
Blood Tests ▼
  • Troponin: Elevated in myocardial infarction, also in PE, myocarditis, pericarditis.
  • D-dimer: Elevated in PE, DVT, but non-specific.
  • BNP/NT-proBNP: Elevated in heart failure.
  • FBC: Anaemia may exacerbate angina.
  • LFTs/Amalyse: For suspected GI causes.
Imaging ▼
  • Chest X-ray: For pneumonia, pneumothorax, heart failure, widened mediastinum (aortic dissection).
  • CT Pulmonary Angiogram: Gold standard for PE diagnosis.
  • Echocardiogram: For suspected aortic dissection, pericardial effusion, wall motion abnormalities.
  • CT Aortogram: For suspected aortic dissection.
