Pericarditis

Acute & Recurrent • Adult • Last Updated: Sep 28, 2023
Life Threatening
Suspect Cardiac Tamponade

If patient is hypotensive or has these signs:

  • Beck's Triad: Hypotension + JVD + Muffled Heart Sounds
  • Pulsus Paradoxus: >10 mmHg drop in BP on inspiration
  • Tachycardia, dyspnoea, cool extremities
URGENT ACTION:
• Get senior help immediately
• Urgent Echocardiography
• Prepare for Pericardiocentesis
Life Threatening
Suspect Purulent Pericarditis

Consider if patient is unwell with signs of Sepsis and other causes excluded.

URGENT ACTION:
• Seek early senior/specialist input
• Broad-spectrum antibiotics
• Drainage (Pericardiocentesis)

Diagnosis: Clinical Criteria

"2 of 4" Rule Required

Presence of at least 2 confirms acute pericarditis.

  • Chest Pain
    Sharp, pleuritic. Worse on inspiration/supine. Better sitting forward. Radiates to trapezius ridge.
  • Friction Rub
    Scratchy/squeaking ("walking on snow"). May be transient. Hold breath to distinguish from pleural rub.
  • ECG Changes
    New widespread concave (saddle) ST elevation. PR depression.
  • Pericardial Effusion
    New or worsening on Echo. Note: Large effusion may silence the rub.
Key Investigations
Test Key Finding
ECG
Diffuse Concave ST Elevation
+ PR Depression (aVR, V1)
Troponin Elevated in 35-50% (Myopericarditis).
Not prognostic usually.
CRP / ESR Elevated (Inflammation). Use to monitor treatment.
Echo Detect effusion (up to 60%). Assess wall motion (rules out MI).
CXR Usually normal. "Flask-shaped" heart if large effusion (>300mL).

Risk Factors & Aetiology

Epidemiology
Strong Risk
  • Male sex (3:1 viral)
  • Age 20-50 years
  • Transmural MI
  • Cardiac Surgery
  • Neoplasm
Common Causes
  • Idiopathic / Viral (90%): Coxsackie, Echo, HIV, SARS-CoV-2.
  • Autoimmune: SLE, RA, Scleroderma.
  • Post-Injury: Dressler's, Post-cardiotomy.
  • Renal: Uraemia or Dialysis.
High Risk Features

Admit & investigate underlying cause if present:

  • Fever >38°C
  • Subacute course
  • Large effusion (>20mm)
  • Tamponade
  • Failure to respond to NSAIDs (7 days)
  • Immunosuppression / Anticoagulation

Management Plan

First-Line Treatment
1
NSAIDs (Aspirin or Ibuprofen)
Mainstay of therapy.
2
Colchicine
Give to ALL patients, unless tuberculous pericarditis or severe renal failure. Reduces recurrence.
3
Treat Underlying Cause
e.g., Antibiotics for TB, Dialysis for uraemia.
Prognosis & Follow-up
Course Usually benign, especially idiopathic/viral.
Monitoring Serial CRP to track inflammation response.
Complications
  • Chronic Recurrent Pericarditis
  • Cardiac Tamponade
  • Constrictive Pericarditis (rare)
Note on Anticoagulation: Do NOT give therapeutic LMWH if Pulmonary Embolism is uncertain; risk of hemopericardium/tamponade.
