Acute chest pain warrants rapid clinical assessment with continuous monitoring of pulse, BP, and SpO‚ÇÇ. Use C-A-B approach.

Urgent
Immediate Actions
  • Continuous monitoring (HR, BP, SpO‚ÇÇ)
  • 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes
  • IV access
  • Oxygen only if SpO‚ÇÇ < 90%
  • Morphine for severe pain
Caution
Critical Diagnoses to Exclude
  • Acute coronary syndrome (ACS)
  • Aortic dissection
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Cardiac tamponade

History Taking

Pain Characteristics
  • Quality: Pressure, squeezing, sharp, pleuritic
  • Location: Central, radiation to jaw/arm/back
  • Duration: >15 min suggests ACS
  • Radiation: Jaw/arms suggests cardiac
  • Tearing: Radiating to back suggests aortic dissection
Associated Symptoms
  • Nausea, vomiting, diaphoresis (ACS)
  • Dyspnoea (cardiac, PE, pneumothorax)
  • Syncope (aortic stenosis, PE, tamponade)
  • Fever (pneumonia, pericarditis)
  • Haemoptysis (PE, pneumonia)
Risk Factors
  • Cardiac: Smoking, HTN, DM, hyperlipidaemia
  • FH of premature CAD
  • Cocaine use (cardiac ischaemia)
  • Immobilisation, recent surgery (PE)
  • Connective tissue disorders (aortic dissection)
Atypical Presentations
  • Women, elderly, diabetics may lack typical pain
  • May present with dyspnoea only
  • Nausea/vomiting without chest pain
  • Syncope or pre-syncope
  • Unexplained fatigue or weakness

Physical Examination

Cardiovascular
  • Pulse, BP (both arms)
  • JVP, heart sounds, murmurs
  • Signs of heart failure (rales, oedema)
  • Pulsus paradoxus (tamponade)
  • Inter-arm BP difference >20 mmHg (aortic dissection)
Respiratory
  • Respiratory rate, SpO‚ÇÇ
  • Breath sounds (reduced = pneumothorax)
  • Crepitations/rales (pulmonary oedema)
  • Pleural rub (pleuritis, PE)
  • Tracheal deviation (tension pneumothorax)
Other Systems
  • Chest wall tenderness (musculoskeletal)
  • Abdominal exam (GI causes)
  • Unilateral leg swelling (DVT/PE)
  • Skin rash/herpes zoster
  • Marfanoid features (aortic dissection)

Initial Investigations

Within 10 min
ECG
  • ST elevation/depression
  • Q waves (previous MI)
  • New LBBB (consider STEMI)
  • T-wave inversion (ischemia)
  • Pericarditis: diffuse ST elevation
  • PE: S1Q3T3 pattern
Urgent
Chest X-ray
  • Pneumothorax, pneumonia
  • Widened mediastinum (aortic dissection)
  • Globular heart (tamponade)
  • Pulmonary oedema
  • Usually normal in ACS
Serial
Cardiac Biomarkers
  • High-sensitivity troponin (preferred)
  • Repeat at 1-3 hour intervals
  • Negative >3 hrs after pain: low risk
  • CK only if troponin unavailable
  • Biotin can cause false negatives
Baseline
Other Blood Tests
  • FBC (anaemia, infection)
  • Renal profile (baseline, troponin interpretation)
  • Lipase/amylase (pancreatitis)
  • D-dimer (PE workup)
  • LFT (cholecystitis)

Key Diagnostic Algorithms

STEMI
ST-Elevation MI Criteria
  • ST elevation >1mm in ‚â•2 contiguous leads
  • New LBBB with ischemic symptoms
  • Immediate reperfusion therapy indicated
  • Door-to-balloon <90 minutes or fibrinolysis <30 minutes
NSTEMI
Non-ST-Elevation ACS
  • ST depression or T-wave inversion
  • Elevated troponin
  • Urgent invasive strategy if high-risk features
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy
PE
Pulmonary Embolism Workup
  • Wells Score >4: PE likely ‚Üí CTPA
  • Wells Score ‚â§4: D-dimer ‚Üí if negative, exclude PE
  • PERC rule can be used in low-risk patients
  • High-risk PE: hypotension, shock, RV dysfunction
Aortic Dissection
Aortic Dissection Features
  • Sudden tearing pain radiating to back
  • Pulse deficits or inter-arm BP difference
  • Widened mediastinum on CXR
  • CT angiography is imaging modality of choice

Management by Condition

Emergency
STEMI Management
  • Time-critical: Door-to-balloon <90 minutes
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + ticagrelor/prasugrel)
  • Anticoagulation (heparin)
  • Primary PCI is preferred reperfusion strategy
  • If PCI delayed >2h: fibrinolytics (if no contraindications)
  • Beta-blocker, ACE inhibitor, statin
Urgent
NSTEMI/Unstable Angina
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy
  • Anticoagulation (LMWH or fondaparinux)
  • High-risk: invasive strategy within 24 hours
  • Anti-anginal therapy (nitrates, beta-blockers)
  • Statin therapy
  • Consider GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors if high-risk
Specialist
Spontaneous Coronary Artery Dissection (SCAD)
  • Usually managed medically (different from atherosclerotic ACS)
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy for 1 year
  • Beta-blockers for prevention
  • Avoid thrombolytics
  • PCI may worsen outcomes
  • Specialist cardiology referral
Emergency
Aortic Dissection
  • Immediate: Beta-blockade (IV esmolol/labetalol)
  • Systolic BP target: 100-120 mmHg
  • Add vasodilator if needed
  • Stanford A: Urgent surgical repair
  • Stanford B: Medical management (unless complications)
  • CT angiography for diagnosis
Emergency
Tension Pneumothorax
  • Immediate needle decompression: 2nd ICS MCL
  • Followed by chest tube insertion
  • Do not wait for imaging
  • Clinical diagnosis based on hypotension, JVD, absent breath sounds
  • Consider surgical consult if recurrent
High-Risk
Massive Pulmonary Embolism
  • Hemodynamic support (fluids, inotropes)
  • Thrombolysis if no contraindications
  • Surgical embolectomy or catheter-directed therapy
  • IVC filter if anticoagulation contraindicated
  • ICU admission
  • Anticoagulation with heparin
Standard
Submassive PE
  • Anticoagulation (LMWH, fondaparinux, DOAC)
  • Consider thrombolysis if RV dysfunction
  • Oxygen if hypoxic
  • Analgesia for pleuritic pain
  • Hospital admission for monitoring
  • Long-term anticoagulation (minimum 3 months)
Emergency
Cardiac Tamponade
  • Urgent pericardiocentesis if hemodynamic compromise
  • Emergency echo-guided drainage
  • Fluid bolus may temporize
  • Treat underlying cause
  • Surgical consult for recurrent cases
  • Pericardial window if needed
Medical
Pericarditis
  • NSAIDs (avoid in MI)
  • Colchicine to prevent recurrence
  • Treat underlying cause
  • Avoid anticoagulation if risk of tamponade
  • Monitor for tamponade
  • Outpatient follow-up if stable
Supportive
Stable Angina
  • Anti-anginal therapy (nitrates, beta-blockers, CCB)
  • Secondary prevention (aspirin, statin, ACEi)
  • Risk factor modification
  • Cardiac rehabilitation referral
  • Consider revascularization if symptomatic
  • Lifestyle modifications
Medical
GORD/Peptic Ulcer Disease
  • PPI therapy (omeprazole 20-40mg daily)
  • H. pylori testing and treatment if positive
  • Lifestyle modifications
  • Avoid NSAIDs
  • Consider endoscopy if alarm features
  • Cardiac workup first if any uncertainty
Supportive
Musculoskeletal Pain
  • NSAIDs or acetaminophen
  • Local heat or ice
  • Physical therapy if recurrent
  • Avoid provoking activities
  • Topical NSAIDs
  • Reassurance and follow-up if no red flags
Medical
Anxiety/Panic Disorder
  • Reassurance after excluding organic causes
  • Counseling/CBT referral
  • SSRIs if recurrent
  • Breathing exercises
  • Consider beta-blocker for somatic symptoms
  • Follow-up with primary care

General Management Principles

Oxygen Therapy
  • Target SpO‚ÇÇ 94-98% (88-92% if COPD risk)
  • Do NOT give oxygen routinely in normoxic ACS patients
  • Liberal oxygen may increase mortality in acute illness
Pain Management
  • Morphine for severe pain (ACS, PE, dissection)
  • Nitrates for cardiac ischemia (avoid if hypotensive)
  • NSAIDs for musculoskeletal pain (avoid in cardiac patients)

Disposition and Follow-up Planning

Admit
Admit to CCU/ICU
  • STEMI, NSTEMI with high-risk features
  • Unstable angina with ongoing pain
  • Aortic dissection (Type A or complicated Type B)
  • Massive PE (hemodynamic instability)
  • Cardiac tamponade
  • Tension pneumothorax (post-decompression)
Admit
Admit to Ward
  • NSTEMI (stable)
  • Submassive PE requiring monitoring
  • Pericarditis (initial evaluation)
  • Severe pneumonia
  • Pancreatitis, cholecystitis
  • SCAD (initial management)
Discharge
Discharge Home
  • Stable angina (risk-stratified)
  • Costochondritis
  • GORD (after cardiac exclusion)
  • Anxiety/panic disorder (after exclusion)
  • Viral pleuritis
  • Mild musculoskeletal pain
Specialist
Specialist Follow-up
  • Cardiology: All ACS, SCAD, stable angina
  • Cardiothoracic surgery: Aortic dissection
  • Respiratory: PE, severe pneumonia
  • Gastroenterology: Pancreatitis, cholecystitis
  • Rheumatology: Connective tissue disorders
  • Psychiatry: Recurrent anxiety disorders

Specific Follow-up Recommendations

Post-PCI/MI Follow-up
  • Cardiology clinic within 2-4 weeks
  • Medication optimization
  • Cardiac rehabilitation referral
  • Echo at 6-12 weeks (LV function)
  • Lipid profile at 4-12 weeks
  • Continued dual antiplatelet therapy
PE Follow-up
  • Repeat imaging if persistent symptoms
  • Anticoagulation for minimum 3 months
  • Thrombophilia testing if unprovoked
  • Consider IVC filter if recurrent
  • Doppler legs for DVT
  • Cardiology review if RV dysfunction
Aortic Dissection Follow-up
  • Regular imaging (CT/MRI) at 1, 3, 6, 12 months
  • Blood pressure control (target <120/80)
  • Beta-blocker therapy
  • Avoid heavy lifting/straining
  • Genetic testing if connective tissue disease suspected
  • Lifelong surveillance for aneurysm formation
Stable Angina Follow-up
  • Primary care review annually
  • Cardiology if symptoms worsen
