DVT Prevention for Travellers

Clinical Decision Support Tool | NICE CKS Guidelines (November 2023)

For healthcare professionals assessing and managing travel-related venous thromboembolism risk in patients aged 16+

Assessment
Key Principle: Risk assessment must be individualized for each travel occasion as relative risk may change over time.
Risk Factor Assessment

Evaluate both travel-specific and patient-specific risk factors:

High-Risk Factors

  • Active malignancy
  • Recent birth (within 6 weeks)
  • Previous unprovoked VTE
  • Previous travel-related VTE
  • Significant immobility (e.g., lower limb immobilization)
  • Recent major surgery (within 4 weeks)
  • Two or more combined risk factors (see right)

Additional Risk Factors

  • Pregnancy
  • Obesity (BMI ‚â•30 kg/m¬≤)
  • Hormonal treatment (OCP/HRT)
  • Family history of VTE
  • Chronic venous insufficiency
  • Extremes of height (<1.60m or >1.90m)
  • Older age (risk increases from 40+)
  • Recent trauma/surgery
Travel Duration Assessment

Risk increases after 4 hours of continuous travel and continues to rise with duration.

Critical Threshold: Assess all journeys anticipated to last ‚â•4 hours by air, car, bus, or train.
Risk Stratification Algorithm

Categorize patients into risk groups to guide management:

Low Risk

No pre-existing risk factors

  • Healthy individuals
  • No risk factors listed above
  • Travel duration considerations only

High Risk

Any high-risk factor present

  • Active malignancy
  • Recent surgery/immobility
  • Previous VTE (unprovoked or travel-related)
  • Postpartum (<6 weeks)
  • Multiple moderate risk factors
Diagnosis & Risk Stratification
Note: This section focuses on risk stratification rather than diagnostic confirmation, as DVT prevention is a prophylactic intervention.
Low Risk Classification General Advice Only

Patients with no pre-existing risk factors regardless of travel duration.

Management: General measures only. No pharmacological prophylaxis required.
High Risk Classification Specialist Input

Patients with any high-risk factor or multiple moderate risk factors.

Management: Requires specialist advice. Consider LMWH and/or compression stockings.
Decision Pathway

Systematic approach to risk assessment:

  1. Step 1: Determine travel duration (‚â•4 hours?)
  2. Step 2: Screen for high-risk factors
  3. Step 3: Count additional risk factors
  4. Step 4: Classify as low or high risk
  5. Step 5: Apply appropriate prevention strategy
  6. Step 6: Provide tailored patient education
Reassessment: Required for each travel occasion as risk factors may change.

Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH) Specialist Prescribing

For high-risk patients where travel is unavoidable:

Indication: High-risk patients after specialist assessment
Timing: Administer 2-4 hours before flight
Duration: Cover outgoing and return flights
Formulation: Pre-filled syringes for ease of use
Counseling Points:
  • Provide letter for security/customs regarding needles/syringes
  • Warn about increased bleeding/bruising risk
  • Advise on safe storage and disposal
  • Ensure training if self-administering
  • Urgent medical attention for severe headache or uncontrolled bleeding
Graduated Compression Stockings Consider

Role of compression therapy in travel-related DVT prevention:

Prescription vs OTC: Stockings not prescribable solely for travel DVT prevention.

Compression Classes:

  • Class 1 (14-17 mmHg): Superficial varicose veins, pregnancy
  • Class 2 (18-24 mmHg): Moderate varicose veins, leg ulcer prevention
  • Class 3 (25-35 mmHg): Gross varicose veins, post-thrombotic syndrome
Flight Socks: Proprietary flight socks (14-17 mmHg) available OTC may be cheaper than prescription for non-exempt patients. Higher ratings (18-21 mmHg) also available.
Fitting: Correct fit is crucial. Measure ABPI if arterial disease suspected. Contraindicated if ABPI <0.8.
Aspirin Prophylaxis NOT Recommended
Do NOT recommend aspirin for travel-related DVT prevention.
  • No evidence of benefit
  • Risk of bleeding outweighs any potential benefit
  • Patients already on aspirin should NOT increase dose
  • If LMWH prescribed, don't discontinue aspirin without specialist advice
Special Populations
Pregnancy & Postpartum High Risk

Tailored approach for pregnant women and those up to 6 weeks postpartum:

<4 Hours

No special measures required

‚â•4 Hours

General measures + consider LMWH/stockings

Postpartum: Women within 6 weeks of birth are automatically high risk. Must discuss with obstetrician.
  • No additional risk factors: General measures only
  • With additional risk factors: Discuss LMWH with obstetrician
  • Consider graduated compression stockings
  • Follow RCOG guidelines for air travel in pregnancy
Joint Replacement or Fracture Consult Required

Specific considerations for orthopedic patients:

Hip/Knee Replacement:

  • Advise checking with orthopaedic surgeon, airline, and insurance
  • No consensus on timing - individualized advice needed
  • Consider delaying travel if possible

Plaster Cast:

Airline Restrictions:
  • Short flights (<2h): Usually restricted for 24 hours post-cast
  • Long flights (>2h): Usually restricted for 48 hours post-cast
  • If essential: Cast must be split full length
  • Pneumatic splints: Release some air for altitude expansion
Recent DVT/PE Specialist Management

Management of patients with recent venous thromboembolism:

New Diagnosis (<2 weeks): Delay air travel for 2 weeks if possible. If unavoidable, seek specialist advice.
Stable on Anticoagulants (‚â•2 weeks):
  • Ensure stability on anticoagulant therapy
  • Reassure low risk of further thrombosis
  • Provide general travel measures
  • No additional prophylaxis typically needed
Follow-up & Patient Education
Post-Travel Warning Signs URGENT

Educate all patients to seek urgent medical attention if they develop:

Symptoms of DVT:

  • Unilateral leg swelling (one leg more affected than the other)
  • Calf pain or tenderness
  • Increased warmth in affected limb
  • Skin discoloration

Symptoms of Pulmonary Embolism:

  • Sudden shortness of breath
  • Chest pain (worse with breathing)
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Coughing up blood
  • Lightheadedness or fainting
Timing: Most travel-related DVTs occur within 1-2 weeks of travel. Risk returns to baseline by 8 weeks.
Follow-up Timeline
  • Immediate post-travel: Advise vigilance for 2 weeks
  • At 2 weeks: Risk significantly decreased
  • At 8 weeks: Baseline risk restored
  • Any time: If symptoms develop, seek urgent care
Documentation: Record risk assessment and advice given in patient notes for future reference.
Patient Information Resources

Provide patients with written information covering:

  • Explanation of travel-related DVT risk
  • General preventive measures
  • Specific recommendations (if high risk)
  • Warning signs and symptoms
  • When and where to seek medical help
  • Insurance documentation requirements
  • For LMWH users: Administration guidance and travel letter
Reassurance: Emphasize that risk is very low in healthy individuals without risk factors.
