Emergency Situations

Quick identification and management of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and severe hypoglycemia

Key Features of DKA
  • Hyperglycemia (random plasma glucose > 11 mmol/L)
  • Ketosis (blood ketones ≥ 3 mmol/L or urine ketones 2+ or more)
  • Metabolic acidosis (bicarbonate < 15 mmol/L or pH < 7.3)
Higher risk in children
Mortality from cerebral edema ~25%
Emergency referral required

Immediate Actions

  • Urgent hospital admission
  • IV fluid resuscitation
  • Insulin infusion
  • Electrolyte monitoring

Red Flags

  • Altered consciousness
  • Signs of cerebral edema
  • Severe dehydration
  • Kussmaul breathing
Key Features of Severe Hypoglycemia
  • Blood glucose < 3.9 mmol/L
  • Neuroglycopenic symptoms (confusion, seizures, coma)
  • Unable to self-treat
More common with intensive insulin therapy
8% of deaths in people < 56 years
Immediate treatment required

Immediate Actions

  • IM glucagon 1mg (if available)
  • IV dextrose 10-20%
  • Monitor until fully recovered
  • Review insulin regimen

Red Flags

  • Repeated episodes
  • No warning symptoms
  • Overnight episodes
  • Impaired awareness

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Random plasma glucose ≥ 11.1 mmol/L with symptoms
  • Fasting plasma glucose ≥ 7.0 mmol/L
  • HbA1c ≥ 48 mmol/mol

Confirming Type 1

  • Diabetes-specific autoantibody testing
  • C-peptide measurement
  • Clinical features (acute onset, weight loss)
When to Suspect Type 1 in Adults
  • Age < 50 years at diagnosis
  • Normal BMI (≤ 25 kg/m²)
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Ketoacidosis at presentation
  • Personal/family history of autoimmune disease
Urgent Referral Required

Children and young people presenting in primary care with suspected diabetes should be referred to and seen by a multidisciplinary paediatric diabetes team on the same day.

Key Symptoms

  • Polyuria
  • Polydipsia
  • Weight loss
  • Fatigue

Signs of DKA

  • Abdominal pain
  • Vomiting
  • Dehydration
  • Altered consciousness

Management Overview

Structured Education
  • Offer to all adults with type 1 diabetes
  • Offer to children from diagnosis, updated annually
  • Include carbohydrate counting
  • Include insulin management
Glucose Monitoring
  • Offer real-time CGM to adults
  • Offer intermittently scanned CGM as alternative
  • Offer real-time CGM to children
  • Offer blood ketone testing strips
Insulin Therapy
  • Multiple daily injections or pump therapy
  • Basal-bolus regimen preferred
  • Consider pump if inadequate control
  • Intensive therapy for children
Cardiovascular Risk
  • Offer statins to adults ≥40 years
  • Offer statins to adults with CVD
  • BP target ≤ 140/90 mmHg
  • Annual risk assessment

Annual Review

Check Frequency Target
HbA1c Every 3-6 months ≤ 48 mmol/mol
Blood pressure Annually ≤ 140/90 mmHg
Foot examination Annually No ulceration
Retinal screening Annually No retinopathy
Urine albumin Annually ACR < 3 mg/mmol

Special Considerations

Pregnancy
  • Pre-conception counseling
  • Tight glucose control
  • Folic acid 5mg daily
Driving
  • Inform DVLA
  • Test glucose before driving
  • Carry rapid-acting glucose

Complications

Diabetic Kidney Disease
  • 30-50% develop microalbuminuria
  • Progressive condition
  • Annual screening essential
  • ACE-I/ARB if proteinuria
Diabetic Retinopathy
  • Most develop within 20 years
  • Can cause vision loss
  • Annual screening essential
  • Early treatment preserves vision
Diabetic Foot Disease
  • 16-26% develop neuropathy
  • Risk of ulceration
  • Annual examination essential
  • Prompt referral for ulcers
Cardiovascular Disease
  • Relative risk: 2.3 (men), 3 (women)
  • Leading cause of death
  • Statins for primary prevention
  • BP control essential
Psychological Complications

Common Issues

  • Diabetes distress (20-40%)
  • Depression (2× general population)
  • Anxiety
  • Eating disorders

Management

  • Regular screening
  • Access to mental health professionals
  • Consider diabetes-specific therapy
  • Support groups
