Sarcoidosis Clinical Reference

Quick reference tool for primary care clinicians (Last updated: January 2024)

RED FLAGS: Cardiac symptoms (arrhythmias, heart failure, syncope), neurological symptoms (facial palsy, seizures), severe hypercalcemia, or progressive respiratory symptoms require urgent referral.

When to Suspect Sarcoidosis

  • Incidental finding of hilar adenopathy on chest X-ray
  • Abnormal LFTs (especially elevated ALP)
  • Hypercalcaemia
  • Dyspnoea, persistent cough, wheezing
  • Skin changes (erythema nodosum, lupus pernio)
  • Ocular abnormalities (uveitis)
  • Fatigue (present in up to 80% of cases)

Initial Assessment

Clinical Examination

  • Respiratory examination
  • Eye examination (if symptoms)
  • Skin examination
  • Palpate lymph nodes
  • Abdominal examination

Initial Investigations

  • Chest X-ray
  • Blood tests: LFTs, U&E, calcium
  • Full blood count
  • 12-lead ECG
  • Lung function tests

Disease Staging

Stage Chest X-ray Findings Prognosis
0 Normal Good
I Enlarged nodes only 55-90% remission
II Nodes + parenchymal changes 40-70% remission
III Parenchymal changes without nodes ~20% remission
IV Fibrosis Poor

Management

Suspected
Confirmed

Referral

Refer all people with suspected sarcoidosis to appropriate secondary care physician based on organ systems involved.

Urgent Referral Indications

  • Cardiac symptoms (arrhythmias, heart failure)
  • Neurological symptoms (facial palsy, seizures)
  • Severe hypercalcaemia
  • Progressive respiratory symptoms

Primary Care Role

  • Monitor for fatigue and mood changes
  • Prescribe medications under shared-care arrangements
  • Offer self-care advice
  • Refer for occupational therapy/pulmonary rehab
  • Re-refer if new/worsening symptoms develop

Differential Diagnosis

  • Tuberculosis
  • Lung cancer
  • Lymphoma
  • Congestive heart failure
  • Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis
  • Occupational lung disease (berylliosis, silicosis)
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis

Prognosis

Good Prognostic Factors

  • Erythema nodosum
  • Hilar adenopathy
  • Anterior uveitis
  • Stage I-II disease
  • Age < 40 years

Poor Prognostic Factors

  • Age > 40 years
  • African American/African Caribbean origin
  • Lupus pernio
  • Posterior uveitis
  • CNS/cardiac involvement
  • Stage III-IV disease

Approximately two-thirds of people experience disease remission within 2-5 years. 10-30% develop chronic disease requiring prolonged treatment.

CLINICAL TIP: Be aware that sarcoidosis can affect almost any organ system. Consider sarcoidosis in patients with multi-system symptoms, even if respiratory symptoms are mild or absent.
