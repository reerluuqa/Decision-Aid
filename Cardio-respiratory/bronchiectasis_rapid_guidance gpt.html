Right now: rapid decision support

Prioritise safety, then phenotype the problem (suspected disease vs infective exacerbation).

Immediate admission triggers

Arrange hospital admission if comorbidity/psychosocial factors make community care unsafe, or if IV antibiotics / serious illness suspected (cardiorespiratory failure or sepsis).

RED FLAGS
  • Cyanosis
  • Confusion
  • Marked breathlessness, rapid respiration, or laboured breathing
  • Peripheral oedema
  • Temperature ‚â• 38¬∞C
If symptoms worsen rapidly/significantly at any time, reassess for pneumonia or more serious illness.

Core actions (non-admitted)

Start treatment promptly: send sputum for culture & sensitivities before antibiotics but do not delay antibiotics while waiting.

RECOMMENDED
  • Antibiotic course 7‚Äì14 days (review when cultures return)
  • If on long-term antibiotics: stop and prescribe a different class for the exacerbation
  • Ensure airway clearance technique; urgent physiotherapy if not taught / not coping
  • Modify antibiotic if no improvement, guided by cultures
Do not routinely offer inhaled corticosteroids unless another indication (e.g. asthma/COPD). Trial long-acting bronchodilator if significant breathlessness.
TRIGGER
When to suspect an infective exacerbation
Use symptoms to decide: treat now vs reassess other diagnoses.
Suspect exacerbation if there is a change in ‚â•1 common symptom, or if new symptoms develop.
  • Change in symptoms: ‚Üë sputum volume or purulence, worsening dyspnoea, ‚Üë cough, ‚Üë fatigue/malaise
  • New symptoms: fever, pleuritic pain, haemoptysis

Suspected bronchiectasis: what to do today

First-contact workup + referral for definitive diagnosis (HRCT and aetiology testing).
WHEN TO SUSPECT Hover tips
Adults ‚Äî key ‚Äúthink bronchiectasis‚Äù patterns
  • Persistent mucopurulent/purulent sputum (especially with risk factors)
  • Cough > 8 weeks, especially with sputum
  • RA with chronic productive cough / recurrent chest infections
  • COPD with frequent exacerbations (‚â•2/yr) and/or stable sputum culture with Pseudomonas
  • Severe/poorly controlled asthma
  • IBD with chronic productive cough
Common features: daily large-volume purulent sputum, dyspnoea, fever, fatigue, rhinosinusitis, weight loss, chest pain, haemoptysis.

Children ‚Äî ‚Äúdon‚Äôt-miss‚Äù prompts

  • Recurrent/persistent wet cough > 6 weeks
  • Severe pneumonia with incomplete resolution; recurrent pneumonia
  • Persistent/unexplained signs or CXR abnormalities
  • Localized chronic bronchial obstruction; oesophageal/upper airway structural/functional disorders
  • Unexplained haemoptysis; exertional breathlessness
On exam (children): clubbing/cyanosis/chest deformity may occur, but persistent crackles/wheeze are less common.

Primary care investigations (same-day/soon)

  • Sputum culture (colonizing pathogens)
  • Chest X‚Äëray (exclude other pathology; baseline; may help if severe)
  • Spirometry (severity + coexistent COPD/asthma)
  • SpO‚ÇÇ
  • FBC incl. differential
  • Other tests guided by findings to confirm/rule out underlying causes
  • Document smoking; weight/BMI; screen for anxiety/depression (esp. severe breathlessness)

Referral (do not ‚Äúsit on‚Äù suspected disease)

Refer all people with suspected bronchiectasis to a respiratory consultant for investigations to confirm diagnosis, determine cause, and guide treatment.

RESPIRATORY REFERRAL
Secondary care commonly uses HRCT (thin-section) to establish diagnosis and conducts structured aetiology testing (e.g., immunoglobulins; CF testing in relevant age/phenotype; ABPA testing; PCD evaluation; etc.).
WHAT RESPIRATORY WILL DO
Secondary care workup (aetiology & severity)
Useful to set expectations, reduce delays.
Common elements include imaging confirmation and targeted cause-finding.
  • HRCT (thin-section) to establish bronchiectasis
  • Testing for CF in children, adults ‚â§40, and older adults with compatible phenotype
  • Screen for gross antibody deficiency (IgG/IgA/IgM)
  • ABPA testing (total IgE + Aspergillus-specific IgE / skin prick)
  • Pneumococcal antibody levels; PCD evaluation in appropriate phenotype
  • Selected tests: RF/anti-CCP/ANA/ANCA, HIV, A1AT; GI investigations
  • Bronchoscopy for localized disease to rule out endobronchial lesion/foreign body

Exacerbation pathway

Make the disposition decision first; then treat (culture first, but no delay).
NEED HELP? Escalation
Seek specialist advice if:
  • Not improving with repeated oral antibiotic courses
  • Sputum bacteria resistant to available oral options
  • Unable to tolerate oral therapy
If previously on long-term antibiotics: after treating exacerbation, prophylaxis may need switching based on cultures‚Äîinform respiratory team.
CHECKLIST Copy-ready
Treat in community (if safe)
  • Send sputum for culture/sensitivities before antibiotics
  • Start oral antibiotic (typical duration 7‚Äì14 days)
  • If on long-term antibiotics: stop and choose a different class
  • Airway clearance technique during exacerbation; urgent physiotherapy if needed
  • Review when cultures return; change therapy if no improvement

Follow-up & monitoring

Identify high-risk phenotypes that need specialist review; standardise annual review.

Who needs secondary care follow-up?

High-risk people should be reviewed in secondary care (and all children).

  • ‚â• 3 exacerbations/year
  • Chronic colonisation: Pseudomonas, MRSA, or non-tuberculous mycobacteria
  • Declining lung function / advanced disease
  • ABPA
  • Long-term antibiotic therapy
  • Associated RA, immune deficiency, IBD, ABPA, or primary ciliary dyskinesia
  • Considering lung transplantation

Annual review checklist (primary care for lower risk)

  • Symptoms + impact on daily life; consider pulmonary rehabilitation if breathlessness affects life
  • BMI; if <20, seek specialist nutritional advice
  • Exacerbation frequency; refer if ‚â•3/year (or fewer but high morbidity)
  • Sputum sample for culture (specialist follow-up for chronic Pseudomonas/NTM/MRSA)
  • MRC dyspnoea scale, spirometry, SpO‚ÇÇ
  • Adherence to airway clearance exercises
  • Smoking status; cessation support
  • Immunisation: pneumococcal, COVID‚Äë19, seasonal influenza
Do not repeat chest X‚Äërays unless clinically indicated.

Prevention of exacerbations (specialist-led)

Key principle: optimise non-antibiotic measures first; consider prophylaxis only with repeated exacerbations and specialist input.
PROPHYLAXIS
When and how to consider long-term antibiotics
For repeated exacerbations‚Äîspecialist initiation recommended.
Do not routinely offer antibiotic prophylaxis. Seek specialist advice for repeated exacerbations; people with ‚â•3 exacerbations/year may be offered prophylaxis by respiratory specialist.
ADULT OPTIONS (SPECIALIST)
  • Azithromycin 500 mg three times/week or 250 mg daily
  • Erythromycin ethyl succinate 400 mg BD
  • Start azithromycin 250 mg three times/week to minimise adverse effects, then titrate
  • Minimum duration ~6 months; up to 1 year may be required
  • Concurrent Pseudomonas: first-line usually inhaled colistin
SAFETY BEFORE MACROLIDES
  • Optimise airway clearance + pulmonary rehab first
  • Baseline ECG (QTc) + baseline LFTs
  • Sputum microbiology including NTM; avoid macrolide monotherapy if NTM identified
  • Avoid macrolides for 2 weeks before NTM testing
  • After starting: LFT at 1 month, then every 6 months; ECG at 1 month (stop if QTc prolongation)
Shared decision-making points: benefits (reduced exacerbations) vs antimicrobial resistance, adverse effects (GI, hearing/balance, cardiac), and drug interactions.
SELF-MANAGEMENT
After an exacerbation: what to give the patient
A lean plan reduces delays and recurrence.
  • Personalised advice on lifestyle: diet, exercise, smoking cessation
  • Respiratory physiotherapy details / airway clearance technique
  • Recognising early signs of exacerbation; when to seek help and urgency
  • When to take rescue medication (if prescribed)
  • Contact details for respiratory team
  • Consider prophylaxis need (specialist advice) and other treatments

Differentials to actively consider

Bronchiectasis can co-exist with asthma/COPD‚Äîavoid anchoring.
DIFFERENTIALS Search-aware
  • Asthma (may co-exist; inspiratory squeaks/crackles suggest bronchiectasis)
  • COPD (may co-exist; bronchiectasis often has coarse crackles; COPD has diminished breath sounds)
  • Chronic sinusitis (squeaks/crackles uncommon)
  • Pneumonia (shorter symptom duration; systemic features e.g. fever >38¬∞C, sweats, myalgia)
  • Lung cancer (persistent cough, haemoptysis, weight loss, persistent hoarseness)
  • Interstitial lung disease (typically dry cough + fine crepitations)
  • Tuberculosis (persistent productive cough ¬± breathlessness/haemoptysis)
PRACTICAL Avoid misses
Quick heuristics
  • Longstanding productive cough + recurrent infections ‚Üí bronchiectasis higher on list
  • Worsening systemic features or pleuritic pain ‚Üí re-check pneumonia/sepsis
  • Unexplained haemoptysis, weight loss, hoarseness ‚Üí consider malignancy pathway
  • Risk factors/exposure ‚Üí keep TB on radar
Arrange investigations to help rule out alternative diagnoses as indicated.
