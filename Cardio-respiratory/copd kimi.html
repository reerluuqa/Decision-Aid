COPD Management Algorithm

NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary - Primary Care Pathway

Last revised: October 2025 | For adults aged ≥35 years

Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk factor identification

  1. Suspect COPD in patients ≥35 years with:
    • Exposure to risk factors (smoking, occupational/environmental)
    • Symptoms: persistent breathlessness, chronic cough, sputum production, wheeze
    Urgent: Hemoptysis, rapid decline, suspicious chest X-ray <40 years old: Consider α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  2. Take detailed history:
    Smoking

    Pack-years = (cigarettes/day ÷ 20) × years smoked

    Occupational

    Dusts (coal, silica, grains), fumes, chemicals

    Impact

    Use MRC dyspnea scale or CAT score

MRC Dyspnoea Scale

1 Not troubled by breathlessness except during strenuous exercise
2 Short of breath when hurrying or walking up a slight hill
3 Walks slower than contemporaries on level, or stops for breath
4 Stops for breath after walking about 100m or few minutes on level
5 Too breathless to leave house, or breathless when dressing/undressing
MRC 1-2: Mild symptoms MRC 3-4: Moderate-severe MRC 5: Very severe
  1. Physical examination may show:
    • Hyperinflation, wheeze, crackles, cyanosis
    • Cor pulmonale signs: raised JVP, peripheral oedema
  2. Initial investigations:
    Chest X-ray

    Exclude lung cancer, TB, bronchiectasis

    Full blood count

    Identify anaemia or polycythaemia

    Spirometry

    Post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.7 confirms COPD

Diagnosis & Severity

Spirometry-based confirmation and grading

  1. Spirometry confirmation:
    • Perform post-bronchodilator spirometry (15-20 mins after 400mcg salbutamol)
    • FEV1/FVC <0.7 confirms persistent airflow obstruction
    • Consider alternative diagnoses if older patient without typical symptoms
    Do not rely on reversibility testing for diagnosis
  2. Severity grading (post-bronchodilator FEV1):
    Stage 1: Mild

    FEV1 ≥80% predicted

    Stage 2: Moderate

    FEV1 50-79% predicted

    Stage 3: Severe

    FEV1 30-49% predicted

    Stage 4: Very Severe

    FEV1 <30% predicted or <50% with respiratory failure

  3. Differential diagnosis to consider:
    • Asthma (variable symptoms, nocturnal symptoms, atopy)
    • Bronchiectasis (copious sputum, childhood pneumonia)
    • Heart failure (breathless when lying flat, fine crepitations)
    • Lung cancer (persistent cough, haemoptysis, weight loss)
    • Tuberculosis (productive cough, systemic symptoms)

α1-Antitrypsin Deficiency

Consider in patients:

  • Age <40 years with COPD symptoms
  • Minimal smoking history
  • Family history of COPD/liver disease
Requires specialist referral and family screening

Treatment & Management

Pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions

Essential for All Patients

Smoking Cessation

Offer at every opportunity - most effective intervention

Vaccinations

Influenza (annual), pneumococcal (once-only), COVID-19

Pulmonary Rehabilitation

Refer if MRC ≥3 or recent hospitalization

Self-Management Plan

Personalized plan for exacerbation recognition

  1. Initial pharmacological treatment (based on GOLD ABCE groups):
    Group Characteristics Initial Therapy
    A 0-1 exacerbations (not hospitalized), mMRC 0-1 or CAT <10 Bronchodilator (prefer long-acting)
    B 0-1 exacerbations (not hospitalized), mMRC ≥2 or CAT ≥10 LABA + LAMA
    E ≥2 exacerbations or ≥1 hospitalized LABA + LAMA (consider +ICS if eos >300)
    LABA: Salmeterol, Formoterol, Indacaterol LAMA: Tiotropium, Aclidinium, Glycopyrronium ICS: Risk of pneumonia - discuss with patient
  2. Inhaled corticosteroid considerations:
    • Add to LABA+LAMA if blood eosinophils >300 cells/µL
    • Do NOT use LABA+ICS alone in COPD
    • Increased risk of pneumonia - warn patient
  3. Other pharmacological options:
    Mucolytics

    Consider if chronic productive cough

    Theophylline

    Specialist use, monitor plasma levels

    Roflumilast

    Severe COPD with chronic bronchitis (FEV1<50%)

    Azithromycin

    Prophylaxis if ≥3 exacerbations/year + hospitalization

When to Refer

Urgent: Lung cancer suspicion, cor pulmonale, rapid decline Specialist: Diagnostic uncertainty, severe disease, α1-antitrypsin deficiency Assessment: Oxygen therapy, long-term steroids, surgery

Follow-up & Monitoring

Regular review and exacerbation management

  1. Review frequency:
    • Mild/moderate/severe COPD: At least annually
    • Very severe COPD: At least twice yearly
    • Severe COPD with interventions: Specialist review
  2. At each review assess:
    Symptoms

    MRC scale, exacerbation frequency, impact on life

    Inhaler Technique

    Check technique and adherence before stepping up

    BMI & Smoking

    Record BMI, smoking status - offer support

    Spirometry

    Monitor FEV1 decline (≥500ml/5y = rapid progression)

    Vaccinations

    Ensure influenza, pneumococcal, COVID-19 up to date

    Comorbidities

    Screen for anxiety, depression, CVD, osteoporosis

  3. Exacerbation management:

    Recognizing Exacerbations

    Acute worsening of symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variation:

    • Increased breathlessness, cough, sputum volume/purulence
    • Upper respiratory symptoms, fever, increased wheeze
    • Reduced exercise tolerance, fatigue, ankle swelling
    Admit if: Severe dyspnea, cyanosis, SpO2<90%, confusion, acute confusion Home management possible if: No red flags, can cope at home

    Home Treatment

    1. Increase short-acting bronchodilator frequency
    2. Prednisolone 30mg daily for 5 days (if increased breathlessness)
    3. Antibiotic if sputum purulent and increased volume/thickness:
      • First choice: Amoxicillin, doxycycline, or clarithromycin for 5 days
  4. Post-exacerbation follow-up (6 weeks):
    • Assess residual symptoms and optimize management
    • Review inhaler technique and adherence
    • Consider referral/re-referral for pulmonary rehabilitation
    • Provide rescue medications (steroids/antibiotics) for future exacerbations
    • Investigate if ≥3 courses steroids/antibiotics in past year

Special Considerations

Air Travel

Assess if FEV1<30%, bullous disease, recent exacerbation

Driving

Notify DVLA only if complications with syncope/dizziness

Palliative Care

Consider if life expectancy <6-12 months
