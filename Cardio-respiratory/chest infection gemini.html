Chest Infections - Adult

Last Revised: January 2025
Covers Acute Bronchitis & Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP) for age 12+.

URGENT: Sepsis & Referrals
  • Sepsis: Be alert for signs of sepsis in anyone with acute respiratory infection.
  • Immediate Referral: Refer to hospital if signs of cardiorespiratory failure, sepsis, or symptoms are not improving as expected with antibiotics.
  • Oxygen: Arrange urgent admission if pulse oximetry shows hypoxia (e.g., <90% in young people is severe).

Assessment: Bronchitis vs. Pneumonia

Clinical judgement is key. No single symptom is diagnostic, but patterns exist.

Acute Bronchitis

  • Cough: Predominant symptom. Lasts 3-4 weeks.
  • Examination: Usually mildly ill. Wheeze often present. Rhonchi may clear with coughing.
  • Systemic: Fever is usually mild or absent.
  • Cause: Usually viral.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

  • Cough: Plus other features (dyspnoea, sputum, pain).
  • Examination: Moderately/Severely ill. Focal chest signs (dullness, course crepitations, bronchial breathing).
  • Systemic: Temp ≥38Â°C, tachycardia, tachypnoea, sweating/shivers.
  • Diagnosis: Assess mortality risk using CRB-65.
Point-of-Care CRP: Consider if diagnosis is uncertain (no clinical pneumonia but unsure about antibiotics).
CRP <20 mg/L: Do not offer antibiotics.

Management Pathways

Key Principle: Do not routinely offer antibiotics. Usually self-limiting (3-4 weeks).

1. Self-Care Advice

  • Fluids, Paracetamol/Ibuprofen.
  • Honey or Pelargonium.
  • Explain antibiotics only shorten cough by ~0.5 days and have side effects.
  • Do not use mucolytics or bronchodilators (unless asthmatic).

2. When to Prescribe Antibiotics?

Immediate Prescription: Only if systemically very unwell.

Consider Immediate or Backup Prescription if high risk:

  • Heart, lung, kidney, liver, neuromuscular disease, or immunosuppression.
  • Age >65 with 2+ criteria OR Age >80 with 1+ criteria:
    • Hospital admission in last year
    • Diabetes (Type 1 or 2)
    • History of congestive heart failure
    • Current oral corticosteroids

3. Antibiotic Choice (If indicated)

Adults (18+):
  • 1st Line: Doxycycline 200mg stat then 100mg OD for 4 days (5 days total).
  • Alternative: Amoxicillin 500mg TDS (5 days).
  • Alternative: Clarithromycin 250-500mg BD (5 days).
Young People (12-17):
  • 1st Line: Amoxicillin 500mg TDS (5 days).
  • Doxycycline is contra-indicated in pregnancy/younger children (Note: generally avoid in pregnancy).

1. Site of Care (CRB-65 Decision)

  • Score 0: Treat at home (low risk <1% mortality).
  • Score 1-2: Consider hospital assessment (1-10% mortality).
  • Score ≥3: Urgent hospital admission (>10% mortality).

2. Antibiotic Choice (Managed in Community)

Always offer antibiotics for CAP. Duration: 5 days.

Adults (Low Severity / CRB-65 = 0)

  • 1st Line: Amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 5 days.
  • Penicillin Allergy / Atypical suspected:
    • Doxycycline 200mg stat then 100mg OD (5 days total).
    • OR Clarithromycin 500mg BD (5 days).
    • OR Erythromycin (in pregnancy) 500mg QDS (5 days).

Adults (Moderate Severity / CRB-65 = 1-2)

If managing in community:

  • Dual Therapy (Atypical cover): Amoxicillin 500mg TDS PLUS Clarithromycin 500mg BD (or Erythromycin).
  • Penicillin Allergy: Doxycycline OR Clarithromycin monotherapy.

3. Follow Up

  • Expect improvement: Fever resolves ~1 week, Chest pain/sputum ~4 weeks.
  • Review: If symptoms worsen or don't improve after 3 days.
  • Chest X-ray: Arrange at 6 weeks if symptoms persist or high risk of malignancy (smokers >50y).

Prescribing Information & Safety

Amoxicillin Penicillin
  • Contraindication: Penicillin hypersensitivity.
  • Caution: Renal impairment (reduce dose), Glandular fever (rash risk).
  • Interaction: Methotrexate (toxicity risk), Warfarin (monitor INR).
Doxycycline Tetracycline
  • Pregnancy/Breastfeeding: Not recommended (tooth discoloration).
  • Caution: Hepatic impairment, SLE, Myasthenia Gravis.
  • Adverse Effects: Photosensitivity, Oesophageal irritation.
  • Interaction: Absorption reduced by antacids/iron/zinc. Avoid with Retinoids (intracranial hypertension risk).
Clarithromycin / Erythromycin Macrolides
Statin Interaction: Simvastatin: Do not prescribe (or stop statin temporarily).
Atorvastatin: Avoid concurrent use or use lowest dose.
  • QT Interval: Contraindicated if history of QT prolongation or taking QT prolonging drugs.
  • Contraindications: Severe hepatic failure + renal impairment.
  • Warfarin: Monitor INR (effect increased).
