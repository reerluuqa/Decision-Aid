What is the Common Cold?

A mild, self-limiting upper respiratory tract infection characterized by nasal stuffiness and discharge, sneezing, sore throat, and cough. Caused by a wide range of viruses from several different families.

Rhinovirus

Most common cause (30-50% of cases)

Coronavirus

10-15% of cases (not including COVID-19)

Duration

7 days in adults, 10-14 days in children

Red Flags

Infants

  • Temperature ≥38°C in children <3 months
  • Temperature ≥39°C in children 3-6 months
  • Bulging fontanelle with high-pitched crying
  • Significant feeding difficulties

All Ages

  • Signs of upper airway obstruction (stridor, drooling)
  • Severe headache, stiff neck, photophobia
  • Persistent high fever (>5 days)
  • Shortness of breath or chest pain

Complications

Sinusitis

0.5-2.0% of cases

Lower Respiratory Tract Infections

Acute bronchitis, pneumonia

Acute Otitis Media

Follows ~20% of colds in children

Bronchiolitis

In very young children and babies

Diagnosis in Adults & Older Children

Most adults and older children reliably self-diagnose the common cold within a day of developing symptoms. The diagnosis is clinical based on history.

Common Symptoms

  • Sore or irritated throat - often the first symptom
  • Nasal irritation, congestion, nasal discharge, and sneezing
  • Cough develops in about 30% of people
  • Hoarse voice caused by associated laryngitis
  • General malaise

Less Common Symptoms

  • Fever - unusual in adults and typically low grade
  • Headache and myalgia - more often associated with influenza
  • Loss of taste and smell
  • Eye irritability
  • Feeling of pressure in the ears or sinuses

Diagnosis in Young Children & Infants

Ask about the child's symptoms and examine to exclude complications or alternative diagnoses.

Common Symptoms

  • Restlessness or irritability
  • Nasal congestion, nasal discharge, and sneezing
  • Cough - occasionally vomiting may follow a bout of coughing
  • Fever - common in preschool children (38-39°C)

Examination Points

  • Check temperature
  • Examine fontanelle for bulging
  • Check cervical lymph nodes
  • Examine ears for signs of acute otitis media
  • Examine throat for inflammation

Differential Diagnosis

Serious Meningitis

Infants: High fever, drowsiness, blank staring expression, vomiting, loss of appetite, high-pitched screaming, floppiness, bulging fontanelle, non-blanching rash.

Older children/adults: Fever, vomiting, stiff neck, photophobia, severe headache, muscular pains, fits, confusion.

Serious Upper Airway Obstruction

Characterized by stridor, drooling, or inability to swallow. May indicate peritonsillar or retropharyngeal abscesses, or epiglottitis.

Common Influenza

If symptoms of myalgia or fever are prominent, suspect influenza. Can range from asymptomatic to severe, life-threatening infection.

Other COVID-19

Symptoms can mimic common cold: headache, runny nose, sore throat. May also include fever, new continuous cough, shortness of breath, fatigue, loss of appetite, anosmia and ageusia.

Initial Management

  • Reassure that common cold is self-limiting and complications are rare
  • Explain symptom relief and rest are the most appropriate management
  • Arrange follow-up for those at risk of complications due to comorbidities
  • Immediate referral if signs of serious complications or alternative diagnosis

Treatment Options

Paracetamol

For fever and pain in all ages

Ibuprofen

For fever and pain (not for children under 6 months)

Fluids & Rest

Adequate hydration and rest recommended

Antibiotics

Ineffective for viral infections

Antihistamines

Ineffective and may cause adverse effects

Self-Care Measures

For All Ages

  • Steam inhalation to relieve congestion
  • Salt water gargle for sore throat
  • Nasal saline drops for congestion
  • Regular hand washing with soap and hot water
  • Avoid sharing towels

For Children & Infants

  • Vapour rubs on chest and back (avoid nostril area)
  • Nasal saline drops to help with feeding
  • Discourage sharing of toys with infected child
  • For children over 6 years: various OTC products available

When to Follow Up

High-Risk Groups

  • Immunocompromised patients
  • People with chronic respiratory conditions
  • People with diabetes
  • People with congestive heart failure
  • Smokers

Follow-up Timing

  • Arrange follow-up according to clinical judgment
  • Earlier review if symptoms worsen
  • Consider complications if symptoms persist beyond expected duration
  • Immediate review if red flags develop

QIPP - Options for Local Implementation

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)

  • Review appropriateness of NSAID prescribing, especially in high-risk groups
  • If needed, use ibuprofen (1,200 mg/day or less) or naproxen (1,000 mg/day or less)
  • Use lowest effective dose and shortest duration
  • Consider gastroprotective treatment when prescribing NSAIDs
