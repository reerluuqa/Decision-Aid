Initial Assessment
‚ñº
Immediate Evaluation Urgent
Check airway, breathing, circulation. Obtain vital signs immediately. Look for signs of trauma, active bleeding, or acute distress.
History Taking Caution
Detailed history of the event including prodrome, triggers, duration, and recovery. Ask about cardiac history, medications, and family history.
Physical Examination Standard
Complete cardiovascular and neurological exam. Check orthostatic vitals. Look for murmurs, neurological deficits, or signs of dehydration.

Diagnostic Workup
‚ñº
ECG Evaluation Critical
12-lead ECG within 10 minutes. Look for arrhythmias, conduction blocks, QT prolongation, or signs of ischemia. Continuous monitoring if abnormal.
Risk Stratification Important
Use validated risk scores (e.g., OESIL, SFSR). High-risk features include age >65, cardiac history, abnormal ECG, or syncope without prodrome.
Selective Testing As Needed
Consider echocardiogram, lab tests, or imaging based on clinical suspicion. Avoid routine comprehensive testing in low-risk patients.

Treatment & Management
‚ñº
High-Risk Patients Admit
Immediate admission for monitoring. Consider cardiology consultation. Treat underlying cause (e.g., pacemaker for bradyarrhythmias).
Low-Risk Patients Discharge
Safe for discharge with precautions. Provide syncope education, hydration advice, and clear return instructions.
Vasovagal Syncope Conservative
Lifestyle modifications: adequate hydration, salt intake, avoiding triggers. Consider tilt training or compression stockings if recurrent.

Follow-up Care
‚ñº
Discharge Instructions Important
Clear written instructions about warning signs, activity restrictions, medication compliance, and when to seek immediate care.
Primary Care Follow-up Routine
Schedule follow-up within 1-2 weeks. Review test results, reassess symptoms, and adjust treatment plan as needed.
Specialist Referral Selective
Consider cardiology, neurology, or electrophysiology referral based on underlying cause and risk stratification.
