Initial Assessment

1
Clinical Presentation
Assess for characteristic symptoms and risk factors
14 days+ cough Age < 6 months high risk
Key symptoms:
  • Paroxysmal coughing fits
  • Inspiratory 'whoop' (less common in infants/adults)
  • Post-tussive vomiting
  • Apnoeic attacks (infants)
  • Cough lasting ≥14 days without apparent cause
2
Infectious Period Assessment
Determine contagiousness and isolation requirements
Infectious until 21 days or 48h antibiotics
Exclusion periods:
  • Children: 48h antibiotics OR 14 days from cough onset
  • Healthcare workers: 48h antibiotics OR 21 days from cough onset
  • Avoid contact with infants <1 year and pregnant women
3
Risk Stratification
Identify patients requiring urgent intervention
Admit if apnoea, seizures, pneumonia Low threshold for infants <6 months
High-risk groups:
  • Infants <6 months (severe illness/death risk)
  • Pre-existing pulmonary disease
  • Older adults
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Case-fatality ratio ~1% in infants <2 months

Diagnostic Confirmation

4
Notification & Testing
Whooping cough is a notifiable disease
Notify UKHSA within 3 days Select appropriate test
Test selection by age/symptom duration:
  • Culture/PCR: All ages, <21 days symptoms
  • Serology: >16 years, >14 days symptoms, >1 year post-vaccination
  • Oral Fluid Test: 2-16 years, >14 days symptoms, >1 year post-vaccination
Confirmation requires: Clinical features + positive lab test
5
Differential Diagnosis
Consider alternative causes of persistent cough
Rule out other causes
Other infectious causes:
  • Bordetella parapertussis
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae
  • Chlamydia pneumoniae
  • Adenoviruses, RSV (children)
Non-infectious causes:
  • Asthma, COPD
  • Post-infectious cough
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux
  • Lung malignancy (adults)

Treatment & Management

6
Antibiotic Treatment
Initiate macrolide therapy within 14-21 days of cough onset
Best within 14 days Can extend to 21 days for contacts
First-line macrolides:
  • Infants <1 month: Clarithromycin preferred (7 days)
  • Children ≥1 month: Azithromycin (3 days) OR Clarithromycin (7 days)
  • Non-pregnant adults: Azithromycin (3 days) OR Clarithromycin (7 days)
  • Pregnant women: Erythromycin (7 days)
Alternatives: Co-trimoxazole (7 days) if macrolides contraindicated
7
Symptomatic Care
Supportive management during recovery
Most cases managed at home Cough may persist for weeks
Supportive care:
  • Rest and adequate fluid intake
  • Paracetamol/ibuprofen for symptomatic relief
  • Monitor for complications (see below)
  • Advice on transmission prevention
  • Ensure vaccinations up to date after recovery
8
Complications
Monitor and manage potential complications
Severe complications more common in infants
Serious complications:
  • Apnoea, pneumonia (secondary bacterial)
  • Seizures, cerebral hypoxia, brain damage
  • Encephalopathy (rare in adults)
  • Pneumothorax, rib fractures
  • Death (1% case-fatality in infants <2 months)
Minor complications:
  • Otitis media, subconjunctival haemorrhage
  • Epistaxis, facial oedema
  • Umbilical/inguinal hernias, rectal prolapse
  • Urinary incontinence, weight loss

Follow-up & Prevention

9
Contact Management
Identify and protect high-risk contacts
Protect vulnerable contacts Prophylaxis for priority groups
Priority groups for prophylaxis:
Group 1 (Vulnerable):
  • Unimmunised infants <2 months (varies by gestation/maternal vaccine)
  • Infants 2 months to <1 year with incomplete vaccination
Group 2 (Transmitters to vulnerable):
  • Pregnant women ≥32 weeks gestation
  • Healthcare workers with infant/pregnant patient contact
  • Household contacts of infants in Group 1
Prophylaxis: Same antibiotics as treatment, exclude asymptomatic contacts
10
Vaccination
Ensure optimal vaccination coverage
Maternal vaccine 16-32 weeks Childhood schedule completion
Vaccination schedule:
  • Pregnant women: 16-32 weeks (ideally), can be given up to 38 weeks
  • Infants: 6-in-1 vaccine at 8, 12, 16 weeks
  • Pre-school: 4-in-1 booster at 3 years 4 months
  • Contacts: Complete schedule if unimmunised/partly immunised (<10 years)
  • Healthcare workers: Booster if no dose in previous 5 years
11
Public Health Measures
Implement outbreak control measures
Notify all cases to UKHSA Institutional outbreak management
Public health actions:
  • Complete notification form within 3 days
  • UKHSA provides test kits and prophylaxis advice
  • Institutional outbreak management (schools, nurseries, hospitals)
  • Enhanced surveillance during outbreaks
  • Health education on vaccination and hygiene
