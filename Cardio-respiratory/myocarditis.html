Myocarditis

Clinical Reference

Last Updated: Nov 04, 2025 | BMJ Best Practice Based

Adverse Prognosis & Emergency Indicators

Watch for these critical risk factors requiring immediate escalation:

Cardiogenic Shock Ventricular Arrhythmias Biventricular Dysfunction Late Gadolinium Enhancement (MRI) Giant Cell Myocarditis
Definition
Core

Inflammation of the myocardium without predominant acute/chronic ischaemia.

  • Non-ischaemic myocardial inflammation
  • Heterogeneous group of disorders
  • Inflammatory cellular infiltrate ± myocyte injury
Classification
TypePresentation
Fulminant Acute viral illness, severe compromise, rapid deterioration. High risk, but good recovery if supported.
Acute Insidious onset, established ventricular dysfunction. May progress to DCM.
Chronic Active Relapsing, LV dysfunction, inflammation.
Epidemiology
  • True incidence: Difficult to determine (insidious).
  • Autopsy studies: 1%–9% of routine exams.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death: ~10% in young adults; up to 6% in young athletes.
  • Children: Annual incidence ~2/100,000. Males > Females (58% vs 42%).
Case History #1 (Adult)
Example

43M, No PMH. 3 days progressive fatigue, dyspnoea on exertion & supine, lower-extremity swelling.

Context: Viral prodrome (fevers, myalgias, respiratory symptoms) 2 weeks prior.

Exam: JVD elevation, bilateral rales, HR 104, audible S3 gallop.
Case History #2 (Child)
Example

4M, Previously healthy. 4-day history: abd pain, vomiting, low-grade fever. Now lethargic.

Key: Children often mimic common illness (abd pain, vomiting). Respiratory symptoms are late.

Exam: Tachycardia (130), laboured breathing, poor tone, poor capillary refill, S3 gallop.

Diagnostic Algorithm

1st Line Tests (Order All)
Routine
TestExpected Findings in Myocarditis
12-lead ECG Non-specific ST/T-wave changes. ST elevation/depression frequent. Arrhythmias (esp. in children).
Chest X-Ray Pulmonary congestion/oedema. Cardiomegaly (suggests pericardial effusion or chronic DCM).
Troponin (I/T) Elevated (More reliable than CK/CK-MB). Note: May be normal in some cases.
BNP / NT-proBNP Elevated due to ventricular distension/CHF.
2D Echocardiogram Global/regional wall motion abnormalities. Dilatation. Systolic/Diastolic dysfunction. Look for clots.
Advanced Imaging
Specific

Cardiac MRI

  • Indication: Distinguish from acute MI, tissue characterization.
  • Pediatrics: Preferred non-invasive modality.
  • Criteria: Lake Louise Criteria (2018) - presence of both T2 and T1 findings indicates inflammation.

FDG PET-CT

  • Useful in chronic myocarditis (where MRI less accurate).
  • Helps differentiate Cardiac Sarcoidosis vs Giant Cell Myocarditis.
Endomyocardial Biopsy (EMB)
High Risk / Specific

ACC Recommendations (Adults):

  • DO: Immune checkpoint inhibitor myocarditis (any stage).
  • DO: Symptomatic (Stage C) + LV dysfunction, arrhythmias, eosinophilia, or if MRI unavailable.
  • DO: Advanced (Stage D) myocarditis.
  • DO NOT: Low-risk Stage C (Normal EF, stable, no LGE on MRI).

When it helps:

  • Giant Cell Myocarditis (needs aggressive immunosuppression).
  • Hypersensitivity Myocarditis.
  • Viral PCR detection.

Causes & Triggers

Infectious (Most Common)
Viral
Coxsackie B Adenovirus Parvovirus B19 HHV-6 EBV CMV Influenza A/B SARS-CoV-2 Hepatitis C HIV-1
Bacterial
Streptococcus Mycoplasma Corynebacterium Staphylococcus Borrelia (Lyme) TB
Parasitic/Fungal
Trypanosoma cruzi (Chagas) Toxoplasmosis Schistosomiasis Candida
Non-Infectious
Drugs & Toxins
  • Anthracyclines: Doxorubicin, Daunorubicin.
  • Immunotherapies: Checkpoint inhibitors (Nivolumab, Ipilimumab, Pembrolizumab).
  • COVID Vaccines: mRNA vaccines (rare).
  • Others: Cocaine, Amphetamines, Ethanol, Carbon Monoxide, Penicillins, Clozapine.
Systemic Disorders
  • Autoimmune: SLE, Rheumatoid Arthritis, IBD.
  • Giant Cell Myocarditis (Idiopathic).
  • Sarcoidosis (Granulomatous).
  • Thyrotoxicosis, Wegener's Granulomatosis.
Pathophysiology (Three-Phase Model)
Phase 1: Virus invades myocytes (viraemia).
Phase 2: Immunological activation (Macrophages, NK cells).
Phase 3: Cellular/Humoral activation → Myonecrosis & Auto-antibodies.

Management Strategy

Standard Care
Most Patients
  • Supportive Care: Rest, activity restriction.
  • Heart Failure Therapy: Conventional HF guidelines (Beta-blockers, ACEi/ARBs, Diuretics).
  • Arrhythmia Management: Treat symptomatic arrhythmias.
  • Anticoagulation: Consider if LV dysfunction or clots detected.
Aggressive/Severe
Fulminant or Shock
  • Inotropes: Hemodynamic support.
  • Mechanical Circulatory Support (MCS): IABP, VA-ECMO, VAD.
  • Transplantation: If refractory to therapy.
  • Immunosuppression:
    - Giant Cell: High dose steroids + Cyclosporine/Azathioprine.
    - Hypersensitivity: Stop drug + Corticosteroids.
Follow-up & Monitoring

Activity Restriction

Recommend restriction from competitive sports for 3-6 months (or until normalization of LV function, ambulatory ECG, and biomarkers).

Prognosis

Variable. Most viral cases resolve spontaneously. Mild-moderate HF usually improves. Fulminant has high risk but good recovery if supported.

Key Risk Markers

Electrical instability, low EF, Late Gadolinium Enhancement, Giant Cell histology.
