Accelerated/Malignant Hypertension - EMERGENCY

BP ≥ 180/120 mmHg with signs of retinal haemorrhage and/or papilloedema. Requires immediate hospital admission.

Hypertension Classification

Stage Clinic BP ABPM/HBPM
Stage 1 140/90 - 159/99 135/85 - 149/94
Stage 2 160/100 - 179/119 150/95 - 179/119
Stage 3 ≥180/120 ≥180/120
Source: NICE NG136, 2019

Diagnosis

If clinic BP ≥ 140/90 mmHg, offer ABPM to confirm diagnosis
ABPM: Use at least 14 measurements during daytime hours
Hypertension confirmed if average ABPM/HBPM ≥ 135/85 mmHg
If clinic BP ≥ 180/120 mmHg with signs of end-organ damage - refer for same-day specialist assessment
Source: NICE NG136, 2019

Treatment Targets

Age < 80 years

<140/90

Age ≥ 80 years

<150/90
For people with treated hypertension, aim for clinic BP below target
For people with diabetes: <140/80 mmHg if under 80 years
Source: NICE NG136, 2019

Red Flags

Accelerated/malignant hypertension (≥180/120 mmHg with end-organ damage)
Severe hypertension in pregnancy
Hypertension with symptoms of target organ damage (chest pain, visual changes, neurological symptoms)
Resistant hypertension (uncontrolled with 4+ antihypertensives)
Source: NICE NG136, 2019

Treatment Steps

Step Treatment Notes
Step 1 ACE inhibitor or ARB for adults < 55 years
CCB for adults ≥ 55 years or Black African/African-Caribbean		 Consider CCB for younger adults if ACEI/ARB not tolerated
Step 2 Combine ACEI/ARB + CCB Or ACEI/ARB + thiazide-like diuretic if CCB not tolerated
Step 3 ACEI/ARB + CCB + thiazide-like diuretic Consider low-dose spironolactone (25mg) if potassium ≤ 4.5 mmol/L
Step 4 Consider higher-dose thiazide-like diuretic
Consider adding beta-blocker or alpha-blocker		 Seek specialist advice if still uncontrolled
Resistant 4+ antihypertensives including optimal doses Refer for specialist assessment
Source: NICE NG136, 2019

Secondary Causes

Renal: Chronic kidney disease, renal artery stenosis, polycystic kidney disease
Endocrine: Primary hyperaldosteronism, phaeochromocytoma, Cushing's, thyroid disorders
Drug-induced: COCP, steroids, NSAIDs, alcohol, sympathomimetics
Other: Obstructive sleep apnoea, coarctation of the aorta
Source: NICE CKS Hypertension, 2025

Investigations

Urine dipstick for proteinuria, haematuria
Blood tests: electrolytes, creatinine, eGFR, fasting glucose, lipid profile
ECG for left ventricular hypertrophy
Consider specialist investigations if secondary cause suspected
Source: NICE NG136, 2019

Quick Clinical Tips

  • White-coat effect: Discrepancy >20/10 mmHg between clinic and home/ambulatory readings
  • Masked hypertension: Normal clinic readings but elevated home/ambulatory readings
  • Measure BP in both arms at initial assessment; use higher reading for subsequent measurements
  • For people with diabetes, CKD, or CVD, consider treatment at lower BP thresholds
  • Review medication annually and adjust as needed
