Assessment

Acute Limb Ischaemia
Emergency Presentation
‚ñº

The Six Ps: Not all need to be present for diagnosis

  • Pain - Constantly present and persistent
  • Pallor - Or cyanosis/mottling of limb
  • Pulselessness - Ankle pulses always absent
  • Poikilothermia - Perishing cold limb
  • Paraesthesia - Reduced sensation/numbness
  • Paralysis - Loss of motor power
Embolus vs Thrombosis: Embolus = acute onset, distinct demarcation; Thrombosis = gradual onset, bluish limb
Intermittent Claudication
Most common presentation
‚ñº
  • Fatigue, discomfort, cramping or pain in lower extremity muscles after walking a predictable distance
  • Relieved by rest within 10 minutes
  • Reproducible by walking the same distance again
  • Not present at rest or exacerbated by position
  • Calves most commonly affected (distal before proximal)
  • Symptoms may be unilateral or bilateral
  • Atypical terms: "tired", "giving way", "sore", "hurts"
Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischaemia
End-stage disease
‚ñº
  • Chronic rest pain (worse at night)
  • Patients may sleep with leg hanging out of bed or in chair
  • Dependent rubor, pallor on elevation
  • Skin changes: dry, shiny, hair loss, thickened toenails
  • Non-healing foot wounds, ischaemic ulcers
  • Tissue loss over pressure areas, gangrene of toes
  • Absent foot pulses (may be palpable in distal embolisation)
Urgent Referral
Risk Factors
Cardiovascular risk assessment
‚ñº

Strong Risk Factors:

  • Smoking - Most powerful predictor (4-fold risk)
  • Diabetes mellitus - 2-4 fold increased risk
  • Hypertension - OR 1.5-2.2
  • Hypercholesterolaemia - Elevated LDL, triglycerides

Other Factors:

  • Older age, known atherosclerotic disease elsewhere
  • Chronic kidney disease, elevated homocysteine
  • Multiple risk factors: 1 factor = 1.5x risk; 3+ factors = 10x risk
Physical Examination
Systematic assessment
‚ñº
  • Examine both legs and feet for chronic limb-threatening ischaemia signs
  • Palpate: abdominal aorta, femoral, popliteal, foot pulses
  • Auscultate for femoral, aortic, or carotid bruits
  • Assess for muscle atrophy, skin changes, ulceration
  • Check for dependent rubor and elevation pallor
ABPI Measurement
Essential diagnostic tool
‚ñº

Method:

  • Person resting and supine (if possible)
  • Measure systolic BP in both arms with appropriately sized cuff
  • Use Doppler probe to measure posterior tibial, dorsalis pedis, and peroneal arteries
  • Calculate: Highest ankle pressure √∑ Highest arm pressure
ABPI Ratio Interpretation Action
< 0.5 Chronic limb-threatening ischaemia Urgent specialist referral
0.5 - 0.9 Peripheral arterial disease Confirm diagnosis
0.91 - 0.99 PAD may be present Further investigation
1.0 - 1.4 Normal Consider other diagnoses
> 1.4 Arterial calcification (incompressible) Unreliable - seek advice
Note: If ankle pulse undetectable on Doppler, consider acute limb ischaemia and seek immediate vascular advice

Diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis
Conditions that may mimic PAD
‚ñº

Acute Limb Ischaemia Mimics:

  • Acute compressive peripheral neuropathy (compartment syndrome)
  • Acute deep vein thrombosis (pulses usually palpable)
  • Chronic peripheral neuropathy (diabetic - pulses present)
  • Low cardiac output with chronic PAD

Intermittent Claudication Mimics:

  • Foot/ankle arthritis - not quickly relieved
  • Hip arthritis - lateral hip/thigh pain
  • Nerve root compression - sharp radiating pain
  • Spinal stenosis - bilateral, worse on standing
  • Chronic compartment syndrome, venous claudication

Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischaemia Mimics:

  • Arthritis, cellulitis, gout
  • Plantar fasciitis
Diagnostic Confirmation
When to confirm diagnosis
‚ñº

Diagnose PAD when:

  • Clinical features consistent with PAD AND
  • ABPI ‚â§ 0.9, OR
  • ABPI 0.91-0.99 with high clinical suspicion

Do not exclude PAD if:

  • ABPI is normal or raised but clinical suspicion remains
  • Patient has diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, or renal failure (calcified vessels)
  • In these cases, seek vascular surgical advice

Treatment

Acute Limb Ischaemia Emergency
Immediate vascular referral
‚ñº
‚ö° Immediate Actions
  • Arrange emergency assessment by vascular specialist
  • Time-critical: 6% amputation rate if reperfused within 12 hours
  • 12% amputation rate at 13-24 hours, 20% after 24 hours

Secondary Care Management:

  • Endovascular therapies: Catheter-directed thrombolysis, mechanical thrombus extraction
  • Surgical interventions: Thromboembolectomy, endarterectomy, bypass surgery
  • Amputation: If limb is unsalvageable
Mortality 15-20%. One third of deaths from metabolic complications (acidosis, hyperkalaemia)

Refer urgently to vascular multidisciplinary team (unless palliative care)

Pain Management:

  • Paracetamol ¬± weak or strong opioids (short-term)
  • Add laxatives/anti-emetics for opioid side effects
  • Consider neuropathic pain agents if needed
  • Refer to pain management service if:
    • Pain uncontrolled and revascularisation inappropriate

  • Discuss modifiable risk factors
  • Foot care advice: daily inspection, avoid barefoot, well-fitting shoes
  • DVLA guidance on driving
Intermittent Claudication Stable
Conservative management first
‚ñº

1. Supervised Exercise Programme

  • 2 hours supervised exercise weekly for 3 months
  • Exercise to point of maximal pain
  • If unavailable: unsupervised 30 min, 3-5x/week
  • Walk until symptoms onset, rest, repeat

2. Pharmacological Therapy

Naftidrofuryl Oxalate

When to use: After supervised exercise has failed, and patient prefers not to have angioplasty/bypass

Dose: 100-200 mg three times daily

Review: After 3-6 months, discontinue if no benefit

Cautions: History of hyperoxaluria or calcium kidney stones

Adverse effects: GI upset, oesophagitis, liver damage (rare), kidney stones (very rare)

3. Revascularisation

  • Consider referral if:
    • Risk factor modification advice reinforced
    • Supervised exercise hasn't improved symptoms
  • Options: Angioplasty or bypass surgery
Cardiovascular Risk Management
Secondary prevention for all PAD patients
‚ñº

Mandatory for ALL PAD patients:

  • Smoking cessation - Most critical intervention
  • Lipid modification - Statins for all (see QOF indicators)
  • Diabetes control - Target HbA1c (each 1% ‚Üë = 28% risk ‚Üë)
  • Hypertension management - Optimize BP control
  • Antiplatelet therapy - Unless contraindicated
  • Diet and weight management

Follow-up

Long-term Monitoring
Disease progression and complications
‚ñº

Intermittent Claudication Prognosis (5 years):

  • Most remain stable (10-20% worsen)
  • 5-10% develop chronic limb-threatening ischaemia
  • 1-2% require amputation (5% if diabetes)
  • 10-15% die from cardiovascular causes
  • 20% have non-fatal cardiovascular event

Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischaemia:

  • 50-90% undergo revascularisation
  • ~33% require amputation within 1 year without revascularisation
  • 5-year mortality 50% (worse than many cancers)
  • 3-fold excess risk of MI, stroke, vascular death vs claudication

Monitoring Schedule:

  • Regular clinical review (3-6 monthly)
  • Repeat ABPI if symptoms change
  • Annual cardiovascular risk assessment
  • Foot inspection at every visit
  • Monitor for depression/anxiety
Patient Education & Lifestyle
Self-management strategies
‚ñº

Foot Care (Crucial):

  • Daily foot inspection
  • Keep feet clean and moisturised (avoid between toes)
  • Careful nail cutting or podiatry referral
  • Never walk barefoot
  • Wear well-fitting shoes, avoid tight socks
  • Treat cuts/blisters immediately
  • Avoid extreme temperatures

Exercise & Activity:

  • Continue exercise programme long-term
  • Gradual progression to maintain fitness
  • Avoid isometric exercises if severe disease
  • Weight-bearing activities as tolerated

Driving:

  • Must inform DVLA if limb weakness affects vehicle control
  • May need adapted vehicle controls
  • Annual license review may be required
Red Flags - Seek Urgent Help
When to call emergency services
‚ñº
  • Sudden severe leg pain with pale/blue limb
  • Loss of sensation or inability to move foot/toes
  • Cold limb with absent pulses
  • Rapidly worsening rest pain
  • New ulcers or black areas on foot/toes
  • Signs of infection in ischaemic foot
  • Chest pain or stroke symptoms (associated CV disease)
Acute limb ischaemia is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY - Call 999 immediately
Prognosis Overview
Long-term outcomes
‚ñº

Key Mortality Data:

  • PAD patients have 3x higher CV mortality than general population
  • ABPI <0.9 or >1.4 indicates increased major CV event risk
  • CLTI: 50% 5-year all-cause mortality
  • Acute limb ischaemia: 15-20% mortality
  • CLTI 3x higher risk of MI, stroke, vascular death vs claudication

Progression Risk Factors:

  • Increasing age, male sex
  • Low (<0.5) or high (>1.4) ABPI
  • Continued smoking
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Concurrent cardiovascular disease
Remember: PAD is a marker for systemic atherosclerosis. Aggressive CV risk management is essential
