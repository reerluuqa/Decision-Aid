Clinical Reference Tool

ACUTE LIMB ISCHAEMIA - MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Sudden decrease in limb perfusion threatening limb viability. Seek immediate vascular specialist advice.

Six Ps of Acute Limb Ischaemia

  • Pain - constantly present and persistent
  • Pallor - or cyanosis or mottling
  • Pulselessness - ankle pulses always absent
  • Poikilothermia - perishing with cold
  • Paraesthesia - reduced sensation or numbness
  • Paralysis - power loss

Typical Presentation

Fatigue, discomfort, cramping or pain in lower extremities after walking a predictable distance, relieved by rest (within 10 minutes) and reproduced by walking the same distance again.

Location

Usually occurs in distal extremity before proximal - calves more commonly affected than thigh, buttocks or hips.

Prognosis (5 years)

Most people have stable claudication; 10-20% develop worsening symptoms; 5-10% develop chronic limb-threatening ischaemia; 10-15% die of cardiovascular causes.

Chronic Rest Pain

Worse at night due to decreased blood pressure when sleeping. People may sleep with leg hanging out of bed or in a chair.

Physical Signs

Dependent rubor, pallor on elevation, dry/shiny skin, hair loss over dorsum of foot, muscle atrophy, thickened toenails.

Tissue Loss

Non-healing foot wounds, ischaemic ulcers (usually over pressure areas), gangrene (usually of toes).

Prognosis

High risk of amputation and premature death. 5-year all-cause mortality of 50%. 50-90% will need revascularization.

Management Goals

Make a diagnosis of peripheral arterial disease
Offer appropriate initial and subsequent management
Manage any co-existing cardiovascular risk factors
Refer people with PAD, when appropriate, to other healthcare professionals

Urgent Referral Required

ABPI < 0.5 suggests chronic limb-threatening ischaemia - refer urgently for specialist vascular assessment
