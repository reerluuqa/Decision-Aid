Long QT Syndrome (LQTS)

Clinical Reference: Diagnosis, Risk Stratification & Management

Overview Subtypes (1-3) Diagnosis & Score Acquired Causes Management
Definition Core

Characterised by a prolonged QT interval on ECG (congenital or acquired). Associated with high risk of sudden cardiac death (SCD) due to ventricular tachyarrhythmias.

Benchmarks:
QTc > 450ms (Males)
QTc > 460ms (Females)
Epidemiology Stats
  • Prevalence: ~1 in 2,500
  • Mean age at presentation: 14 years
  • Female predominance in adulthood
  • Untreated SCD rate: <5% (asymptomatic) to ~50% (symptomatic)
  • Most common: Romano-Ward (AD), rare: Jervell & Lange-Nielsen (AR + Deafness)

Classification

LQT1 (30-35%)

KCNQ1 mutation. Loss of K+ current (IKs). Exercise trigger (swimming).

LQT2 (25-30%)

KCNH2 mutation. Loss of K+ current (IKr). Startle/Auditory trigger.

LQT3 (5-10%)

SCN5A mutation. Gain of Na+ current. Sleep/Rest trigger.

The "Big 3": Clinical Comparison

Type 1 (LQT1) IKs

ECG Pattern: Broad-based, prolonged T wave.

Trigger: Physical exercise (especially Swimming), high adrenergic tone.

Gene: KCNQ1 (Loss of function).

Type 2 (LQT2) IKr

ECG Pattern: Low amplitude, notched T wave in multiple leads.

Trigger: Sudden arousal, Startle (alarm clock, phone), acute emotional stress, postpartum.

Gene: KCNH2 (Loss of function).

Type 3 (LQT3) INa

ECG Pattern: Long isoelectric ST segment, late-appearing T wave.

Trigger: Rest, Sleep, Bradycardia. High risk of events during sleep.

Gene: SCN5A (Gain of function).

Rare Syndromes
Jervell and Lange-Nielsen (JLN):
Autosomal Recessive. Severe sensorineural deafness + LQTS. Very high cardiac risk.
Andersen-Tawil (LQT7):
Periodic paralysis, ventricular arrhythmias, dysmorphic features (low-set ears, scoliosis).

Diagnostic Approach
  1. Resting ECG: Measure QTc. Use lead II or V5. Beware computer overestimation.
  2. Correct Electrolytes: Correct K+, Mg2+, Ca2+ before diagnosing acquired LQTS.
  3. History: Check triggers (Exercise? Startle? Sleep?), seizures misdiagnosis.
  4. Exercise Test: Useful for LQT1 (QTc fails to shorten appropriately).
  5. Genetic Testing: Recommended if Schwartz score ‚â• 3.5. Enables family screening.
Red Flag ECG:
QTc > 500ms is a strong predictor of cardiac events.

Acquired LQTS: "The Big 3"

Drug-Induced
Antiarrhythmics (Sotalol, Amiodarone, Quinidine) Antibiotics (Macrolides, Fluoroquinolones) Antipsychotics (Haloperidol, Ziprasidone) Methadone Tricyclic Antidepressants

Electrolytes
  • Hypokalaemia (Most common)
  • Hypomagnesaemia
  • Hypocalcaemia
Pathology
  • Bradyarrhythmias (AV block, sinus bradycardia)
  • CNS Lesions (Subarachnoid hemorrhage, Stroke)
  • Malnutrition/Starvation
  • Hypothyroidism
Acute Management Urgent
  • Stabilize: IV access, cardiac monitoring. Correct K+ > 4.0 mmol/L and Mg2+.
  • Torsades de Pointes: IV Magnesium Sulfate 2g over 1-2 mins. Temporary pacing if pause-dependent.
  • Cardiac Arrest: ACLS protocol. Avoid Class IA/III antiarrhythmics (they prolong QT).
Chronic Management Standard
  • Lifestyle Modification: Avoid QT-prolonging drugs. Treat electrolyte issues immediately. Avoid intense exercise in LQT1 (swimming).
  • Beta-Blockers: First-line therapy (especially Nadolol or Propranolol). Effective in LQT1 and LQT2.
  • ICD (Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator): For survivors of cardiac arrest, or patients with breakthrough events on beta-blockers.
