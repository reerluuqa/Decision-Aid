COPD Clinical Decision Support

Based on NICE Guidelines (Last revised October 2025)

What to Ask

  • Onset, variability, and progression of symptoms (breathlessness, cough, sputum)
  • Impact on daily life and occupation
  • Previous exacerbations or hospitalizations
  • Exposure to risk factors (smoking history in pack-years, occupational/environmental)
  • Past medical history and comorbidities (anxiety, depression, cardiovascular disease)
  • Family history (consider alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency if <40 years)

Key Points

Suspect COPD in people aged over 35 years with a risk factor and one or more of: breathlessness, chronic cough, regular sputum production, frequent lower respiratory infections, or wheeze.

What to Examine

  • Vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, respiratory rate)
  • General appearance (cachexia, cyanosis)
  • Chest examination (hyperinflation, use of accessory muscles, wheeze, crackles)
  • Cardiovascular examination (raised JVP, peripheral oedema - consider cor pulmonale)
  • Calculate BMI (low BMI associated with increased mortality)

Investigations

  • Post-bronchodilator spirometry (FEV1/FVC < 0.7 confirms airflow obstruction)
  • Consider chest X-ray to exclude alternative diagnoses
  • Full blood count (to identify polycythemia or anemia)
  • Consider alpha-1-antitrypsin level if <40 years or family history

Red Flags

  • Chest pain - consider other causes
  • Haemoptysis - consider lung cancer
  • Unexplained weight loss - consider malignancy
  • Cor pulmonale signs (peripheral oedema, raised JVP)

What to Prescribe

Initial Pharmacological Treatment

For symptomatic patients with COPD:

  • Short-acting bronchodilator (SABA or SAMA) as needed for breathlessness
  • Consider LAMA or LABA for patients with persistent symptoms or exacerbations
  • Consider combination LAMA/LABA for patients with persistent symptoms despite monotherapy

When to Review

  • Review 4-6 weeks after starting new treatment
  • Annual review for all patients (including spirometry)
  • Review inhaler technique at initiation and at least annually

What to Ask

  • Current symptom burden (use CAT or mMRC scale)
  • Frequency and severity of exacerbations in past year
  • Current inhaler use and technique
  • Smoking status (if current smoker, offer cessation support)
  • Physical activity levels
  • Vaccination status (influenza, pneumococcal)
  • Mental health screening (anxiety, depression)

Key Points

Management should be personalized based on symptoms, exacerbation risk, and comorbidities. Pulmonary rehabilitation should be offered to all appropriate patients.

What to Examine

  • Assess breathlessness using Medical Research Council (MRC) dyspnoea scale
  • Check for signs of cor pulmonale (peripheral oedema, raised JVP)
  • Calculate BMI (monitor for weight loss)
  • Assess oxygen saturation (consider ABGs if ≤92%)
  • Review inhaler technique

What to Prescribe

Pharmacological Treatment by Severity

For patients with persistent symptoms:

  • Group A (Low symptoms, low risk): SABA/SAMA PRN
  • Group B (High symptoms, low risk): LAMA or LABA
  • Group C (Low symptoms, high risk): LAMA or LABA + ICS if blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL
  • Group D (High symptoms, high risk): LAMA/LABA ± ICS if blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL
Medication Efficacy Common Side Effects Cost
SABA (e.g., Salbutamol) Rapid symptom relief Tremor, tachycardia Low
LAMA (e.g., Tiotropium) Improves lung function, reduces exacerbations Dry mouth Medium
LABA (e.g., Formoterol) Improves lung function, symptoms Tremor, tachycardia Medium
ICS (e.g., Beclomethasone) Reduces inflammation, exacerbations Oral thrush, dysphonia, pneumonia risk Medium-High

When to Review

  • Review 4-6 weeks after starting new treatment
  • Annual review for all patients
  • Review more frequently if frequent exacerbations or deteriorating symptoms
  • Review inhaler technique at least annually

Non-Pharmacological Management

  • Refer to pulmonary rehabilitation (especially if MRC ≥3)
  • Offer smoking cessation support
  • Provide self-management plan
  • Consider nutritional advice
  • Advise on exercise and physical activity
  • Ensure vaccinations are up to date

What to Ask

  • Onset and duration of increased symptoms
  • Change in dyspnoea, sputum volume, and sputum purulence
  • Previous exacerbation history and hospitalizations
  • Current medications and adherence
  • Comorbidities (heart failure, diabetes, etc.)
  • Home oxygen use

Key Points

An acute exacerbation is defined as an acute worsening of respiratory symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variations that requires additional therapy.

What to Examine

  • Vital signs (respiratory rate, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, oxygen saturation)
  • Use of accessory muscles, ability to speak in sentences
  • Chest examination (wheeze, crackles, signs of consolidation)
  • Signs of right heart failure (raised JVP, peripheral oedema)
  • Mental status (confusion, drowsiness)

Red Flags - Hospital Admission Required

  • Severe breathlessness at rest
  • Confusion or drowsiness
  • Cyanosis
  • Peripheral oedema
  • Oxygen saturation < 92% on room air
  • Unable to care for self at home
  • Rapid deterioration despite treatment
  • Acidotic hypercapnic respiratory failure (PaCO2 > 6.0 kPa, pH < 7.35)

What to Prescribe

Pharmacological Treatment

For patients managed in the community:

  • Short-acting bronchodilators: Increase frequency/dose of SABA (e.g., salbutamol 2-4 puffs every 2-4 hours PRN)
  • Oral corticosteroids: Prednisolone 30mg daily for 5 days (no taper required)
  • Antibiotics: Consider if increased sputum purulence
Antibiotic Option Indications Dosage Duration
Amoxicillin First-line if no recent antibiotic use 500mg TDS 5 days
Doxycycline First-line if recent antibiotic use or penicillin allergy 200mg on day 1, then 100mg daily 5 days
Clarithromycin Second-line or if penicillin allergy 500mg BD 5 days
Co-amoxiclav Second-line if treatment failure 500/125mg TDS 5 days

Oxygen Therapy

  • Target oxygen saturation 88-92% for most patients
  • Use Venturi mask to deliver controlled oxygen if required
  • Monitor ABGs if oxygen therapy required or if known hypercapnia

When to Review

  • Review within 48 hours if not admitted to hospital
  • Consider earlier review if high-risk patient
  • Review inhaler technique after exacerbation
  • Review maintenance therapy 2-4 weeks after exacerbation

What to Ask

  • Adherence to prescribed medications
  • Inhaler technique (ask patient to demonstrate)
  • Continued smoking status and exposure to other risk factors
  • Presence of comorbidities (heart failure, anxiety, depression)
  • Symptoms of alternative diagnoses (GERD, obstructive sleep apnoea)
  • Frequency of rescue inhaler use

Key Points

Before escalating treatment, ensure correct diagnosis, assess adherence and inhaler technique, and exclude alternative diagnoses or comorbidities.

What to Examine

  • Repeat spirometry (if not done within 12 months)
  • Chest X-ray (to exclude alternative diagnoses)
  • Full blood count (to check for polycythemia or anemia)
  • Consider blood eosinophil count (to guide ICS use)
  • Consider echocardiogram if signs of cor pulmonale
  • Consider sleep study if symptoms suggest OSA

Treatment Optimization

Pharmacological Options

  • Review inhaler technique and device choice - Consider spacer or nebulizer if needed
  • Step-up therapy: Add LAMA if on LABA alone or vice versa
  • Consider triple therapy: LAMA + LABA + ICS for patients with frequent exacerbations
  • Consider roflumilast: For severe COPD with chronic bronchitis and frequent exacerbations
  • Consider prophylactic antibiotics: For patients with frequent exacerbations despite optimal therapy
Additional Treatment Indications Benefits Side Effects
Roflumilast Severe COPD with chronic bronchitis and frequent exacerbations Reduces exacerbations Diarrhoea, weight loss, nausea, headache
Prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., Azithromycin) Frequent exacerbations despite optimal inhaled therapy Reduces exacerbation frequency Diarrhoea, hearing loss, QT prolongation, antimicrobial resistance
Mucolytics (e.g., Carbocisteine) Chronic cough with sputum production May reduce exacerbations GI upset

Non-Pharmacological Options

  • Consider long-term oxygen therapy if persistent hypoxia
  • Consider non-invasive ventilation if hypercapnic respiratory failure
  • Treat comorbidities (e.g., heart failure, anxiety, depression)

  • When to Review

    • Review 4-6 weeks after treatment optimization
    • Monitor for side effects of new medications
    • Reassess symptom control and exacerbation frequency

When to Refer to Specialist

  • Diagnostic uncertainty (e.g., atypical presentation, young age <40)
  • Rapid decline in FEV1 or progressive symptoms despite optimal therapy
  • Frequent severe exacerbations requiring hospital admission
  • Complications such as cor pulmonale or respiratory failure
  • Consideration for lung volume reduction surgery or lung transplantation
  • Assessment for long-term oxygen therapy
  • Assessment for non-invasive ventilation
  • Occupational lung disease suspected
  • Alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency suspected

Key Points

Consider referral to a respiratory specialist when diagnosis is uncertain, symptoms are not controlled with optimal therapy, or complications develop.

Urgent Referral Required

  • Suspected lung cancer (unexplained weight loss, haemoptysis, chest pain)
  • Severe or rapidly progressive breathlessness
  • Acute confusion or significant drowsiness
  • Cyanosis or severe hypoxia
  • Signs of severe right heart failure

Referral Pathways

Referral Type Indications Expected Timeline
Routine Respiratory Outpatient Diagnostic uncertainty, treatment optimization 4-6 weeks
Urgent Respiratory Outpatient Rapidly declining symptoms, frequent exacerbations 2 weeks
Same-Day Assessment Severe symptoms, hypoxia, confusion Same day
Emergency Admission Acute severe exacerbation, respiratory failure Immediate

Information to Include in Referral

  • Reason for referral (specific question)
  • Relevant history (onset, progression, risk factors)
  • Current symptoms and impact on daily life
  • Current medications and response to treatment
  • Spirometry results (including post-bronchodilator values)
  • Recent blood test results
  • Imaging results (chest X-ray, CT if done)
  • Comorbidities and their management
  • Smoking status and cessation attempts
  • Patient's understanding of condition and preferences

Other Referrals

  • Pulmonary Rehabilitation: All patients with MRC ≥3
  • Smoking Cessation Services: All current smokers
  • Dietitian: If BMI < 20 or significant weight loss
  • Physiotherapy: For airway clearance techniques
  • Occupational Therapy: For energy conservation techniques
  • Mental Health Services: If anxiety or depression
  • Social Services: For home support needs
  • Palliative Care: For advanced disease
