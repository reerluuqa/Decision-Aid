Tuberculosis Quick Reference

Clinical Knowledge Summary
Last revised: August 2025
What is Tuberculosis?

TB is an infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex, primarily affecting the lungs but can affect any organ.

Key facts: Active pulmonary TB accounts for 52.8% of all cases in the UK. If left untreated, one person with active pulmonary TB may infect 10-15 people per year.
Types of TB
  • Active TB: Symptomatic disease with evidence of progressive infection
  • Latent TB: Asymptomatic infection with immune response but no clinical disease
  • Pulmonary TB: Affects the lungs (most common)
  • Extrapulmonary TB: Affects other organs (lymph nodes, bones, etc.)
  • MDR-TB: Resistant to isoniazid and rifampicin
  • XDR-TB: Resistant to most anti-TB drugs

Prevalence in England
  • Annual TB incidence: 7.8 per 100,000 (2021)
  • 52.8% of cases are pulmonary TB
  • 76.4% of cases in people born outside UK
  • 35% of cases reported in London
  • 60.6% of cases in males
  • Strong correlation with deprivation
  • 13.1 per 100,000 in most deprived areas
  • 2.1 per 100,000 in least deprived areas
When to Suspect Active TB
RED FLAG: Be aware that the onset of active TB can be insidious, and early stages can be difficult to detect, leading to diagnostic delays.

Suspect active TB in any person who is at high risk of developing TB and has general symptoms of:

Pulmonary TB Symptoms
  • Persistent productive cough
  • Breathlessness
  • Haemoptysis (usually late feature)
Arrange chest X-ray
Extrapulmonary TB Symptoms
  • Lymphadenopathy (painless, rubbery)
  • Bone/joint pain, back pain
  • Abdominal/pelvic pain
  • Urinary symptoms
  • Headache, vomiting, confusion
  • Skin lesions
Special Considerations in Children

Children may present with non-specific symptoms and signs:

  • Poor weight gain or faltering growth
  • Fatigue
  • Persistent fever
WARNING: Young children are at increased risk of developing more severe active disease, particularly extrapulmonary TB.

Suspected Extrapulmonary TB

Arrange investigations based on likely site of disease:

  • Lymph node: Ultrasound, biopsy
  • Bone/joint: X-ray, MRI/CT
  • Abdominal: Ultrasound, CT
  • Renal: Urine tests, ultrasound
  • Pericardial: Echocardiogram
  • CNS: CT/MRI, lumbar puncture
  • General: Chest X-ray (always)
  • Any site: Tissue sample for histology
Special Considerations
False negative results are more common in children and immunocompromised people, such as those with HIV.

Be aware that people with multidrug-resistant TB may have:

  • History of previous TB treatment
  • Contact with known multidrug-resistant TB case
  • Origin from a country with high MDR-TB prevalence

Treatment Principles
Standard Drug Regimen

Standard treatment for drug-susceptible TB typically involves:

  • Intensive phase (2 months): Isoniazid, Rifampicin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol
  • Continuation phase (4 months): Isoniazid and Rifampicin
Special Considerations
  • MDR-TB: Requires specialist management with alternative regimens
  • Pregnancy: Standard treatment can be used with monitoring
  • Children: Weight-adjusted dosing essential
  • Liver disease: Adjustments may be needed
Supporting Patients

Information to Provide:

  • Nature of the disease
  • Treatment duration and importance of adherence
  • Possible side effects
  • Infection control measures

Special Support:

  • Directly observed therapy for under-served groups
  • Accommodation for homeless patients
  • Interpretation services if needed
  • Social services referral when appropriate
Who Should Be Screened

Asymptomatic people at high risk of infection should be screened for TB through active case-finding:

High Priority
  • Close contacts of active TB cases
  • New entrants from high-prevalence countries
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Healthcare workers
Consider Screening
  • Prisoners and prison staff
  • Homeless people
  • Drug users
  • People with alcohol misuse
