Wilson's Disease Management Algorithm

Based on BMJ Best Practice Guidelines (January 2025)

Assessment

Comprehensive clinical evaluation and initial investigations

Key Diagnostic Factors High Suspicion

Common Findings

  • Elevated serum aminotransferases (AST/ALT) in 40-60% of patients
  • Behavioral abnormalities - temper tantrums, anxiety, memory loss, impulsiveness
  • Kayser-Fleischer rings present in 95% neurological, 44-62% hepatic presentations
  • Tremor - fine, intentional, or rest tremor
  • Dysarthria - slurred speech, hypophonia, echolalia
  • Dystonia & rigidity - especially hands, causing postural/gait abnormalities
  • Incoordination - affects handwriting, buttoning, walking
  • Sloppy/small handwriting (micrographia)

Uncommon but Important

  • Acute liver failure (20% of presentations)
  • Jaundice, ascites, peripheral edema
  • Spider angiomata, gynecomastia
  • Encephalopathy, dysphagia
  • Coombs-negative hemolytic anemia

Risk Factors Genetic

Strong Risk Factors

  • ATP7B gene mutation - autosomal recessive, two mutated copies required
  • Family history - first-degree relatives must be screened
  • Consanguinity - higher prevalence in certain populations

Most patients are compound heterozygotes with >700 known pathogenic mutations

Initial Investigations Essential

Liver Function Tests

Almost always abnormal in hepatic presentation. Check AST, ALT, bilirubin, albumin, INR

Slit-lamp Examination

Detect Kayser-Fleischer rings - gold-brown copper deposits in Descemet's membrane

Serum Ceruloplasmin

Normal: 200-350 mg/L. <50 mg/L strongly suggests Wilson's disease

24-hour Urinary Copper

>100 micrograms consistent with Wilson's disease. >40 micrograms suggests further investigation

Important Notes

No single test is diagnostic. Ceruloplasmin can be normal in ~20% of patients and low in other conditions (protein-losing enteropathy, end-stage liver disease, nephrotic syndrome). Urinary copper can be elevated in cholestatic diseases.

Differential Diagnosis Exclude First

Hepatic Disorders

  • Hepatitis B/C - Check viral serologies
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Autoantibody screen positive
  • Steatohepatitis - Obesity, metabolic syndrome features
  • Alcohol-related cirrhosis - History of alcohol excess
  • Hemochromatosis - Transferrin saturation >50% in men, >45% in women
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency - Low AAT levels, early emphysema

Neurological Disorders

  • Parkinson's disease - Usually >50 years, dopaminergic agents improve symptoms
  • Essential tremor - Isolated tremor, positive family history
  • Psychiatric disease - No KF rings, normal ceruloplasmin
  • Multiple sclerosis - Sensory abnormalities, MRI findings differ

Other Conditions

  • Hemolytic anemia - Check Coombs test, other hemolysis workup
  • Aceruloplasminemia - Undetectable ceruloplasmin, CP gene mutation
  • Congenital disorders of glycosylation - Normal 24-hour urine copper

Family Screening Mandatory

First-Degree Relatives

All first-degree relatives of a newly diagnosed patient must be screened:

  • Molecular testing for ATP7B mutations or haplotype studies (preferred)
  • Liver function tests, serum ceruloplasmin, serum copper
  • 24-hour urinary copper excretion
  • Slit-lamp examination for KF rings
  • Liver ultrasound if indicated

Asymptomatic children should start treatment at 2-3 years of age

Treatment

Pharmacological therapy and management algorithms

Acute Hepatic Failure Emergency

Prognostic Scoring

Nazer Score Adults

Based on bilirubin, AST, and prothrombin time (PT). Maximum score: 12

  • ≥10: Urgent liver transplantation
  • 7-9: Evaluate clinical trajectory
  • ≤6: Pharmacological therapy
New Wilson Index (NWI) Pediatrics/Adults

Based on bilirubin, INR, AST, WBC, albumin. Score 0-20

  • ≥11: High mortality without transplant
  • ≤10: Consider medical therapy

Severe Acute Liver Failure

Nazer ≥10 OR NWI ≥11: Urgent referral to liver transplantation center

Immediate Action

~20% of patients present with acute liver failure (jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy, ascites, renal failure)

Mild to Moderate Hepatic Failure

Nazer ≤9 OR NWI ≤10: Pharmacological therapy with oral chelation + zinc

Monitor Closely

Combination therapy recommended. Monitor 24-hour urinary copper and clinical response. Normalization may take 1-12 months.

Pharmacological Therapy Lifelong Treatment

Penicillamine (D-isomer)

Mechanism: Oral chelator, increases urinary copper excretion

Dosing: Children 20mg/kg/day (max 1000mg); Adults 750-1500mg/day in divided doses

Adverse Effects: Fever, lymphadenopathy, thrombocytopenia, proteinuria, nephrotoxicity, bone marrow toxicity, dermatological changes. ~30% discontinue due to adverse effects.

Warning: Neurological Worsening Paradoxical worsening in 10-50% of patients with neurological symptoms

Trientine (Trientine Hydrochloride)

Mechanism: Oral chelator, promotes renal copper excretion

Dosing: Children <13y: 20mg/kg/day (max 1500mg); ≥13y & Adults: 750-1250mg/day (max 2000mg)

Advantages: Lower risk of neurological worsening, better adverse effect profile than penicillamine

Adverse Effects: Pancytopenia, colitis, iron deficiency, SLE (rare)

Note: Increasingly Used As first-line therapy due to better tolerability

Zinc Salts (Zinc Acetate/Sulfate)

Mechanism: Inhibits intestinal copper absorption

Dosing: Children <10y: 25mg 2-3x daily; ≥10y & Adults: 25-50mg 3x daily

Advantages: Minimal adverse effects, comparable efficacy to penicillamine, better tolerated

Adverse Effects: Gastric irritation (main), change salt if needed

Monitoring: 24-hour urinary copper <1.6 micromol/day (<100 micrograms)

Ongoing Management Maintenance

Symptomatic Patients (Neurological or Hepatic)

Initial Therapy: Oral chelation (penicillamine or trientine) OR zinc monotherapy

Maintenance: Switch to zinc OR continue lower-dose chelation after 1-5 years when:

  • Clinically well
  • Normal hepatic synthetic function
  • Normal aminotransferases and non-ceruloplasmin-bound copper
  • 24-hour urinary copper <1.6 micromol/day on chelation
Monitor Adherence

Asymptomatic Patients

Treatment: Zinc or oral chelation therapy

Children: Start treatment at 2-3 years of age

Preference: Zinc preferred in children due to favorable safety profile

Preventive
Neurological Worsening Risk

Patients with neurological symptoms at presentation are at risk for paradoxical worsening during initiation of chelation therapy. Start slowly and monitor closely for at least 12 months.

Incidence: 10-50% with penicillamine, less with trientine, rare with zinc

Special Populations Adjust Dosing

Pregnancy

Pre-conception: Discuss stability, genetic counseling, medication safety

During Pregnancy: Continue treatment

  • Penicillamine/Trientine: Reduce to 25-50% of pre-pregnancy dose
  • Zinc: No dose reduction needed
  • All are teratogenic (3% birth defect rate reported)

Breastfeeding: Discuss individually - may lack copper in milk, penicillamine excreted

Multidisciplinary Care

Children

Symptomatic: Prompt treatment to prevent progression

Asymptomatic: Start treatment at 2-3 years of age

Decompensated cirrhosis: May be treated successfully with chelation (monthly monitoring)

Transplant: Use NWI score for pediatric transplant decisions

Dietary Management Adjunctive

Copper Restriction

Important: Dietary restriction alone is NOT sufficient

First Year: Low-copper diet recommended during initial treatment phase

Avoid: Nuts, chocolate, most shellfish, soy-based products, organ meats, mushrooms

Check: Supplements and water supply for high copper content

Long-term: Few studies support prolonged restriction after first year

Refer to Dietitian

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications, and long-term prognosis

Monitoring Schedule Regular Follow-up

Frequency

Minimum: Every 6 months

During Initiation: More frequent (monthly for acute liver failure)

Adherence: Measure 24-hour urinary copper and zinc to check compliance

Assessment Components

  • History: New neurological/psychiatric symptoms, new medications
  • Examination: Liver disease stigmata, neurological examination
  • Laboratory: Liver function tests, serum copper, ceruloplasmin
  • Urinary Studies: 24-hour urinary copper and zinc excretion
  • Hematology: FBC with differential (if on chelation)

Treatment Targets

Urinary Copper: <1.6 micromol/day (<100 micrograms) on maintenance

Non-ceruloplasmin-bound copper: Normalize with effective treatment

Liver function: Improvement in 2-6 months

Neurological symptoms: Improvement in 50-60% over 1-3 years

Screen
Neurological Worsening Details

Can occur after initiation of chelation (10-50% with penicillamine) or less commonly with zinc. Usually in patients with neurological symptoms at presentation.

Outcomes: 24.2% recover completely, 27.3% partially, 39.8% no improvement

Prevention: Start chelation slowly, monitor for 12 months with movement disorder consultant

Prognosis Generally Good

Positive Indicators

  • Early treatment initiation → normal quality and quantity of life
  • Liver function improves within 2-6 months
  • Neurological symptoms improve in 50-60% over 1-3 years
  • Adequate compliance → normal lifespan (unless additional liver insult)

Poor Prognostic Factors

  • Severe neurological involvement at start of therapy → more residual symptoms
  • Decompensated cirrhosis with portal hypertension → persistent variceal bleeding risk
  • Poor compliance → ongoing copper toxicity

Variceal bleeding risk lessens over years of medical therapy

Hepatocellular Carcinoma Surveillance

Recommended for patients with Wilson's disease and cirrhosis

No increased risk of HCC compared to other cirrhosis etiologies when on treatment

Patient Discussions Essential

Counseling Points

  • Importance of lifelong medication compliance
  • Maintaining low-copper diet in initial phases
  • Multidisciplinary team involvement (nutritionist, psychiatrist, neurologist, physiotherapist)
  • Screening of first-degree relatives
  • Monitoring for adverse effects

Adherence Support

Regular follow-up with healthcare team critical for:

  • Medication adherence monitoring (urinary copper/zinc levels)
  • Early detection of adverse effects
  • Adjustment of therapy based on response
  • Psychosocial support for chronic disease management
