Pregnancy-Associated Dyspepsia

Clinical Reference Algorithm for Fast Decision-Making

Definition

  • Upper GI symptoms during pregnancy: heartburn, acid reflux, upper abdominal discomfort, belching, nausea
  • Primarily caused by gastro-oesophageal reflux due to hormonal changes and increased intra-abdominal pressure

Diagnosis

  • Clinical diagnosis based on symptoms
  • Ask about: heartburn, regurgitation, acid taste, impact on quality of life
  • Examination usually normal
  • Investigations rarely needed in primary care

Red Flags

  • Dysphagia
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • GI bleeding
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Epigastric or supraclavicular mass
  • Age ≥55 with weight loss

Management Approach

1

Lifestyle Advice

  • Smaller, frequent meals (every 3 hours)
  • Avoid eating 3 hours before bedtime
  • Identify and avoid triggers (caffeine, fatty/spicy foods, chocolate, carbonated drinks)
  • Elevate head of bed by 10-15 cm
  • Sleep on left side
  • Maintain healthy weight and regular physical activity
  • Avoid aggravating medications (NSAIDs, etc.)
  • Stop smoking
2

First-Line Pharmacological Treatment

  • Antacids (aluminium/magnesium combinations) as required
  • Alginate products as required
  • Liquid formulations preferred (faster acting)
  • Avoid sodium bicarbonate or magnesium trisilicate products
  • Calcium-containing products for short-term use only
  • Avoid taking within 2 hours of iron/folate supplements
3

Second-Line Treatment (if symptoms persist)

  • Consider acid-suppressing drugs only after shared decision-making about risks/benefits
  • Options:
    • H2 receptor antagonist: famotidine (if clearly needed)
    • Proton pump inhibitor: omeprazole (preferred PPI)
  • Document discussion about limited safety data in pregnancy

Referral Criteria

Immediate/Same Day

  • Significant acute GI bleeding

Urgent (to gastroenterologist)

  • Features suggestive of malignancy (see red flags)

Non-urgent

  • Symptoms not responding to primary care treatments
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Severe symptoms affecting ability to eat adequately

Obstetric Referral

  • If symptoms suggest other pregnancy-related disorders (pre-eclampsia, HELLP syndrome)

Key Considerations

  • Symptoms typically resolve after delivery
  • Pre-existing reflux symptoms are a risk factor
  • Severity often increases with gestational age
  • Consider safety in lactation if symptoms persist postpartum

Based on NICE CKS Guidance: Dyspepsia - pregnancy-associated (June 2024)
