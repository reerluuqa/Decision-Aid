CRITICAL: Immediate Action Required

Admit to Hospital

  • Extremely painful, acutely thrombosed external haemorrhoids presenting within 72 hours of onset (reduction or excision may be needed).
  • Incarcerated internal haemorrhoids: Prolapsed, swollen, unable to reduce, and thrombosed (risk of gangrene).
  • Perianal sepsis: Rare but life-threatening complication.

2-Week Wait (Suspected Cancer)

Refer via suspected cancer pathway if anal or colorectal cancer is suspected.

  • Red Flags: Unexplained weight loss, change in bowel habit >6 weeks, rectal bleeding with palpable mass, family history of CRC.
  • Note: Serious pathology cannot be discounted at any age. Refer if doubt exists.

Primary Care Management

Lifestyle & Conservative

  • Dietary Fibre: Whole grains, fruits, veg. Gradual increase to reduce gas.
  • Hydration: Adequate fluids with high fibre to prevent obstruction.
  • Hygiene: Keep clean and dry. Pat dry, do not rub.
  • Toilet Habit: Avoid straining and withholding stool.

Pharmacology

  • Analgesia: Paracetamol for pain.
  • Avoid: Opioids (cause constipation) and NSAIDs (if bleeding present).
  • Topicals: Can offer for symptomatic relief, but advise OTC purchase usually.
  • Laxatives: If constipated (refer to Constipation CKS topic).

Non-Urgent Referral

Refer for assessment/surgery if:

  • Grade 4 or large Grade 3 haemorrhoids not responsive to conservative care.
  • Perianal haematoma < 24hrs (for clot evaluation).
  • Combined internal/external with severe symptoms.
  • Problematic bleeding or chronic irritation/leakage.
  • Large skin tags causing hygiene issues.
Secondary Care Procedures
Best Outpatient

Rubber Band Ligation

Strangulates haemorrhoid; it sloughs off. Best outpatient option. ~20% need repeat banding.

Alternative

Sclerotherapy / IRC

Injection (Phenol) or Infrared Coagulation. Effective for Grade 1-2. Short-term benefit.

Surgical

Haemorrhoidectomy

Gold standard for severe/Grade 3-4. Best long-term effect, low recurrence (<20%).

Efficacy Questioned

Haemorrhoidal Artery Ligation (HAL)

Ligation of arteries + mucopexy. Recent evidence suggests frequent repeat procedures needed.
