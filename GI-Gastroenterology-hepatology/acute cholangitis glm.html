Acute Cholangitis Quick Reference

BMJ Best Practice • Last updated: Jul 18, 2023
MEDICAL EMERGENCY: Cholangitis can quickly become a life-threatening septic infection requiring immediate diagnosis and treatment.

Red Flags / Emergency Signs

Fever >39°C
Hypotension
Mental status changes
PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio <300
Serum creatinine >177 μmol/L
Requires vasopressors

Grade I

Mild

Grade II

Moderate

Grade III

Severe

Key Diagnostic Features

Charcot's Triad
RUQ/Upper abdominal pain
Jaundice
Fever
Pale/clay-colored stools
Pruritus

Risk Factors

  • Age >50 years
  • Cholelithiasis
  • Benign/malignant stricture
  • Post-procedure injury
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis

Laboratory Tests

Test Expected Findings
WBC Count >10.0 × 10⁹/L
LFTs Hyperbilirubinemia, raised ALP, transaminases
CRP Raised
Blood Cultures Positive in 21-71% (usually gram-negative)

Imaging

  • Transabdominal ultrasound - First-line for all patients
  • CT scan - If ultrasound inconclusive
  • MRCP - If high suspicion but ultrasound/CT negative
  • ERCP - Diagnostic and therapeutic

Initial Management

  • Start IV broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately
  • Stabilize hemodynamics with IV fluids
  • Correct electrolyte imbalances
  • Provide adequate analgesia
  • Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics

Biliary Decompression

  • ERCP - First-line for most patients
  • PTC - If ERCP not possible
  • Urgent decompression - Within 12-24 hours for severe cases
  • Surgery - Last resort, typically avoided

Antibiotic Options

First-line Options

  • Piperacillin/tazobactam
  • Imipenem/cilastatin
  • Cefuroxime + metronidazole

Penicillin-allergic

  • Metronidazole + ciprofloxacin
  • Metronidazole + levofloxacin
  • Metronidazole + gentamicin

Treatment Algorithm

1

Suspect Cholangitis

Look for Charcot's triad (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain) and risk factors

2

Initial Assessment

Check for signs of sepsis, organ dysfunction, and determine severity grade

3

Start Treatment

Begin IV fluids and broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately

4

Diagnostic Imaging

Order urgent transabdominal ultrasound, followed by CT/MRCP if needed

5

Biliary Decompression

Arrange ERCP within 12-24 hours for severe cases, 24-72 hours for moderate cases

6

Follow-up Care

Monitor response, adjust antibiotics based on cultures, consider cholecystectomy if indicated

Complications

  • Sepsis with shock
  • Vascular collapse
  • Multi-organ failure
  • Hepatic abscess
  • Biliary peritonitis
  • Death (if untreated)

Special Considerations

  • Older patients may present with non-specific symptoms
  • HIV patients may develop HIV cholangiopathy
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors can cause cholangitis
  • IgG4-related disease requires glucocorticoid treatment
  • Recurrent oriental pyogenic cholangitis more common in SE Asia
