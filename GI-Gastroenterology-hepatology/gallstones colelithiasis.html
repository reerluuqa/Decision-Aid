Suspect Acute Cholangitis (Charcot's Triad)

Features: Fever + Jaundice + RUQ Pain.

Suspect Acute Biliary Pancreatitis

Features: Epigastric pain radiating to back, vomiting. Likely stone distal to ampulla.

Action: Urgent Lipase/Amylase. Aggressive IV Fluids. NPO. See Acute Pancreatitis protocol.

Suspect Acute Cholecystitis

Features: Pain >5 hours, Fever, Murphy's Sign (+), WBC elevated.

Action: Hospital admission. IV Antibiotics (if systemic signs). Surgical consult for Cholecystectomy. See Acute Cholecystitis protocol.

Definition

Solid concretions (stones) in the gallbladder. Most (>90%) are cholesterol stones.

Prevalence: 10-15% adults. Asymptomatic in >80%.

Key Stats

  • Annual complication rate: 0.1% - 0.3%
  • Symptomatic patients: >50% have recurrent pain
  • Most common GI cause for hospitalization

Diagnosis & Workup

1st Line Investigation

Abdominal Ultrasound

Gold standard for gallstones. Checks for wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid, and duct dilation.

Gold Standard

Laboratory Interpretation

Test Uncomplicated Colic Obstruction / Choledocholithiasis
LFTs Normal Elevated Bilirubin & ALP
ALT Normal Early transient spike (if stone passes)
WBC Normal Elevated (if Cholecystitis/Cholangitis)
Lipase Normal Elevated >3x ULN (if Pancreatitis)

Next Steps (If High Suspicion but US Negative)

MRCP

Sensitivity 90% / Specificity 95% for duct stones. Reduced sensitivity for <5mm stones.

EUS (Endoscopic Ultrasound)

Better for small stones. Useful if MRCP negative but clinical suspicion high. Contraindicated in bypass patients.

Management Pathways

Is the Patient Symptomatic?

YES (Biliary Colic)

Acute Pain Control
  • 1st Line: NSAID (Diclofenac/Indometacin)
  • Add: Antispasmodic (Hyoscine)
  • If severe: Opioid (Buprenorphine preferred - less sphincter spasm)
Definitive Treatment

Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy

Offer to all symptomatic patients. Elective day-case is standard.

NO (Incidental Finding)

Expectant Management

Do not treat.
Exceptions for Cholecystectomy:

  • Porcelain gallbladder (Cancer risk)
  • Stones >3 cm
  • Sickle cell disease / Immunocompromised

Choledocholithiasis (Common Bile Duct Stones) Management

Treat even if asymptomatic (Risk of cholangitis/pancreatitis).

Non-Surgical Options (Not Recommended)

Litholysis (Bile Acids) +/- Shock Wave Lithotripsy: Symptomatic stones recur in ~1/3 of patients. Not recommended as standard alternative to surgery.
