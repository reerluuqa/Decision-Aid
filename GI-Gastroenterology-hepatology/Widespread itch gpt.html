Widespread Itch (Pruritus) — Primary Care Quick Reference

Age ≥ 12 yrs
Designed for rapid in‑consultation use. Based on NICE CKS: Itch – widespread (Jan 2024). Focus: no primary skin disease/rash.
Assessment Investigations Stepwise Management First‑line Measures Dosing Referral

Algorithm — Fast Decisions

Step 1
Initial Assessment
  • History: onset >6wks (chronic), nocturnal itch, triggers (heat/water/stress/drugs/alcohol/travel), impact (QoL), contacts (scabies), family hx.
  • Red flags: fatigue, fever, night sweats, weight loss.
  • Examination: exclude dermatoses/infestation; excoriations; jaundice/anaemia/uraemia/thyroid signs; nodes / hepatosplenomegaly.
Step 2
Investigations (if no primary skin disease)
  • FBC & film, ESR/CRP, LFTs, U&E, iron/ferritin.
  • Consider: TFTs, HbA1c, bone chemistry, HIV, Hep B/C, CXR.
  • Re‑evaluate periodically — itch can precede systemic disease.
Step 3
Determine Pathway
  • Known/suspected cause → treat underlying cause ± refer (renal, hepatic, endocrine, haematological, malignancy, neuropathic, psychogenic).
  • Unknown cause → symptom control + supportive care.
Step 4
First‑Line Measures (All)
  • Emollients: soap substitute + regular moisturiser; consider menthol 0.5–1% in aqueous cream if inadequate relief.
  • Self‑care: short lukewarm bathing (<20 min), avoid harsh soaps/perfumes, pat dry, trim nails, cool indoor air/humidify, loose cotton/silk, avoid alcohol/caffeine/spicy foods.
  • Address sleep disturbance, anxiety/depression; manage secondary infection/excoriations.
Step 5
Pharmacological Symptom Relief
  • Non‑sedating antihistamine (off‑label for itch) trial 2–3 weeks: Cetirizine 10 mg OD; Loratadine 10 mg OD; Fexofenadine 180 mg OD.
  • Nocturnal itch (short‑term 2–3 weeks): Hydroxyzine 25 mg nocte (adults); Chlorphenamine 4 mg nocte.
  • Not routinely recommended in primary care: topical anaesthetics/antihistamines/capsaicin/corticosteroids/calcineurin inhibitors/doxepin; oral corticosteroids.
Step 6
Escalation / Referral
  • Persistent symptoms despite above, or concerning systemic features → refer (Dermatology ± relevant specialty).
  • Dermatology options may include: SSRIs, mirtazapine, TCAs, gabapentin/pregabalin, immunosuppressants, phototherapy, biopsy as indicated.
Key reminders: Itch may precede other disease signs; re‑check if new systemic symptoms develop. Offer support for psychological distress. Treat secondary infection promptly.

Antihistamine Dosing & Safety (Quick View)

Drug Typical dose Key cautions / interactions
Cetirizine 10 mg once daily (trial 2–3 weeks) Avoid in ESRD (eGFR <15). If eGFR 30–59 → ½ dose; eGFR 15–29 → 5 mg every 48 h. Possible drowsiness; ↓ effect of betahistine.
Loratadine 10 mg once daily Severe hepatic impairment → alternate‑day dosing. Rare sedation; may ↓ betahistine effect.
Fexofenadine 180 mg once daily Few cautions. Interactions: antacids (separate by 2 h), rifampicin ↓ levels; others possible (e.g., MAOI, leflunomide, mirabegron).
Hydroxyzine (nocte) Adults: 25 mg at night (short‑term) Avoid with QT‑prolongation risks/drugs. Many cautions (hepatic/renal impairment, glaucoma, urinary retention, etc.). Multiple interactions; CNS depressants ↑ sedation.
Chlorphenamine (nocte) 4 mg at night (short‑term) Anticholinergic & sedative effects; caution in hepatic impairment, glaucoma, urinary retention; many CNS‑depressant interactions.

Non‑pharmacological Essentials

  • Emollient as wash & moisturiser; consider menthol 0.5–1% if poor relief.
  • Short lukewarm bathing; avoid bubble baths/high‑pH soaps/perfumes.
  • Pat dry; keep nails short; cool, humidified rooms; loose cotton/silk clothing.
  • Avoid alcohol, caffeine, spicy food when flaring.
When to Refer: persistent itch despite stepwise care; suspected systemic disease; psychogenic causes not manageable in primary care; need for biopsy or advanced therapies.

Avoid (Primary Care)

  • Topical anaesthetics, topical antihistamines, capsaicin, corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, doxepin.
  • Oral corticosteroids for itch relief.
