Traveller's Diarrhoea

Clinical Reference Tool | Last updated: Sep 03, 2024
Overview
Diagnosis
Management
Prevention
Definition

Traveller's diarrhoea (TD) is defined as ≥3 unformed stools in 24 hours accompanied by at least one of the following: fever, nausea, vomiting, cramps, tenesmus, or bloody stools (dysentery) during a trip abroad, typically to a destination with deficiencies in water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) infrastructure.

It is usually a benign, self-limited illness lasting 3-5 days.
Epidemiology
Risk Level Destinations Affected Travellers
Low Northern Europe, Australia, NZ, US, Canada, Singapore, Japan <10%
Moderate Mediterranean, South Africa, Caribbean 10-20%
High South/Southeast Asia, Central/South America, Africa >30% (up to 60%)
Seasonal Variation: Higher incidence during hot and wet months (especially in South Asia)
Aetiology
  • 80-90% Bacterial: Enterotoxigenic E. coli (most common), enteroaggregative E. coli, Shigella, Campylobacter jejuni, Salmonella
  • 5-15% Viral: Rotavirus (children), norovirus (cruise ships), astrovirus, sapovirus
  • ~10% Protozoal: More common in persistent diarrhoea (>14 days) - Giardia, Entamoeba, Cryptosporidium
Regional Variations: E. coli most common in Latin America; Campylobacter more prevalent in South/Southeast Asia
Classification

By Severity

  • Mild: Tolerable, not distressing, doesn't interfere with activities
  • Moderate: Distressing or interferes with planned activities
  • Severe: Incapacitating, prevents planned activities; all dysentery considered severe
  • Persistent: Lasting >14 days

By Aetiology

  • Bacterial: Most common, usually watery, responds to antibiotics
  • Viral: Usually self-limited, doesn't respond to antibiotics
  • Parasitic: More persistent (>14 days), doesn't respond to standard antibiotics
Red Flags
Seek immediate medical attention for:
  • High fever (>38.5°C or 101°F)
  • Visible blood in stool (dysentery)
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Signs of dehydration (decreased urination, dry mouth, dizziness)
  • Symptoms worsening after treatment
  • No improvement after 24-36 hours of appropriate antibiotic therapy
Diagnostic Criteria
1
Classic TD: ≥3 unformed stools/24h with at least 1 additional symptom (cramps, nausea, vomiting, fever, blood in stool)
2
Recent travel to a higher-risk destination is necessary for diagnosis
3
Self-diagnosis while still travelling is the usual approach
Risk Factors
Travel to high-risk destinations
Age <30 years
Adventure travel
Visiting friends and relatives (VFR travelers)
Travel during hot and wet seasons
Deployed military populations
Lack of caution in food and water selection
Proton-pump inhibitor use (weak risk factor)
Investigations
Test When to Order Notes
Stool culture and sensitivity Severe or persistent symptoms; failed empirical therapy Takes longer to process; may fail to detect causative agent
Multi-pathogen molecular PCR When rapid results needed; traditional methods failed Highly sensitive but doesn't provide antibiotic sensitivities
Protozoal stool antigens Persistent diarrhoea (>14 days) Tests for Cryptosporidium, Entamoeba histolytica, Giardia
Stool ova and parasite examination Persistent diarrhoea (>7 days) May miss Cryptosporidium; not as reliable as antigen test for Giardia
C. difficile stool toxin Recent antibiotic exposure; inflammatory colitis Important consideration in returning travelers
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Key Differentiating Features
Food poisoning Predominant vomiting; earlier onset (1-6 hours after meal); doesn't respond to antibiotics
Irritable bowel syndrome Diarrhoea or constipation with abdominal pain; no fever; symptom relief after bowel movement
Secondary disaccharidase deficiency Exacerbation with dairy products; usually resolves in days to a week
C. difficile colitis Persisting diarrhoea with fever, abdominal pain; often following antibiotic use
Inflammatory bowel disease Persistent diarrhoea, abdominal pain, fever; elevated inflammatory markers
Emergency Actions
Seek Immediate Care
For patients with any of the following:
  • High fever (>38.5°C or 101°F)
  • Visible blood in stool (dysentery)
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Signs of dehydration
  • Symptoms worsening after treatment
  • No improvement after 24-36 hours of appropriate antibiotic therapy
Rehydration
First Priority

Adults

  • Any preferred liquid if ORS unpalatable
  • Avoid excessive sugar-sweetened beverages (can cause osmotic diarrhoea)
  • Bowel rest should be avoided

Children & Infants

  • Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) are critical
  • Spoon-feeding ORS if child is vomiting
  • Continue breastfeeding despite diarrhoea
Antibiotic Therapy

When to Use

1
Not recommended for mild infection (tolerable, not distressing)
2
Consider for moderate infection (distressing or interferes with activities)
3
Always recommended for severe infection (incapacitating, dysentery, febrile)

Antibiotic Options

Antibiotic When to Use
Azithromycin First-line; covers dysentery; preferred for severe cases
Fluoroquinolones Second-line; consider resistance in Southeast Asia
Rifaximin Second-line; less effective in Asia (invasive pathogens)
Rifamycin Alternative to rifaximin; for non-invasive E. coli
Duration: Take antibiotics only until feeling better (usually 1-3 days), except rifaximin and rifamycin (full 3-day course). If symptoms persist after 24 hours, continue for up to 3 days.
Adjunctive Therapies

Loperamide

  • Use: Controls cramping and diarrhoea
  • Dosage: 4 mg initially, then 2 mg after each unformed stool (max 16 mg/day)
  • Contraindications: Visible blood in stool, high fever, children <6 years, pregnant women
  • Time to effect: 1-2 hours

Bismuth Subsalicylate

  • Use: Anti-diarrhoeal agent for mild infections
  • Dosage: 524 mg orally four times daily
  • Contraindications: Pregnant women, children <3 years
  • Side effects: Salicylate toxicity (tinnitus), blackening of tongue/stools
Special Populations

Pregnant Women

  • Rehydration is critical
  • Azithromycin is treatment of choice for moderate to severe infection
  • Avoid loperamide and bismuth subsalicylate

Children

  • Rehydration is priority over antibiotics
  • Azithromycin is treatment of choice
  • Alternatives: Rifaximin (≥12 years) or fluoroquinolones (with caution)
  • Avoid loperamide (<6 years) and bismuth subsalicylate (<3 years)
Food and Water Safety

Avoid

  • Ice in drinks
  • Tap water (including for brushing teeth)
  • Salads and uncooked vegetables
  • Previously peeled fruits
  • Raw or undercooked foods
  • Unpackaged condiments and sauces
  • Food from street vendors
  • Buffet food with poor turnover

Safe Options

  • Thoroughly cooked food served hot
  • Boiled or bottled (properly sealed) water
  • Commercially packaged foods
  • Fresh breads
  • Fruits peeled by yourself
  • Hot beverages like coffee or tea
  • Carbonated bottled beverages
  • Alcohol (in moderation)
Prophylactic Options
Not recommended for most travelers due to potential side effects and contribution to antibiotic resistance

When to Consider

  • Short-term critical itineraries (diplomatic missions, professional sports)
  • Chronically ill or immunocompromised patients (<3 weeks)

Recommended Options

  • Rifaximin: 200 mg orally 1-3 times daily
  • Rifamycin: 388 mg orally twice daily
  • Bismuth subsalicylate: 524-1048 mg orally four times daily (reduces incidence by >60%)
Important: Prophylaxis should be started before departure and discontinued after travel. Bismuth subsalicylate safety not established for >4 weeks use.
Travel Kit Recommendations

Essential Items

  • Oral rehydration salts
  • Antibiotics (azithromycin, rifaximin, or rifamycin)
  • Loperamide
  • Antiseptic hand gel
  • Water purification tablets or filter

Optional Items

  • Bismuth subsalicylate
  • Thermometer
  • Disposable eating utensils
  • Medical information card
  • Emergency contact information
Post-Travel Considerations
1
Persistent diarrhoea (>14 days): Seek medical evaluation for parasitic infection or post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome
2
Recent antibiotic use: Consider C. difficile testing if symptoms persist
3
Post-travel irritable bowel syndrome: Occurs in ~5% of travelers who experienced TD during travel
