Definition & Classification

Chronic, relapsing-remitting inflammatory disease of the large bowel, limited to intestinal mucosa with continuous inflammation.

Classification

Ulcerative proctitis: Limited to rectum (more common in adults)
Left-sided colitis: Doesn't extend beyond splenic flexure
Extensive colitis: Extends beyond splenic flexure (more common in children)

Key Features

Diffuse, continuous inflammation
Limited to intestinal mucosa
Usually affects rectum with variable proximal extension
Part of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)

When to Suspect Ulcerative Colitis

Key Symptoms

Bloody diarrhoea persisting >6 weeks
Rectal bleeding (if malignancy not suspected)
Faecal urgency and/or incontinence
Nocturnal defecation
Tenesmus (persistent urge to pass stool)
Abdominal pain (particularly left lower quadrant)
Pre-defecation pain relieved by defecation
Non-specific symptoms: fatigue, malaise, anorexia, fever (suggests severe disease)
Weight loss, faltering growth, or delayed puberty (in children)

Examination Findings

Pallor, clubbing or aphthous mouth ulcers
Abdominal distension, tenderness or mass (left lower quadrant)
Signs of malnutrition/malabsorption
Extra-intestinal manifestations (eye, skin, joint signs)
Note: Physical examination may be normal in people with mild or moderate disease

Essential Investigations in Primary Care

Blood Tests

Full blood count (anaemia, thrombocytosis)
Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR)
Urea and electrolytes
Liver function tests (including albumin)
Thyroid function tests
Ferritin, vitamin B12, folate, vitamin D

Other Investigations

Coeliac serology
Stool microscopy and culture (including C. difficile toxin)
Faecal calprotectin (raised in active inflammation)
Note: Investigation results may be normal in a person with active ulcerative colitis

Referral Criteria

Refer to Secondary Care:

All suspected cases of ulcerative colitis
For confirmation of diagnosis and specialist management
NICE quality standard: specialist assessment within 4 weeks of referral

Red Flags

Severe symptoms at presentation
Significant weight loss
Systemic symptoms (fever, tachycardia)
Signs of dehydration
Abdominal distension/tenderness

Management Principles

Follow specialist management plans to induce and maintain remission

Monitor for Complications

Toxic megacolon (life-threatening, requires colectomy)
Bowel obstruction/perforation
Intestinal strictures
Anaemia
Malnutrition/faltering growth (children)
Colorectal cancer (increased risk)
Pouchitis (post-colectomy)

Extra-intestinal Manifestations

Disease Activity-Related

Pauci-articular arthritis (<5 large joints)
Erythema nodosum (tender red nodules on anterior tibia)
Aphthous mouth ulcers
Episcleritis
Metabolic bone disease
Venous thromboembolism

Not Related to Disease Activity

Axial arthritis (sacroiliitis, spondylitis)
Polyarticular arthritis (≥5 joints)
Pyoderma gangrenosum
Uveitis
Hepatobiliary conditions (e.g., primary sclerosing cholangitis)

Prognosis

Disease Course

Lifelong condition with relapses and remissions
Up to 90% experience relapse after first presentation
15-25% require hospitalization for acute severe flare
Colectomy rate in acute severe UC: ~23%
Mortality during acute flare: <1%

Poor Prognostic Factors

Severe symptoms at presentation
Early relapse or active disease in first 2 years
Extensive disease
Raised inflammatory markers
Age <50 years (especially childhood-onset)
Poor compliance with treatment
