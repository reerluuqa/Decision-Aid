Dyspepsia - Pregnancy-associated

Age 13-60 (Female) | Primary Care Management

REFER IMMEDIATELY (Same-day) if: Significant acute GI bleeding.
Urgent Referral (2-week wait):
  • Dysphagia
  • Age 55+ with weight loss
Urgent Referral (Gastroenterology):
  • Hoarse voice
  • Persistent cough
  • Upper/lower GI bleeding
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Epigastric/Supraclavicular mass
üíö Management Ladder

1Self-Care & Lifestyle (First-line)

Eat smaller, frequent meals (every 3h). Avoid late-night eating. Avoid irritants (caffeine, spicy, fatty foods, alcohol). Sleep on left side. Elevate head of bed 10-15cm.

2Pharmacological (If uncontrolled)

Antacids/Alginates (Al/Mg combinations). Take "as required". Liquid preferred.

3Severe/Refractory (Last Resort)

PPIs (e.g., Omeprazole) or H2 antagonists. Use only if severe. Discuss risks/benefits (shared care). Note: Ranitidine withdrawn.

üîç Diagnosis
  • Clinical diagnosis based on symptoms (Heartburn, reflux, epigastric pain).
  • Exam usually normal.
  • Exclude other causes (Hyperemesis, Pre-eclampsia, HELLP).
‚ö° Prescribing Safety
  • Interactions: Avoid antacids within 2 hours of Iron or Folate supplements (reduces absorption).
  • Avoid: Sodium bicarbonate, Magnesium trisilicate in pregnancy.
  • Renal Impairment: Avoid/Reduce Mg salts.
üìã Key Differential Diagnoses
Pregnancy Related:
Nausea/vomiting, Hyperemesis, Pre-eclampsia, HELLP, Acute Fatty Liver.
Non-Pregnancy Related:
Peptic Ulcer, Pancreatitis, Cholecystitis, Cardiac Disease, Infections (Hepatitis).

When to suspect: Predominantly GORD symptoms (heartburn, acid reflux). Diagnosis is clinical.


History: Ask about quality of life impact, aggravating factors (lying flat, meals), medications (NSAIDs), and previous history. Exclude alarm features.


Examination: Usually normal. No routine investigations needed in primary care unless red flags present.

Self-Care (First Line)

  • Diet: Small, frequent meals. Avoid triggers (fatty, spicy, caffeine, alcohol).
  • Positioning: Elevate head of bed 10-15cm. Sleep on left side.
  • Weight: Avoid excessive weight gain. Regular physical activity.

Pharmacological

Antacids & Alginates: First-line if lifestyle fails. Use Al/Mg combinations. Calcium-containing antacids short-term only. Avoid Sodium bicarbonate/Magnesium trisilicate.

Acid Suppressing Drugs (Last Resort): Only consider if severe and uncontrolled. Discuss risks.

  • Omeprazole: Preferred PPI if needed. Manufacturer advises can be used, but data is limited.
  • Ranitidine: Withdrawn. Do not prescribe.
  • Famotidine: Alternative H2 antagonist, but manufacturer advises caution in pregnancy.
Urgency Trigger Action
Immediate (Same-day) Significant acute GI bleeding Emergency Referral
Urgent (2-week wait) Dysphagia OR Age 55+ with weight loss Urgent Cancer Referral
Urgent Features of malignancy (Mass, Lymphadenopathy, persistent cough) Gastroenterology
Non-Urgent Symptoms unresponsive to treatment, diagnostic doubt Gastroenterology

Antacids/Alginates

  • Cautions: Renal impairment (risk of Mg/Al accumulation). Hepatic impairment (avoid Na-containing products in fluid retention).
  • Interactions: May reduce absorption of Iron/Folate. Space doses by 2 hours.

Acid Suppressants

Due to lack of high-quality safety data, only prescribe if benefit outweighs risk. See BNF/eMC for full details.

Causes: Hormonal changes (reduced lower esophageal sphincter pressure), increased intra-abdominal pressure, delayed gastric emptying.

Prognosis: Symptoms usually resolve shortly after delivery. Serious complications (strictures, bleeding) are rare unless pre-existing disease.
