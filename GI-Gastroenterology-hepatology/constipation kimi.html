Constipation Management

Interactive algorithm based on NICE CKS Guidelines (November 2025)

Adult focused Pregnancy included Red flags identified

Assessment

Red Flag Screening

Refer urgently if any present:
  • Sudden change in bowel habit
  • Rectal bleeding or bloody stools
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Persistent abdominal pain
  • Iron deficiency anemia
  • Family history of colorectal cancer/IBD

Urgent Referral Suspected colorectal cancer

History Taking

  • Duration and pattern of constipation
  • Stool frequency & consistency (Bristol Stool Chart)
  • Straining, incomplete evacuation sensation
  • Manual evacuation methods used
  • Associated symptoms: pain, bloating, nausea
  • Urinary symptoms or incontinence
  • Impact on quality of life
  • Previous treatments tried

Medication Review

Common constipating drugs:
  • Opioids (80% develop constipation)
  • Iron/calcium supplements
  • Antimuscarinics, TCAs, antipsychotics
  • Antiepileptics, antihistamines
  • Calcium-channel blockers (verapamil)

Physical Examination

  • General nutritional status & weight
  • Abdominal examination: distension, masses, palpable colon
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE):
    • Anal fissures, hemorrhoids, rectocele
    • Rectal masses or retained stool
    • Pelvic floor dysfunction (paradoxical contraction)
    • Leakage or manual evacuation needed
Diagnosis

Functional Constipation

Rome IV Criteria: <3 spontaneous bowel movements/week with ≥2 of:
  • Straining during >25% of defecations
  • Lumpy/hard stools >25% of time
  • Sensation of incomplete evacuation >25% of time
  • Sensation of anorectal blockage >25% of time
  • Manual maneuvers needed >25% of time

Faecal Loading & Impaction

Diagnose if:
  • Hard, lumpy stools (large & infrequent or small & frequent)
  • Manual measures required for evacuation
  • Overflow faecal incontinence
  • Rectal mass on DRE

Classification

  • Short-duration: <3 months
  • Chronic: ≥3 months
  • Functional (Primary): No known cause
  • Secondary (Organic): Medication or medical condition

Investigations

Usually not required for functional constipation. Consider if:
  • Red flags present
  • Refractory to treatment
  • Unexplained symptoms

Tests: FBC, TSH, HbA1c, electrolytes, calcium

Treatment

Initial Self-Management

Lifestyle Measures:
  • Diet: 30g fiber/day gradually increased
  • Fluids: Adequate hydration (1.5-2L/day)
  • Exercise: Regular physical activity
  • Toileting: Regular, unhurried routine
  • Respond immediately to defecation urge

Stepped Laxative Approach

1 Bulk-forming (ispaghula)
2 Osmotic (macrogol)
3 Lactulose alternative
4 Stimulant laxative
Key Principles:
  • Adjust dose based on response
  • Combine different classes if needed
  • Titrate to achieve soft stool ≥3x/week
  • Gradual weaning after 2-4 weeks

Opioid-Induced Constipation

Do NOT use bulk-forming laxatives
  • Osmotic laxative + Stimulant laxative
  • Consider naldemedine if ineffective

Special Consideration 80% of opioid users develop constipation

Faecal Loading/Impaction

Disimpaction Protocol:
  1. High-dose oral macrogol (hard stools)
  2. Add stimulant laxative (soft stools)
  3. Suppositories: bisacodyl (soft) or glycerol (hard)
  4. Mini-enemas: docusate or sodium citrate
  5. Retention enemas: arachis oil overnight + phosphate

Warning Diarrhoea may occur before complete disimpaction

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

Stepwise approach:
  • First-line: Bulk-forming (ispaghula)
  • Second-line: Osmotic (lactulose/macrogol)
  • Third-line: Glycerol suppository
  • Contact UKTIS/UKDILAS if uncertain

Avoid Senna Removed from recommendations June 2025

Chronic Constipation

Resistant Cases:
  • Try ≥2 laxatives at max tolerated doses for ≥6 months
  • Consider prucalopride (4-week trial)
  • Biofeedback for pelvic floor dyssynergia
  • Surgery (rare): subtotal colectomy
Follow-up

Review Strategy

  • Review regularly based on clinical need
  • Wean laxatives gradually over months
  • Reduce dose when soft stool ≥3x/week achieved
  • Stop stimulant laxatives first
  • Adjust other laxatives to maintain regularity
  • Treat relapses early with increased doses

Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral
  • Suspected colorectal cancer
  • Suspected secondary cause needing specialist care
Consider Referral
  • Symptoms persist despite optimal management
  • Need for specialist investigations
  • Faecal incontinence (continence service)
  • Dietary support needed (dietitian)

Specialist Investigations

  • Endoscopy: Flexible sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy
  • Imaging: CT colonography, abdominal X-ray
  • Physiology: Anorectal manometry, defecography
  • Transit: Radio-opaque marker studies

Prognosis

  • Variable prognosis depending on cause
  • 48.5% have symptoms >10 years
  • Not life-threatening but affects quality of life
  • 63,427 admissions in England (2013-14)
  • 159,997 bed days consumed

Long-term Management Often requires ongoing lifestyle measures ± laxatives
