Urgent Assessment Required

üö® Alarm Symptoms Screening

Assess for alarm features that may indicate serious pathology:

  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Hematemesis or melena (vomiting blood/black stools)
  • Iron-deficiency anemia
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Family history of upper GI malignancy
  • New onset dyspepsia in patients >55 years

Action: If any alarm features present ‚Üí Urgent referral for endoscopy within 2 weeks

üåø Lifestyle & Trigger Factors

  • Weight assessment (BMI)
  • Dietary triggers: coffee, chocolate, fatty/spicy foods
  • Meal patterns: size, timing (avoid 3-4 hours before bed)
  • Smoking status
  • Alcohol consumption
  • Stress, anxiety, and depression screening
  • Medication review (NSAIDs, aspirin, etc.)

üíä Medication Review

Identify potential exacerbating medications:

  • NSAIDs, aspirin
  • Corticosteroids
  • Anticholinergics
  • Calcium-channel blockers
  • Bisphosphonates
  • Antidepressants (TCAs)

Consider reducing/stopping if possible

üß´ H. pylori Testing

Test if status unknown/uncertain:

  • Carbon-13 urea breath test (first-line)
  • Stool antigen test (alternative)

Test requirements:

  • Stop PPIs for 2 weeks before testing
  • Stop antibiotics for 4 weeks before testing

Serology only if breath/stool tests unavailable

Diagnosis

Confirm functional dyspepsia and classify

‚ñº

üìã Definition

Functional dyspepsia (non-ulcer dyspepsia) refers to patients with dyspepsia symptoms and normal endoscopy findings:

  • No gastric or duodenal ulcer
  • No gastric malignancy
  • No esophagitis

Most common diagnosis in 70-80% of patients undergoing endoscopy for dyspepsia

‚öïÔ∏è Sub-types (Rome IV Criteria)

Epigastric Pain Syndrome

  • Intermittent epigastric pain/burning
  • Localized to epigastrium
  • Not generalized or lower abdominal

Post-prandial Distress Syndrome

  • Post-prandial fullness
  • Early satiety
  • May coexist with epigastric pain

üî¨ H. pylori Test Interpretation

POSITIVE

Prescribe eradication therapy (see Treatment)

NEGATIVE

Offer acid suppression therapy (PPI or H2RA for 1 month)

Differential Diagnosis

ü§î Consider Alternative Causes

If atypical features or poor response to therapy:

  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GORD)
  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Biliary disease (gallstones, cholecystitis)
  • Cardiac causes (angina)
  • Medication-induced dyspepsia
  • Functional GI disorders (IBS overlap)

Treatment

Management strategies and medication regimens

‚ñº
First-line Management

üå± Lifestyle Modifications

  • Weight loss if overweight/obese
  • Avoid trigger foods: coffee, chocolate, tomatoes, fatty/spicy foods
  • Smaller meals and avoid late evening meals (3-4 hours before bed)
  • Smoking cessation
  • Reduce alcohol to recommended limits
  • Stress management: relaxation strategies, psychological therapy referral
  • Antacids/alginate for short-term symptom relief only

Refractory/Recurrent Symptoms

üîÑ Management of Persistent Symptoms

  1. Reassess: Check for new alarm features, adherence, lifestyle measures
  2. Consider re-testing H. pylori ONLY if:
    • High local antibiotic resistance
    • Poor compliance with first-line therapy
    • Need for NSAIDs/aspirin (especially with ulcer history)
    • Family history of gastric malignancy
    • Severe/persistent symptoms
    • Patient anxiety/request
  3. Test of cure timing: At least 4 weeks after antibiotics, 2 weeks after PPIs
  4. If positive: Second-line eradication (see below)
  5. If negative: Continue acid suppression or switch therapy

Follow-up

Long-term management and referral criteria

‚ñº

üìÖ Long-term Acid Suppression

  • Annual review of symptoms and treatment
  • Step down to lowest effective dose
  • On-demand therapy if appropriate
  • Consider stopping if no co-morbidity requiring long-term therapy
  • Self-treatment with antacids/alginate for breakthrough symptoms only

Avoid long-term continuous antacid/alginate use

üß™ When to Re-test H. pylori

Re-test ONLY if specific indications:

  • High local antibiotic resistance rates
  • Poor compliance with initial therapy
  • Initial test performed too soon after PPIs/antibiotics
  • Need for NSAIDs/aspirin, especially with ulcer history
  • Family history of gastric malignancy
  • Severe, persistent, or recurrent symptoms
  • Patient anxiety/request for confirmation

Timing: Minimum 4 weeks after antibiotics, 2 weeks after PPIs

Test of choice: Carbon-13 urea breath test (first-line)

Referral Indicators

üè• Specialist Referral

Refer to gastroenterology if:

  • Refractory/recurrent symptoms despite optimal primary care management
  • Second-line eradication regimen unsuccessful
  • Limited antibiotic options (hypersensitivity, high resistance)
  • Unexplained persistent symptoms with normal investigations
  • Need for specialist investigations:
    • Barium swallow/meal
    • Gastric scintigraphy (for delayed emptying)
    • Hydrogen breath tests (SIBO, lactose/fructose intolerance)
  • Consideration of specialist therapies:
    • Tricyclic antidepressants (for pain modulation)
    • Probiotics as adjunctive therapy

üîÆ Prognosis & Monitoring

Natural History
  • 70% have persistent symptoms at 1 year if untreated
  • Lifetime recurrence risk ~50%
  • Functional disorder with impaired quality of life
H. pylori Positive Cases
  • 36% improve spontaneously
  • 7% improve with eradication therapy
  • 57% have persistent symptoms 3-12 months

Advise patient to seek urgent review if develop:

  • New or worsening dysphagia
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Vomiting blood or coffee-ground material
  • Black, tarry stools
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Severe or worsening pain
