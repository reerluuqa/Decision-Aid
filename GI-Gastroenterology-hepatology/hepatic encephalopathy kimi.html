History & Examination

  • Assess mental status changes: confusion, disorientation, altered sleep-wake cycle
  • Check for asterixis (flapping tremor) - extend arms, dorsiflex wrists
  • Evaluate for underlying liver disease: jaundice, ascites, spider nevi
  • Screen for precipitating factors: GI Bleeding Infection Constipation Sedatives/Opioids

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Precipitating factors: GI bleed, infection, constipation, sedatives
  • Neurological signs: Altered mental status, asterixis, hyperreflexia
  • Liver disease signs: Jaundice, ascites, palmar erythema
  • Mood disturbances: Euphoria, anxiety, apathy, irritability

High Risk Features

Grade 3-4 HE, cerebral edema signs, uncontrolled GI bleeding, severe hyponatraemia

Diagnosis

Initial Investigations

  • Liver function tests: Elevated bilirubin, INR, AST/ALT
  • Serum ammonia: Correlates with severity but not diagnostic alone Normal value suggests alternative diagnosis
  • Electrolytes: Check for hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia
  • Glucose: Rule out hypoglycaemia
  • FBC & CRP: Screen for infection
  • Coagulation profile: Elevated PT/INR

West Haven Criteria for HE Grading

0 Minimal: No clinical signs, subtle psychometric changes Subclinical
1 Grade 1: Trivial lack of awareness, euphoria, anxiety, sleep disturbance Covert
2 Grade 2: Lethargy, disorientation to time, inappropriate behavior, asterixis Overt
3 Grade 3: Somnolence to semi-stupor, responsive to stimuli, gross disorientation Severe
4 Grade 4: Coma, Glasgow Coma Scale monitoring required Life-threatening

Imaging & Additional Tests

  • Head CT/MRI: Rule out intracerebral hemorrhage, stroke
  • Abdominal ultrasound: Check for portal/hepatic vein thrombosis
  • EEG: Decreased brain wave frequency in HE
  • Paracentesis: Rule out spontaneous bacterial peritonitis

Acute Management

Immediate Priorities

Supportive care + Reverse precipitating factors + Exclude alternative diagnoses

  • Airway protection: Head of bed 30¬∞, intubate if Grade 3-4

Alternative agents

Secondary Prevention

  • Lactulose: Continue for secondary prophylaxis after first episode
  • Rifaximin: Add-on therapy for recurrent HE (‚â•2 episodes/6 months)
  • Avoid precipitants: Sedatives, constipation, GI bleeding
  • Liver transplant evaluation: MELD >15 or recurrent/persistent HE

Patient Education

  • Avoid psychoactive medications (opioids, benzodiazepines, alcohol)
  • Maintain regular bowel movements (titrating lactulose)
  • Seek immediate medical attention for GI bleeding or infection
  • Driving restrictions: Avoid driving after overt HE episode
