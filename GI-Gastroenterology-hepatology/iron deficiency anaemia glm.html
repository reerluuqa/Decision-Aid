Iron Deficiency Anemia

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference Tool

Last Updated: Apr 29, 2025
RED FLAG: Severe anemia (Hb < 50 g/L) may cause high-output heart failure.
Definition

The World Health Organization defines anemia as hemoglobin:

  • <130 g/L (13 g/dL) in men aged ≥15 years
  • <120 g/L (12 g/dL) in non-pregnant women aged ≥15 years
  • <110 g/L (11 g/dL) in pregnant women
Key Facts
  • Most common type of anemia worldwide
  • Global prevalence of anemia: ~33%
  • More common in young children and premenopausal women
  • UK prevalence: 3% of men, 8% of women
  • US prevalence: 3-5% in non-pregnant women aged 16-49
Red Flags

Immediate Actions
Pathophysiology
Iron Functions
  • Formation of hemoglobin
  • Myoglobin production
  • Heme enzymes (cytochromes)
  • Essential for RBC production
Regulation
  • No regulatory pathway for iron excretion
  • Physiological loss: 1-2 mg daily
  • Pregnancy loss: ~580 mg total
  • Hepcidin decreases iron absorption

Diagnostic Approach
1
Initial Evaluation

Clinical evaluation focusing on unusual cravings (pica), restless legs syndrome, and physical findings (glossitis, angular stomatitis, nail changes).

2
Initial Laboratory Tests

Full blood count, peripheral smear, reticulocyte count, and iron profile.

3
Confirmatory Tests

Iron profile evaluation showing low serum iron, increased TIBC, transferrin saturation <16%, and low serum ferritin.

4
Underlying Cause Investigation

Coeliac serology, urinalysis, H. pylori testing, and GI tract investigations for most patients.

Further Investigations
Recommended for All Patients
  • Coeliac serology
  • Urinalysis
  • H. pylori testing
GI Tract Investigations

Upper and lower GI endoscopy for men and post-menopausal women with newly diagnosed IDA, unless:

  • Menstruating women
  • History of obvious blood loss from another system
  • Ethnicity increases risk of thalassaemia
Special Considerations

For patients with unclear serum studies, consider:

  • Bone marrow biopsy
  • Transferrin receptor-ferritin index
  • Monitored trial of iron
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Key Differentiating Features
Anaemia of chronic disease Signs of underlying chronic disease; ferritin often elevated
Thalassaemias More severe microcytosis; normal RDW; target cells on smear
Sideroblastic anaemias Erythrocyte dimorphism; ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow
Lead intoxication History of lead exposure; increased lead level
Iron Replacement Therapy
Oral Iron

First-line treatment for most patients with IDA.

IV Iron

Indicated for patients who cannot tolerate or absorb oral iron.

Emergency Treatment
Severe Anemia (Hb < 50 g/L)

May require immediate intervention.

Refractory IDA

Consider these causes if not responding to treatment.

Follow-up & Monitoring
Response Assessment
  • Retest hemoglobin after 4 weeks of therapy
  • Rise of 20 g/L (2 g/dL) indicates adequate response
  • Continue therapy for 3-6 months after correction
  • Aim to normalize ferritin levels
Long-term Management
  • Treat underlying cause of iron deficiency
  • Eradicate H. pylori if present
  • Consider dietary modifications
  • Prophylactic iron in high-risk groups
Patient Discussion Points
Dietary Advice
  • Increase iron-rich foods (red meat, poultry, fish)
  • Combine with vitamin C sources
  • Avoid tea/coffee with meals
  • Consider cooking in iron pots
Medication Counseling
  • Take iron supplements as prescribed
  • Common side effects: constipation, nausea
  • Take on empty stomach if tolerated
  • Stools may become dark/black
