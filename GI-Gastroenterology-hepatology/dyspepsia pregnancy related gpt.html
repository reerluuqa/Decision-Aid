Pregnancy‑Associated Dyspepsia — One‑Page Algorithm

Adapted from NICE Clinical Knowledge Summaries (CKS), last revised June 2024. Designed for rapid decision‑making at the point of care. Avoids dark UI for easy clinic printing.

13–60 years, pregnant Primary care (UK) NICE CKS: Dyspepsia (pregnancy‑associated)

1) Initial assessment

  • Symptoms: heartburn, regurgitation, acid taste; severity/quality‑of‑life impact.
  • Triggers: supine position, large/late meals; review current medicines (NSAIDs, CCBs, anticholinergics, antidepressants).
  • History: prior reflux/dyspepsia; response to OTCs.
  • Exam: usually normal.
  • Tests: not routinely required; secondary care if atypical/severe (e.g., endoscopy, manometry/pH). H. pylori testing can wait until postpartum unless otherwise indicated.

2) Red flags → urgent action

  • Dysphagia
  • GI bleeding / haematemesis
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Hoarse voice or persistent cough
  • Lymphadenopathy; epigastric/supraclavicular mass

Action: same‑day for significant acute GI bleeding; urgent cancer pathway if malignancy suspected.

3) Consider differentials

Pregnancy‑related

NVP / Hyperemesis Pre‑eclampsia HELLP AFLP (rare)

Other causes

Peptic ulcer disease Pancreatitis Cholecystitis Upper GI cancer Oesophageal motility/eosinophilic oesophagitis Cardiac disease

4) Stepwise management

4A. Lifestyle & self‑care (first‑line)

  • Small, frequent meals; avoid eating <3 h before bed.
  • Avoid irritants: alcohol, caffeine, chocolate, citrus, carbonated drinks, spicy/fatty foods.
  • Food diary; maintain physical activity; avoid excessive weight gain.
  • Elevate head of bed 10–15 cm; prefer left‑lateral sleep.
  • Stop smoking; avoid/adjust trigger medicines if appropriate.
  • Provide patient info (e.g., NHS: Indigestion & heartburn in pregnancy).

4B. Medicines (if lifestyle inadequate)

First‑line: Antacids / alginates PRN — aluminium/magnesium combos or alginate products.
  • Calcium products → occasional/short‑term only.
  • Avoid: sodium bicarbonate; magnesium trisilicate.
  • Administration: liquids often faster/more effective than tablets.
  • Interactions: separate antacids from iron/folate by ≥2 hours.

If severe/persistent despite above

  • Consider acid‑suppression after shared decision‑making (limited pregnancy safety data; primary care last‑resort or secondary care).
  • H2RA: famotidine may be used only if clearly needed per manufacturer.
  • PPI: omeprazole may be used if benefit > risk (limited data; possible small risks discussed in literature). Pantoprazole manufacturer reports no evidence of harm in available data.

5) When to refer

Immediate (same‑day)

Significant acute GI bleeding.

Urgent (cancer pathway)

Features suggestive of malignancy. Note that classic alarm features can be harder to interpret in pregnancy.

Non‑urgent gastro

  • Refractory to antacids/alginates/primary‑care options.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty.

Obstetric referral

If features suggest pre‑eclampsia, HELLP, AFLP, or other pregnancy‑related disorder (use clinical judgement for urgency).

6) Prognosis

Symptoms commonly persist through pregnancy and usually resolve after delivery. Serious complications are rare unless pre‑existing reflux disease.
