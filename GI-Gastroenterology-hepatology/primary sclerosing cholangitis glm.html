Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis

Quick Reference Guide
Emergency: Sudden deterioration in clinical status or biochemical tests may indicate dominant stricture or cholangiocarcinoma. Immediate evaluation required.
Red Flags
  • Sudden worsening pruritus, fevers, and jaundice
  • Rapid increase in alkaline phosphatase
  • Weight loss and decreased appetite
  • New onset abdominal pain
  • Signs of liver failure (ascites, encephalopathy)
Key Facts
  • Cholestatic liver disease causing bile duct destruction
  • Predominantly affects young and middle-aged men
  • 50-80% have associated inflammatory bowel disease
  • May be asymptomatic at diagnosis
  • No effective medical therapy available
At Risk Population
  • Male (2:1 male-to-female ratio)
  • Age 25-45 years (median 36-39)
  • Patients with IBD (especially ulcerative colitis)
  • First-degree relatives of PSC patients
Positive Actions
  • Screen all IBD patients with annual liver function tests
  • Refer advanced cases to liver transplantation center
  • Colonoscopy surveillance for colorectal cancer
  • Bone mineral density scanning at diagnosis

PSC Variants
  • Large-duct PSC: Classic form involving intra- and extrahepatic ducts visible on cholangiography
  • Small-duct PSC: Affects only small bile ducts (normal cholangiography, abnormal histology)
  • PSC-AIH overlap: Features of autoimmune hepatitis with cholangiographic abnormalities
Diagnostic Red Flags
  • Alkaline phosphatase > 3x upper limit of normal
  • Marked elevation in aminotransferases (>5x ULN) suggests overlap with autoimmune hepatitis
  • IgG4 > 5.6 g/L suggests IgG4-associated disease
  • Normal cholangiogram with typical biochemical/histological findings suggests small-duct PSC

MRCP
Preferred diagnostic test showing multi-focal stricturing and dilation
ERCP
Only if MRCP non-diagnostic or therapeutic intervention needed
Diagnostic Criteria
  • Cholangiography with multi-focal stricturing of intra- and/or extrahepatic bile ducts
  • Biochemical evidence of cholestasis
  • Exclusion of secondary causes
Histological Staging
  • Stage 1: Bile duct injury and portal inflammation with minimal fibrosis
  • Stage 2: Expansion of portal tracts, periportal fibrosis
  • Stage 3: Fibrous septa, bridging fibrosis, ductopenia
  • Stage 4: Cirrhosis
Management Approach: Treat symptoms and complications, with liver transplantation for end-stage liver disease. Consider all patients for clinical trials.
Medical Management
  • No effective medical therapy available
  • Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) may improve biochemistry but no proven survival benefit
  • Immunosuppressors not effective for classic PSC
  • Corticosteroids only for PSC-AIH overlap or IgG4-associated disease
Endoscopic Management
  • Dominant strictures: balloon dilation with or without stenting
  • Bacterial cholangitis: biliary drainage and antibiotics
  • Bile duct stones: extraction
  • Brush cytology/FISH for cholangiocarcinoma surveillance
Transplantation
  • Only treatment option for advanced disease
  • Indications: decompensated cirrhosis, recurrent cholangitis, intractable pruritus
  • 5-year survival post-transplant: 85-90%
  • Recurrence in 20-25% of transplanted patients
Management Algorithm
Initial Assessment 100%
Confirm Diagnosis
MRCP, laboratory tests, exclude secondary causes
Assess Disease Stage
Fibrosis staging, dominant strictures, cholangiocarcinoma screening
Manage Symptoms
Pruritus (cholestyramine), fat malabsorption, nutritional support
Treat Complications
Dominant strictures, cholangitis, cholangiocarcinoma
Monitor Progression
Regular LFTs, imaging, surveillance for complications
Refer for Transplant
When advanced disease or complications develop
Major Complications: Liver failure and cholangiocarcinoma are the leading causes of death in PSC patients.
Cholangiocarcinoma
  • Occurs in 7-15% of PSC patients
  • Risk increases with disease duration
  • Difficult to diagnose early
  • Poor prognosis (median survival < 12 months)
  • Surveillance with CA 19-9 and imaging recommended
Dominant Strictures
  • Present in 40-50% of patients
  • Defined as stenosis ≤1.5mm in common bile duct or ≤1mm in hepatic duct
  • Can cause jaundice, pruritus, cholangitis
  • Require endoscopic dilation with or without stenting
Bacterial Cholangitis
  • Recurrent episodes in 6-15% of patients
  • Present with fever, right upper quadrant pain, jaundice
  • Require antibiotics and biliary drainage
  • ERCP may be needed if not responding to antibiotics
Metabolic Complications
  • Fat malabsorption leading to steatorrhea
  • Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)
  • Osteoporosis (screen at diagnosis and every 2-4 years)
  • Cholestasis-induced pruritus

Disease Progression Monitoring
  • Transient elastography or magnetic resonance elastography to stage fibrosis
  • Mayo PSC Risk Score for prognosis calculation
  • Serial imaging to detect dominant strictures or cholangiocarcinoma
  • Monitor for symptoms of fat malabsorption and vitamin deficiency
When to Refer to Transplant Center
  • Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) score ≥ 15
  • Refractory bacterial cholangitis
  • Intractable pruritus severely affecting quality of life
  • Recurrent variceal bleeding despite standard therapy
  • Suspected cholangiocarcinoma (for evaluation)
Medication Monitoring
  • UDCA: Monitor LFTs, dose 13-23 mg/kg/day
  • Cholestyramine: Monitor fat-soluble vitamin levels
  • Rifampin: Monitor LFTs, CBC
  • Vitamin supplements: Monitor levels periodically
