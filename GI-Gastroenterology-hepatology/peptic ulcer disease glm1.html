Last updated: Jul 30, 2025

EMERGENCY PRESENTATION

Recognize sudden presentation with bleeding (haematemesis/melaena), shock, or peritonitis. Resuscitate immediately using ABC principles and refer urgently.

Key Symptoms & Diagnosis

Typical Pain
Chronic, upper abdominal pain related to meals, often nocturnal
Pointing Sign
Patient can localize pain with one finger
Diagnostic Test
Endoscopy is definitive; consider barium radiography if endoscopy unavailable
H. pylori Testing
Urease test, histology, urea breath test, or stool antigen test

Major Risk Factors

H. pylori Infection
90% of duodenal ulcers, 70% of gastric ulcers
NSAID Use
20% incidence in chronic users vs 5% in non-users
Smoking
Prevalence nearly double in smokers vs non-smokers
Increasing Age
Risk increases with age, especially >60 years

Complications

Complication Signs Action
Bleeding Haematemesis, melaena, hypotension
Perforation Rigid abdomen, peritonitis
Pyloric Stenosis Nausea, vomiting, early satiety
Penetration Severe pain radiating to back

Prevention Strategies

NSAID Users
Co-prescribe PPIs, especially in high-risk patients (>60 years, history of PUD)
H. pylori Eradication
Prevents recurrence of PUD and bleeding
ICU Patients
Prophylactic PPIs for high-risk patients (mechanical ventilation, coagulopathy)

Prognosis

Good with Treatment
Most ulcers heal with appropriate therapy
Recurrence
Common if H. pylori not eradicated or NSAIDs continued
Poor Prognosis Factors
Advanced age, comorbidities, bleeding, perforation
