Acute Abdomen

Assessment & Management Tool

Initial Resuscitation (ABCDE)

Immediate assessment of Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, and Exposure. Monitor vital signs and obtain large-bore IV access.

Ruptured AAA

CRITICAL

Fluid Restriction: Aggressive fluid resuscitation before surgery increases perioperative death.

  • Maintain systolic BP between 80 and 90 mmHg (Permissive Hypotension).
  • Avoid if SBP drops < 70 mmHg (High mortality).
  • Urgent surgical/vascular consult immediately.

Ectopic Pregnancy

URGENT

Rule out for ALL women of childbearing age.

  • Pregnancy Test (Urine/Serum) is mandatory.
  • If suspected: Blood type & cross-match.
  • Urgent Gynecology consult (Time is ovary).

Mesenteric Ischemia

HIGH RISK

Pain out of proportion to physical signs.

  • Risk factors: Atrial Fibrillation, Age > 60, Vascular disease.
  • CT Angiography recommended.
  • Elevated Lactic Acid (serial).

Prophylactic Antibiotics

ACTION

Start immediately for:

  • Perforated Viscus
  • Appendicitis
  • Diverticulitis
  • Ruptured AAA
  • Mesenteric Ischemia

Other Red Flags

  • Testicular Torsion: Urgent Urology consult. Time is testicle.
  • MI: Consider in epigastric pain with sweating. Obtain ECG/Troponin.
  • Perforation: Erect CXR for free air under diaphragm.

Analgesia

Do NOT withhold pain medication.

Opioid analgesia does not increase diagnostic error risk or mask signs significantly. It improves patient comfort and allows better exam.

History Key Points

Onset

Sudden (Perforation, Stone) vs Gradual (Obstruction, Diverticulitis).

Character

Sharp/Localized (Parietal/Peritonitis) vs Dull/Colicky (Visceral/Obstruction).

Radiation

Groin: Renal Colic
Back: Pancreatitis, AAA, Dissection
R Scapula: Gallbladder/Liver

Special Groups (High Risk)

These groups often present atypically.

Group Presentation/Caveats
Older People Perforated ulcers often present without peritonitis. Comorbidities blunt response.
Pregnant Uterus displaces organs (Appendix can be RUQ). Physiologic leukocytosis is normal.
Immunocompromised Altered inflammatory response. Low threshold for CT. Typhlitis risk.

Physical Exam Signs

Abdomen Wall
  • Guarding/Rigidity: Peritonitis/Perforation.
  • Rebound Tenderness: Parietal irritation.
Specific Signs
  • Murphy's Sign: Cholecystitis (arrest of inspiration on RUQ palpation).
  • Cullen's Sign: Periumbilical bruising (Hemorrhagic Pancreatitis).
  • Grey Turner's Sign: Flank bruising (Retroperitoneal hemorrhage).
Auscultation
  • Tinkling: Early obstruction.
  • Silent: Late obstruction, Perforation, Ischemia.

Laboratory Tests

Test Indication / Key Finding
Lipase Pancreatitis Preferred over Amylase. >3x normal is diagnostic.
FBC Leukocytosis (Infection/Inflammation). Note: Normal in elderly/immunocompromised.
Lactate Ischemia Elevated in Mesenteric Ischemia.
Urinalysis Pyelonephritis (Infection), Stones (Blood). Rules out urinary source.
Beta-hCG Rule out Ectopic All females of childbearing age.

Imaging

CT Abdomen (IV Contrast)

Gold standard for most acute pathologies (Obstruction, Ischemia, Abscess). Check renal function first.

Ultrasound

First line for Pregnancy and Biliary (Gallstones) pathology. Safe for kids.

XR (Erect Chest/Abd)

Rarely changes management. Useful for free air (perforation) or obstruction if CT unavailable.

Common Causes

Non-specific abdominal pain Renal Colic Biliary Colic Cholecystitis Appendicitis Diverticulitis Ectopic Pregnancy Acute Pancreatitis

Uncommon but Critical

  • AAA Dissection/Rupture
  • Acute Mesenteric Ischaemia
  • Myocardial Infarction
  • Testicular/Ovarian Torsion
  • Boerhaave's Syndrome (Oesophageal rupture)
  • Perforated Viscus

Diagnosis Exclusion

Non-specific abdominal pain (NSAP) is a diagnosis of exclusion.

In a large study, NSAP was the most common diagnosis. However, ensure follow-up as:

  • ~2% are readmitted within 30 days.
  • Patients > 60 discharged with NSAP have a higher risk of underlying cancer (missed diagnosis).
