Assessment

Comprehensive history and examination

‚ñº

ü§± Infant History

  • Crying episodes: Onset, duration, frequency, timing, change in tone/pitch
  • Red flag cries: Weak, abnormally high-pitched, or continuous cry Urgent
  • Systemic red flags: Fever, apnoea, cyanosis, abnormal breathing, bilious vomiting, weight loss, bloody stool Urgent
  • Gestational history: Birth gestation, trauma, medical conditions, congenital abnormalities (tongue-tie, cleft palate)
  • Growth: Birth weight, weight gain/loss, serial measurements
  • Associated symptoms: Reflux, constipation, rash (suggesting other causes)
  • Feeding: Pattern, problems, timing vs crying, formula preparation, winding technique, other fluids/foods
  • Comfort measures: Dummy/pacifier use, sleeping pattern

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß Parental/Carer History

  • Coping mechanisms: Responses to crying, impact on family and siblings
  • Mental health: Insomnia, stress, anxiety, depression, postnatal depression Caution
  • Expectations: Beliefs, ideas, concerns about crying, feeding, sleep
  • Support system: Family support, health visitor, nursery nurse, social worker
  • Obstetric history: Pregnancy, labour, post-partum complications
  • Medical history: Atopy, allergies, alcohol, smoking
  • Breastfeeding: Maternal breast/nipple pain, nipple shields, diet (caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods)

üë∂ Infant Examination

  • Appropriate clothing and temperature
  • Signs of fever, dehydration, raised ICP
  • Weight and serial measurements
  • Skin rash or itch
  • Oral anatomy (tongue-tie, palate, jaw, lips)
  • Oral thrush, nasal congestion
  • Muscle tone, neurological deficits
  • Signs of bruises, petechiae, trauma Urgent

ü§ù Parent-Infant Interaction

  • Observe parent/carer interaction and handling
  • Breastfeeding assessment by trained professional if concerns
  • Maternal breast examination if breastfeeding problems

Diagnosis

Rome IV criteria and differential diagnosis

‚ñº

üìä Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria

Infantile colic is diagnosed when ALL of the following are present:

  • Age: Symptoms start and stop before 5 months of age Stable
  • Crying pattern: Recurrent, prolonged crying/fussing/irritability without obvious cause Stable
  • Unsoothable: Cannot be prevented or resolved by caregivers Stable
  • No red flags: No failure to thrive, fever, or illness Stable

Note: Modified Wessel's criteria (3 hours/day, 3 days/week, 3 weeks) are no longer recommended due to arbitrary nature and cultural dependency.

‚ö†Ô∏è Differential Diagnosis

Be aware: signs of organic disease can be non-specific

Sudden-Onset Symptoms (Urgent)

  • Intussusception/volvulus: bile-stained vomit, blood in stool, abdominal distension/tenderness/mass Urgent
  • Appendicitis or peritonitis Urgent
  • Pyloric stenosis: projectile vomiting in infants up to 2 months Urgent
  • Incarcerated hernia, testicular/ovarian torsion Urgent
  • Infection/sepsis Urgent
  • Corneal abrasion Urgent
  • Hydrocephalus: macrocephaly, vomiting, lethargy Urgent
  • Accidental trauma or child maltreatment Urgent

Persistent Symptoms (Evaluate)

  • Hunger, dehydration, feeding difficulties Caution
  • Temperature discomfort (too hot/cold) Caution
  • Skin issues: rash, eczema, nappy rash Caution
  • Wind/inadequate winding Caution
  • Constipation or anal fissure Caution
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) Caution
  • Non-IgE mediated food allergy or transient lactose intolerance Caution
  • Seizures or infantile spasms Caution
  • Congenital/metabolic disorders Caution

Treatment & Management

Primary care strategies and support

‚ñº

üéØ Key Management Steps

  • Reassurance: Explain that infantile colic is common and usually resolves by 5 months Stable
  • Education: Provide information about the self-limiting nature Stable
  • Support: Identify and address parental concerns Stable
  • Review: Arrange follow-up based on clinical judgement Stable

ü§± Breastfeeding Support

  • Encourage continuation of breastfeeding wherever possible Stable
  • If problems suspected ‚Üí refer to breastfeeding specialist Caution
  • Assess latch, positioning, and milk transfer
  • Address maternal nipple pain or trauma

üéµ Soothing Strategies to Recommend

  • Hold the baby through crying episodes Stable
  • Reduce environmental stimuli Stable
  • Gentle motion (pram, rocking crib) Stable
  • 'White noise' (vacuum, hairdryer) Stable
  • Warm bath Stable
  • Optimal winding technique during/after feeds Stable

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß Parental Wellbeing Support

  • Encourage asking family/friends for support Stable
  • Suggest peer support groups with other parents Stable
  • Advise resting when baby sleeps Stable
  • Important safety advice: If unable to cope, put baby in safe place (cot) and take 'time out' Stable
  • Screen for postnatal depression and anxiety Caution

üö´ DO NOT Recommend

  • Simeticone drops (Infacol¬Æ) No evidence
  • Lactase drops (Colief¬Æ) No evidence
  • Maternal diet modification (breastfeeding) No evidence
  • Changing infant formula No evidence
  • Probiotic supplements No evidence
  • Herbal supplements No evidence
  • Manipulative therapies (spinal/cranial osteopathy) No evidence

These interventions lack evidence of effectiveness and may cause unnecessary cost and delay appropriate management.

Follow-up & Referral

Monitoring and escalation criteria

‚ñº

üìà Review Strategy

  • Timing: Based on clinical judgement and severity Stable
  • Frequency: Early review if severe distress or parental anxiety Stable
  • Duration: If symptoms persist >5 months ‚Üí reconsider diagnosis Caution
  • Red flags: If new concerning features develop Caution

üè• Referral Criteria

Seek specialist advice or refer to paediatrician if:

  • Parents/carers unable to cope despite primary care support Urgent
  • Suspected faltering growth or weight loss Urgent
  • Symptoms persist beyond 5 months of age Urgent
  • Suspected underlying cause that cannot be managed in primary care Urgent
  • Severe or worsening symptoms Caution
  • Significant parental mental health concerns Caution
  • Child protection concerns Urgent

üîÑ Alternative Diagnoses to Consider

  • Cow's milk protein allergy
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD)
  • Constipation
  • Food allergy
  • Lactose intolerance

üìû Referral Pathways

  • Paediatrician: Medical concerns, red flags
  • Health Visitor: Routine support, monitoring
  • Nursery Nurse: Feeding and care advice
  • Breastfeeding Specialist: Lactation issues
  • Mental Health: Parental anxiety/depression
  • Social Services: Child protection concerns

‚úÖ Prognosis & Reassurance

Expected Course:

Infantile colic is self-limiting and usually improves by 3-4 months, resolving by 5-6 months. Conflicting evidence exists about long-term risks of psychological/behavioral conditions or allergic disorders.
