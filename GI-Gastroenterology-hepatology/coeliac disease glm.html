Coeliac Disease

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference

Target: Primary Care / NHS

Last Revised: June 2025

Coeliac disease is common (1% prevalence) but often undiagnosed.

Presentation varies widely (GI to extra-intestinal). Maintain a low threshold for testing.

GI Presentation

  • Persistent diarrhoea / steatorrhoea
  • Abdominal pain, bloating, cramping
  • Weight loss
  • Constipation
  • Acid reflux

Extra-Intestinal & Associated

  • Anaemia: Iron, B12, or Folate deficiency (unexplained or refractory).
  • Fatigue/Tiredness: Persistent.
  • Autoimmune: Type 1 Diabetes, Autoimmune Thyroid disease, Autoimmune Liver disease.
  • Neurological: Peripheral neuropathy, ataxia.
  • Misc: Mouth ulcers, Dermatitis Herpetiformis, Infertility, recurrent miscarriage.

High Risk Groups (Consider Screening)

  • First-degree relative with coeliac disease.
  • Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus.
  • Autoimmune Thyroid Disease.
  • Down's, Turner's, or Williams syndrome.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Osteoporosis/Osteopenia.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) symptoms.
  • Unexplained raised transaminases.
CRITICAL STEP: GLUTEN CHALLENGE

Ensure patient has eaten gluten in more than one meal a day, for at least 6 weeks before testing. Do not start a gluten-free diet until diagnosis is confirmed.

1. Arrange Serology

First line: Tissue Transglutaminase IgA (tTGA) + Total IgA.

  • If tTGA IgA Positive: Refer to Gastroenterology.
  • If Total IgA Deficient: This causes false negatives. Order IgG-based tests (IgG tTGA, IgG EMA, or IgG DGP).
Interpretation Guide:
Positive Serology → Biopsy (Adults/Young People)
Positive Serology → Paediatrician (Children)

2. Referral & Confirmation

Adults & Young People

Refer to Gastroenterologist for endoscopic intestinal biopsy to confirm diagnosis.

Children

Refer to Paediatric Gastroenterologist. Diagnosis may involve biopsy or specific HLA testing/serology combinations per guidelines (ESPGHAN).

Dermatitis Herpetiformis

Refer to Dermatologist for skin biopsy.

What if Serology is Negative?

  • If clinical suspicion is low: Coeliac disease unlikely. Re-test if symptoms change.
  • If clinical suspicion is high (e.g., severe symptoms, family history): Refer to specialist anyway for consideration of further testing (HLA typing or biopsy).
The only effective treatment is life-long Gluten-Free Diet (GFD).

Dietary Education

Refer to Dietitian: Specialist education is essential for the patient and family/carers.

Avoid (Gluten-containing):

  • Wheat, Barley, Rye (and varieties like spelt, kamut).
  • Bread, pasta, flour, cakes, biscuits.
  • Beer (malt), standard sauces, processed foods (check labels).
  • Oats: Only pure, uncontaminated oats (monitor response). Avoid standard oats due to cross-contamination.

Safe Alternatives:

  • Rice, corn, potato, quinoa, millet, buckwheat.
  • Products labelled "Gluten-free" or with the Crossed Grain symbol.

Monitoring (Annual Review)

Arrange annual review in primary care.

Action Notes
Symptom Review Ask about adherence, inadvertent gluten exposure, and lingering symptoms.
Weight / BMI / Height Monitor nutritional status and growth (children).
Blood Tests FBC, Ferritin, B12, Folate, LFTs, TFTs, Calcium, Vit D.
Coeliac Serology To assess dietary adherence (alongside dietary review).

Prescribing (Gluten-Free Foods)

  • Prescription: Gluten-free items can be prescribed on the NHS.
  • ACBS Endorsement: Must be endorsed 'ACBS' on the prescription.
  • Variation: Availability varies by UK region (England: Bread/Flour mixes only; Scotland/Wales/NI: wider range including pasta/oats).
  • Vaccinations: Offer Pneumococcal, Meningococcal, and Influenza vaccines if hyposplenism or asplenia is present.

Urgent Situations & Complications

Non-Responsive / Refractory Disease

Refer to specialist if symptoms persist despite strict Gluten-Free Diet for 6-12 months and gluten exposure is excluded.

  • Refractory Coeliac Disease (RCD): Rare. Risk of enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL).
  • Type 2 RCD: Abnormal intraepithelial lymphocytes. Poor prognosis, high risk of lymphoma.

Malignancy Risks

Untreated Coeliac disease increases risk of malignancy. Maintain high suspicion for:

  • Small Bowel Adenocarcinoma: >30-fold increased risk.
  • Lymphoma (Non-Hodgkin's & EATL): 2-4 fold increased risk.
  • Use 2-week wait pathways if cancer is suspected.

Other Complications to Monitor

  • Hyposplenism: Increased risk of severe encapsulated bacterial infections.
  • Osteoporosis: Due to malabsorption of Calcium/Vit D.
  • Ulcerative Jejunitis: Rare complication of RCD.
  • Neurological: Gluten Ataxia, Peripheral neuropathy.
  • Psychological: Depression, Anxiety, Eating disorders.
  • Reproductive: Fertility issues, pregnancy complications.
