Immediate Action Required

  • Rectal Cancer Suspected: Refer using 2-week suspected cancer pathway.
  • Serious Underlying Cause Suspected: If fissure looks atypical (irregular, lateral, multiple) or IBD/STI suspected, refer to specialist urgently.
  • Elderly Patient: Anal fissure is less common; consider referring to general/colorectal surgeon.
  • Child Maltreatment: Consider this if fissure present in child and constipation/IBD excluded.

Diagnosis & Classification

Feature Acute (< 6 weeks) Chronic (≥ 6 weeks)
Appearance Superficial, well-demarcated edges. Wider, deeper. Visible muscle fibers. Swollen edges, skin tag sentinel pile.
Location Usually Posterior Midline (Primary). Anterior midline common in women.
Secondary (Red Flag) Multiple, lateral, or irregular outline.
Key Symptoms: Severe sharp pain on defecation (often followed by burning hours later). Bright red blood on stool/paper.

Background Info

Causes

Primary: Trauma (hard/loose stool), increased sphincter tone, local ischaemia.

Secondary: Constipation, IBD, STI (HIV, Syphilis, Herpes), Colorectal cancer, Dermatological conditions, Anal trauma.

Prognosis

~50% of acute fissures resolve within 6-8 weeks with conservative treatment. 40% progress to chronic. Recurrence is common if lifestyle measures stop.

Complications

Failure to heal, recurrent fissure, anorectal fistula, infection/abscess, faecal impaction (due to withholding stool).

Management Protocol

For All Patients (First Line)

  • Dietary Measures: Ensure stools are soft and easy to pass. Adequate fibre (whole grains, fruits, veg) + Fluids.
  • Hygiene: Keep anal region clean and dry to aid healing.
  • Avoid Straining: Advise against 'stool withholding' and undue straining.
  • Pain Relief:
    • Sitting in warm shallow bath (several times/day).
    • Paracetamol or Ibuprofen for burning pain.
    • Avoid Opioids (cause constipation).
  • Topical Anaesthetic: Consider Lidocaine 5% ointment (Adults) if extreme pain.

Pharmacological (Adults)

Rectal Glyceryl Trinitrate (GTN) 0.4% Ointment

Consider if primary fissure and symptoms for 1+ week without improvement.

  • Dose: Apply twice daily for 6–8 weeks.
  • Warn about Headache (occurs in ~25% of people).
  • NOT for children, pregnancy, or breastfeeding.

Child Specific Pathway

  • Treat constipation if present (see separate CKS topics).
  • Review after 2 weeks.
  • Refer to Paediatrician if unhealed after 2 weeks OR if in significant pain.

Unhealed / Recurrent (Adult)

Scenario: Unhealed after 6–8 weeks.

  • Asymptomatic / Improved:
    2nd course GTN OR discuss with colorectal surgery.
  • No Improvement:
    Check adherence. If poor -> seek specialist advice (e.g. topical Diltiazem).
  • Adherent but No Improvement:
    Refer to General/Colorectal surgeon.
  • Recurrence:
    Reinforce diet. If adherent -> Refer for investigation.

Prescribing: Rectal GTN 0.4%

Contraindications (Do Not Prescribe)

  • Hypotension / Postural hypotension / Uncorrected hypovolaemia
  • Increased intracranial pressure (head trauma, cerebral haemorrhage)
  • Migraine or recurrent headache
  • Aortic/Mitral stenosis, Hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy
  • Constrictive pericarditis / Tamponade
  • Marked anaemia, Closed-angle glaucoma
  • Age < 18 (Manufacturer advises against)
  • Taking PDE5 inhibitors (e.g., Sildenafil) or other nitrates

Cautions & Adverse Effects

Common Adverse Effect:

Dose-related headache (approx 50-65% of users). Can be mild, moderate, or severe. Treat with simple analgesics.

Use with Caution in:

  • Severe hepatic or renal disease.
  • Cardiac disorders (MI, heart failure) - monitor for hypotension.
  • Elderly (risk of postural hypotension).
  • Pregnancy: Manufacturer advises against. BNF says not known harmful.
  • Breastfeeding: Manufacturer advises against. UK Specialist Pharmacy says compatible (wash hands after use).
