Angular Cheilitis - Clinical Quick Reference

BMJ Best Practice Based | Quick-Scan Clinical Guide

Multi-factorial condition - Treat ALL contributing factors for success

Quick Summary

  • Inflammation of mouth corners - fissures, scaling, erythema, crusting
  • Prevalence: 7 per 1000, mostly elderly
  • Key causes: Candida, Staph aureus, dentures, nutritional deficiencies
  • Treatment success: Requires addressing ALL underlying factors
Risk Factors (Red Flags)
STRONG RISK
  • Age over 75 years - 3x more common
  • Male gender - 2x increase
  • Denture wearers - 3x increased odds
  • Immune deficiency/HIV - 10% have candidiasis
  • Diabetes mellitus - common association
  • Down's syndrome - 25% of patients
  • Xerostomia - isolated or Sjogren's
Etiology Overview
CAUSES
  • Infectious: Candida albicans, Staphylococcus aureus
  • Mechanical: ill-fitting dentures, drooling, lip-licking
  • Nutritional: iron, B12, folate, riboflavin, niacin, zinc
  • Systemic: diabetes, HIV, IBD, Crohn's
  • Drug-related: isotretinoin, indinavir, sorafenib
Epidemiology
Prevalence 7 per 1000
Most affected Older adults
Denture users 3x increased risk
Male predominance ~2x more common
Nutritional deficiency ~25% of cases
HIV association 5.6% to 28.9% prevalence
Clinical Patterns
Pattern 1 Single painful fissure (rhagad)
Pattern 2 Deeper fissure following skin fold
Pattern 3 Multiple fissures radiating outward
Pattern 4 Erythema at corners (no fissures)
Unilateral Usually trauma, short-lived
Bilateral Often infectious or systemic
Diagnosis is primarily clinical - Laboratory tests help identify contributing factors, especially in refractory cases
Key Diagnostic Red Flags
Palatal erythema + dentures Denture stomatitis + candidiasis
Severe pruritus Allergic contact dermatitis
Eczematous dermatitis of face Staphylococcal infection
Chronic unilateral lesion BIOPSY - rule out malignancy
Recalcitrant to treatment Consider HIV testing
Resistant candidiasis Immune deficiency workup
Tongue Signs - Nutritional Deficiencies
Pale, de-papillated tongue Iron deficiency ± anemia
Red, glossy, de-papillated Folate or niacin deficiency
Red atrophic tongue Vitamin B12 deficiency
Reddish-purple, de-papillated Riboflavin deficiency
Smooth shiny red lips + fissures Riboflavin deficiency (cheilosis)
Fissures + alopecia + diarrhea Zinc deficiency

CRITICAL: Treat ALL contributing factors - infection, mechanical, nutritional, and systemic
When to Refer / Escalate
  • Chronic unilateral lesion - biopsy to exclude malignancy
  • Treatment-resistant disease - dermatology referral
  • Recalcitrant candidiasis - immune deficiency workup
  • Poor denture fit - prosthodontic evaluation
  • Severe nutritional deficiency - specialist referral
  • Suspected contact allergy - patch testing
Supportive Measures (ALL Patients)
  • Denture management: Remove at night, clean thoroughly
  • Denture storage: Hypochlorite or chlorhexidine overnight
  • Nutritional correction: Identify and treat deficiencies
  • Oral hygiene: Good dental care and regular check-ups
  • Denture fit: Improve fit or new dentures if needed
  • Habit modification: Stop lip licking, thumb sucking
Treatment Algorithm
1
Clinical Assessment

History, examination, identify all contributing factors

2
Address Mechanical Factors

Improve denture fit, treat drooling, stop lip-licking habits

3
Screen & Treat Nutritional Deficiencies

Iron, B12, folate, riboflavin, niacin, zinc

4
Empiric Topical Therapy

Miconazole, nystatin, or mupirocin based on suspected cause

5
Evaluate Response (2-4 weeks)

If improvement - continue; If no response - investigate further

6
Recalcitrant Disease

Systemic fluconazole ± amphotericin-B lozenges, consider biopsy

7
Chronic/Recurrent Cases

Dermal fillers, prosthodontic evaluation, specialist referral

Drug selection depends on infectious agent and patient medication history

First-Line Antifungals

Topical Miconazole
Children & Adults: 2% cream, apply twice daily for 7-14 days
After meals and at bedtime. Effective against mixed infections (gram-positive bacteriostatic action)
Topical Nystatin
Children & Adults: 100,000 units/g, apply twice daily until healing complete
First-line in patients taking warfarin, phenytoin, or sulfonylureas (no systemic absorption)
Topical Amphotericin-B
Apply to affected area 3-4 times daily for 7-14 days
⚠ Not currently available in some countries. Alternative to miconazole when drug interactions are concern.

Second-Line / Systemic Therapy

Oral Fluconazole
Children: 6 mg/kg single dose day 1, then 3 mg/kg/day × 13 days
Adults: 200 mg single dose day 1, then 100 mg daily × 13 days
First-line systemic antifungal for recalcitrant disease
Amphotericin-B Lozenges
Often used with fluconazole
⚠ Not currently available in some countries

Antibacterial Therapy (Staphylococcal Colonization)

Topical Mupirocin
Children & Adults: 2% ointment, apply four times daily for 7-14 days
Valuable for treating staphylococcal colonization. If nares colonized, treat nares as well.
Polymyxin-Containing Preparations
Apply to affected area 3-4 times daily
Alternative to mupirocin for staphylococcal colonization
Drug Interaction Warning - Miconazole

Miconazole is absorbed systemically and may potentiate warfarin, phenytoin, and sulfonylureas. Use nystatin or amphotericin-B instead in these patients.

Prevention focuses on addressing mechanical factors, nutritional status, and oral hygiene
Primary Prevention
  • Good oral hygiene - reduces incidence
  • Proper denture fit - key preventive measure
  • Xylitol/chlorhexidine gum - reduces in elderly ≥60 years
  • Regular dental check-ups - early problem detection
  • Balanced diet - prevent nutritional deficiencies
Secondary Prevention
  • Xylitol gum - prevents maceration in lip folds
  • Petroleum jelly - barrier to prevent maceration
  • Frequent re-application - topical agents at mouth corners
  • Denture hygiene - overnight cleaning and storage
  • Nutritional supplements - if deficiencies identified
Patient Education Points

✓ DO

  • Remove dentures at night
  • Clean dentures thoroughly before re-insertion
  • Store dentures in hypochlorite/chlorhexidine
  • Apply topical medications frequently
  • Maintain good oral hygiene
  • Attend dental follow-up appointments

✗ AVOID

  • Trauma to mouth corners
  • Lip licking or thumb sucking
  • Leaving dentures in overnight
  • Poor denture hygiene
  • Delaying treatment of underlying conditions
  • Non-compliance with medications
Complications
Acquired linear anetoderma Long-term, low likelihood
Chronic pain/discomfort If untreated
Secondary bacterial infection If barrier disrupted
Malignancy Chronic unilateral lesions
Prognosis
Good with appropriate treatment
