Bariatric Surgery Sequelae & Complications

Updated Reference: Nov 2025

CRITICAL Immediate Threats (< 30 Days & Emergencies)

Anastomotic Leak
Tachycardia >110-120 bpm Fever >38.5°C Abdo Pain/Anxiety Respiratory Distress
IMMEDIATE ACTION: ➔ Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics + Resuscitation.
➔ CT Abdomen/Pelvis w/ IV & Oral contrast.
➔ Surgical Exploration if in doubt.
PE / DVT
SOB Chest Pain Tachycardia
DIAGNOSIS & TX: ➔ Lower Extremity Doppler (DVT).
➔ CT Angiography (PE).
➔ Anticoagulation.
Internal Hernia
Pain >3 months post-op Epigastric/Radiation to back Intermittent obstruction
WORKUP: ➔ CT w/ IV contrast (Findings often non-specific).
➔ High suspicion -> Laparoscopic Exploration.
➔ Close all hernia spaces.
Early Obstruction
Vomiting Distension Pain
CAUSES: ➔ Distal Anastomosis kinking (Common).
➔ Internal Hernia.
➔ CT-guided decompression if stable.
Abdominal Compartment Sx
Oliguria/Renal Failure Intra-abdominal Pressure >20 mmHg Resp Failure
MEASURE: ➔ Bladder pressure via Foley.
TX: ➔ If >25 mmHg -> Surgical Decompression.

Procedure Specific Complications

Stricture (Gastro-jejunostomy)

Most common complication. 3-6 weeks post-op.

Vomiting Solids -> Liquids Dysphagia
TX: Endoscopic balloon dilation (10-15mm). Often requires 2+ dilations.
Marginal Ulcer

Just distal to anastomosis. Strongly linked to smoking & NSAIDs.

Epigastric Pain Perforation Signs
TX: PPI/Acid reduction. Remove suture material endoscopically. Revision if fistula present.
Incisional Hernia
Open Surgery: 20% risk Lap: <1% risk
Staple Line Leak

Risk ~0.7%. May be harder to treat than bypass.

TX: Endoscopic stenting (may respond), nutritional support, possible surgery.
GERD / GORD
New-onset in 8-30% Exacerbates pre-existing
Mgmt: Medical therapy 1st line.
Refractory: If BMI >35 -> Convert to RYGB. If BMI <35 -> Magnetic Sphincter Augmentation or Diversion.
Dilation

Long-term dilation leading to weight regain.

TX: Surgical revision (e.g., Re-sleeve or bypass).
Band Slippage / Prolapse
>5% incidence Obstruction Abdo Pain
IMMEDIATE: Deflate band (remove fluid).
Imaging: Plain film (Phi angle changes).
Persistent: Surgical revision/removal.
Erosion

Band erodes through gastric wall.

Port site cellulitis Infection tracking
TX: Antibiotics + Band Removal.
Pseudoachalasia

Esophageal dilation/widening.

TX: Deflate band -> Remove if persistent.

Metabolic & Nutritional Complications

CRITICAL WARNING: Thiamine (B1) Deficiency

Risk in prolonged vomiting (e.g., stricture, hyperemesis). Can lead to Wernicke's Encephalopathy.

DO NOT give Dextrose-containing IV fluids first!
Result: Permanent neurological injury.

CORRECT PROTOCOL: ➔ Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate + Thiamine (Vit B1) IV + Multivitamin.
Routine Supplementation
Multivitamin With Thiamine, B12, Iron (Twice daily)
Calcium Citrate preferred (absorption > Carbonate)
Vitamin D High incidence of deficiency
Vitamin B12 Required if proximal bowel bypassed
Iron Polysaccharide complex (better tolerated)
Bone Metabolism

All surgeries lower femoral neck density. Malabsorptive procedures have higher risk.

  • Monitor Calcium & Vitamin D.
  • Risk of Secondary Hyperparathyroidism.
  • Roux-en-Y causes greater bone loss than Sleeve.
Pregnancy
Recommendations:
  • Delay pregnancy until weight stable.
  • Sleeve/Bypass: ~1 year.
  • Band: ~2 years.
Outcomes:
  • Lower birth-weight.
  • Risk of small for gestational age.
  • Monitor nutrition closely.
