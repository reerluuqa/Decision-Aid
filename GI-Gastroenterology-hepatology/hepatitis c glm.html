Hepatitis C: Quick-Scan

Last revised: May 2025
Immediate Actions Required Arrange same-day assessment for Acute HCV. Urgent referral for Chronic HCV. Notify local Health Protection Team.

Who to Test (Screening)

High Risk

Offer screening to:

  • People who have ever injected drugs.
  • Received blood transfusion before 1991.
  • Received blood products before 1986.
  • Babies born to mothers infected with Hep C.
  • Prisoners & young offenders.

Demographic Risks

Offer screening to:

  • Born/brought up in country with high prevalence (>2%) (Africa, Asia, E. Europe, etc.).
  • People who have tested positive for HIV or Hep B.
  • Close contacts of chronic carriers.
  • Men who have sex with men (HIV+).

Clinical Indicators

Test if:

  • Unexplained abnormal LFTs (elevated ALT).
  • Non-specific symptoms with risk factors.
  • Steatosis or unexplained liver disease.

Referral Protocol

Acute HCV

Same-day assessment or immediate specialist advice.

Chronic HCV

Urgent Referral to hepatologist/gastroenterologist.

Notification

Notify local Health Protection Team immediately.

Baseline Investigations (Primary Care)

Arrange these whilst awaiting specialist management.

Investigation Rationale
Full Blood Count Check for anaemia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs) Note: May not accurately indicate severity of damage.
U&E, Creatinine, eGFR Assess renal function (CKD is an extrahepatic manifestation).
Clotting Screen Assess coagulation status.
HbA1c & TFTs Check for diabetes and thyroid issues (associated comorbidities).
Hep B & HIV Tests Check for co-infection status.

Information & Advice

Lifestyle

  • Stop Alcohol: Accelerates liver disease progression.
  • Stop Smoking: Independent risk factor for fibrosis.
  • Healthy Weight: Reduces risk of fatty liver/NAFLD.

Prevention

  • Do not donate blood, semen, or organs.
  • Do not share razors, toothbrushes, or drug paraphernalia.
  • Vaccinate against Hep A and B.

Sexual Transmission

Risk is low in heterosexual monogamous relationships but higher in HIV+ MSM.

Advise: Use condoms to protect against Hep B/HIV and reduce Hep C risk.

Definition

Blood-borne virus (Flaviviridae family). Slow progressive disease of the liver.

View Genotypes

Genotypes 1, 2, 3: Worldwide (Most UK infections are 1 & 3).

Genotype 4: North Africa, Middle East.

Genotype 5/6: South Africa, Hong Kong.

Transmission

Primarily blood-to-blood contact.

  • Sharing needles (PWID).
  • Historic blood transfusions (pre-1991).
  • Vertical (Mother to baby): 4-8% risk (higher if co-infected with HIV).
  • Sexual: Uncommon heterosexually; higher risk in HIV+ MSM.

Prognosis

Acute: 15-45% clear spontaneously within 6 months.

Chronic: 55-85% develop chronic infection.

Progression: 10-30% develop cirrhosis in 20 years.

Treatment: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) >90% success rate.

Clinical Features

Acute HCV

Usually asymptomatic (>60%). If symptomatic:

  • Fatigue, fever, aches.
  • Nausea, abdominal pain.
  • Jaundice (rare).

Complications

  • Liver Cirrhosis & Failure.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
  • Extrahepatic: Depression, Diabetes, Cryoglobulinaemia.
