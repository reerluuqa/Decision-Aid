Key Diagnostic Factors
Rectal bleeding - Common but may indicate benign disease
Change in bowel habit - Especially increased frequency or looser stools
Rectal mass - Palpable in 40-80% of rectal cancer cases
Abdominal pain - Common but non-specific
Anaemia - Present in 90% of right-sided colon cancer
Family history - Increases risk, especially in first-degree relatives
Increasing age - Greatest risk factor for sporadic cases
Red Flag Symptoms
Immediate referral required:
  • Rectal bleeding with change in bowel habit
  • Palpable rectal or abdominal mass
  • Unexplained iron-deficiency anaemia
  • Intestinal obstruction symptoms
Urgent investigation needed:
  • Unexplained weight loss with abdominal pain
  • Persistent change in bowel habit >6 weeks
  • New onset rectal bleeding in patients >50 years
Clinical Approach
Initial Assessment

Full history, physical exam including digital rectal examination

Baseline Tests

FBC, liver biochemistry, renal function

FIT Testing

Quantitative faecal immunochemical test if appropriate

Imaging

Colonoscopy (preferred) or CT colonography

Staging

CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, MRI pelvis for rectal cancer

Strong Risk Factors
Increasing Age

Median age at diagnosis: 66 years

Family History

1 affected first-degree relative: 2.24x risk

2 affected first-degree relatives: 3.97x risk

Genetic Syndromes
  • Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)
  • Lynch syndrome (HNPCC)
  • MUTYH/MYH-associated polyposis
  • Hamartomatous polyposis syndromes
Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Risk increases with extent and duration

Obesity

Increased risk of developing and dying from disease

Moderate Risk Factors
Red and Processed Meat Consumption

High intake associated with increased risk

Smoking

Long-term smoking increases incidence and mortality

Alcohol Consumption

Moderate to heavy consumption increases risk

Physical Inactivity

50% reduction in risk with highest activity levels

Low Vitamin D

Associated with increased colorectal cancer risk

Weaker Risk Factors
Acromegaly

Estimated 2x increased risk

Lack of Dietary Fibre

Weak protective effect suggested by most studies

High Carbohydrate Intake

May increase risk in men

Microplastics

May increase risk (emerging evidence)

Male Sex

Slightly more common in men than women

Epidemiology
Incidence Trends
  • 3rd most common cancer in developed world
  • Significant increase in younger adults (<50 years) since 1990
  • US incidence: 36.5 per 100,000 people (2017-2021 data)
  • Global age-standardised incidence: 26.7 per 100,000 people
Mortality Trends
  • US mortality dropped 56% from 1970 to 2021
  • Global age-standardised mortality: 13.7 per 100,000 people
  • Rising mortality in adults <55 years since mid-2000s
  • Higher mortality in black people and Alaska Natives
First-Line Investigations
Full Blood Count

Baseline assessment and to check for anaemia

Interpretation: Anaemia present in 90% of right-sided colon cancer
Liver Biochemistry

Baseline assessment and to check for metastases

Interpretation: May be normal even when liver metastases present
Renal Function

Baseline assessment and to assess for future treatments

Interpretation: May be abnormal if advanced pelvic disease compresses ureters
Quantitative FIT

Detects small amounts of blood in faeces

Interpretation: ≥10 μg Hb/g faeces = urgent referral (within 2 weeks)
Imaging & Endoscopy
Colonoscopy

Preferred investigation if no obstruction

Findings: Ulcerating or exophytic mucosal lesion that may narrow bowel lumen
CT Colonography

Alternative if colonoscopy contraindicated or incomplete

Findings: Similar to colonoscopy but less specific
CT Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis

Preoperative staging to detect metastases

Findings: Colonic wall thickening, enlarged lymph nodes, liver/lung metastases
MRI Pelvis (Rectal Cancer Protocol)

Local staging for rectal cancer

Findings: Tumour invasion of mesorectal fascia, perirectal nodal involvement
Genetic Testing
Lynch Syndrome Testing

Recommended for all patients diagnosed with colorectal cancer

RAS and BRAF Testing

Recommended for all patients with metastatic colorectal cancer

Emerging Tests
Advanced Optical Imaging

Narrow-band imaging and magnifying chromoendoscopy may reduce unnecessary surgical referrals

Blood-based Tests

Liquid biopsy (e.g., Septin 9) may be used for patients who refuse other screening methods

Circulating Tumour DNA

May predict recurrence after surgery and response to immune checkpoint inhibitors

Management Approach
Surgical Resection

Main curative treatment for localized disease

Multi-modality Treatment

Chemotherapy, radiotherapy, immunotherapy, resection of metastases

Neoadjuvant Therapy

For locally advanced rectal cancer

Adjuvant Therapy

For high-risk stage II and stage III disease

Palliative Care

For advanced/metastatic disease

Systemic Therapy Options
Chemotherapy
  • 5-FU based regimens
  • Oxaliplatin-based regimens
  • Irinotecan-based regimens
Targeted Therapy
  • Anti-EGFR (for KRAS/NRAS wild-type)
  • Anti-VEGF (bevacizumab)
  • BRAF inhibitors (for BRAF V600E mutation)
Immunotherapy
  • Pembrolizumab (for MSI-H/dMMR tumors)
  • Nivolumab ± ipilimumab (for MSI-H/dMMR tumors)
Emergency Management
Intestinal Obstruction

Immediate hospital admission required

Peritonitis

Surgical emergency

Significant Bleeding

May require urgent intervention

Follow-up & Monitoring
Post-treatment Surveillance
  • Clinical review: Every 3-6 months for 5 years
  • CEA monitoring: Every 3-6 months for 5 years
  • CT scan: Annually for 5 years (high-risk patients)
  • Colonoscopy: 1 year after surgery, then as indicated
Long-term Complications
  • Bowel dysfunction
  • Stoma-related issues
  • Chemotherapy-induced neuropathy
  • Radiation enteritis
Prevention Strategies
Primary Prevention
  • Healthy diet (high fiber, low red/processed meat)
  • Regular physical activity
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Limit alcohol and avoid smoking
  • Adequate vitamin D intake
Secondary Prevention
  • Screening for average-risk individuals starting at age 45
  • Colonoscopy every 10 years
  • Annual FIT
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years
  • CT colonography every 5 years
High-risk Screening
  • Family history: Start 10 years before youngest case
  • FAP: Annual sigmoidoscopy starting at age 10-12
  • Lynch syndrome: Colonoscopy every 1-2 years starting at age 20-25
  • IBD: Colonoscopy 8-10 years after diagnosis
