Overview & Definition

Definition

Involuntary passage of faeces that causes a social or hygienic problem.

Not a diagnosis, but a symptom

Prevalence
  • General Pop: ~8-12%
  • Care Homes: Up to 60%
  • Note: Liquid stool incontinence is 3x more common than solid.
Key Risk Factors
Demographics
  • Frail / Older age
  • Female (Obstetric injury)
  • Obesity
Medical
  • Neurological disease (MS, Stroke, Spina Bifida)
  • Diabetes Mellitus
  • Dementia / Cognitive Impairment
  • Urinary Incontinence
Surgical / Trauma
  • Childbirth (3rd/4th degree tear)
  • Pelvic radiotherapy
  • Anal/Rectal surgery
  • Colonic resection

Patient Assessment

Actively yet sensitively enquire. Do not assume symptoms are caused by existing conditions.

Anamnesis (History)
  • Pattern: Frequency, triggers, post-defecation soiling?
  • Quantity: Small leak vs complete evacuation?
  • Sensation: Urgency? No forewarning?
  • Stool: Consistency (Bristol Chart), bleeding, mucus?
  • Obstetric: Parity, difficult delivery, birth weight?
  • Impact: Social isolation, skin soreness?
Physical Exam
  • Abdominal: Masses, distension?
  • Anal Inspection: Soreness, excoriation, tags?
  • Perineal Descent: Assess prolapse
  • Digital Rectal Exam (DRE):
    • Anal tone
    • Voluntary squeeze
    • Faecal loading
    • Masses (strictures, haemorrhoids)
Identify Contributing Factors
  • Loose stools / Diarrhoea
  • Medication side effects
  • Limited toilet access
  • Pelvic organ prolapse
  • Rectal prolapse
  • Perianal pain
  • Lactose intolerance
  • Excessive caffeine/alcohol
  • Artificial sweeteners

Condition Criteria (Colorectal)
Colorectal Cancer
  • Age 40+ with unexplained weight loss AND abdominal pain
  • Age 50+ with unexplained rectal bleeding
  • Age 60+ with iron-deficiency anaemia OR change in bowel habit
  • Any age: Rectal/abdominal mass
Anal Cancer Unexplained anal mass or ulceration
Urgent Referral
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: If suspected (systemically unwell, bloody diarrhoea). Do NOT give anti-diarrhoeals
  • Multiple Sclerosis: Prompt referral to neurologist.
  • Acute Sphincter Injury: Obstetric/trauma (Urgency based on judgement).
Non-Urgent / Routine
  • Haemorrhoids: 4th degree, or large 3rd degree non-responsive to measures.
  • Persistent Symptoms: If initial management fails, refer for specialized management (Biofeedback, SNS, Surgery).

Initial Management

1. Address Underlying Cause
  • Review & modify exacerbating medications
  • Treat constipation/overflow (disimpaction)
  • Treat diarrhoea (e.g. infection, IBS)
  • Smoking cessation advice
2. Lifestyle & Conservative
  • Diet: Identify trigger foods (Spicy, dairy, caffeine, artificial sweeteners).
  • Fluids: Adjust intake based on stool consistency.
  • Toilet Access: Ensure ease of access, privacy, clothing adjustment.
  • Hygiene: Provide barrier creams/anal wipes.
3. Therapies
Bowel Retraining

Attempt to defecate at regular times, usually after a meal (gastrocolic reflex).

Pelvic Floor Muscle Training

Referral for physiotherapy if weakness identified. Essential for post-obstetric injury.

Practical Support

Offer choice of appropriate pads/products immediately to cope with symptoms during assessment.

Drug Safety & Prescribing

Drugs to Review (Culprits)

Can cause or exacerbate incontinence.

Type Examples
Alters Sphincter Tone Nitrates, CCBs, Beta-blockers, SSRIs, Sildenafil
Loose Stools Metformin, Orlistat, Laxatives, SSRIs
Constipation (Overflow) Opioids, Codeine, Digoxin, TCAs, Aluminium Antacids
Reduced Alertness Benzodiazepines, Antipsychotics, TCAs
Treatment Options

Use after underlying cause addressed.

Drug Use For Note
Loperamide Loose stools / Urgency First line
Codeine / Dihydrocodeine Loose stools Use with caution (constipation risk)
Laxatives Overflow (Impaction) Follow disimpaction protocol
