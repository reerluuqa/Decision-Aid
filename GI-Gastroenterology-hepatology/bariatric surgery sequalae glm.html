Quick Reference - Red Flags
Enteric Leak/Sepsis
Tachycardia >110-120 bpm, fever >38.5°C, respiratory distress, abdominal/back pain, anxiety
Pulmonary Embolism
Shortness of breath, chest pain, tachycardia, most common cause of death post-bariatric surgery
Compartment Syndrome
Intra-abdominal pressure >20 mmHg, oliguria, respiratory failure, requires surgical decompression
Prolonged Vomiting
Risk of thiamine deficiency and Wernicke's encephalopathy, avoid dextrose-containing IV fluids

Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass
High Malabsorption

Second most common bariatric procedure. Creates a small gastric pouch (15-30 mL) connected to jejunum, bypassing most of the stomach and duodenum.

Expected Weight Loss 63% of excess body weight (long-term)
Key Advantages Significant weight loss, metabolic improvements, resolution of type 2 diabetes
Special Considerations Gastric remnant and biliopancreatic limb inaccessible to standard endoscopy or ERCP
Sleeve Gastrectomy
Moderate Restriction

Most common bariatric procedure due to technical simplicity. Resects greater curvature of stomach, leaving a narrow, banana-shaped stomach.

Expected Weight Loss 58-62% of excess body weight (5-11 years)
Key Advantages No foreign body, no need for adjustment, endoscopic access to stomach remains
Special Considerations High incidence of new-onset GERD (8-30%), potential for sleeve dilation over time
Adjustable Gastric Band
Low Malabsorption

Least frequently performed bariatric operation. Device placed around stomach just distal to gastro-esophageal junction to restrict gastric volume.

Expected Weight Loss 49% of excess body weight (long-term)
Key Advantages Adjustable, reversible, less invasive
Special Considerations Device-related reoperations common, lower long-term weight loss than other procedures
Biliopancreatic Diversion (with/without Duodenal Switch)
High Malabsorption

Moderately restrictive but causes substantial malabsorption. Larger stomach pouch than RYGB with much greater bowel bypass.

Expected Weight Loss 73% of excess body weight (long-term)
Key Advantages Highest weight loss among bariatric procedures
Special Considerations Highest risk of nutritional deficiencies, biliopancreatic limb inaccessible to endoscopy
Early Complications (< 30 days)
Enteric Leak or Sepsis Emergency
  • Occurs in 2-3% of cases
  • May occur at proximal/distal anastomosis, staple line, or stomach
  • Abdominal pain, back/shoulder pain, anxiety
  • Tachycardia >110-120 bpm, fever >38.5°C
  • Respiratory distress, deviation from expected recovery
Emergency Action
  • Start broad-spectrum IV antibiotics immediately
  • Begin fluid resuscitation
  • CT scan with IV contrast (oral contrast if possible)
  • Surgical exploration if diagnosis uncertain
Deep Vein Thrombosis & Pulmonary Embolism Emergency
  • Most common cause of death post-bariatric surgery
  • 1-2% develop PE, mortality 0.2-0.6%
  • Shortness of breath, chest pain, tachycardia
  • Symptoms may overlap with enteric leak
Emergency Action
  • CT angiography for PE diagnosis
  • Doppler ultrasound for DVT diagnosis
  • Anticoagulation therapy
  • Consider IVC filter in selected cases
Gastrointestinal Hemorrhage Urgent
  • Occurs in 1-4% of cases
  • Upper GI: hematemesis from proximal anastomosis or staple line
  • Lower GI: bright red blood per rectum from distal anastomosis
  • Intraperitoneal bleeding possible from staple line or surgical injury
Management
  • Review medications, discontinue anticoagulants
  • Observe if stable and bleeding not excessive
  • Endoscopy and/or re-exploration if unstable or excessive bleeding
Compartment Syndrome Emergency
  • Intra-abdominal pressure >20 mmHg
  • Caused by abdominal sepsis, bleeding, or bowel obstruction
  • Oliguria and renal failure may be first manifestations
  • Respiratory failure due to diaphragmatic interference
Emergency Action
  • Measure bladder pressure via transducer
  • Pressure >25 mmHg requires surgical decompression
  • Optimize fluid balance, evacuate intraluminal contents
Late Complications (> 30 days)
Vomiting due to Stricture Urgent
  • Most common complication of RYGB (0.6-27%)
  • Symptoms appear 3-6 weeks post-surgery
  • Increasing intolerance of solids, then liquids
  • Associated dysphagia, nausea, vomiting
Management
  • Confirm with endoscopy
  • Endoscopic balloon dilation to 10-15 mm
  • Consider thiamine repletion with prolonged emesis
  • Multiple dilations often required
Internal Abdominal Hernia Emergency
  • Occurs in ~3% of cases, typically 3+ months post-surgery
  • Epigastric pain radiating to back
  • May be acute or chronic, mimics biliary colic
  • Patients may have multiple similar episodes
Emergency Action
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast
  • Note: imaging may be negative even with hernia
  • Definitive diagnosis through surgical exploration
  • Reduce bowel, resect necrotic intestine, close hernia spaces
Marginal Ulcer Urgent
  • Common source of pain/perforation after gastric bypass
  • Related to acid exposure or foreign bodies at anastomosis
  • Strongly associated with gastrogastric fistula
  • Risk factors: smoking, diabetes, peptic ulcer history
Management
  • Diagnose with upper endoscopy
  • Treat with acid reduction therapy
  • Remove suture material endoscopically if possible
  • Surgical revision if gastrogastric fistula present
Biliary Complications Urgent
  • ~30% experience biliary complications due to rapid weight loss
  • Chronic cholecystitis (70%), cholelithiasis (36%)
  • Acute cholecystitis (up to 18%), pancreatitis (up to 9%)
  • Higher incidence in laparoscopic RYGB vs sleeve gastrectomy
Management
  • Consider prophylactic ursodiol post-surgery
  • Cholecystectomy for symptomatic cholelithiasis
  • Routine intraoperative cholangiography during cholecystectomy
  • Evaluate for internal hernia during cholecystectomy
Metabolic and Nutritional Complications
Thiamine (Vitamin B₁) Deficiency Urgent
  • Risk with severe vomiting after bariatric surgery
  • Can lead to Wernicke's encephalopathy
  • Neurological symptoms: ataxia, confusion, blurred vision
Emergency Action
  • Avoid dextrose-containing IV fluids (may cause permanent injury)
  • Use normal saline or Ringer's lactate with thiamine
  • Add multivitamin supplementation
  • Immediate thiamine repletion without waiting for lab results
Protein Deficiency Urgent
  • Occurs after malabsorptive procedures bypassing large intestine portions
  • Incidence: 13% after RYGB, 18% after BPD-DS
  • Signs: edema, alopecia, asthenia
  • Labs: hypoalbuminemia, anemia
Management
  • Milder cases: increased oral protein intake
  • Severe cases: total parenteral nutrition
  • Surgical revision in refractory cases
Micronutrient Deficiencies Routine
  • Iron deficiency (up to 50% in bypass procedures)
  • Vitamin B₁₂ and folate deficiency
  • Vitamin D deficiency (very common)
  • Zinc deficiency (common after BPD-DS)
Supplementation Regimen
  • Multivitamin with iron twice daily
  • Calcium citrate with vitamin D
  • Iron polysaccharide complex (better tolerated)
  • Regular bloodwork to customize regimen
Bone and Mineral Metabolism Routine
  • Reduced bone density and negative impact on bone metabolism
  • Greater risk following malabsorptive procedures
  • Decreased femoral neck bone density in all procedures
  • Elevated bone turnover correlated with BMI change
Management
  • Close monitoring of calcium and vitamin D
  • Supplementation to prevent secondary hyperparathyroidism
  • Moderate sunlight exposure
  • Regular bone density assessment
