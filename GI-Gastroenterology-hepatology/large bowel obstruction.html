Large Bowel Obstruction

Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk stratification

Key Diagnostic Factors Urgent

  • Intermittent abdominal pain: Cramping, often severe, assess continuously for ischaemia
  • Abdominal distention: Tympanic abdomen along large bowel distribution
  • Nausea/vomiting: Less frequent than small bowel obstruction, may be faeculent in later stages
  • Absolute constipation: Sudden failure to pass flatus or stool
  • Continuous pain: May indicate bowel ischaemia or impending perforation

History Taking Essential

Onset & Duration

  • Abrupt onset: Suggests volvulus
  • Gradual onset: May indicate malignant process
  • Duration: Document timeline of symptom progression

Associated Symptoms

  • Fever: May indicate perforation, sepsis, or inflammatory cause
  • Weight loss: Suggests underlying malignancy
  • Rectal bleeding: May indicate rectal malignancy
  • Dietary changes: Low fibre, red meat consumption

Examination Findings

Abdominal Exam

  • Palpate for tender caecum (imminent perforation risk)
  • Assess distention and tympany
  • Check for rigidity (peritonitis)
  • Bowel sounds (hyperactive → absent)

Rectal Exam

  • Hard faeces: faecal impaction
  • Soft stools: partial obstruction
  • Empty rectum: proximal obstruction
  • Palpable mass: rectal cancer

Risk Factors Stratify

Strong Risk Factors

  • Current/previous malignancy
  • Diverticular disease
  • Colonic volvulus

Other Factors

  • Hernia (rare)
  • Gynaecological conditions
  • Previous abdominal surgery
  • Laxative abuse
RED FLAG: Palpable caecal tenderness indicates imminent perforation – requires immediate surgical evaluation

Diagnosis

Investigations and differential diagnosis

Imaging CT Required

Urgent CT abdomen & pelvis: Should be performed as soon as possible (ideally < 24 hours) to differentiate mechanical obstruction from pseudo-obstruction

CT Findings to Identify

  • Level and cause of obstruction
  • Evidence of perforation/dilatation
  • Bowel ischaemia signs
  • Closed-loop obstruction (surgical emergency)
  • Ileocaecal valve competency

Laboratory Investigations

Blood Tests

  • FBC: WBC >10,000 suggests perforation/necrosis
  • Electrolytes: Low potassium common
  • CRP: >75 mg/L indicates inflammation
  • Urea/Creatinine: Elevated suggests dehydration
  • Group & Save: If surgery anticipated

Special Tests

  • ABG: Elevated lactate indicates poor perfusion
  • Glucose: Rule out DKA (can cause abdominal pain)
  • β-hCG: In women of childbearing age
  • ECG: If >50 years or cardiac history

Differential Diagnoses

Condition Differentiating Features
Acute Colonic Pseudo-obstruction Impressive distension with minimal tenderness, associated with severe illness/post-op
Chronic Megacolon Long-standing constipation history, slow colonic transit
Toxic Megacolon Initial colitis, systemic sepsis signs
Key Point: CT is superior to plain radiography and can differentiate mechanical from functional obstruction while identifying complications

Treatment

Management strategies by cause

Initial Supportive Care Immediate

Resuscitation

  • Airway: Ensure patent airway
  • Breathing: Give oxygen if needed
  • Circulation: IV fluids for shock/dehydration
  • Disability: Assess consciousness
  • Exposure: Full examination

Interventions

  • NG Tube: Decompress distended small bowel
  • Catheter: Monitor urine output
  • Analgesia: Morphine sulfate 5-10mg Q4H
  • Nil by Mouth: Rest the bowel

Malignant Obstruction

Two main options: Emergency surgery OR stenting (bridge to surgery or palliative)

Stenting Criteria Selective

  • No signs of sepsis/ischaemia
  • No perforation/peritonitis
  • No closed-loop obstruction
  • Caecum < 9cm diameter
  • Left-sided lesions (better outcomes)

Surgical Options Emergency

  • Right Hemicolectomy: For caecal/ascending lesions
  • Hartmann's Procedure: Sigmoid resection + colostomy
  • Subtotal Colectomy: If caecum torn/unviable

Colonic Volvulus

Endoscopic Decompression First-line

  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy to untwist
  • 43-84% recurrence rate
  • Elective resection after successful decompression

Emergency Surgery If:

  • Perforation/peritonitis
  • Ischaemia suspected
  • Endoscopic decompression fails
  • Signs of sepsis

Benign Strictures & Diverticular Disease

Diverticular Disease - Medical Management

  • Admit for IV antibiotics & fluids
  • Oral antibiotics if mild disease (ambulatory care)
  • Stool softeners

Surgical Indications

  • Bleeding, abscess, perforation
  • Obstruction not resolving
  • Fistula formation
  • Failure of conservative management

Palliative Care Setting

Focus: Symptom control and quality of life

When Surgery NOT Indicated

  • Diffuse intra-abdominal cancer
  • Multiple palpable masses
  • Massive ascites
  • Poor performance status
  • Previous abdominal radiotherapy

Conservative Management

  • Nil by mouth
  • IV/SC hydration
  • NG tube for vomiting
  • Laxatives (avoid stimulants if colic)
  • Fentanyl patch for pain (less constipating)

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications & prognosis

Monitoring

Malignant Obstruction

  • Clinical & CT surveillance for 5 years
  • Colonoscopy to exclude synchronous/metachronous lesions
  • Regular follow-up with colorectal team

Volvulus/Strictures

  • No formal monitoring if colon confirmed clear
  • Educate on recurrence symptoms
  • High-fibre diet for sigmoid volvulus prevention

Complications High Risk

Complication Timeframe Likelihood
Bowel Perforation Short-term High
Most common in caecum (thinnest wall). Tenderness over caecum = imminent perforation.
Sepsis Short-term High
Due to bacterial translocation. Follows perforation without surgical intervention.

Prognosis

Colonic Volvulus

  • Mortality: 6-9% post-operative
  • Recurrence: 43-84% without surgery
  • Hartmann's procedure mortality: up to 57%

Colorectal Malignancy

  • Reduced 5-year survival with obstruction
  • High morbidity & mortality
  • Emergency surgery has higher risk than elective
Key Message: Early diagnosis and appropriate management within 24 hours significantly improves outcomes. Multidisciplinary team input is essential.
