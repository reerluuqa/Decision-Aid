Assessment

Initial evaluation and diagnostic factors

Key Diagnostic Factors
  • Urge to move legs ‚Äì Usually accompanied by uncomfortable sensations (dysaesthesias)
  • Worsens with rest ‚Äì Begins or worsens during periods of rest or inactivity
  • Relieved by movement ‚Äì Partially or totally relieved by movement, stretching, or massage
  • Evening/night predominance ‚Äì Occurs exclusively or predominantly in the evening/night
  • Location ‚Äì Primarily lower extremities; may involve upper extremities/torso in severe cases
    • Risk Factors & Triggers
    Iron Deficiency Pregnancy Family History Certain Medications Female Sex

    Medications that worsen RLS:

    • Antidepressants (except bupropion) - Caution
    • Sedating antihistamines - Caution
    • Antipsychotics - Caution
    • Metoclopramide - Caution
    • Caffeine/Alcohol - Minimize
    Physical Examination & History
    Focus areas: Review medications, caffeine/alcohol intake, sleep patterns, family history, symptoms of iron deficiency, pregnancy status, and co-existing sleep disorders (especially obstructive sleep apnea).

    Diagnosis

    Confirm RLS using established criteria

    IRLSSG Diagnostic Criteria

    All 5 criteria must be met:

  • An urge to move legs, usually with unpleasant sensations
  • Symptoms begin/worsen during periods of rest/inactivity
  • Symptoms are partially/totally relieved by movement
  • Symptoms occur/worsen in the evening/night
  • Not accounted for by another medical/behavioral condition
    • Clinical Diagnosis
    ICSD-3-TR Criteria

    Criteria A-C must be met:

  • A: Urge to move legs with unpleasant sensations that:
    • Begin/worsen during rest/inactivity
    • Are relieved by movement
    • Occur predominantly in evening/night
  • B: Not better explained by mimics (leg cramps, arthritis, etc.)
  • C: Cause distress, sleep disturbance, or functional impairment
    • Severity Assessment

    IRLSSG Rating Scale (0-40):

    Mild: 1-10 Moderate: 11-20 Severe: 21-30 Very Severe: 31-40

    Johns Hopkins Severity Scale:

    • 0 = No symptoms
    • 1 = Bedtime only (within 60 min before bedtime)
    • 2 = Evening and bedtime (starting ‚â•6 p.m.)
    • 3 = Day and night (before 6 p.m. or all day)
    Differential Diagnosis
    Condition Differentiating Features
    Periodic Limb Movement Disorder Movements during sleep only; patient unaware; no urge to move
    Akathisia Mental/CNS restlessness; no circadian pattern
    Peripheral Neuropathy Numbness, impaired balance; EMG/NCS abnormal
    Nocturnal Leg Cramps Muscle hardening present
    Investigations
    Mandatory: Iron studies in ALL patients
    • Serum iron, ferritin, TIBC, TSAT
    • Measure in early morning after overnight fast
    • TSAT >45% ‚Üí No iron treatment (risk of overload)

    Further work-up: If ferritin <112 pmol/L (<50 ng/mL), investigate cause of iron deficiency (GI bleeding, malabsorption, etc.)

    Treatment

    Evidence-based management pathways

    General Approach to All Patients
    Lifestyle Modifications Iron Assessment Review Medications

    Lifestyle Interventions:

    • Massage, exercise, stretching, warm baths before bedtime
    • Mental alerting activities (video games, puzzles) during boredom
    • Minimize caffeine/alcohol, especially near bedtime
    • Treat co-existing sleep disorders (OSA, depression, anxiety)

    Iron Supplementation Protocol:

    Parameter Action
    Ferritin ‚â§169 pmol/L (‚â§75 ng/mL) or TSAT <20% Oral or IV iron replacement
    Ferritin 169-225 pmol/L (75-100 ng/mL) IV iron replacement
    Ferritin >225 pmol/L (>100 ng/mL) Stop treatment (if no ongoing deficiency)

    Recheck ferritin at 3-4 months, then every 3-6 months

    Intermittent RLS (Non-Pregnant)

    1st Line: Lifestyle Modifications

    For symptoms not frequent enough for daily pharmacotherapy

    2nd Line: Carbidopa/Levodopa (On-Demand)

    Dose: 12.5/50 mg to 50/200 mg IR at bedtime when required

    Extended-release: 25/100 mg to 50/200 mg for symptoms waking patient during night

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations: Use intermittently only; risk of augmentation and impulse control disorders

    3rd Line: Low-Potency Opioid or Benzodiazepine Receptor Agonist

    • Codeine: 30 mg (max 180 mg/day)
    • Tramadol: 50 mg IR (max 200 mg/day) or 100 mg ER
    • Zolpidem: 5-10 mg at bedtime (lower in women/older adults)
    • Temazepam: 7.5-30 mg at bedtime
    Caution: Abuse potential
    Chronic Persistent RLS (Non-Pregnant)

    1st Line: Gabapentinoid

    Gabapentin Enacarbil (FDA-approved): 600 mg ER once daily

    Alternative: Gabapentin or Pregabalin (dose per specialist)

    First-line pharmacotherapy
    Benefits patients with comorbid insomnia, anxiety, or chronic pain

    2nd Line: Dopamine Agonist

    If gabapentinoid contraindicated/not tolerated

    • Pramipexole: 0.125-0.5 mg at night
    • Ropinirole: 0.25-4 mg at night
    • Rotigotine patch: 1-3 mg/24 hours
    ‚ö†Ô∏è High augmentation risk: 40-70% over 10 years with pramipexole/ropinirole; use lowest possible dose

    3rd Line: Opioid

    For moderate to severe RLS or significant augmentation

    • Codeine: Start 30 mg, max 180 mg/day
    • Tramadol: Start 50 mg, max 200 mg/day
    Screen for abuse risk; sign opioid contract
    Refractory RLS (Non-Pregnant)
    Definition: Unresponsive to monotherapy with tolerable doses of gabapentinoid or dopamine agonist due to reduced efficacy, augmentation, or adverse effects
    Refer to Specialist

    Management:

    1st: Optimize Iron Stores

    IV iron if ferritin 169-225 pmol/L or TSAT <20%

    2nd: Combination Therapy

    Add second agent from different class; reduce dose of initial drug

    • Gabapentinoid + Dopamine agonist
    • Add opioid if severe

    3rd: Opioid Monotherapy

    Low-dose opioid (codeine or tramadol) monotherapy in screened patients

    Long-acting/extended-release preferred

    Very effective for severe, refractory cases; dose escalation uncommon when used appropriately

    Pregnancy & Lactation

    1st Line: Lifestyle + Iron Supplementation

    Primary management for most pregnant patients

    • Moderate exercise, yoga, massage, compression devices
    • Oral iron (avoid vitamin C due to debated safety)
    • Treat OSA, avoid aggravating factors

    2nd Line: Low-Dose Clonazepam

    Second/third trimesters for severe cases

    Avoid first trimester if possible; use lowest effective dose for shortest duration

    3rd Line: Low-Dose Oxycodone

    For severe, refractory symptoms in 2nd/3rd trimesters

    Neonate monitoring for withdrawal

    Lactation:

    • Options: Low-dose clonazepam, gabapentin, tramadol (severe cases)
    • Avoid: Levodopa/carbidopa and dopamine agonists (inhibit prolactin)
    Special Populations: Renal Disease

    Prevalence: ~30% of ESRD patients; ~20% of hemodialysis patients discontinue dialysis prematurely due to RLS

    Management:
    • IV iron or erythropoietin if indicated
    • Dose adjustments needed (renal excretion of gabapentinoids and most dopamine agonists)
    • Transdermal rotigotine is metabolized hepatically - no dose adjustment needed
    • RLS often improves after kidney transplant

    Follow-up

    Monitoring and long-term management

    Monitoring Parameters
    Parameter Frequency Notes
    Iron studies (ferritin, TSAT) Every 3-6 months Until ferritin >225 pmol/L; restart if symptoms worsen
    Symptom severity (IRLSSG scale) Each visit Assess treatment efficacy
    Augmentation signs Each visit Watch for symptom worsening, earlier onset, spreading
    Impulse control disorders Each visit Screen if on dopamine agonists
    Complications

    Initiation & Maintenance Insomnia

    Likelihood: High

    RLS causes insomnia ‚Üí daytime sleepiness ‚Üí worsens RLS (positive feedback loop)

    Management: Treat RLS urgently; chronic insomnia may persist despite RLS control and require separate treatment

    Augmentation (Drug-Induced Worsening)

    • Definition: Increase in symptom severity, earlier onset, shorter latency at rest, or spread to other body parts
    • Incidence: 40-70% with pramipexole/ropinirole over 10 years; 36% with rotigotine patch over 5 years
    • Risk factors: High dose, long duration, low ferritin, early onset RLS
    • Management: Switch to rotigotine, or add gabapentinoid/opioid with dopamine taper
    Prognosis

    Primary RLS

    Tends to progress in severity and frequency with age

    May lead to chronic insomnia and significant quality of life impairment

    Life expectancy normal

    Secondary RLS

    Often resolves after treating underlying cause:

    • Iron supplementation for iron deficiency
    • Post-delivery resolution in pregnancy
    • Kidney transplant for uremic RLS
    • Discontinuation of offending medications
    Patient Education & Resources

    Key Counseling Points:

    • Avoid caffeine/alcohol near bedtime
    • Regular sleep schedule
    • Moderate exercise (avoid overexertion)
    • Mental alerting activities during periods of inactivity
    • Importance of iron supplementation if deficient
    • Risks of augmentation with dopaminergic agents
    • Screen for impulse control disorders
