SUICIDE RISK ASSESSMENT

800,000 people die by suicide annually. Depression is the most common underlying condition. Always take suicidal ideation seriously.

Immediate Red Flags

  • Active suicidal ideation with plan
  • Recent suicide attempt
  • Statement of intent or preparation
  • Access to lethal means
  • Psychotic symptoms with depression
  • Catatonic depression

Immediate Actions Required

Do NOT leave patient unattended
  • 1. Assess risk level using validated tool (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale)
  • 2. Remove access to lethal means (medications, weapons, etc.)
  • 3. Consider emergency psychiatric evaluation / involuntary hold if high risk
  • 4. Notify crisis team / psychiatric liaison
  • 5. Document all findings and safety plan

Definition

Depression is a mental state characterized by persistent low mood, loss of interest and enjoyment in everyday activities, neurovegetative symptoms, and reduced energy, causing varying levels of social and occupational dysfunction.

Note: Mood may present as anxious or irritable rather than sad, especially in children and adolescents.

Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR)

Major Depressive Episode: 5+ symptoms present during same 2-week period, representing a change from previous functioning.

Depressed mood Anhedonia Weight change Sleep disturbance Psychomotor changes Fatigue Guilt/Worthlessness Concentration Suicidal ideation

Symptom Assessment Checklist

Core Depressive Symptoms

  • Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day
  • Markedly diminished interest or pleasure (anhedonia)
  • Significant weight loss or gain / appetite change
  • Insomnia or hypersomnia
  • Psychomotor agitation or retardation
  • Fatigue or loss of energy

Additional Symptoms

  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Diminished ability to think or concentrate
  • Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide
  • Libido changes
  • Social withdrawal / impaired relationships
  • Impaired school or work performance

Severity Assessment

Depression Classifications

Major Depressive Disorder

Single episode or recurrent. Characterized by persistent low mood, anhedonia, and neurovegetative symptoms causing significant impairment.

Common Any Age

Persistent Depressive Disorder

Chronic depression lasting ≥2 years (1 year in adults). Includes chronic MDD and dysthymia. Wide range of presentations and severity.

Chronic ≥2 Years

Bipolar Disorder

Recurrent episodes of mania/hypomania AND depression. Bipolar I requires ≥1 manic episode. Bipolar II has hypomania + major depression.

2.5% Prevalence Screen Required

Postnatal Depression

Depressive episode within 4 weeks of childbirth (perinatal specifier). May include guilt, ambivalent feelings toward infant, impaired bonding.

0-60% Postpartum

Seasonal Affective Disorder

Seasonal pattern with onset autumn/winter, remission spring/summer. Atypical symptoms: hypersomnia, hyperphagia, weight gain.

0.4-9.7% Winter Onset

Disruptive Mood Dysregulation

Severe recurrent temper outbursts + persistent irritable mood. Onset age 6-18, before age 10. Distinguish from ADHD/ODD.

Children Ages 6-18

Risk Factors by Population

Adults
  • Older age
  • Personal or family history of depression
  • Recent stress or trauma
  • Co-existing medical conditions (diabetes, cancer, stroke, MI, obesity)
  • Certain medications (corticosteroids)
  • Female sex
Children & Adolescents
  • Positive family history of depression
  • Other parental psychopathology
  • History of stressful life events or trauma
  • Female sex (especially post-puberty)
  • Comorbid psychiatric disorders
  • Chronic medical illnesses
  • Neighbourhood/social instability
  • Poverty
Perinatal
  • Recent childbirth (within 4 weeks)
  • Personal or family history of perinatal depression
  • Psychological, social, and biological factors
  • Limited social support
  • Complications during pregnancy or delivery
  • History of mood disorders
Bipolar Disorder Risk
  • Early age of mood disorder onset
  • Family history of bipolar/suicide
  • Poor response to antidepressants
  • Highly recurrent mood episodes
  • Comorbid anxiety or substance misuse
  • Pattern of psychosocial instability

Pharmacological Treatments

First-Line Antidepressants
  • • SSRIs (Sertraline, Escitalopram, Fluoxetine)
  • • SNRIs (Venlafaxine, Duloxetine)
  • • Mirtazapine (for insomnia/weight loss)
Monitor for Suicide Risk

Increased suicide risk in first weeks of treatment, especially in younger patients. Monitor closely.

Bipolar Depression
  • • Mood stabilizers (Lithium, Valproate)
  • • Atypical antipsychotics (Quetiapine, Lurasidone)
  • • Avoid antidepressants as monotherapy

Psychological Treatments

Evidence-Based Therapies
  • • CBT - Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
  • • IPT - Interpersonal Therapy
  • • BA - Behavioral Activation
  • • ACT - Acceptance & Commitment Therapy
  • • Psychodynamic therapy
Combination Therapy

Combination of medication and psychotherapy shows superior outcomes to either treatment alone.

Lifestyle & Support
  • • Regular physical activity
  • • Sleep hygiene optimization
  • • Social support engagement
  • • Light therapy (SAD)
  • • Stress reduction techniques
Critical Prescribing Notes

Antidepressant discontinuation syndrome: Taper gradually over ≥4 weeks.
Drug interactions: Check for serotonergic interactions (especially SSRIs + SNRIs + tramadol/triptans).
Pregnancy: Weigh risks vs benefits; consult local formulary.
Bipolar screening: Always screen for bipolar disorder before initiating antidepressants.
