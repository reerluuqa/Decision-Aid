Functional Neurological & Somatic Disorders

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference (BMJ Best Practice)

üí° Key Concept: Rule-In, Not Exclusion

FND is diagnosed on the basis of positive 'rule in' features on neurological examination. It is no longer a diagnosis of exclusion. Look for incongruency and inconsistency.

Functional Limb Weakness

Positive Rule-In Signs:

  • Hoover's Sign: Weakness of hip extension returns to normal during contralateral hip flexion against resistance.
  • Hip Abductor Sign: Weakness of unilateral hip abduction returns to normal with attempt at bilateral hip abduction.
  • Give-way weakness: Sudden collapse after several seconds of full resistance.

Functional Tremor

Entrainment Test:

  • Ask patient to copy rhythmic movements (e.g., tapping thumb/finger) with one hand.
  • Positive if: Tremor in the other hand stops, entrains to the same rhythm, or patient cannot copy the movement.
  • Look for variability in amplitude/frequency and distractibility.

Functional Seizures (PNES)

Suggestive Features:

  • Prolonged duration: Typically > 2 minutes, often > 5 minutes.
  • Eyes: Tightly closed (vs. open in epilepsy).
  • Motor: Side-to-side head shaking, pelvic thrusting, asynchronous limb movements (stop/start quality).
  • Other: Tearfulness, hyperventilation, recall of events (variable).

Visual & Sensory Signs

Functional Visual Loss:

  • Tubular Vision: Visual field at 150cm is same width as at 50cm (should conically increase).
  • Spiraling: Field becomes more constricted the longer Goldmann perimetry continues.

Sensory:

  • Sharp demarcation (midline split), non-anatomical distribution.

üß∞ Core Treatments

Physiotherapy + CBT are the mainstays.
  • Physiotherapy: Retraining movement patterns. Essential for motor FND.
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy: Addresses unhelpful illness beliefs and emotional processing.
  • Comorbidity Management: Treat depression, anxiety, or PTSD if present (benefits overall recovery).

üë• Epidemiology

  • Prevalence (FND): ~10% of neurology outpatients.
  • Incidence: 4-12 per 100,000 (US/Europe).
  • Sex: Strong female predominance (~75%).
  • Comorbidity: Often co-exists with depression, anxiety, or PTSD.

üß† Aetiology & Pathophysiology

Cause: Not fully understood. Not purely "psychological."

Mechanisms:

  • Abnormal connectivity between limbic (emotion) system and motor networks.
  • Disruption in neural circuits linking volition, movement, and perception.
  • Increased selective/biased attention to symptoms.
  • Somatic Symptom Disorder: Heightened awareness of bodily sensations interpreted as illness.
