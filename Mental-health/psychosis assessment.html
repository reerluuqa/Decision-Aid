EMERGENCY: Acute Psychosis & Safety

Acute Agitation Management

First-Line Pharmacological Options

  • Benzodiazepines: Lorazepam 1-2mg PO/IM (may repeat q30-60min)
  • Antipsychotics: Haloperidol 2-5mg IM (max 10mg/day)
  • Combination: Often used together for rapid tranquilization

Key Safety Considerations

  • Command hallucinations directing harm
  • Poor insight with impaired judgment
  • History of violence or aggression
  • Inability to contract for safety

Hospitalization Criteria

Immediate admission required if: danger to self/others, grave functional impairment, or patient cannot guarantee safety. Involuntary admission may be necessary based on local laws.

Suicide & Homicide Risk Assessment

üö® Red Flag Indicators

  • Command hallucinations (especially violent)
  • Active suicidal ideation with plan
  • History of prior attempts
  • Access to means
  • Command homicidal ideation
  • Acute intoxication combined with psychosis

‚úì Protective Factors

  • Future-oriented thinking
  • Strong social support
  • Reason to live (children, family, religion)
  • Previous treatment success
  • Ability to contract for safety

Critical Statistic

~20% of patients with schizophrenia attempt suicide; ~10% die by suicide. This is significantly higher than general population. Always ask directly about suicidal thoughts.

Delirium with Psychosis - Medical Emergency

Delirium is a medical emergency with high morbidity/mortality. Distinguish from primary psychosis:

Features Suggesting Delirium

  • Fluctuating level of consciousness
  • Acute/subacute onset (hours to days)
  • Disorientation to time/place
  • Poor attention and memory
  • Visual hallucinations common
  • Sleep-wake cycle disruption

Common Causes (SEARCH)

  • Sepsis, Sleep deprivation
  • Electrolytes, Encephalitis
  • Alcohol withdrawal, Adrenergic drugs
  • Respiratory failure, Renal failure
  • Cardiac, Constipation, Codeine/meds
  • Hypoglycemia, Hypoxia, Hyperthermia

Schizophrenia (DSM-5-TR)

Diagnostic Criteria

Two or more of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period (at least 1 must be delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech):

  • Delusions
  • Hallucinations
  • Disorganized speech
  • Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  • Negative symptoms (diminished emotional expression, avolition)

Duration Requirements

  • Active Phase: ‚â•1 month of symptoms
  • Total Episode: ‚â•6 months of continuous signs
  • Functional Decline: Significant disturbance in work, relations, or self-care

Exclusion Criteria

  • Not attributable to substance/medical condition
  • Rule out schizophrenia spectrum with mood disorder features
Bipolar I Disorder - Manic Episode Criteria

A. Distinct Period of Abnormally & Persistently Elevated/Expansive/Iritable Mood (‚â•1 week)

B. During mood disturbance, ‚â•3 of following (4 if only irritable):

  • Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity
  • Decreased need for sleep
  • Pressured speech
  • Flight of ideas/racing thoughts
  • Distractibility
  • Increased goal-directed activity or agitation
  • Excessive risky behavior

Key Distinction

Bipolar I: Requires ‚â•1 manic episode (may have hypomanic/depressive episodes). Bipolar II: No full manic episode; ‚â•1 hypomanic + ‚â•1 major depressive episode.

Schizoaffective Disorder

An uninterrupted period of illness during which there is:

Concurrent Mood + Psychotic Episode

  • Major mood episode (manic OR depressive)
  • PLUS ‚â•2 psychotic symptoms:
    • Delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, disorganized/catatonic behavior, OR negative symptoms

Critical Distinguishing Feature

  • ‚â•2 weeks psychosis WITHOUT prominent mood symptoms (during total illness course)
  • Mood symptoms present for majority of total illness duration

RULE-OUT: Medical & Toxic Causes

Medical Conditions Causing Psychosis

üß† Neurological

  • Epilepsy (8x increased risk)
  • Traumatic brain injury
  • Brain tumor
  • Multiple sclerosis (~5%)
  • Stroke (~7% new onset)
  • Dementia
  • CNS infections
  • Wilson's disease

ü¶† Infectious

  • Encephalitis (HSV, autoimmune)
  • HIV
  • Neurosyphilis
  • Meningitis
  • Prion diseases (CJD)
  • Neurocysticercosis

‚öóÔ∏è Metabolic/Endocrine

  • Thyroid dysfunction
  • Hyperparathyroidism
  • Cushing's syndrome
  • Acute hepatic porphyria
  • Hypo/hyperglycemia
  • Electrolyte imbalance

üíä Nutritional Deficiencies

  • Vitamin B12 - psychiatric + neuro symptoms
  • Thiamine (B1) - Korsakoff's psychosis
  • Folate - commonly alcohol-related
  • Niacin (B3) - pellagra with dermatitis

üî¨ Autoimmune

  • Autoimmune encephalitis (NMDAR, LGI1)
  • Lupus cerebritis (~3% with psychosis)
  • Anti-psychotic antibodies
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes
Substance-Induced Psychosis

üö´ Recreational Drugs (Common)

  • Cannabis - dose-related risk (2-6x), early use increases risk
  • Amphetamines - stimulant psychosis
  • Cocaine - paranoia, tactile hallucinations
  • Phencyclidine (PCP) - severe psychosis
  • Alcohol - withdrawal or chronic use
  • Hallucinogens - LSD, psilocybin

üíä Prescription/OTC (Less Common)

  • Anticholinergics
  • Dopamine agonists
  • Corticosteroids
  • Stimulants
  • Antihistamines
  • Dextromethorphan
  • Thyroid hormones

Heavy Metals & Toxins

Consider organophosphate exposure (pesticides), lead, mercury, or arsenic poisoning if occupational exposure or unusual presentation. Organophosphates: look for cholinergic crisis (DUMBBELSS).

"Organic" Features - Consider Medical Cause

Features suggesting secondary (organic) rather than primary psychosis:

  • NEW onset at age >40
  • Visual hallucinations (vs. auditory in primary)
  • Fluctuating consciousness or attention
  • Disorientation to time/place/person
  • Fever, rash, or systemic signs
  • Focal neurological deficits
  • New headaches or seizure activity
  • Rapid progression (days-weeks)
  • Severe cognitive impairment
  • Abnormal vital signs
  • History of head trauma
  • Known substance use
  • Recent medication changes
  • Occupational exposures
  • Travel history (infectious)
  • Dietary restrictions/vegan
  • Family history of neurological disease
  • Acute medical illness
  • No prior psychiatric history
  • Poor response to antipsychotics

INITIAL WORKUP & MANAGEMENT

Initial Laboratory Workup (ALL Patients)

üî¨ Essential Labs

  • Complete Blood Count (CBC)
  • Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (glucose, electrolytes, kidney function)
  • Liver Function Tests (including GGT)
  • Thyroid Function Tests (TSH, free T4)
  • Urgent glucose (rule out hypo/hyperglycemia)

üß™ Additional Essential Tests

  • Urine Drug Screen (amphetamines, benzodiazepines, cocaine, cannabis, opioids, PCP)
  • Calcium and Parathyroid Hormone
  • Vitamin B12 and Folate levels
  • Thiamine and Niacin levels
  • HIV and Syphilis serology
Neuroimaging Indications

Brain Imaging Indications (MRI preferred, CT if MRI unavailable)

URGENT imaging for: new onset psychosis with headache, focal neuro signs, head trauma, or age >50. Routine imaging in first-episode psychosis is controversial but increasingly recommended.

Indications for Brain Imaging:

  • History of head trauma
  • Focal neurological signs on exam
  • New, severe, or worsening headache
  • Atypical psychotic symptoms (especially visual hallucinations)
  • Late age of onset (>40 years)
  • Seizure activity
  • Cognitive deficits suggesting dementia
  • Signs of raised intracranial pressure
Additional Tests (Based on Clinical Suspicion)

When Suspicious

  • Autoimmune: ANA, ESR, specific antibodies
  • Wilson's: Ceruloplasmin, 24hr urine copper, slit-lamp (KF rings)
  • Porphyria: Urine PBG during attack, 24hr urine porphyrins
  • Cushing's: 24hr urinary free cortisol, dexamethasone suppression test

Additional Testing

  • ECG: Before starting QT-prolonging antipsychotics
  • EEG: If temporal lobe epilepsy suspected
  • LP: If encephalitis suspected, after imaging rules out mass lesion
  • Chest imaging: If brain metastases or thymoma suspected

If Malnourished

  • Pellagra (niacin deficiency)
  • Korsakoff's (thiamine deficiency)
  • Folate/B12 deficiency
  • Consider erythrocyte transketolase
Complete Assessment Checklist

History Taking

  • Onset and course of symptoms
  • History of head trauma
  • Seizure history/timing
  • Neurological symptoms (headache, weakness, vision, speech)
  • Recreational drug use (alcohol, cannabis, stimulants, etc.)
  • Prescription and OTC medications
  • Occupational/chemical exposures
  • Dietary history (vegan, eating disorders)
  • Family history (psychiatric, neurological, metabolic)
  • Travel history

Physical & Mental State Exam

  • Vital signs (including temperature)
  • Level of consciousness (fluctuating?)
  • Neurological exam (focal signs, gait, movement)
  • Signs of malnutrition/deficiency
  • Thyroid/endocrine signs
  • Mental status: mood, affect, thought process
  • Thought content (delusions, suicidal/homicidal ideation)
  • Insight and judgment
  • Cognitive screening
  • Collateral history (family/carer)
