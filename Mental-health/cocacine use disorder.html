Cocaine Use Disorder

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Emergency Assessment

URGENT: Immediate evaluation required

Assess for signs of cocaine intoxication requiring emergency management:

  • Loss of consciousness or altered mental status
  • Chest pain or cardiovascular symptoms
  • Focal neurological complaints (weakness, aphasia, visual changes)
  • Seizure activity
  • Severe agitation or psychosis
  • Hyperthermia (temperature >40°C)
1

If acute intoxication present:

Immediate emergency department referral for acute management

Route-Specific Findings:

  • Intranasal: Sinusitis, nasal septum perforation, epistaxis
  • Smoking: Oral burns, blisters, respiratory issues ("crack lung")
  • Injection: Track marks, skin infections, abscesses
  • Levamisole-contaminated: Skin necrosis, agranulocytosis, ear lobe necrosis

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors:

Adverse childhood events (abuse, neglect, family conflict)
Family history of substance use disorders
Polysubstance use
Co-occurring mental health disorders

Other Factors:

  • Male sex: Higher prevalence in males (3995 vs 1845 per 100,000)
  • Genetic predisposition: Polymorphisms affecting dopamine metabolism
  • Environmental: Social marginalization, peer influence
  • Route of administration: Smoking/injection increases addiction risk

Diagnosis

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria

Requires ≥2 criteria in 12 months

Severity classification:

Mild

2-3 symptoms present

Moderate

4-5 symptoms present

Severe

≥6 symptoms present

Diagnostic Criteria Checklist:

  • Using larger amounts or over longer period than intended
  • Persistent desire or unsuccessful attempts to cut down
  • Great deal of time spent obtaining, using, or recovering from cocaine
  • Craving or strong desire to use
  • Failure to fulfill major role obligations due to use
  • Continued use despite social/interpersonal problems
  • Reduction of important activities due to use
  • Recurrent use in hazardous situations
  • Continued use despite physical/psychological problems
  • Tolerance (need for increased amounts)
  • Withdrawal symptoms or use to relieve withdrawal

Remission Specifiers:

  • Early Remission: No criteria met for 3-12 months (except craving)
  • Sustained Remission: No criteria met for ≥12 months (except craving)

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Distinguishing Features Differentiating Tests
Amphetamine/Methamphetamine Use Longer-lasting effects (days vs hours), dental erosion, skin lesions Urine toxicology for amphetamines
Thyrotoxicosis Weight loss prominent, thyroid enlargement, bruit TFTs: ↑T4, ↓TSH
Mood Disorder Similar symptoms but persistent regardless of substance use Clinical interview, PRIME-MD, urine screen negative
Psychotic Disorder Psychosis unrelated to cocaine use episodes History, urine toxicology negative
Personality Disorder Long-standing pattern, may be comorbid Clinical assessment over time
Synthetic Cannabinoids/Cathinones Similar symptoms, not detected by standard urine screens TOF spectroscopy (specialized centers)

Screening Tools

Healthcare Settings:

  • USPSTF Recommendation: Screen adults ≥18 years when treatment services available
  • UK - ASSIST-Lite: Recommended for substance use screening in adults
  • Trauma Centers: Bedside screening for high-risk trauma patients

Special Populations:

  • Pregnancy: Ask about substance use at all antenatal visits
  • Self-report: Accurate compared with urine toxicology in pregnant women
  • Workplace: Point-of-care immunoassay tests (urine/saliva)
Self-report screening is reliable

Treatment

General Treatment Approach

Acute intoxication requires emergency management first

Core Principles:

  • Create welcoming, non-judgmental, trauma-sensitive environment
  • Don't require abstinence as condition for treatment initiation
  • Proactively engage patients at all stages of readiness for change
  • Address co-occurring mental and physical health conditions
  • Develop individualized treatment plans responsive to patient needs
  • Focus on therapeutic strategies over punitive measures

Psychosocial treatments are primary options - Pharmacotherapy has limited efficacy but may be considered in certain cases.

Psychosocial Interventions

Contingency Management (CM)

Strong evidence for efficacy
  • Operant behavioral techniques (voucher-based reinforcement)
  • Rewards achievement of therapeutic goals
  • Increases abstinence period and reduces drug intake
  • Large body of evidence for stimulant use disorders

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

  • Recognition of triggers and teaching coping skills
  • Mixed results in trials but skills may persist after treatment
  • More effective for severe symptoms or comorbid depression
  • Computerized CBT as effective as traditional CBT

Motivational Interviewing

  • Patient-centered counseling approach
  • Increases motivation for behavior change
  • Effective compared to no treatment
  • Less effective compared to other active interventions

Pharmacotherapy Options

Emerging evidence for adjunctive medications (limited efficacy):

Medication Evidence Best for
Bupropion More efficacious than placebo Co-occurring nicotine/tobacco use or depression
Topiramate Some evidence from systematic review Co-occurring alcohol use disorder
Modafinil Mixed results Patients without alcohol use disorder
Amfetamines (extended-release) Systematic review supports efficacy Co-occurring ADHD or alcohol use disorder
Disulfiram No effect in systematic review Not recommended for routine use
Symptomatic pharmacotherapy (benzodiazepines, antipsychotics) may be warranted for severe distress

Potential Complications

Complication Timeframe Likelihood Management
Cocaine Toxicity Short-term Medium Emergency management, aggressive sedation
Hyperactive Delirium Short-term Medium Cooling, antipsychotics (monitor QT)
Cocaine-Induced Psychosis Short-term Medium Antipsychotics, supportive care
Hemorrhagic Stroke Short-term Low Standard stroke management
Acute MI Variable Low Avoid beta-blockers, standard MI care
Acute Kidney Injury Variable Medium Supportive, treat underlying cause
Hepatitis/HIV Variable Medium Screening, treatment, prevention

Chronic Complications:

  • Neurocognitive impairment: Executive dysfunction, memory deficits
  • Cardiomyopathy: LV hypertrophy, arrhythmias
  • Pulmonary: Fibrosis, emphysematous changes ("crack lung")
  • Gastrointestinal: Ulcers, perforation, ischemia
  • Dermatologic: Chronic skin ulcers, necrotizing fasciitis (rare)
  • Psychiatric: Persistent mood disorders, increased suicide risk

Prognosis

Withdrawal Management:

Cocaine withdrawal does not require medically supervised withdrawal
  • Many patients can be safely discharged with drug counseling referral
  • Symptoms: dysphoria, fatigue, vivid dreams, increased appetite, bradycardia

Treatment Outcomes:

50%

Partial Treatment Completion

In England 2022-2023, ~50% did not complete treatment successfully

Risk Factors

Predictors of Relapse

Younger age, frequent recent use, longer duration of use

90+ days

Treatment Duration

Participation >90 days associated with greater improvement

Long-term Outlook:

  • High rates of relapse are common
  • Continuing care improves outcomes, especially with family/social issues
  • Mutual support groups helpful for maintenance
  • Addressing co-occurring disorders improves prognosis
  • Social support and stable housing are crucial for recovery

Patient Education & Prevention

Secondary Prevention Measures:

  • Cardiovascular risk reduction: Diet, exercise, weight management, reduce alcohol, smoking cessation
  • Harm reduction: Naloxone prescription if fentanyl contamination risk
  • Fentanyl test strips: Available for drug checking in some areas

Patient Counseling Points:

  • Discuss triggers (staying awake, performance, weight loss, chemsex)
  • Explain progression from occasional to problematic use
  • Encourage avoiding friends/family who use substances
  • Recommend mutual support groups (SAMHSA, FRANK)
  • Breastfeeding: Cocaine passes into milk, bottle feed for 24h after use

Support Resources:

UK FRANK: Free 24-hour drug advice service
SAMHSA: Find support groups and local programs
