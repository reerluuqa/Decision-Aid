Suicide Risk Mitigation

Quick Reference for Clinicians

IMMEDIATE RISK INDICATORS

If any of these are present, urgent intervention is required

Red Flags
Current suicidal plan (especially premeditated)
History of self-harm (50-100x increased risk)
History of mental illness (90% of suicide cases)
Access to lethal means (28x increased risk)
History of childhood abuse/neglect
Family history of suicide
Male sex (3x higher death rate)
Prison inmate status
Assessment Approach
Consider your own safety before seeing the patient
Use compassionate, therapeutic approach
Ask direct questions about suicidal thoughts
Seek consent to involve family/carers early
Take detailed history of suicidal thoughts
Conduct mental state examination
Undertake risk formulation
Assessment Guidelines
Assessment Component Key Points
Suicidal Thoughts Frequency, intensity, persistence, intended outcome
Planning Internet research, method, place/time, preparation
Means Access to lethal methods, especially firearms
History Previous attempts, self-harm, psychiatric history
Protective Factors Social support, reasons for living, coping skills

Important: Risk assessment tools should not be used in isolation to predict future suicide or inform management decisions. Focus on individualized assessment and intervention.

Key Assessment Questions
Opening Questions:
"Is life worth living?"
"Have you had thoughts of suicide?"
"Have you ever thought about ending your life?"
Follow-up Questions:
"What has stopped you acting on those thoughts so far?"
"Have you made any plans?"
"Do you have access to [method]?"

Clinical Tip: Asking about suicidal thoughts does not increase risk. Many patients are relieved to discuss these thoughts.

Safety Planning
Remove access to lethal means
Develop collaborative safety plan
Identify warning signs and coping strategies
Provide emergency contacts
Follow-up arrangements
Treatment Considerations
Treat underlying mental illness
Psychotherapy (CBT, DBT)
Involve family/support network
Consider hospitalization if high risk
Regular follow-up appointments
Key Principles
Suicide is not inevitable - with right support, people can recover
Every contact with health services represents an opportunity to intervene
Suicidal thoughts are more common than most people realize
Shift from "characterizing, predicting, and managing risk" to "compassion, safeguarding, and safety planning"
