Generalised Anxiety Disorder — Primary Care Algorithm

Adults ≥18y • Based on guidance last revised Apr 2025 • For rapid reference in consultations

Purpose A compact, one‑page aide‑mémoire for diagnosing and managing GAD in primary care. Prioritises speed, clarity, and safety checks.

1) Suspect & Diagnose

Core features

  • Chronic, excessive, uncontrollable worry, most days for >6 months.
  • ≥3 symptoms: restlessness, fatigue, poor concentration, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.
  • Functional impairment or significant distress.

Screen & assess

  • GAD‑2/GAD‑7 for severity & tracking.
  • Assess suicide risk and comorbidities (depression, other anxiety disorders).

Think differentials / contributors

  • Situational/adjustment anxiety, depression, panic, social anxiety, OCD, PTSD.
  • Medical: hyperthyroidism, pulmonary or cardiac disease, infection, IBS, phaeochromocytoma.
  • Substances/meds (e.g., salbutamol, theophylline, corticosteroids, caffeine; withdrawal states).

History / exam pointers

  • Nature, duration, triggers, trauma; family history; OTC/herbals; support.
  • Exam often normal; may see ↑HR, tremor, exaggerated startle.

2) Stepped Care Management

Step 1 — For all
  • Explain diagnosis early; provide education and self‑care (sleep hygiene, exercise; limit alcohol/caffeine).
  • Active monitoring at clinically appropriate intervals.
  • Treat the most severe comorbid disorder first; optimise long‑term conditions.
Step 2 — If no improvement
  • Low‑intensity CBT‑based options (by preference): guided self‑help (5–7 sessions), psychoeducational CBT group (6×2h), or non‑facilitated CBT self‑help (≥6 weeks).
Step 3 — Marked impairment or failed Step 2
  • High‑intensity CBT or applied relaxation (12–15 × ~1h).
  • Medication:
    • 1st‑line SSRI — often sertraline (commonly used in practice; off‑label for GAD). If ineffective: switch to another SSRI (e.g., escitalopram, paroxetine) or an SNRI (duloxetine, venlafaxine).
    • If SSRIs/SNRIs not tolerated → pregabalin may be considered.
  • Under 30s on SSRI/SNRI: discuss small ↑ risk of suicidal thinking/self‑harm; arrange close early monitoring (see below).
  • Avoid routine benzodiazepines or antipsychotics (short benzo course only for acute crises).
Step 4 — Complex / refractory or high risk
  • Refer for specialist assessment of needs/risks; consider combined therapy.
  • Offer any Step 1–3 interventions not yet tried; support carers where relevant.
Pregnancy
  • Prefer high‑intensity psychological therapy first‑line.
  • If considering medication: weigh maternal/foetal risks including risk of not treating. SSRIs/SNRIs after ~20 weeks: counsel about possible neonatal withdrawal and PPHN. Seek perinatal psychiatry advice.

3) Monitoring & Follow‑up

  • Under 30s on SSRI/SNRI: review at 1 week; then monitor suicide risk weekly for 1 month.
  • Others: first review at 4–6 weeks (earlier if high risk). Use GAD‑7 to track response.
  • Check adherence and adverse effects; adjust dose or switch as needed.
  • If effective, continue medication for ≥12 months to reduce relapse risk.
  • No response to drug → try alternative drug or high‑intensity therapy. Partial response → consider combination.
  • Reassess for referral; continuously reassess suicide risk, especially with comorbid depression.

4) Key Prescribing Notes (summary)

SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram, paroxetine)

  • Benefits emerge by ~6 weeks and build over time; plan to continue for ≥1 year.
  • Common AEs: GI upset, insomnia, agitation, sexual dysfunction; consider hyponatraemia and ↑ bleeding risk (consider gastroprotection if indicated).
  • Warn about discontinuation symptoms; taper slowly.
  • Escitalopram: avoid with prolonged QT or interacting QT‑prolonging drugs.

SNRIs (duloxetine, venlafaxine)

  • Licensed for GAD; monitor BP (especially early/after dose increases); control HTN before starting venlafaxine.
  • Similar continuation principles: benefits by ~6 weeks; plan ≥1 year if effective.

Pregabalin

  • Option if SSRI/SNRI not tolerated; prescribe with caution and monitor for misuse/dependence potential.

Benzodiazepines

  • Not routine for GAD in primary care; consider only short‑term during crises.

Note: Sertraline commonly used first‑line for cost‑effectiveness and interaction profile but is off‑label for GAD — obtain and document informed consent.

GAD‑7 Quick Reference

Items & scoring

Ask: Over the last 2 weeks, how often have you been bothered by…

  1. Feeling nervous, anxious, or on edge
  2. Not being able to stop or control worrying
  3. Worrying too much about different things
  4. Trouble relaxing
  5. Being so restless that it is hard to sit still
  6. Becoming easily annoyed or irritable
  7. Feeling afraid, as if something awful might happen

Scoring

  • Responses: Not at all=0, Several days=1, More than half the days=2, Nearly every day=3. Total 0–21.
  • Cut‑offs: 5=mild, 10=moderate, 15=severe.

When to Refer / Seek Specialist Advice

  • Complex comorbidity, treatment‑refractory symptoms, very marked functional impairment, or elevated self‑harm risk.
  • Uncertainty around medication in pregnancy (prefer perinatal mental health input).
  • Need for combined/advanced psychological interventions.
