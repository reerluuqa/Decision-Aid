Assessment

History Critical

  • Document exposure to dopamine receptor-blocking agents for ≥3 months (≥1 month if age >60)
  • Verify timing: movements present ≥4 weeks during exposure or within 4 weeks of withdrawal
  • Review all medications: antipsychotics, metoclopramide, SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, lithium, cinnarizine
  • Assess impact on quality of life using Impact-TD scale

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

  • • Cumulative exposure to dopamine blockers
  • • Age >50 years
  • • Use of typical (first-gen) antipsychotics

Other Risk Factors

  • • Post-menopausal women
  • • African-American ethnicity
  • • Diabetes mellitus
  • • Brain/CNS injury
  • • Dementia
  • • Smoking
  • • Alcohol/substance misuse
  • • History of acute dystonic reactions

Physical Examination Use AIMS

  • Observe oro-bucco-lingual movements: lip smacking, puckering, tongue writhing, facial grimacing
  • Assess for other tardive phenomena: dystonia, akathisia, tremor, myoclonus, chorea
  • Rate severity using Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale (AIMS)
  • Document with video if possible
  • Consider neurologist referral for atypical presentations

Investigations Usually Clinical

Routine tests not required for classic presentations with clear drug exposure history.

For atypical features, consider:

  • CT/MRI: abrupt onset or unilateral symptoms (rule out stroke, diabetic striatopathy)
  • Metabolic panel: rule out severe hyperglycemia
  • Genetic testing: CAG repeats if Huntington's disease suspected

Diagnosis

DSM-5-TR Criteria Clinical Diagnosis

  • Involuntary movements of tongue, jaw, trunk, or extremities
  • Exposure to dopamine receptor-blocking agents ≥3 months (≥1 month if >60 years)
  • Symptoms persist ≥4 weeks
  • Symptoms develop during exposure or within 4 weeks of stopping oral drug (8 weeks for long-acting injectable)
  • No other condition explains the movements

Schooler-Kane Criteria Research Use

  • ≥3 months cumulative antipsychotic exposure
  • Mild dyskinesia in 2+ body areas OR moderate in 1 area
  • Movements present ≥3 months
  • No other identifiable etiologies

Severity Scales

  • AIMS: 12-item scale (0-4 per item) for movement severity
  • Impact-TD: Assesses social, psychological, physical, vocational impact (0-3 per domain)

Differential Diagnoses Rule Out

Key differentiators: History of drug exposure, movement pattern, suppressibility, family history

Treatment

Treatment Algorithm Stepwise Approach

Withdraw or Modify Causative Drug

Gradually reduce dose and discontinue if possible. Consider switching to:

  • Atypical antipsychotics (quetiapine, clozapine)
  • Pimavanserin for Parkinson's psychosis
  • Non-dopamine blockers (ondansetron) for nausea

Deep Brain Stimulation Refractory Cases

Bilateral globus pallidus interna stimulation for severe, distressing TD

Prerequisites: Psychiatric condition stabilized, failed pharmacotherapy

Key Treatment Principles

  • Balance TD severity with risk of psychiatric relapse
  • Never maintain high-dose dopamine blockers to "mask" TD
  • Abrupt discontinuation may worsen TD or cause withdrawal emergent syndrome
  • Coordinate care between psychiatrist and neurologist
  • Document baseline AIMS score before starting treatment

Follow-up

Prognosis Variable Course

  • Symptoms may persist for months to years after drug discontinuation
  • Remission rates: 2-33% after stopping dopamine blockers
  • Some patients may have complete or partial remission even while continuing causative drug
  • Response to treatment is unpredictable

Quality of Life Impact Significant

TD substantially worsens quality of life and is associated with increased mortality in schizophrenia

  • Social insecurity and anger related to visible movements
  • Psychological distress from stigmatization
  • Potential impact on employment and relationships

Monitoring Schedule Regular Assessment

  • At each visit: Assess for new involuntary movements
  • Every 3-6 months: Repeat AIMS score
  • Annually: Evaluate Impact-TD score
  • VMAT2 inhibitor therapy: Monitor for depression/suicidality

Prevention Paramount

Prevention is better than treatment: Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration possible

  • Regular screening for TD in all patients on dopamine blockers
  • Consider alternative medications when possible
  • Patient education about early signs
  • Multidisciplinary approach to care
