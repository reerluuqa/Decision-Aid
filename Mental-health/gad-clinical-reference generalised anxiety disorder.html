Definition

GAD is defined as at least 6 months of excessive worry about everyday issues that is disproportionate to any inherent risk, causing distress or impairment. The worry is hard to control and not confined to features of another mental health disorder.

üìä

Epidemiology

  • US lifetime prevalence: 9% | 1-year: 2.9%
  • Primary care prevalence: 4-7.9%
  • Female-to-male ratio: Approximately 2:1
  • Comorbid with depression: ~43% lifetime
  • Often precedes other mental health disorders

Key Risk Factors

  • Family history of anxiety (OR 6.1)
  • Female sex (2:1 ratio)
  • Physical/emotional trauma
  • Chronic physical health conditions
  • Other anxiety disorder present
  • Life stressors / bullying
  • Childhood maltreatment

Diagnosis of Exclusion

Rule out these before confirming GAD:

  • Physical health conditions (thyroid, cardiac)
  • Other mental health disorders
  • Medication adverse effects
  • Substance misuse
üö® GAD is frequently under-diagnosed and under-treated. Maintain high clinical suspicion in primary care.

History Taking Checklist

  • Family history of anxiety/depression
  • History of trauma or substance misuse
  • Previous treatment responses
  • Current stress levels
  • Physical health problems
  • Effect on occupational/social function
  • Medication review (including OTC/herbal)

Physical Exam Findings

Normal exam: Usually normal if no co-existing conditions

Abnormal findings may include:

  • Trembling, shakiness
  • Exaggerated startle response
  • Increased heart rate (not discrete episodes)
  • Generalised muscular tension

Suicide Risk

Always assess for suicidal ideation in patients with comorbid depression.

  • SSRIs associated with small increased suicidality risk in age <24
  • NNH = 143 (suicidality) vs NNT = 3 (benefit)
  • Close monitoring in early weeks and after dose changes
  • Consider hospitalization if severe depression with suicidal ideation
Safety Assessment Questions:
  1. Is the patient at current risk?
  2. Can patient/family adhere to recommendations?

Red Flag Presentations

  • Chest pain - Rule out cardiac pathology first
  • Exertional symptoms - Consider cardiac workup
  • Severe hypertension + tachycardia - Rule out phaeochromocytoma
  • Acute onset in elderly - Consider organic causes
  • Psychotic features - Consider other diagnoses
  • Medication-induced anxiety - Review current meds

Benzodiazepine Risks

NICE guidelines recommend AGAINST benzodiazepines for GAD except as short-term crisis measure.

Major Risks

  • Falls (especially elderly)
  • Memory impairment
  • Dependence with withdrawal
  • Accidents
  • Death in overdose (esp. with opioids)

Key Points

  • Physiological dependence in 2-4 weeks
  • Avoid in substance misuse history
  • Maximum 2-4 weeks duration
  • Rapid discontinuation = withdrawal risk
  • Long-acting agents may reduce rebound

Treatment Algorithm Overview

1

Mild Symptoms

Watchful waiting + Psychoeducation or self-help resources. Consider CBT.

2

Moderate Symptoms

First-line: CBT OR Medication (SSRI/SNRI)

Consider combination if partial response.

3

Severe / Treatment-Resistant

Combination: CBT + Medication

Consider specialist referral. Evaluate for augmentation strategies.

üß†

Psychotherapy - First Line

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

  • NNT ‚âà 2 - Highly effective
  • 47% achieve symptom reduction to normal levels
  • Benefits persist up to 12 months post-treatment
  • Can be delivered face-to-face, online, or via apps
  • Consider for patients who cannot/will not take medication

Other Non-Drug Options

  • Mindfulness / meditation training
  • Applied relaxation (NICE recommended)
  • Exercise (high-intensity > low-intensity)
  • Yoga (short-term benefit)
  • Sleep hygiene measures
  • Problem-solving therapy
‚è∞

Duration of Treatment

Acute Phase

Response typically begins 2-4 weeks after starting antidepressant. Continue initial dose for up to 4 weeks before assessing response. Full benefit by 12 weeks.

Maintenance Phase

Once good response achieved, continue treatment for at least 1 year to prevent relapse. Discontinue slowly (max 25% dose reduction per month).

Avoid as Initial Treatment

Benzodiazepines

  • Only short-term (2-4 weeks) crisis use
  • Not for long-term GAD management
  • Dependence, falls, cognitive impairment
  • High mortality with opioids in overdose

Quetiapine

  • Metabolic syndrome risk
  • Weight gain, QTc effects
  • Poor tolerability
  • Do NOT offer as initial treatment
  • Requires specialist initiation

Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)

  • Imipramine, clomipramine - limited evidence for GAD
  • HIGH RISK IN OVERDOSE
  • Cardiac adverse effects
  • Safety concerns limit use

Validated Screening Tools

Short, robust, freely available tools recommended:

GAD-2 (2-item)

Cutoff: ‚â•3 suggests anxiety

Very brief screening tool. If positive, follow with GAD-7.

GAD-7 (7-item)

Scoring:

  • 0-4: Minimal anxiety
  • 5-9: Mild anxiety
  • 10-14: Moderate anxiety
  • 15-21: Severe anxiety

Validated for primary care. Useful for tracking treatment response over time.

HAM-A (Clinician-rated)

Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale - clinician-administered assessment of psychic and somatic anxiety symptoms. Often administered at 2-week intervals to monitor treatment response.

SCARED (Children/Adolescents)

Screen for Child Anxiety Related Disorders - 41-item inventory with child, parent, and combined versions. Performs well in community and clinical settings.

Perinatal Screening

Under-recognised throughout pregnancy and postnatal period.

ACOG Recommendation

Screen all women receiving well-woman, pre-pregnancy, antenatal, and postnatal care at multiple timepoints using validated tools (e.g., GAD-7).

NICE Recommendation

Consider GAD-2 first (cutoff ‚â•3). If positive, use GAD-7 for further assessment.

Prevalence during pregnancy/postnatal: Approximately 2.2% to 10%

Children & Adolescents

Diagnostic Criteria Modification

Only 1 key symptom required (vs 3 in adults) for GAD diagnosis in children.

First-Line Treatment

  • Psychoeducation
  • Anxiety management training
  • CBT (recommended first-line for moderate/persistent GAD)
  • e-therapy (computerised CBT like SPARX)

Second-Line: Medication

Consider if psychotherapy ineffective. Must be initiated under specialist supervision.

  • SSRIs first-line pharmacotherapy
  • Combination SSRI + CBT shows best outcomes
  • Response may take 6-8 weeks

‚ö†Ô∏è SSRI Suicidality Risk

Small increased risk in age <24. NNH = 143 vs NNT = 3. Close monitoring essential, especially early treatment and after dose changes.

Pregnancy & Postnatal

First-Line Treatment

CBT recommended first-line, especially for mild-moderate anxiety.

  • Online interventions effective in studies
  • Videoconferencing psychotherapy also effective
  • Applied relaxation (NICE recommended)

When Medication Needed

Consider for severe or disabling anxiety. Balance fetal risks with maternal/fetal risks of untreated illness.

  • Sertraline - Often preferred (extensive safety data)
  • Escitalopram - Reasonable alternative
  • Consult perinatal psychiatry specialist

‚ö†Ô∏è Medication Risks

  • SSRIs: Slight pregnancy duration reduction, hypertensive disease of pregnancy, postnatal hemorrhage risk
  • Benzodiazepines: ‚Üë Caesarean delivery, ventilatory support for newborn
  • All cross placenta - discuss risks/benefits
üë¥

Older Adults

  • GAD most common anxiety disorder in elderly
  • Prevalence: 1.3% to 4.7%
  • SSRIs effective and generally well-tolerated
  • CBT may be especially helpful
  • Benzodiazepines increase fall risk - avoid if possible
  • Rule out organic causes (thyroid, cardiac, medications)
üîó

Comorbid Considerations

Comorbid Major Depression

  • Treatment similar to GAD alone
  • SSRI/SNRI effective for both
  • Buspirone and pregabalin NOT recommended
  • CBT for GAD also improves depressive symptoms
  • Close monitoring for suicidality if using sedatives

Comorbid Physical Conditions

  • Cardiovascular disease, cancer, respiratory disease
  • Diabetes, polycystic ovary syndrome
  • Higher anxiety prevalence in chronic illness
