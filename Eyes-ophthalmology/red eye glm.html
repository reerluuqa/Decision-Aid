Red Eye Assessment Tool

Emergency Referral Required

Refer to emergency eye service for same-day assessment if any red flags are present

Red Flag Symptoms

Visual Changes

Reduced visual acuity, blurred vision, halos around lights

Pain

Moderate to severe pain, headache, pain on pupillary constriction

Photophobia

Light sensitivity (also consider meningitis)

Pupil Abnormalities

Unequal or misshapen pupils, abnormal reactions, fixed mid-dilated pupil

Corneal Defects

Staining with fluorescein, haze, hyphaema, hypopyon

Trauma History

High-velocity injury, foreign body, penetrating lesion, chemical injury

Neonatal Cases

Conjunctivitis in first 28 days of life

Emergency Actions

Chemical Injury

Immediately irrigate with water or 0.9% saline until pH returns to normal

Suspected Acute Glaucoma

Hard eye, ciliary injection, mid-dilated pupil, halos around lights

Corneal Ulcer

Foreign body sensation, photophobia, discharge, pain, corneal staining

Trauma

Penetrating injury, high-velocity foreign body

Neonatal Conjunctivitis

Conjunctivitis with discharge in first 28 days of life

Assessment Approach

1

History

Onset, duration, unilateral/bilateral, visual changes, pain severity, discharge, photophobia, contact lens use, trauma history

2

Visual Acuity

Check using Snellen chart - reduced acuity is a red flag

3

External Examination

Check for discharge, eyelid swelling, trichiasis, entropion, ectropion

4

Conjunctival Examination

Pattern of redness (ciliary flush is serious), evert upper lid if foreign body suspected

5

Fluorescein Examination

Corneal staining indicates abrasion or ulcer

6

Pupil Assessment

Check size, shape, direct and consensual responses, relative afferent pupillary defect

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Features Action
Acute Glaucoma Hard eye, severe pain, halos, mid-dilated pupil
Anterior Uveitis Pain, photophobia, ciliary injection, constricted pupil
Corneal Ulcer Foreign body sensation, photophobia, staining
Endophthalmitis Recent surgery, reduced vision, pain, hypopyon
Scleritis Severe pain, deep redness, possible vision loss
Condition Key Features Action
Conjunctivitis Discharge, itching, normal vision, diffuse redness
Subconjunctival Haemorrhage Localized red area, no pain, normal vision
Episcleritis Segmental redness, usually painless, normal vision
Corneal Abrasion Foreign body sensation, pain, staining with fluorescein
Blepharitis Crusting at lid margin, itching

Management Options

Same-Day Referral Required

Refer to emergency eye service immediately for:

  • Acute glaucoma
  • Corneal ulcer
  • Penetrating eye injury
  • Chemical injury (after irrigation)
  • Endophthalmitis
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis

Refer Within 24 Hours

Refer to local eye clinic for:

  • Anterior uveitis
  • Scleritis
  • Corneal foreign body that cannot be removed in primary care
  • Contact lens-related red eye with pain

Manage in Primary Care

  • Subconjunctival haemorrhage: Reassure, check BP if hypertensive
  • Episcleritis: Reassure, NSAIDs may help
  • Conjunctivitis: Treat according to type (allergic, infective)
  • Corneal abrasion: Remove foreign body if possible, prescribe antibiotics
  • Blepharitis: Lid hygiene, warm compresses
  • Dry eye: Artificial tears, lubricants
