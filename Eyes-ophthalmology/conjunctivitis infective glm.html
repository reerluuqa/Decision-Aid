Types of Infective Conjunctivitis

Viral Conjunctivitis
  • Most common cause (up to 80% of cases)
  • Usually caused by adenovirus
  • Watery discharge
  • Often accompanied by upper respiratory infection
  • Pre-auricular lymphadenopathy common
  • Self-limiting (resolves in ~7 days)
Bacterial Conjunctivitis
  • Second most common cause
  • Purulent or mucopurulent discharge
  • Eyelids often stuck together on waking
  • Common in children (50-75% of cases)
  • Usually resolves in 5-10 days
  • May benefit from antibiotics
Ophthalmia Neonatorum
  • Conjunctivitis within first 4 weeks of life
  • Chlamydia: watery/mucopurulent discharge (5-14 days)
  • Gonorrhea: copious purulent discharge (first 5 days)
  • Requires urgent assessment and treatment
  • Risk of serious complications if untreated

Assessment Approach

Key Assessment Points

1
History
Onset, duration, discharge character, itching, vision changes, recent exposure
2
Visual Acuity
Check with Snellen chart - reduced acuity is a red flag
3
Examination
Conjunctiva, cornea, pupils, eyelids, lymph nodes
4
Special Tests
Fluorescein staining if corneal involvement suspected

Examination Findings

Feature Viral Bacterial
Discharge Watery Purulent/Mucopurulent
Conjunctiva Follicles, petechial hemorrhages Diffuse redness
Lymphadenopathy Common (pre-auricular) Uncommon (except hyperacute)
Corneal involvement Subepithelial infiltrates (adenovirus) Rare (except in contact lens wearers)

Management Approach

Primary Care Management

1
Self-Care Advice
Warm compresses, eyelid hygiene, avoid contact lenses
2
Antibiotics (if bacterial)
Chloramphenicol or fusidic acid first-line
3
No antibiotics (if viral)
Supportive care only, self-limiting condition
4
Infection Control
Hand hygiene, avoid sharing towels, stay home from school/work if discharge

DISCUSS WITH/REFER TO OPHTHALMOLOGY

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Recurrent or persistent symptoms (>2 weeks)
  • Contact lens wearers with corneal symptoms
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Severe pain or photophobia
  • Corneal involvement on fluorescein staining

Safety

Chloramphenicol

Formulation Dose Duration
Eye drops (0.5%) 1 drop every 2 hours initially, then reduce to 4 times daily 5 days
Eye ointment (1%) 1cm applied 3-4 times daily 5 days

Advantages: Broad spectrum, inexpensive, widely available

Considerations: May cause transient stinging, rare risk of aplastic anemia

Fusidic Acid

Formulation Dose Duration
Eye drops (1%) 1 drop twice daily 7 days

Advantages: Twice daily dosing, good for Staphylococcus aureus

Considerations: More expensive than chloramphenicol, less broad spectrum

Safety Considerations

  • Chloramphenicol is no longer contraindicated in children under 2 years
  • Contact lens wearers should avoid lenses during treatment
  • Advise on proper eye drop administration technique
  • Warn about temporary blurring of vision after application
  • If no improvement after 48 hours, review diagnosis
