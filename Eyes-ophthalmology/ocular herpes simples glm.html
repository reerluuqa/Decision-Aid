Ocular Herpes Simplex

Last revised: May 2024

URGENT ACTION REQUIRED

Refer all cases of suspected ocular herpes simplex infection to eye casualty or emergency eye service for same-day assessment. Do not initiate drug treatment while awaiting specialist assessment.

Quick Reference

When to Suspect

Unilateral red eye with pain, watering, blurred vision, and history of previous episodes

Urgent Common

Immediate Action

Same-day referral to eye specialist. No treatment initiation in primary care

Emergency

Key Signs

Dendritic ulcer on fluorescein staining, reduced corneal sensation, vesicles on eyelids

Diagnostic

Patient Advice

Avoid touching lesions, wash hands, no contact lenses until 24h after symptoms resolve

Safe Practice

Red Flags

  • Severe eye pain with photophobia
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Corneal opacity or haze
  • Fixed irregular pupil
  • History of immunosuppression
  • Contact lens wearers with symptoms

Key Symptoms & Signs

Common Symptoms

  • Eye pain
  • Eye irritation
  • Photophobia
  • Eye watering
  • Blurred vision

Typical Signs

  • Unilateral red eye
  • Vesicles on eyelids
  • Dendritic ulcer
  • Reduced corneal sensation
  • Hazy cornea

Management Actions

Urgent Referral

Refer all cases to eye casualty or emergency eye service for same-day assessment

Patient Advice

Avoid touching lesions, wash hands, no contact lenses until 24h after symptoms resolve

Documentation

Record symptoms, signs, visual acuity, and any previous history of ocular herpes

Types of Infection

  • Epithelial keratitis (50-80% of cases) - punctate lesions, dendritic ulcer
  • Stromal keratitis - corneal edema, neovascularization, scarring
  • Iritis/Uveitis - inflammation of iris/uveal tract
  • Blepharoconjunctivitis - eyelid and conjunctiva inflammation

Key Assessment Points

  • History of previous episodes?
  • Contact lens use?
  • Known trigger factors?
  • Underlying medical conditions?
  • Visual acuity (Snellen chart)
  • Fluorescein staining of cornea

Differential Diagnosis

  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus
  • Orbital cellulitis
  • Acute glaucoma
  • Iritis/uveitis (other causes)
  • Scleritis/episcleritis
  • Other keratitis (EBV, adenovirus)
  • Corneal abrasion

Background Information

Prevalence & Risk

  • Incidence: 5-15 cases per 100,000/year
  • More common after corneal transplantation
  • Up to 12% have bilateral infection
  • Higher recurrence risk with immunosuppression

Complications

  • Corneal scarring and visual impairment
  • Corneal perforation
  • Secondary infection
  • Secondary glaucoma
