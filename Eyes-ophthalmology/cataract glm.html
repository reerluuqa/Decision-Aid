Cataracts Clinical Reference

Last revised: March 2025

What is a Cataract?

A cataract is the opacification of the crystalline lens that affects vision. Most cataracts are related to ageing, and they can form in one or both eyes.

Classification:

  • Nuclear: Opacification of the nucleus
  • Cortical: Opacification of the cortex
  • Posterior sub-capsular: Opacification of the posterior aspect
Type Age of Onset Common Causes
Age-related 60+ Ageing, oxidative stress
Traumatic Any age Eye injury, radiation
Congenital Birth Genetic, maternal infection
Secondary Any age Diabetes, steroids, UV exposure

Diagnosis

Key Symptoms

  • Gradual, painless reduction in visual clarity
  • Cloudy or blurry vision
  • Halos around lights, glare sensitivity
  • Diplopia or polyopia in one eye
  • Frequent changes in spectacle prescription
  • Reduced color perception (especially blues)

Examination Findings

  • Absent, dull, or shadowed red reflex
  • Reduced visual acuity (Snellen chart)
  • Lens opacity on ophthalmoscopy
  • Opacity visible on slit-lamp examination

Note: In the UK, most cataracts are diagnosed and monitored during routine optometrist appointments.

Screening

In the UK, all babies are screened for congenital cataracts:

  • At birth (within 72 hours)
  • At 6-8 weeks of age

Effectiveness: Variable, with cataracts being missed in over half of cases.

Signs in Children

  • Absent or abnormal red reflex
  • White pupil (leukocoria)
  • Strabismus or nystagmus
  • Not tracking moving objects
  • Not fixing steadily on faces
  • Asymmetry of the white/red reflex in photos

Assessment

History

  • Impact on daily activities (reading, TV)
  • Impact on driving (day and night)
  • Impact on work/occupation
  • Changes in color perception
  • Difficulty recognizing faces
  • Effect on mood and quality of life
  • One or both eyes affected
  • Gradual or sudden visual loss
  • Interest in surgery if offered

    • Examination

    Usually performed by an optometrist:

    • Visual acuity (Snellen chart)
    • External eye examination
    • Fundoscopy with ophthalmoscope
    • Slit lamp examination
    • Pupil size and function assessment
    • Visual field assessment
    • Glare testing
    • Intraocular pressure measurement

    Risk Factors

    • Family history of infant/early childhood eye conditions
    • Genetic syndromes (Down syndrome, etc.)
    • Prenatal infection
    • Sensorineural hearing loss
    • Neurodevelopmental issues

    NIPE Screening Examination

  • Eyelid examination (malformations, skin abnormalities)
  • Eye symmetry and corneal clarity
  • Fixation on face without nystagmus (6-8 week check)
  • Ability to follow bright moving object (6-8 week check)
  • Red reflex assessment

    • Red Reflex Assessment:

    • Dim overhead lights
    • Use ophthalmoscope at arm's length
    • Look for red glow in pupil
    • Abnormal if obscured, white, or asymmetrical

    Referral Guidelines

    Adult Referral Criteria

    Routine Referral:

    • Visual symptoms affecting daily activities
    • Difficulty meeting driving standards
    • Reduced visual acuity (typically 6/12 or worse)
    • Frequent changes in prescription

    Considerations:

    • Occupation-specific visual requirements
    • Patient's visual needs and expectations
    • Impact on quality of life

    Child Referral Criteria

    URGENT Referral (within 1 week):

    • Absent or abnormal red reflex
    • White pupil (leukocoria)
    • Not tracking objects by 3 months
    • Strabismus or nystagmus

    Routine Referral:

    • Family history of congenital cataracts
    • Associated genetic syndromes
    • History of prenatal infection

    Red Flags & Emergency Actions

    Urgent Referral Required

    Adults:

    • Sudden visual loss
    • Painful red eye
    • Sudden onset of floaters or flashing lights
    • History of trauma with visual changes

    Children:

    • Absent or abnormal red reflex
    • White pupil (leukocoria)
    • Not tracking objects by 3 months
    • Strabismus or nystagmus

    Consider Alternative Diagnoses

    Gradual Visual Loss:

    • Refractive error
    • Corneal disease
    • Dry eye syndrome
    • Age-related macular degeneration
    • Primary open-angle glaucoma

    Rapid Visual Loss:

    • Cerebrovascular disease
    • Posterior vitreous detachment
    • Vitreous haemorrhage
    • Retinal detachment
    • Retinal vein thrombosis

    Prognosis

    Without Treatment

    With age-related cataracts, once visual acuity starts declining, the natural history is a steady decline without any chance of recovery. However, the rate and severity of progression is variable and unpredictable.

    With Surgery

    • 95% achieve 6/12 best-corrected vision
    • Up to 61% achieve 6/6 best-corrected vision
    • Improved quality of life
    • Reduced risk of depression, falls and fractures
    • <1 in 200 have reduction in vision post-op

    Without Treatment

    Cataracts in young children cause deprivation amblyopia, leading to serious lifelong visual impairment, even if the cataracts are removed when older.

    With Surgery

    Critical Periods:

    • Unilateral congenital cataracts: 4-6 weeks
    • Bilateral congenital cataracts: 6-8 weeks

    Post-op Considerations:

    • 15-30% risk of developing glaucoma
    • Risk of amblyopia, refractive errors
    • Risk of strabismus, retinal detachment
    • Compliance with post-op treatment crucial
