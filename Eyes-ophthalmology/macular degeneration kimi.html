üìù When to Suspect AMD

Typical presentation in people aged ‚â•50 years:

  • Blurring or distortion of vision (metamorphopsia)
  • Scotoma (black/grey patch in central vision)
  • Difficulty reading or seeing fine detail
  • Objects appearing different in size/color between eyes
  • Photopsia (flickering/flashing lights)
  • Rapid visual deterioration (suggests neovascular AMD)
  • Gradual visual loss (suggests geographic atrophy)
  • Visual hallucinations (Charles Bonnet syndrome) may occur
‚ö†Ô∏è Key Point: Patients may be asymptomatic with retinal signs detected incidentally during routine eye tests.
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flag Symptoms
üö® URGENT: Sudden vision changes or distortion - consider neovascular AMD
üë§ Risk Factors
üî∫ Non-modifiable:
  • Increasing age (especially >75 years)
  • Female sex
  • Family history (71% heritability)
  • Northern European ancestry
üîß Modifiable:
  • SMOKING (strongest modifiable risk)
  • Hypertension
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Obesity (BMI ‚â•30 kg/m¬≤)
  • Poor diet (low omega-3, vitamins, high saturated fat)
  • Physical inactivity
üí° Smoking Cessation: Stopping smoking reduces AMD progression risk. After 20 years smoke-free, risk equals non-smokers.
üîç Examination Findings

Visual acuity: May be reduced, but can be normal in early stages

Fundoscopy findings:

  • Drusen: Pale yellow spots on retina (early stage)
  • Pigmentary changes: Hypo/hyperpigmentation
  • Geographic atrophy: Sharply demarcated depigmented areas
  • Exudative/hemorrhagic changes: Suggest neovascular AMD
üìå Note: Signs may be difficult to detect in primary care depending on expertise. When in doubt, refer for specialist assessment.
üìä Classification

NICE Classification System:

  • No AMD: No signs or small drusen (<63Œºm)
  • Early AMD: Medium drusen (63-124Œºm) or pigmentary abnormalities
  • Intermediate AMD: Large drusen (‚â•125Œºm) or reticular drusen with pigmentary changes
  • Late AMD:
    • Wet active: Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)
    • Dry: Geographic atrophy
    • Intermediate: RPE dysfunction without neovascularization

Confirmation & Differential Diagnosis
‚ñº
üè• Primary Care Actions
‚è±Ô∏è URGENT REFERRAL: Within 1 working day if neovascular AMD suspected

üö® Emergency Pathway

Indication: Suspected neovascular AMD

Timeline: Within 1 working day

Destination: Specialist AMD service, fast-track macular clinic, or hospital eye emergency department

üìÖ Routine Pathway

Indication: Other suspected AMD

Timeline: Within 1 week to optometrist

Action: Direct referral to ophthalmologist if neovascular AMD identified

‚ö†Ô∏è Patient Advice: If delay >1 week or symptoms worsen while waiting, advise immediate attendance at Eye Casualty or seek urgent medical attention.
üî¨ Secondary Care Investigations
  • Slit-lamp biomicroscopy: Identify drusen, pigmentary/exudative/hemorrhagic/atrophic changes
  • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): MANDATORY for diagnosis and monitoring
  • Colour fundus photography: Document retinal appearance
  • Fundus autofluorescence: Visualize geographic atrophy progression
  • Fluorescein angiography: Confirm neovascular AMD, assess lesion type/extent
  • Indocyanine green angiography: Visualize choroidal circulation
‚ùì Differential Diagnosis

Conditions causing painless visual disturbance:

  • Refractive error
  • Corneal disease (e.g., Fuch's dystrophy)
  • Cataract
  • Primary open-angle glaucoma
  • Posterior vitreous detachment/vitreous hemorrhage/retinal detachment
  • Cerebrovascular disease (amaurosis fugax, stroke/TIA)
  • Retinal arterial/venous occlusion
  • Medication toxicity (e.g., hydroxychloroquine, ethambutol)
  • Pituitary tumor/papilledema

Conditions with similar retinal signs:

  • Diabetic maculopathy
  • High myopia with CNV (younger age, different natural history)
  • Inflammatory syndromes causing CNV (with uveitis signs)
  • Central serous retinopathy
  • Macular telangiectasia
  • Pattern dystrophy
  • Macular hole
üí° Tip: Consider hereditary macular dystrophies in patients <50 years with AMD-like features.
üìã Diagnostic Criteria

AMD is characterized by:

  • Drusen (‚â•63Œºm diameter) - intermediate or large
  • RPE abnormalities (hypo/hyperpigmentation)
  • Geographic atrophy (RPE breakdown)
  • Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)
  • Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy (PCV)
  • Reticular pseudodrusen
  • Retinal angiomatous proliferation

üè† Primary Care Management

For Suspected AMD:

  • Urgent referral if neovascular AMD suspected (within 1 working day)
  • Arrange optometrist review within 1 week for other cases
  • Provide advice on symptoms requiring immediate attention

For Confirmed AMD:

üö≠ Lifestyle Interventions

  • Smoking cessation (strongly advised)
  • Healthy balanced diet:
    • Low glycaemic index
    • Rich in fruits & green leafy vegetables
    • High in omega-3 fatty acids (fish)
    • Low saturated fat & cholesterol
  • Control hypertension & cardiovascular risk

2. Older Treatments (Second-line/Specific Cases)

‚ö° Laser Photocoagulation

Destroys new blood vessels. Creates permanent scotoma. Only for lesions not close to foveal center.

üåü Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)

Verteporfin injection + laser treatment. Second-line in combination with anti-VEGF for wet active AMD.

3. Geographic Atrophy Treatment

‚ö†Ô∏è Limited Treatment Options: Mainly counseling, smoking cessation, visual rehabilitation, and nutritional supplements (as above).

üí° DVLA Guidance: Refer to "Assessing fitness to drive - a guide for medical professionals" if uncertain.
ü§ù Support & Rehabilitation

üìú Certification

üîç Low Vision Services

üë• Rehabilitation

üìà Prognosis & Monitoring

Disease Progression Rates:

üìä Early AMD ‚Üí Advanced AMD

  • 5-year risk: 1.3%
  • 10-year progression to large drusen: ~15%

üìä Intermediate AMD ‚Üí Advanced AMD

  • 5-year risk: ~18%
  • With large drusen: 6.3% at 5 years
  • Bilateral large drusen: 26% at 5 years

üëÅÔ∏è Second Eye Involvement

  • 5-year risk: 22-50%
  • Neovascular AMD: 12% at 1 year, 27% at 4 years

üìà Geographic Atrophy Progression

  • 2 years: 31% lose 3 lines of vision
  • 4 years: 53% lose 3 lines of vision
  • Doubling of visual angle in up to 50% over 2 years

Treated Neovascular AMD:

7-year follow-up data:
  • 43% maintained or improved visual acuity
  • 34% lost ‚â•3 lines on visual acuity chart
  • Factors affecting outcome: VA and lesion size at diagnosis, number of injections received
‚ö†Ô∏è Untreated Neovascular AMD:
  • 3 months: 1 line lost
  • 12 months: ~3 lines lost
  • 24 months: ~4 lines lost
  • Rapid progression to moderate/severe visual loss within weeks/months
üîÑ Complications to Monitor

üëÅÔ∏è AMD Complications

  • Visual impairment/blindness
  • Depression (up to 44% in advanced AMD)
  • Charles Bonnet syndrome (visual hallucinations)
  • Falls and fractures
  • Reduced quality of life
  • Limitations in daily activities

üíâ Treatment Complications

  • Endophthalmitis (post-injection)
  • Traumatic lens injury
  • Retinal detachment
  • Photosensitivity (post-PDT)
