Vision Loss Assessment

Last updated: May 13, 2025 • Clinical Reference

Immediate Ophthalmology Consultation Required If:

  • Sudden onset (mins/hours)
  • Neuro symptoms (diplopia, weakness)
  • History of trauma
  • Pain, redness, or orbital signs

Surgical & Medical Emergencies

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma Emergency

Symptoms: Severe periorbital pain, red eye, nausea/vomiting, sudden vision loss.

Risk: Increased IOP. Often misdiagnosed as gastroenteritis.

Urgent Ophthalmology consult & medical therapy (insufficient alone). Surgical consult needed.
Retinal Detachment Surgical

Symptoms: "Curtain" moving across vision, flashes/floaters, peripheral vision loss.

Risk: Myopia, post-cataract surgery, trauma.

Immediate surgical consultation. Rapid decline if fovea detaches.
Arteritic AION / GCA Systemic

Patient: Age ≥50 years.

Symptoms: Sudden severe monocular loss, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, headache.

IMMEDIATE high-dose steroids (IV/Oral) to save other eye & prevent MI.
CRAO Vascular

Symptoms: Painless, sudden, profound monocular vision loss.

Cause: Atherosclerosis, embolus.

Consider IV tPA (select patients, early time points).
Pituitary Apoplexy Neuro

Symptoms: Acute vision loss, severe headache, ocular motility disorder.

Risk: Expansion of existing adenoma, haemorrhage.

Emergent Neurosurgery. Check for hypopituitarism (cortisol).
Post-Op Endophthalmitis Infectious

History: Recent intraocular surgery (<1 month) or injection.

Symptoms: Painful red eye, acute vision loss.

Urgent ophthalmology intervention (Vitreous tap/Injection).
Corneal Ulcer Urgent

Risk: Contact lens wearers (esp. extended wear).

Symptoms: Painful red eye, watery/mucoid discharge, vision loss. May be painless if neurotrophic.

Urgent inpatient medical therapy to prevent perforation.

Diagnostic Approach

Rapidity of Onset
  • Acute (mins-hrs): Vascular occlusion, detachment, inflammation, GCA. (Urgent)
  • Sub-acute (wks-mos): Neoplastic, compressive lesions, uveitis.
  • Chronic (yrs): Cataract, glaucoma (open-angle), dry AMD.
Clinical Presentation
  • Monocular vs Binocular: Monocular = Ocular. Binocular = Systemic/Neuro (rarely ocular).
  • Pain:
    • Severe Pain: Angle-closure glaucoma, ulcer, uveitis.
    • Pain with movement: Optic neuritis.
    • Painless: Retinal detachment, vascular occlusion, AMD.
History & Red Flags
  • Systemic: Diabetes (check glucose), HTN, autoimmune (RA, Lupus).
  • Meds: Ethambutol, Isotretinoin, Phosphodiesterase inhibitors (ED), Amiodarone.
  • Trauma: Always assess acuity after stabilization. Watch for retrobulbar haemorrhage.
Immediate Physical Exam
  • Visual Acuity: Critical baseline.
  • Pupils: Check for Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD). Indicates retina/optic nerve issue.
  • Confrontation Fields: Detect homonymous defects (Stroke).
  • Intraocular Pressure: If suspect Glaucoma.

Common Differentials & Key Features

Condition Key Symptoms / History Action / Notes
Wet AMD Age ≥50, smoker, distortion (metamorphopsia), painless loss. Refer to retinal specialist (~72 hrs). Anti-VEGF.
Dry AMD Slow progression, drusen, central blur. Non-urgent referral. Amsler grid monitoring.
Diabetic Retinopathy History of diabetes (may be new diagnosis). Floaters/bleeds. Urgent Ophtho if proliferative/vitreous haemorrhage. Routine screening otherwise.
Retinal Vein Occlusion Acute, painless, diffuse haemorrhages on fundus. Anti-VEGF, laser, steroids.
Optic Neuritis Young female, pain on movement, demyelination history (MS). MRI Brain. Urgent but not immediate surgical.
Cataract Painless, progressive, discovered often when covering good eye. Routine referral.
Vitreous Haemorrhage Sudden floaters, diffuse loss, often diabetics or trauma. B-scan Ultrasound. Treat underlying cause.
Stroke / TIA Homonymous hemianopia, neuro deficits, sudden. Urgent Neurological consult / CT.

General Management Principles

Labs & Imaging

Acute Mono (Age ≥50): CBC, ESR, CRP (Rule out GCA).

Transient Loss: Lipid profile, CBC, Carotid Doppler, Echo.

Neuro Signs: MRI Brain (Contrast + DWI) > CT. Look at Sella Turcica.

Systemic Work-up

Blood Glucose: Measure in all acute vision loss patients.

Autoimmune: Suspect if Vasculitis, Sarcoidosis, Behcet's.

Infectious: Syphilis, TB, Lyme if optic nerve dysfunction.
