Neutropenic Sepsis Quick Reference

Last revised: August 2024

Medical Emergency

Arrange emergency transfer to hospital for all people with suspected neutropenic sepsis

Red Flags for Neutropenic Sepsis

  • Known neutropenia or risk factors for neutropenia (e.g., undergoing chemotherapy)
  • Temperature greater than 38°C OR hypothermia
  • Chills, shivers, rigors
  • Confirmed infection or symptoms indicating possible infection
  • Tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension
  • Increased capillary refill time, mottled or ashen skin
  • Altered mental state, reduced urinary output
  • Person appears unwell or deteriorating unexpectedly

Definition

Neutropenic sepsis is defined as:

  • Neutrophil count of 0.5 x 109/L or lower
  • AND either a temperature higher than 38°C OR any symptoms and/or signs of sepsis

Febrile neutropenia is the most common complication of anticancer treatment and describes the presence of fever in a person with neutropenia.

Key Points

  • Sepsis can be challenging to identify in people who are neutropenic, as there may be minimal or atypical symptoms and/or signs
  • People with neutropenic sepsis may not present with fever and may instead present with hypothermia
  • Medication such as corticosteroids may mask an elevated temperature
  • Local protocols may have different temperature and neutrophil threshold criteria

Emergency Assessment

Refer immediately for urgent assessment in secondary or tertiary care

Note that the complete assessment should be made in secondary or tertiary care, and that measurements in community settings should not delay transfer of the patient.

Emergency Transfer

Key Assessment Points

History

  • Known causes or risk factors for neutropenia
  • Recent fever or rigors
  • Symptoms suggesting focus of infection
  • Features suggesting dehydration
  • Altered behavior, mental state, or cognition
  • Cancer details: type, treatment timing

Examination

  • General appearance, consciousness level
  • Temperature (may be hypothermia)
  • Heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure
  • Capillary refill time and oxygen saturation
  • Skin: mottled, ashen, cyanosis, rash
  • Possible source of infection

Assessment Tools

Tool Purpose Application
Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) Assess level of consciousness Score eye, verbal, motor responses
AVPU Scale Quick consciousness assessment Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive
SOFA Score Assess organ dysfunction Higher scores indicate increased mortality risk

Emergency Actions

  1. Arrange emergency transfer to hospital for all people with suspected neutropenic sepsis
  2. Provide appropriate emergency management in primary care, if necessary, prior to hospital transfer
  3. Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics as soon as possible if delay in transfer is anticipated
Emergency Transfer

Goals of Management

  • Support primary healthcare professionals to be aware of when to suspect neutropenic sepsis
  • Arrange emergency transfer to hospital for all people with suspected neutropenic sepsis
  • Provide appropriate emergency management in primary care, if necessary
  • Provide advice and information to survivors and their families/carers
  • Arrange follow-up as needed

Management Considerations

In Hospital

  • Urgent assessment of neutrophil count
  • Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Fluid resuscitation if hypotensive
  • Vasopressor therapy if needed
  • Source identification and control

Follow-up

  • Advice and information to survivors
  • Support for families/carers
  • Monitoring for post-sepsis syndrome
  • Psychological support if needed
  • Adjustment of chemotherapy regimens

Risk Factors for Neutropenic Sepsis

Neutropenia Characteristics

  • Severe neutropenia (ANC ≤ 0.5 x 109/L)
  • Prolonged neutropenia (>7 days)
  • Rapid decline in neutrophil count

Patient Factors

  • Age: infants and >60 years
  • Haematological malignancy
  • Advanced malignancy
  • Previous febrile neutropenia

Treatment Factors

  • High-intensity chemotherapy
  • Corticosteroids
  • Antibiotics at onset of febrile neutropenia
  • Haematopoietic stem cell transplantation

Other Factors

  • Comorbidities (diabetes, liver/renal disease)
  • Central venous access device
  • Total parenteral nutrition
  • Prolonged hospital admission

Risk Stratification

Mortality rates vary with the Multinational Association of Supportive Care in Cancer (MASCC) prognostic index:

Low Risk: Mortality < 5%

Medium Risk: Mortality 5-20%

High Risk: Mortality up to 40%

Prevention Strategies

  • Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) prophylaxis
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis in high-risk patients
  • Dose adjustments of chemotherapy regimens
  • Patient education on early warning signs
  • Prompt reporting of fever or symptoms

Potential Complications

Life-threatening

  • Death (mortality up to 21% or higher)
  • Septic shock
  • Multi-organ failure
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)

Organ-specific

  • Acute kidney injury (AKI)
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
  • Heart failure
  • Central nervous system dysfunction

Infectious Complications

  • Invasive and atypical infections (Legionella, Mycoplasma)
  • Reactivation of latent viruses (CMV, EBV)
  • Invasive fungal infections (Candida, Aspergillus)
  • Opportunistic infections due to impaired immune response

Post-Sepsis Syndrome

Affects 40% of people admitted to hospital with sepsis and may include:

Physical Impairments

  • Neurological or neuromuscular issues
  • Loss of digits or limbs
  • Long-term respiratory or renal dysfunction
  • Chronic pain and fatigue

Cognitive & Psychological

  • Difficulty with complex tasks
  • Memory problems
  • Anxiety about recurrent infection
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder

Prognosis

  • Neutropenic sepsis is a potentially life-threatening, time-critical medical emergency
  • People who are immunocompromised with neutropenia have lower survival rates from sepsis than immunocompetent people
  • Mortality rates are lower than 5% in low-risk patients and estimated at 40% for people at high risk
  • Neutropenia is independently associated with a poor outcome and increased mortality
