Uveitis Quick Scan
Updated: Aug 22, 2025
POTENTIALLY BLINDING CONDITION

Uveitis is inflammation of the uvea. All types are potentially blinding and require referral to an experienced ophthalmologist.

DEFINITION

Inflammation of the vascular area between the retina and sclera. Affects iris (anterior), ciliary body, or choroid (posterior).

Rapid Assessment
Feature Anterior Uveitis Posterior Uveitis
Pain Yes (Common) No (Painless)
Redness Yes (Ciliary flush) No/Minimal
Vision Blurred Decreased
Key Signs Cells/Flare, Keratic Precipitates Vitreous cells, Choroiditis

Red Flags (Synechiae)

Look for Iris adherence to lens (Posterior) or cornea (Anterior). Common in chronic anterior uveitis.

Duration Classification
  • Acute: Sudden onset (hours/days), ≤ 3 months.
    Causes: HLA-B27, Infection, Trauma, Idiopathic.
  • Chronic: Persistent > 3 months, relapses within 3 months of stopping therapy.
    Causes: Sarcoidosis, Fuchs', TB, Idiopathic. Note: Not associated with HLA-B27.
  • Recurrent: Episodes separated by ≥3 months of inactivity.
Management Protocol
Treat Systemic + Ocular
1
Acute Non-Infectious Anterior Uveitis

First-line: Topical ophthalmic corticosteroids (e.g., Prednisolone acetate 1%).

Cycloplegics (e.g., Cyclopentolate) for pain relief and to prevent synechiae.

2
Intermediate & Posterior Uveitis

Requires more aggressive therapy: Local corticosteroid injections OR systemic corticosteroids, or other immunosuppressants.

3
Infectious Uveitis (Rule out first!)

Do NOT use steroids alone if infection is suspected. Treat cause (Antiviral for HSV/VZV/CMV, Antibiotics for TB/Syphilis/Toxoplasmosis) + adjunctive steroids.

Clinical Reference

Aetiology (Causes)
Click to expand
Infectious (30-60%)
  • Viral: HSV, VZV, CMV, HIV
  • Bacterial: Syphilis, TB, Lyme
  • Parasitic: Toxoplasmosis, Toxocariasis
Non-Infectious
  • HLA-B27 associated (Ankylosing Spondylitis, etc.)
  • Sarcoidosis, Behçet's, SLE
  • Multiple Sclerosis, IBD
  • Trauma / Drug-induced
Key Investigations
Click to expand
  • Clinical Exam: Slit-lamp (Cells/Flare), Dilated Fundus (Vitreous haze).
  • First Episode Unilateral: Usually no further tests needed if non-granulomatous.
  • Bilateral/Recurrent: FBC, ACE (Sarcoidosis), Syphilis Serology (RPR/VDRL/FTA-ABS), HLA-B27.
  • PPD/TB Testing: If HIV, alcohol abuse, or endemic exposure.
Demographic Clues
Click to expand
Age 20-60: Most Common

Young adults: HLA-B27, MS, Behçet's.

Middle-aged: Sarcoidosis, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada.

Ancestry/Risk:

Middle East/Asia: Behçet's, VKH. White people: HLA-B27. Black people: Sarcoidosis risk.

Masquerade Syndromes

Disorders that mimic uveitis but are NOT autoimmune/infectious.

  • Neoplastic: Intra-ocular lymphoma, Leukemia.
  • Non-Neoplastic: Retinitis Pigmentosa.
Consider if: Poor response to steroids, atypical presentation, older adult.
