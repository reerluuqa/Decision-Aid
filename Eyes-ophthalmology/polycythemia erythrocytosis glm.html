Polycythaemia/Erythrocytosis Clinical Reference

Last revised: September 2024
URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED if polycythaemia vera is suspected and/or the person is experiencing symptoms of hyperviscosity
Red Flag Symptoms
  • Chest and abdominal pain
  • Headache with 'fullness' in head/neck
  • Blurred vision or temporary vision loss
  • Pruritus (especially with warm water)
  • Abdominal discomfort (splenomegaly)
  • History of thrombosis or Budd-Chiari syndrome
Diagnostic Criteria
Parameter Threshold
Haematocrit (Male) > 0.52
Haematocrit (Female) > 0.48
Red cell mass > 25% above predicted
Immediate investigation Hct > 0.6 (M) / > 0.56 (F)
Primary Erythrocytosis (Polycythaemia Vera)
  • Neoplastic proliferation of hematopoietic cells
  • May include thrombocytosis, neutrophil leucocytosis
  • ~95% have JAK2 V617F mutation
  • ~4% have JAK2 exon 12 mutations
  • Risk of thrombosis 1.6x higher than background
Secondary Erythrocytosis
  • Increased erythropoietin production
  • Often due to chronic hypoxia (COPD, sleep apnoea)
  • Kidney disorders (renal artery stenosis, tumors)
  • Performance-enhancing drugs
  • Hereditary genetic mutations
Idiopathic Erythrocytosis
  • No identifiable cause
  • May have latent cause that becomes apparent
  • Sometimes resolves spontaneously
Apparent Erythrocytosis
  • Increased Hct/Hb with normal red-cell mass
  • Caused by reduced plasma volume
  • Risk factors: high BMI, diuretics, smoking, alcohol
  • No further investigations needed
1
Initial Assessment

Check if erythrocytosis is found incidentally on FBC. Repeat after minimum 1 week to confirm persistence.

2
History & Examination
  • Age over 40 years?
  • Personal/family history of thrombosis?
  • Obesity, smoking, alcohol excess?
  • Cardiac/respiratory symptoms?
  • Sleep disturbances?
3
Initial Investigations
  • FBC (Hb, Hct, WBC, platelets, MCV)
  • LFTs, U&E, e-GFR
  • Urine dipstick
4
Further Investigations (if indicated)
  • Serum erythropoietin
  • JAK2 V617F mutation test
  • Serum ferritin (if low MCV)
  • Serum uric acid (optional)
  • Abdominal ultrasound (if splenomegaly suspected)

Immediate Referral Required

  • Suspected polycythaemia vera
  • Symptoms of hyperviscosity
  • JAK2 V617F negative but other features of myeloproliferative disease
  • Elevated erythropoietin without chronic hypoxic disorder
  • Suspected cyanotic heart disease
  • Suspected hypoxic lung disease
  • Suspected sleep apnoea
  • Suspected renal disease
Polycythaemia Vera
  • URGENT REFERRAL to haematologist
  • Annual follow-up in primary care
  • Monitor for transformation to myelofibrosis or AML
  • Manage cardiovascular risk factors
Secondary Erythrocytosis
  • Refer to appropriate specialist for underlying cause
  • Monitor haematocrit levels (target: 0.54)
  • Consider venesection if Hct elevated
  • Manage cardiovascular risk factors
Apparent Erythrocytosis
  • Address factors reducing plasma volume:
  • Smoking cessation
  • Reduce alcohol consumption
  • Weight management
  • Blood pressure control
  • Consider alternative to thiazide diuretics
  • No further investigations needed
Cardiovascular Risk Management
  • Assess risk at diagnosis and annually (QRISK3)
  • Manage hyperlipidaemia
  • Manage diabetes
  • Manage hypertension
  • Encourage smoking cessation
Clinical Tip: Iron replacement should be used judiciously in people with erythrocytosis as it may exacerbate the condition.
