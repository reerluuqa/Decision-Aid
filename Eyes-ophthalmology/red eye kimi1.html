Red Flag Signs & Symptoms Emergency

‚ö†Ô∏è Immediate Referral Required

  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Moderate to severe pain
  • Significant headache
  • Photophobia (especially if severe)
  • Significant ciliary injection
  • Fluorescein staining with significant ulcer
  • Unequal or misshapen pupils
  • Abnormal pupillary reactions
  • Pain on pupillary constriction
  • Loss of red reflex
  • Corneal defect/haze
  • Hyphaema or hypopyon

‚ö° High-Risk Features

  • High-velocity injury (hammering, chiseling)
  • Glass-related injury
  • Foreign body or penetrating lesion
  • Chemical injury
  • Contact lens use
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis (<28 days)

Differential Diagnosis
‚ñº

Serious & Sight-Threatening Causes

üö® Acute Glaucoma Emergency

  • Symptoms: Severe unilateral pain, headache, blurred vision with halos, nausea/vomiting
  • Signs: Hard tender eye, ciliary injection, fixed mid-dilated pupil, reduced VA
  • Precipitants: Pupil dilating drops, tricyclic antidepressants

üî• Anterior Uveitis Urgent

  • Symptoms: Pain worse with ciliary muscle contraction, photophobia, watering
  • Signs: Ciliary injection, constricted/distorted pupil, variable vision
  • Associations: Ankylosing spondylitis, other inflammatory disorders

üëÅÔ∏è Corneal Causes Emergency

  • Ulcer (Bacterial/Viral/Fungal): Foreign body sensation, photophobia, blurred vision, discharge, severe injection
  • Contact Lens-Related: Similar to ulcer, high-risk in lens wearers
  • Foreign Body: History of wind exposure or tools, visible foreign body, normal VA usually

üë∂ Neonatal Conjunctivitis Emergency

  • Definition: Conjunctivitis with discharge in first 28 days
  • Serious Causes: Gonorrhea (risk of corneal ulceration), Chlamydia (may have systemic infection)
  • Differentiate: From lacrimal duct obstruction (no conjunctival inflammation)

‚ö° Scleritis Urgent

  • Symptoms: Severe deep boring pain
  • Associations: Rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, other connective tissue diseases
  • Signs: Reduced VA, abnormal pupil reactions, risk of globe perforation

ü¶† Endophthalmitis Emergency

  • History: Recent intraocular surgery (within 2 weeks)
  • Symptoms: Markedly reduced vision, severe pain/headache
  • Signs: Hypopyon (pus in anterior chamber)

üí• Trauma Emergency

  • Penetrating Injury: High-velocity injuries (hammering, chiseling), glass injuries
  • Foreign Body: May not always be visible, treat as penetrating until proven otherwise

üß™ Chemical Injury Emergency

  • Risk: Alkali solutions particularly harmful
  • Action: Immediate copious irrigation before assessment
  • Complications: Long-term ocular surface problems

Non-Sight-Threatening Causes

üòä Conjunctivitis Primary Care

  • Allergic: Itching, watery discharge, bilateral, history of atopy
  • Infective: Bacterial (purulent) or viral (watery), may be unilateral initially

ü©∏ Subconjunctival Hemorrhage Primary Care

  • Appearance: Localized well-demarcated bright red patch
  • Features: No pain, normal VA, normal pupils, no corneal staining
  • Risk Factors: Straining, coughing, constipation, hypertension, anticoagulants

ü¶† Episcleritis Primary Care

  • Symptoms: Redness, lacrimation, photophobia, generally painless
  • Signs: Segmental redness, normal VA/pupils, no corneal staining
  • Course: Self-limiting, benign condition

ü™® Corneal Abrasion Primary Care

  • Symptoms: Foreign body sensation, pain, photophobia
  • Signs: Fluorescein staining positive, normal pupils
  • Healing: Usually resolves within 24-48 hours

üòä Adnexal Causes Primary Care

  • Dry Eye Syndrome: Burning, gritty sensation, worse with reading/screen use
  • Blepharitis: Crusting at lid margins, irritation, chronic course
  • Ectropion/Entropion: Lid malposition causing exposure or irritation
  • Trichiasis: Misdirected lashes causing corneal irritation

Treatment & Management
‚ñº

Emergency Referral Same Day - Ophthalmology

Immediate referral to emergency eye service if any of the following are suspected:

üö® Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Action: Emergency same-day ophthalmology assessment

Pre-hospital: Seat patient upright, nil by mouth, arrange immediate transfer

üëÅÔ∏è Corneal Laceration

Action: Same-day ophthalmology assessment

Pre-hospital: Do NOT press on globe, shield eye, urgent transfer

ü™® Corneal Foreign Body

Action: Same-day ophthalmology if cannot be removed in primary care

Pre-hospital: Do not attempt removal if deep or central

üí° Contact Lens-Related Ulcer

Action: Emergency same-day assessment

Pre-hospital: Stop lens wear immediately, bring lens/case for culture

ü¶† Corneal Ulcer (Non-Contact Lens)

Action: Same-day ophthalmology assessment

Pre-hospital: Start topical antibiotics if available per protocol

ü¶† Endophthalmitis

Action: Emergency same-day assessment (post-operative infection)

Pre-hospital: Urgent transfer, may need intravitreal antibiotics

‚ú® Intraocular Foreign Body

Action: Emergency same-day assessment

Pre-hospital: CT scan orbit, shield eye, urgent ophthalmology

üë∂ Neonatal Conjunctivitis

Action: Emergency same-day ophthalmology + pediatric assessment

Pre-hospital: Swabs for gonorrhea/chlamydia, systemic evaluation

üí• Penetrating Eye Injury

Action: Emergency same-day assessment

Pre-hospital: Shield eye, NPO, urgent surgical intervention likely

üß™ Chemical Injury IRRIGATE FIRST

Immediate Action: Copious irrigation with water or 0.9% saline until pH normal

Referral: After irrigation if no pH checking facility available

Continue irrigation: During transfer if possible

Urgent Referral Within 24 Hours

üî• Anterior Uveitis

Action: Urgent referral to local eye clinic

Management: Requires slit-lamp confirmation, may need steroid drops

‚ö° Scleritis

Action: Urgent referral to ophthalmology

Management: Systemic NSAIDs or steroids, investigate systemic disease

‚ùì Unclear Diagnosis

Action: Seek ophthalmology advice if diagnosis uncertain after assessment

Management: When in doubt, refer for specialist opinion

Primary Care Management Can be managed in GP

ü©∏ Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

  • Reassurance: Will clear in 1-2 weeks spontaneously
  • BP Check: Measure blood pressure, manage if elevated
  • Anticoagulation: Check INR if on warfarin and manage accordingly
  • Advice: Avoid rubbing eye, no treatment needed

üòä Episcleritis

  • Reassurance: Usually self-limiting, not harmful
  • Symptomatic Relief: Oral NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) may help
  • Lubrication: Artificial tears for comfort
  • Follow-up: Resolves within 1-2 weeks

üëÅÔ∏è Ectropion/Entropion/Trichiasis

  • Red Flags: If exposure keratopathy or corneal irritation signs ‚Üí urgent referral
  • Routine Referral: If no serious features, refer routinely to ophthalmology
  • Management: Surgical correction may be required

Follow-up & Monitoring
‚ñº

Primary Care Follow-up Guidelines

üòä Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

  • Resolution: Should clear within 1-2 weeks
  • Follow-up: If not resolved by 2 weeks, recheck BP and consider hematology review
  • Recurrence: Frequent recurrences warrant hypertension and bleeding disorder screening

ü¶† Episcleritis

  • Course: Usually resolves within 7-10 days
  • Recurrence: Consider investigating for underlying systemic disease if recurrent
  • Referral: Persistent >3 weeks or severe cases ‚Üí ophthalmology

ü§ß Allergic Conjunctivitis

  • Follow-up: Review in 1 week if symptoms persist
  • Escalation: Refer to ophthalmology if no improvement with treatment
  • Chronic: Consider allergy testing if seasonal/perennial

ü¶† Infective Conjunctivitis

  • Bacterial: Review in 3-5 days, should be improving
  • Viral: May take 1-2 weeks to resolve fully
  • Referral: If worsening or not improving by 5-7 days

Post-Referral Monitoring

üè•
Emergency Referrals: Ensure patient attended. If refused, document and advise to seek immediate care if symptoms worsen
‚è∞
Urgent Referrals: Follow up within 24-48 hours to confirm attendance and outcome
üìû
Communication: Liaise with ophthalmology team if any concerns about referral urgency

Safety Netting Advice Important

‚ö†Ô∏è Advise patients to seek URGENT review if they develop:

  • Worsening or severe eye pain
  • Decreasing visual acuity
  • Increased photophobia
  • Colored halos around lights
  • Nausea/vomiting with eye pain
  • Headache with eye symptoms
  • Abnormal pupil appearance
  • Copious purulent discharge
  • Contact lens wearer with worsening symptoms

üìû Routine Follow-up Indicators:

  • Symptoms not improving within expected timeframe
  • Recurrence of red eye episodes
  • New symptoms developing
  • Concerns about diagnosis
  • Need for repeat prescriptions
