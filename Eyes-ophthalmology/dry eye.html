Clinical Summary

Definition

Dry eye disease (DED) is a multifactorial ocular surface disease characterized by:

  • water_drop Loss of tear film homeostasis
  • thermostat Tear film hyperosmolarity and instability
  • inflammation Ocular surface inflammation and damage
  • neurology Neurosensory abnormality
lightbulb Key Point: Symptoms often do NOT correlate with signs. Some patients have severe symptoms with minimal findings, others have significant defects without symptoms.

Differential Diagnosis

Dry eye should always be considered in the differential diagnosis of red eye. Click to expand differentiating features.

Differentiating Signs

  • Watery ‚Üí mucopurulent ‚Üí purulent discharge
  • Preauricular lymphadenopathy (viral)
  • Conjunctival injection (circumcorneal)

Diagnostic Tests

Conjunctival swabs for adenovirus, HSV, HZV, bacteria, chlamydia

Hallmark Feature

ITCH - Itching is the distinguishing symptom

Associated Signs

  • Chemosis (conjunctival swelling)
  • Giant papillae on upper tarsus
  • Bilateral involvement
  • Seasonal or perennial pattern

Key Questions

  • Daily dry mouth > 3 months?
  • Recurrent salivary gland swelling?
  • Need liquids to swallow dry food?

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Anti-Ro/SSA or Anti-La/SSB positive, or RF + ANA ‚â•1:320
  • Ocular staining score ‚â•3
  • Labial salivary gland biopsy: focus score ‚â•1

Severity

Usually more severe DED than non-Sjogren DED

Associated Conditions

  • Rosacea
  • Seborrheic dermatitis

Signs

  • Unstable tear film
  • Rapid tear break-up time
  • Meibomian gland dropout

Key Association

Trauma to upper tarsal plate (contact lens, exposed suture, foreign body)

Hallmark

Large upper tarsal papillae
