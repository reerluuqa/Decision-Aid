Clinical Knowledge Summary - Last Revised July 2025

ACUTE ANGLE CLOSURE GLAUCOMA - MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Recognize and admit people urgently with suspected acute angle closure. This is a sight-threatening medical emergency that is not self-limiting.

Red Flags

Severe eye pain with abrupt onset
Markedly elevated intraocular pressure
Halos around lights
Reduced visual acuity
Fixed mid-dilated pupil
Conjunctival injection

Primary Care Actions

Advise high-risk people to have regular eye examinations
Provide shared care for treated patients
Refer suspected cases promptly
Consider ocular hypertension monitoring

Glaucoma Types

Primary Open-Angle (POAG): Most common, usually asymptomatic until late
Angle Closure (PACG): Can be acute, subacute, or chronic
Ocular Hypertension: Elevated IOP (>21 mmHg) without signs of glaucoma

Diagnosis

Visual field defects
Optic nerve head changes (pathological cupping)
Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP)
Severe eye pain
Markedly elevated IOP
Shallow anterior chamber

Management Goals

Reduce intraocular pressure
Preserve visual function
Prevent progression
Monitor for side effects of treatment

Risk Factors

Risk Factor Type Significance
Elevated IOP High
Main treatable risk factor
Age > 40 Medium
Prevalence increases with age
Family History Medium
First-degree relatives at increased risk
Ethnicity (Black/Asian) Medium
2-3x more common in Black people
Corticosteroid Use Low
May increase IOP in susceptible people
Myopia Low
Associated with increased risk

Prevalence

POAG: ~2% of people >40 years in UK
POAG >75 years: ~10% of UK population
PACG: ~0.4% of people >40 years in UK
Ocular Hypertension: 3-5% of people >40 years

Prognosis

Without Treatment: Slow progression over years
With Treatment: Most won't go blind
Blindness Risk: 5-10% of POAG cases
Non-adherence: Up to 50% of patients fail to receive intended benefits
