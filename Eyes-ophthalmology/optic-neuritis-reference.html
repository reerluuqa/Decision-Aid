URGENT: Red Flags Requiring Immediate Action

Bilateral Simultaneous Onset

Consider NMOSD, MOGAD, or infectious etiology. Order aquaporin-4 and MOG antibodies immediately.

Severe Pain Restricting Eye Movement

Atypical presentation. Rule out orbital cellulitis, cavernous sinus thrombosis, or compressive lesion.

Retinal Hemorrhages / Exudates

Not typical for ON. Consider vascular optic neuropathy, inflammatory vasculitis, or infectious causes.

No Recovery After 3-4 Weeks

Consider atypical etiologies, compressive lesion, or need for plasma exchange. Re-evaluate diagnosis.

Age >50 with Acute Vision Loss

Rule out giant cell arteritis (ESR/CRP urgently). Risk of permanent contralateral blindness.

Progressive Visual Loss >2 Weeks

Not typical for ON. Consider compressive optic neuropathy, infiltrative, or neoplastic causes.

Definition & Overview

Inflammation of the optic nerve. Most common form is idiopathic demyelinating ON, often a clinically isolated syndrome (CIS) of MS.

Classic Presentation

  • Subacute visual loss over 2-7 days, peaks at 1-2 weeks
  • Peri-orbital pain worsened by eye movement (90%)
  • Dyschromatopsia - color desaturation (often > acuity loss)
  • Central/centrocaecal scotoma (only 8% classically)

Key Clinical Signs

  • RAPD - Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (>90% unilateral)
  • Normal disc in 2/3 (retrobulbar ON)
  • Disc edema in 1/3 (papillitis)
  • Visual acuity - 20/200 or worse in 1/3

Quick Reference: Symptoms vs Signs

Patient Symptoms

  • Blurred vision "as if through fog"
  • Eye pain with movement
  • Colors appear "washed out"
  • Uhthoff's: worsening with heat (exercise, bath)
  • Pulfrich's: altered depth perception
  • Photopsias (positive visual phenomena)

Examination Findings

  • RAPD - Swinging flashlight test
  • Reduced visual acuity (variable)
  • Color vision loss (Ishihara)
  • Visual field defect (any pattern)
  • Diffuse defect most common
  • Normal or swollen optic disc

Treatment Algorithm - Acute Episode

1

Confirm Typical ON Presentation

Unilateral, painful, subacute visual loss with RAPD. Rule out red flags above.

Clinical Diagnosis
2

High-Dose IV Methylprednisolone

1000mg IV daily x 3 days (or oral bioequivalent). Faster recovery, reduced recurrence risk.

Methylprednisolone 1g IV/PO daily x 3 days
3

No Oral Steroid Taper Needed

Evidence shows no additional benefit. Oral tail often omitted in practice. Avoid low-dose oral alone.

No taper required
4

Refractory ON - Consider Plasma Exchange

If no improvement after 2-3 weeks of steroids. 5-7 exchanges over 2 weeks.

Plasma Exchange (PLEX)

Treatment Key Points

  • DO Start steroids within 48 hours if possible
  • DO Use IV or oral methylprednisolone 1g/day x 3 days
  • AVOID Low-dose oral prednisone alone (increases relapse risk)
  • CAUTION Gastric protection with PPI if needed
  • IVIG second-line for refractory cases if PLEX unavailable

Special Situations

  • NMOSD/MOGAD: Prolonged oral steroids + steroid-sparing agent (azathioprine, mycophenolate)
  • SLE/Sarcoid: Treat underlying disease; higher dose/longer steroids
  • MS Risk: Abnormal MRI → refer for disease-modifying therapy consideration

Prognosis

  • Good visual prognosis: ~92% achieve 20/40 or better
  • Recurrence risk: ~30% at 5 years
  • Recovery typically over weeks to months
  • Steroids speed recovery but not final outcome

Follow-up Plan

  • Monitor visual recovery over 3-6 months
  • Repeat MRI at 6 months if CIS and normal initial
  • Refer to MS specialist if abnormal MRI
  • Patient education on recurrence symptoms
