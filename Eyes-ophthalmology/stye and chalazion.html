Stye & Chalazion Management

BMJ Best Practice Guidelines (July 2024) | Interactive Clinical Decision Support Tool

Assessment

Key History Points

  • Onset & Duration: Acute (stye) vs. chronic (chalazion)
  • Pain: Eyelid pain only (common); globe pain/red flags for orbital cellulitis
  • Vision Changes: Blurred vision suggests large chalazion or corneal involvement
  • Recurrence: Chronic/recurrent lesions may require biopsy to exclude malignancy
  • Associated Symptoms: Discharge, crusting, constitutional symptoms

Red Flags

Orbital Cellulitis Signs Child with Large Lesion Chronic/Recurrent Lesions Vision Changes
URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED IF: Eye pain with movement, decreased vision, proptosis, marked chemosis, or systemic symptoms suggest orbital cellulitis.

Risk Factors

Blepharitis & Ocular Rosacea
Chronic inflammation obstructs gland drainage
Seborrheic Dermatitis
Altered sebum composition increases stasis
Diabetes Mellitus
May predispose to infections (evidence mixed)
Poor Eyelid Hygiene
Contact lens wear, makeup use
Elevated Serum Cholesterol
Increases gland blockage risk
Age 30-50 Years
Higher sebum viscosity in adults

Diagnosis

Clinical Features

Stye (Hordeolum)

  • Acute, painful, erythematous swelling
  • External: Pustule at lid margin (Zeis/Moll glands)
  • Internal: Diffuse swelling, may point to conjunctiva (meibomian gland)
  • Tender to palpation
  • Resolves in 5-7 days
Infectious

Chalazion

  • Chronic, non-tender nodule
  • Localized inflammatory reaction to sebum
  • No erythema or acute signs
  • Persists weeks to months (up to 6 months)
  • May cause astigmatism if large
Non-infectious

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Acute eyelid pain: Localized, no globe pain
  • Pustule identification: At lid margin (external) or tarsal conjunctiva (internal)
  • Non-tender nodule: Suggests chalazion
  • Lack of constitutional symptoms: Indicates localized process
  • Normal globe examination: No intraocular pathology

Differential Diagnoses

Conditions that may mimic stye/chalazion:
  • Blepharitis: Diffuse lid inflammation vs. discrete nodule
  • Dacryocystitis: Tender swollen mass below medial epicanthus
  • Dacryoadenitis: Pain/swelling at lateral superior lid
  • Pre-septal/Orbital Cellulitis: Diffuse swelling, systemic symptoms, vision changes
  • Malignancy: Persistent ulcerated lesions (biopsy required)
Biopsy for Recurrent/Chronic Lesions CT if Alternative Diagnosis Suspected

Investigations

Typical case: Clinical diagnosis only - no tests required

Consider investigation if:

  • Recurrent or chronic lesions → Biopsy to exclude malignancy
  • Atypical features → CT scan to rule out orbital cellulitis or lacrimal system involvement

Treatment

Stye (Hordeolum) Management

1
Warm Compresses & Massage

4-5 times daily for 10-15 minutes. May be accompanied by gentle massage.

Self-care
2
Lid Hygiene

Wash eyelids with diluted baby shampoo if associated blepharitis.

Routine Care
3
Topical Antibiotics

Consider for copious mucopurulent discharge (e.g., erythromycin 0.5% up to 6×/day)

If Discharge Present

Evidence: Cochrane review found no evidence of effectiveness for internal hordeola

4
Oral Antibiotics

For significant surrounding cellulitis

Cefalexin 250-500mg q6-12h or Amoxicillin/clavulanate 500mg q8h

Chalazion Management

1
Conservative Treatment

Warm compresses & lid hygiene for up to 6 months. >50% resolve spontaneously.

Up to 6 months
2
Antibiotics

NOT indicated (non-infectious condition)

Not Recommended
3
Invasive Therapies (Specialist Referral)

For recurrent, large, or refractory chalazia (>2 months duration)

Intralesional Triamcinolone Incision & Curettage

Similar recurrence rates; both superior to compresses alone

Ophthalmology Referral Criteria

Urgent: Orbital Cellulitis Signs Urgent: Child with Large Chalazion (Risk of Amblyopia) Urgent: Visual Distortion Refractory >2 months Recurrent/Chronic Lesion (Biopsy) Incision & Drainage Needed

Emerging Therapies

  • Acupuncture: Low-certainty evidence for short-term benefit in acute hordeola
  • Microblepharoexfoliation: Adjunctive treatment showing resolution of chalazia
  • Optimized Pulse Technology: Reduces chalazion size and improves meibomian gland function

Follow-up

Monitoring

  • Uncomplicated cases: No follow-up needed if resolved
  • Persistent/recurrent lesions: Ophthalmology follow-up
  • Chronic chalazia: May require corticosteroid injection or excision
  • Suspicious lesions: Biopsy to exclude malignancy

Complications

Chalazion formation from stye Peri-orbital/Orbital Cellulitis (Rare) Cosmetic deformity (Low) Visual disturbance (Large lesions)

Prognosis

Stye (Hordeolum)

  • If treated: Resolves in 5-7 days
  • Early treatment: Symptoms resolve in 1-2 days
  • Recurrence: Common; teach self-recognition
  • Cosmetic outcome: Excellent

Chalazion

  • Resolution: Several weeks to 6 months
  • Spontaneous resolution: >50% of cases
  • Recurrence: Common; exact rate unknown
  • More refractory than hordeola

Patient Education

Apply Warm Compresses Promptly Maintain Lid Hygiene DO NOT Squeeze or Attempt Drainage Replace Makeup Every 3 Months
Prevention Strategies: Hand/face washing, makeup hygiene, contact lens care, treat blepharitis/rosacea, control cholesterol.
