Squint in Children - Quick Reference

Last revised: April 2024
URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED
Constant unilateral exotropia, acquired incomitant squint, or sudden late-onset esotropia (>3-4 years) with no family history or significant hypermetropia
Quick Assessment
1
History: Onset, nature, associated symptoms, past medical history, family history
2
Examination: General inspection, eye movements, corneal light reflex test, cover test
3
Red Flags: Absent red reflex, abnormal head posture, neurological signs
Definition & Classification
Squint (strabismus): Misalignment of the visual axis where eyes are not directed at an object simultaneously
Type Description
Esotropia Eye turns inward (convergent)
Exotropia Eye turns outward (divergent)
Hypertropia Eye turns upward
Hypotropia Eye turns downward
Red Flags & Urgent Referral Indicators
Absent red reflex - May indicate retinoblastoma
Constant unilateral exotropia - Requires urgent assessment
Acquired incomitant squint - May indicate neurological disease
Sudden late-onset esotropia (>3-4 years) with no family history
Abnormal head posture - May indicate superior oblique weakness
Neurological signs - May indicate cerebral palsy or other conditions
Restricted eye movements - May indicate paralytic squint
Constant squint after 3 months - Unlikely to resolve without treatment
Management Approach
  • Recognize and urgently refer children with serious underlying cause
  • Arrange routine referral to paediatric eye service for all other children
  • Provide shared care for children being treated in secondary care
  • Glasses for refractive errors (especially accommodative esotropia)
  • Occlusion therapy for amblyopia
  • Orthoptic exercises
  • Surgical correction in selected cases
Complications & Prognosis
Amblyopia (lazy eye) - Most common complication, can cause permanent vision loss if untreated
Loss of binocular vision - Results in poor stereopsis (depth perception)
Compensatory head postures - May develop to maintain binocular vision
Social/psychological problems - Can affect self-esteem and social integration
Prognosis: Good with timely treatment. Amblyopia needs treatment by age 7 years for best outcomes.
Additional Information
  • UK prevalence: 2.1% in children
  • Esotropias are most common (60.1% of all squints)
  • Prevalence varies by ethnicity (3.3% in white children, 2.1% in African American children)
  • Refractive errors (especially hypermetropia)
  • Poor visual acuity or blindness in one or both eyes
  • Neurodevelopmental conditions (cerebral palsy, Down syndrome)
  • Congenital abnormalities of extra-ocular muscles
  • Neurological diseases (brain tumors, head injury)
  • Low birth weight and prematurity
  • Maternal smoking during pregnancy
  • Anisometropia (unequal refractive power)
  • Hypermetropia (long-sightedness)
  • Family history of squint
  • Corneal light reflex test (Hirschberg): Observe reflected light from corneas
  • Cover test: Detect manifest squint by covering one eye and watching for movement
  • Cover/uncover test: Detect latent squint by covering and uncovering each eye
  • Range of eye movements: Assess if eyes can move fully in all directions
