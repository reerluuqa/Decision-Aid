Corneal Superficial Injury

Quick Reference Tool for Primary Care Assessment

IMMEDIATE REFERRAL REQUIRED

  • Suspected penetrating eye injury or intraocular foreign body
  • Significant orbital or peri-ocular trauma
  • Chemical injury (immediate irrigation needed)
  • Severe pain with irregular, dilated or non-reactive pupils
  • Significant reduction in visual acuity
  • Hyphema (blood in anterior chamber) or hypopyon (pus)
  • Large or deep abrasions, corneal opacity
  • Suspected infection or corneal ulcer
  • Inability to tolerate examination or foreign body removal

Clinical Features

  • Sudden onset eye pain on blinking
  • Foreign body sensation ('gritty' or 'scratching')
  • Lacrimation (tearing)
  • Photophobia
  • Decreased or blurred vision
  • Conjunctival redness
  • Blepharospasm

Assessment Approach

Step Action
History Onset, mechanism, precipitating factors
Visual Acuity Check with Snellen chart
External Exam Check for signs of perforation
Fluorescein Use cobalt blue filter to identify defects

Red Flag Assessment

  • Check pupillary shape and response
  • Look for hyphema or hypopyon
  • Assess for penetrating trauma signs
  • Check for corneal opacity

Common Causes

  • Mechanical trauma (fingernail, twig, paper)
  • Foreign bodies (dust, glass, rust)
  • Chemical or radiation burns
  • Contact lens issues
  • Recurrent erosions

Management in Primary Care

  1. Remove loose superficial foreign bodies if expertise and equipment available
  2. Consider analgesia (paracetamol) and ocular lubricants
  3. Consider prescription of topical antibiotics if risk of infection
  4. Arrange follow-up in 24 hours
  5. Advise patient on eye protection and avoiding contact lenses
Do not prescribe: Topical analgesia or cycloplegics

Prognosis

  • Most uncomplicated small corneal abrasions heal within 1-2 days
  • Lesions affecting >50% of corneal surface may take 4-5 days
  • Chemical injuries prognosis depends on pH concentration and duration of contact

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Features
Penetrating eye injury History of high-velocity injury, visible foreign body
Acute angle-closure glaucoma Severe pain, halos around lights, fixed mid-dilated pupil
Infective keratitis Pain, photophobia, discharge, corneal infiltrate
Recurrent erosion syndrome History of previous corneal injury, pain on waking
Conjunctivitis Discharge, itching, less severe pain

Complications

  • Corneal ulceration
  • Infective keratitis
  • Iritis
  • Recurrent erosion syndrome
