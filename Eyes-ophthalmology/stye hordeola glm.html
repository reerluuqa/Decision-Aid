Styes (Hordeola) Clinical Reference

Emergency Situations

  • Arrange emergency hospital admission for periorbital or orbital cellulitis
  • Same-day ophthalmological assessment for corneal disease, rapid-onset visual loss, or painful/red eye
  • Suspected cancer pathway referral for malignant eyelid cancer

Diagnosis

External Stye

  • Tender, inflamed swelling of eyelid margin
  • Usually around an eyelash follicle
  • May point anteriorly through skin

Internal Stye

  • Tender, inflamed swelling of external eyelid
  • More painful than external stye
  • Located further from lid margin
  • Localized swelling of tarsal plate when eyelid everted

Management

Self-care: Apply warm compress to closed eyelid for 5-10 minutes, 2-4 times daily Routine
Reassure: Styes are usually self-limiting and rarely cause serious complications Routine
Avoid: Eye makeup, contact lenses, and puncturing the stye Routine
Consider: Plucking eyelash or incision and drainage for painful external stye Specialist
Antibiotics: Not routinely indicated, consider topical antibiotic for copious mucopurulent discharge Specialist

Complications

  • Infective conjunctivitis - infection of eyelid margin can spread
  • Meibomian cyst - persistent internal stye may develop into chronic granuloma
  • Cellulitis - untreated stye can lead to localized cellulitis
  • Periorbital cellulitis - infection of tissues in front of orbital septum
  • Orbital cellulitis - infection of tissues behind orbital septum (medical emergency)

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Features
Blepharitis Chronic inflammation of eyelid margin
Meibomian cyst (chalazion) Chronic, non-infectious inflammatory granuloma
Dacryocystitis Pain/swelling in inner corner of eye, redness, purulent discharge
Dacryoadenitis Pain/swelling in upper outer eyelid
Periorbital cellulitis Swelling, redness, tenderness of lids without visual impairment
Eyelid cancer Atypical features: distortion, ulceration, bleeding, chronic/recurrent lesions

Background Information

Definition

Acute, localized infection or inflammation of the apocrine or sebaceous glands of the eyelid, usually caused by staphylococcal infection.

Prognosis

Usually self-limiting, with symptoms resolving within 5-7 days. Recurrence is common in people with persistent risk factors.

Risk Factors

  • Poor eyelid hygiene
  • Chronic blepharitis
  • Acne rosacea
  • Elevated serum cholesterol
  • Decreased immunity (e.g., diabetes)
