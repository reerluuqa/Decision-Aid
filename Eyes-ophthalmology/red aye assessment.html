Red Eye Assessment

Quick Clinical Reference Guide

Scan Urgency First

Emergency Conditions - Immediate Action Required

Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Key Symptoms

  • Severe ocular pain
  • Halos around lights
  • Decreased vision
  • Mid-dilated oval pupil
  • Corneal edema (hazy cornea)
  • Nausea/vomiting

Immediate Action

  • URGENT ophthalmology referral
  • Topical beta-blocker, alpha-agonist, pilocarpine
  • IV acetazolamide if available

Chemical Injury

Key Features

  • Acid or alkali exposure
  • Severe pain, tearing
  • Blurred vision
  • White cornea (alkali burns)

Immediate Action

  • IMMEDIATE irrigation - copious water
  • Check pH until normalized
  • Urgent ophthalmology referral

Penetrating Trauma

Key Features

  • Visible wound/laceration
  • Irregular pupil
  • Shallow anterior chamber
  • Hypopyon
  • Decreased vision

Immediate Action

  • Shield eye - DO NOT patch
  • Nil by mouth
  • URGENT ophthalmology referral

Corneal Ulcer

Key Symptoms

  • Severe pain
  • Photophobia
  • Decreased vision
  • White spot on cornea
  • Contact lens wearer

Immediate Action

  • Fluorescein stain - corneal defect visible
  • Urgent ophthalmology referral
  • Start topical antibiotics if delay

Anterior Uveitis

Key Symptoms

  • Moderate-severe pain
  • Photophobia
  • Blurred vision
  • Small irregular pupil
  • Ciliary flush

Immediate Action

  • Urgent ophthalmology referral
  • Topical steroids may be needed
  • Screen for systemic disease

Scleritis

Key Symptoms

  • Severe deep pain
  • Worse at night
  • Pain with eye movement
  • Purple/blue hue
  • Decreased vision

Immediate Action

  • Urgent ophthalmology referral
  • Screen for autoimmune disease
  • Often requires systemic treatment

Red Flags - Requires Urgent Assessment

ANY of these signs warrant IMMEDIATE specialist referral

Visual Symptoms

  • ⚠ Sudden vision loss
  • ⚠ Significant visual decline
  • ⚠ Halos around lights
  • ⚠ Photophobia with pain
  • ⚠ Diplopia

Pain Indicators

  • ⚠ Severe ocular pain
  • ⚠ Pain with eye movement
  • ⚠ Deep orbital pain
  • ⚠ Pain waking patient at night

Examination Findings

  • ⚠ Corneal opacity/staining
  • ⚠ Irregular pupil shape
  • ⚠ Hypopyon (white cells in AC)
  • ⚠ Shallow anterior chamber
  • ⚠ Corneal edema
  • ⚠ Ciliary flush
  • ⚠ Reduced corneal reflex

History Factors

  • ⚠ Chemical exposure
  • ⚠ Penetrating trauma
  • ⚠ Contact lens use
  • ⚠ Recent eye surgery
  • ⚠ Immunocompromised state
  • ⚠ Known autoimmune disease

Quick Decision Tree

1 Vision normal? No → URGENT referral
2 Pain severe? Yes → Check IOP + cornea
3 Cornea clear? No → Fluorescein test
4 Pupil normal? No → URGENT referral

Diagnostic Approach

History Taking Checklist

□ Onset - sudden or gradual?
□ Laterality - one or both eyes?
□ Pain - absent/mild/moderate/severe?
□ Discharge - type and amount?
□ Vision changes - any decrease?
□ Photophobia - present?
□ Trauma history - any injury?
□ Contact lens use?
□ Recent illness - viral/respiratory?
□ Allergies - seasonal/other?
□ Previous eye problems?
□ Systemic diseases - autoimmune/other?

Examination Checklist

□ Visual acuity - both eyes
□ Pupils - size, shape, reaction
□ External inspection - lid position
□ Conjunctiva - injection pattern
□ Cornea - clarity, staining
□ Anterior chamber - depth, cells
□ Iris - abnormalities
□ Fluorescein staining - defects
□ Extraocular movements - pain/limitation
□ Intraocular pressure - if indicated
□ Preauricular lymph nodes
□ Skin - facial/eyelid involvement

Risk Factors & Associations

Anterior Uveitis Risk Factors

  • Genetic HLA-B27 positive
  • Infectious Tuberculosis
  • Infectious Syphilis
  • Infectious Lyme disease
  • Inflammatory Sarcoidosis
  • Inflammatory Behçet's disease
  • Inflammatory Juvenile chronic arthritis

Scleritis Risk Factors

  • Autoimmune Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Autoimmune Granulomatosis with polyangiitis
  • Autoimmune Systemic lupus erythematosus
  • Autoimmune Relapsing polychondritis

Episcleritis Risk Factors

  • Autoimmune Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Autoimmune Granulomatosis with polyangiitis
  • Autoimmune Systemic lupus erythematosus

Special Considerations

  • COVID-19 May present with viral conjunctivitis features
  • Contact Lens Higher risk of bacterial/fungal ulcers
  • Elderly Increased risk of angle-closure glaucoma
  • Immunocompromised Higher risk of atypical infections

Key Points Summary

Emergency - Refer Immediately

  • Angle-closure glaucoma
  • Penetrating trauma
  • Chemical injury
  • Corneal ulcer with VA loss
  • Scleritis

Urgent - Same Day Review

  • Corneal abrasion
  • Anterior uveitis
  • Contact lens issues
  • Blunt trauma with hyphema risk
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis

Routine - Primary Care Management

  • Bacterial conjunctivitis
  • Viral conjunctivitis
  • Allergic conjunctivitis
  • Blepharitis
  • Dry eye
