Urinary Incontinence in Women

Clinical Knowledge Summary
Last revised: January 2025
Overview Definition Causes Prevalence Complications Assessment Management Referral Updates

Overview

Topic Coverage

This CKS topic covers the primary care management of urinary incontinence (UI) in women from age 18 years onwards.

It does not cover specialist management of UI in women or management of UI in men or children.

Target Audience

Healthcare professionals working within the NHS in the UK, providing first contact or primary healthcare.

Definition

Urinary incontinence is defined as any involuntary leakage of urine.

Stress Urinary Incontinence
Involuntary leakage on effort, exertion, sneezing, or coughing.
Urgency Urinary Incontinence
Involuntary leakage accompanied or immediately preceded by urgency.
Mixed Urinary Incontinence
Involuntary leakage associated with both urgency and physical stress.
Other Types
  • Overflow incontinence (chronic urinary retention)
  • Continuous urinary incontinence
  • Situational incontinence

Causes and Contributing Factors

Stress UI Risk Factors
  • Pregnancy, parity, and vaginal delivery
  • Obesity
  • High-impact activities
  • Pelvic and vaginal surgeries
  • Genetic and family history
  • Constipation
  • Smoking
  • Menopause
  • Certain medications (ACE inhibitors, alpha-blockers)
Urgency UI Risk Factors
  • Neurological conditions (Parkinson's, MS, stroke)
  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Urinary tract obstruction
  • Diabetes
  • Menopause
  • Caffeinated or alcoholic drinks
  • High or low fluid intake
  • Smoking
  • Certain medications (sympathomimetics, antidepressants, diuretics)

Prevalence

Key Statistics
  • Estimated 34% of UK women are living with UI
  • Prevalence increases with age, particularly in postmenopausal period
  • Only 17% of women with UI seek professional help
  • Stress UI is most common (24%), followed by mixed UI (20%) and OAB/UUI (7%)
Global Variation
In women aged 55-106 years, prevalence varies by region:
  • Highest in Asia (45.1%)
  • Lowest in America (25.8%)
Age Patterns
  • SUI peak age: 35-44 years
  • MUI peak age: 55-64 years
  • OAB peak age: 65-74 years
  • UUI has two peaks: ≤24 years and >75 years

Complications

Emotional & Psychological
  • Low self-esteem and self-confidence
  • Anxiety and depression
  • Embarrassment or feelings of shame
Physical
  • Skin breakdown and infections
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Falls (from slipping on urine)
Sexual & Social
  • Reduced intimacy and affection
  • Avoidance of sexual activity
  • Social isolation
  • Sleep disturbance
Economic
  • Cost of absorbent products and laundry
  • Reduced employment and productivity
  • Annual NHS cost: £536 million
  • Total yearly service costs: £743 million

Assessment

History
  • Categorize UI type (SUI, UUI/OAB, MUI)
  • Ask about associated voiding and urinary symptoms
  • Identify underlying causes and contributing factors
  • Ask about medications that may cause or exacerbate UI
  • Inquire about possible complications
Examination
  • General examination (weight, gait, neurological signs)
  • Abdominal examination for palpable bladder
  • Pelvic examination:
    • Observe leakage with cough
    • Check for prolapse, atrophic vaginitis, urethral diverticulum
    • Assess pelvic floor muscle tone and contraction
Investigations
  • Urine dipstick analysis (all women)
  • MSU culture if UTI suspected
  • Renal function assessment if obstruction suspected
  • Bladder diary (minimum 3 days)
Pelvic Floor Muscle Assessment (Oxford Grading)
0-2 (Weak)
  • 0 = No contraction
  • 1 = Flicker or pulsation
  • 2 = Weak contraction without lift
3-5 (Strong)
  • 3 = Moderate contraction with lift
  • 4 = Good contraction against resistance
  • 5 = Strong contraction with squeeze

Management

First-line Treatments
  • SUI Supervised pelvic floor muscle training (≥3 months)
  • UUI/OAB Bladder training (≥6 weeks)
  • MUI Combination of approaches above
Pharmacotherapy for OAB
  • Antimuscarinic medications
  • Mirabegron (β3-adrenoceptor agonist)
  • New (2024) Vibegron (if antimuscarinics unsuitable/ineffective)
  • Desmopressin for troublesome nocturia and urgency incontinence
Lifestyle Modifications
  • Weight loss if BMI >30 kg/m²
  • Fluid intake adjustment
  • Caffeine reduction (especially for OAB)
  • Smoking cessation
  • Management of constipation
Containment Products

Should be offered only as:

  • A temporary coping strategy, or
  • Long-term management if treatment is unsuccessful

Referral Guidelines

Urgent Referral (2-week cancer pathway)
  • Age ≥45 with unexplained visible haematuria without UTI
  • Age ≥45 with visible haematuria persistent/recurrent after UTI treatment
  • Age ≥60 with unexplained non-visible haematuria AND dysuria or raised WBC
Specialist Referral
  • Persistent bladder or urethral pain (urgent if cancer suspected)
  • Voiding difficulty or history of chronic urinary retention
  • Palpable bladder after voiding
  • Clinically benign pelvic mass
  • Associated faecal incontinence
  • Suspected neurological disease
  • Suspected urogenital fistulae
  • History of previous incontinence surgery, pelvic cancer surgery, or radiation therapy
  • Recurrent UTI (consider non-urgent referral for bladder cancer in women ≥60)

Recent Updates

December 2024
Major Review: Vibegron added as treatment option for overactive bladder symptoms if antimuscarinic medicines are unsuitable, ineffective, or not tolerated. Topic restructured for improved navigation.
July 2024
Minor Update: Neuroleptic malignant syndrome and stress cardiomyopathy added as possible adverse effects of duloxetine.
January 2024
Minor Update: Information on licensed dose of desmopressin for women with troublesome nocturia and urgency incontinence added.
September-October 2019
Review: Referral recommendations amended in line with 2015 NICE guideline Suspected cancer: recognition and referral.
October-December 2014
Major Review: Choice of antimuscarinic drugs changed to align with NICE guidance. Mirabegron added as new drug option. New scenarios added for management of mixed UI, overflow incontinence, and urogenital fistula.
