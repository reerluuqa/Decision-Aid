Assessment & Initial Evaluation
Screening, risk factors, and clinical presentation
Screening Recommendations
Shared Decision Making
PSA screening is controversial. Discuss risks/benefits with patients aged 55-69 years. Higher-risk patients (Black ancestry, family history, BRCA mutations) may start at age 40-45.
  • Average risk: Discuss screening at age 50-55
  • High risk: Start screening at age 40-45 (BRCA2 carriers, strong family history)
  • Life expectancy ≥10 years for screening to be beneficial
Key Diagnostic Factors
Age
Most common in men >50 years. Median age at diagnosis: 68 years
Ethnicity
Highest incidence in Black men (194.8 per 100,000) - 2x higher than white men
Family History
2.48x increased risk with one first-degree relative. 4.39x with two or more relatives
Clinical Presentation
Asymptomatic (Most Common)
Detected through PSA screening ± DRE
Urinary Symptoms
Nocturia, frequency, hesitancy, weak stream (more common in advanced disease or BPH)
Systemic Symptoms
Weight loss, bone pain, lethargy (metastatic disease)
Digital Rectal Exam
Asymmetric, indurated, or nodular prostate suggests cancer
Red Flags - Immediate Referral
Suspected Metastatic Disease
Bone pain, neurological symptoms, unexplained weight loss, or markedly elevated PSA (>20 ng/mL)
Confirmatory
Diagnosis & Risk Stratification
PSA interpretation, imaging, biopsy, and staging
PSA Interpretation
Normal Range
Men ≥60 years: PSA <1 ng/mL = very low risk
Men ≥75 years: PSA <3 ng/mL = very low risk
Elevated PSA
PSA >3 ng/mL with suspicious DRE → consider biopsy
PSA density >0.15 ng/mL/g → increased risk
PSA Velocity
Increase >0.75 ng/mL/year may indicate cancer even if PSA in "normal" range
Imaging & Biopsy
Multiparametric MRI
Recommended before biopsy to identify significant lesions (PI-RADS scoring)
Prostate Biopsy
Systematic ± MRI-targeted biopsy required for diagnosis and Gleason grading
Staging Imaging
PSMA-PET/CT or bone scan/CT for high-risk disease (PSA >20, Gleason ≥8, T3-4)
Risk Stratification (NCCN)
Very Low Risk
Must have ALL:
• cT1c
• Gleason ≤6
• PSA <10 ng/mL
• <3 cores positive, ≤50% per core
• PSA density <0.15
Low Risk
• cT1-T2a
• Gleason ≤6
• PSA <10 ng/mL
High Risk
• cT3-4 OR
• Gleason 8-10 OR
• PSA >20 ng/mL
Germline Testing
Indications for Genetic Testing
Offer to patients with: metastatic/regional disease, high/very-high risk localized disease, strong family history, or known pathogenic variants (BRCA1/2, ATM, CHEK2, etc.)
Management
Treatment Options
Risk-based management strategies
Observation & Active Surveillance
When to Consider
Very low-risk or low-risk disease with life expectancy <10 years OR patient preference to avoid treatment side effects
Observation
History & DRE every 12 months
No biopsies
Active Surveillance
PSA every 6 months
DRE every 12 months
Biopsy every 2-5 years
Radiotherapy Options
External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT)
Definitive treatment for localized disease. Moderate hypofractionation preferred (shorter treatment course)
Indications: Low-risk to high-risk disease
Brachytherapy
Permanent seed implant (low-dose rate) or temporary HDR. Boost to EBRT for intermediate-high risk
Indications: Low-risk to high-risk disease (as monotherapy or boost)
Surgical Options
Radical Prostatectomy
Surgical removal of prostate + seminal vesicles. Open, laparoscopic, or robotic approaches
Indications: Localized disease, life expectancy ≥10 years, good performance status
Pelvic Lymph Node Dissection
Performed with radical prostatectomy based on nomogram risk of nodal involvement
Indications: Intermediate to high-risk disease
Androgen Deprivation Therapy (ADT)
Timing & Duration
4-6 months for intermediate-risk; 1.5-3 years for high-risk when combined with radiotherapy
LHRH Agonists
Leuprorelin, goserelin, triptorelin
Combine with anti-androgen for first 7 days to prevent flare
LHRH Antagonists
Degarelix, relugolix
No flare phenomenon; suitable for metastatic disease
Anti-androgens
Bicalutamide, nilutamide, apalutamide, enzalutamide
Second-generation agents for metastatic disease
Treatment by Risk Category
Very Low Risk
Life expectancy <10 years: Observation
Life expectancy ≥10 years: Active surveillance
Intermediate Risk
Active surveillance OR EBRT ± brachytherapy ± ADT (4-6 months) OR radical prostatectomy
High/Very High Risk
EBRT + brachytherapy boost + ADT (1.5-3 years) ± abiraterone OR radical prostatectomy + PLND
Metastatic Disease
First-line Therapy
ADT + docetaxel + abiraterone/apalutamide/darolutamide/enzalutamide (triplet therapy) OR ADT + one second-generation agent (doublet)
Castration-Resistant
Continue ADT + additional agents based on biomarkers and prior therapies
Oligometastatic
Consider metastasis-directed SBRT in addition to systemic therapy
Long-term
Follow-up & Monitoring
Surveillance, complications, and prognosis
Surveillance Schedule
PSA Monitoring
Every 6-12 months for 5 years, then annually
DRE
Consider if recurrence suspected
Imaging
For symptoms or rising PSA
Clinical Review
Every 12 months minimum
PSA Recurrence
Post-Radiotherapy Failure
PSA increase ≥2 ng/mL above nadir = biochemical recurrence
Post-Prostatectomy Failure
PSA persistence (not undetectable) or recurrence (detectable PSA >0.1 ng/mL on two measurements)
PSA Doubling Time (PSADT)
PSADT >10 months
Consider monitoring or secondary hormone therapy
PSADT ≤10 months
High risk for metastases - consider adding second-generation anti-androgen
Common Complications
Urinary Dysfunction
Incontinence, urgency, frequency (more common after surgery/radiotherapy)
Likelihood: High
Hot Flushes
From ADT - can be managed with paroxetine or clonidine
Likelihood: High
Bone Loss
ADT causes rapid BMD loss - assess FRAX score, consider bisphosphonates
Likelihood: High
Erectile Dysfunction
Common after surgery or radiotherapy - consider PDE5 inhibitors or penile rehabilitation
Likelihood: High
Prognosis
5-Year Relative Survival Rates
Localized: 100% | Regional: 100% | Distant: 38%
Gleason Score
Gleason 2-4: Low cancer mortality (6 deaths/1000 person-years)
Gleason 8-10: High mortality (121 deaths/1000 person-years)
BRCA Mutations
BRCA2 carriers: More aggressive disease, poor survival
When to Refer
Urgent Referral
Suspected metastatic disease, urinary retention, or significant symptoms
Routine Referral
Elevated PSA with suspicious DRE or MRI findings requiring biopsy/treatment planning
