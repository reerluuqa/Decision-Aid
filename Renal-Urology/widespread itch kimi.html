Assessment
Comprehensive evaluation of widespread itch

üí¨ History Taking

Itch Characteristics

  • Location and distribution
  • Onset and duration (>6 weeks = chronic)
  • Timing (nocturnal suggests systemic cause)
  • Severity and impact on QoL
  • Relieving/exacerbating factors

Associated Symptoms

  • Fatigue, fever, night sweats
  • Weight loss (red flag)
  • Sensory symptoms (burning, crawling)
  • Stress, anxiety, depression
  • Systemic symptoms of organ disease

Risk Factors

  • Drug history (opioids, statins, ACE inhibitors)
  • Medical comorbidities
  • Family history of systemic disease
  • Household contacts (scabies)
  • Alcohol intake, travel history

üîç Physical Examination

Skin Assessment

  • Primary lesions vs secondary scratch marks
  • Excoriations, lichenification
  • Signs of infestation
  • Dermographism (urticaria/atopy)
  • Skin dryness (xerosis)

Systemic Signs

  • Jaundice (liver disease)
  • Pallor (anaemia)
  • Lymphadenopathy (malignancy)
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Signs of endocrine disorders

üß™ Initial Investigations

Standard Panel: Arrange if no active skin disease to explain itch

Blood Tests

  • Full blood count & blood film
  • Liver function tests
  • Renal function tests
  • Serum iron & ferritin
  • Inflammatory markers (ESR/CRP)

Consider Additional Tests

  • Thyroid function tests
  • HbA1c (diabetes screening)
  • Hepatitis B & C serology
  • HIV test (if risk factors)
  • Chest X-ray (if lymphadenopathy)

üéØ Differential Diagnosis Framework

Dermatological Causes

  • Dry skin (xerosis) - most common in elderly
  • Atopic eczema
  • Contact dermatitis
  • Urticaria
  • Psoriasis
  • Scabies infestation

Systemic Disease (20-30%)

  • Renal disease (uraemia)
  • Liver disease (cholestasis)
  • Haematological disorders (iron deficiency, polycythaemia)
  • Endocrine (diabetes, thyroid)
  • Malignancy (Hodgkin's lymphoma)
  • Neurological (MS, cord lesions)

Other Causes

  • Drug-induced (opioids, statins, ACE inhibitors)
  • Infections (hepatitis B/C, HIV)
  • Menopause
  • Psychogenic/functional itch disorder
  • Delusional infestation
  • Idiopathic (8% of cases)

‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flag Indicators

Immediate Concern: These features warrant urgent referral

Malignancy Clues

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Persistent night sweats
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Abnormal blood film

Psychiatric Emergency

  • Delusional infestation beliefs
  • Severe self-harm from scratching
  • Suicidal ideation secondary to itch
  • Fixed false beliefs about parasites
  • Skin picking causing significant damage

Treatment
Stepwise management approach in primary care
‚ñº

üîÑ Stepwise Management Approach

Principle: Treat underlying cause where identified; provide symptomatic relief

Step 1: Address Underlying Cause

  • Discontinue offending drugs
  • Optimize management of systemic disease
  • Treat specific conditions (scabies, liver disease)
  • Refer to appropriate specialist if needed

Step 2: Self-Care & Emollients

  • Regular emollient use (soap substitute)
  • Avoid excessive bathing
  • Cool environment, humidify air
  • Loose cotton clothing

Step 3: Pharmacological

  • Menthol 0.5-1% in aqueous cream
  • Non-sedating antihistamines
  • Sedating antihistamines for nocturnal itch
  • Avoid topical steroids routinely

üö´ Medications to Avoid or Use with Caution

Not Routinely Recommended

  • Topical corticosteroids (unless inflammation)
  • Oral corticosteroids
  • Topical antihistamines (sensitisation risk)
  • Topical anaesthetics
  • Calamine lotion (drying effect)

Use with Caution

  • Hydroxyzine - QT prolongation risk
  • Sedating antihistamines in elderly
  • Dose adjust for renal/hepatic impairment
  • Warn about driving/sedation

üè• Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral

  • Suspected haematological malignancy
  • Severe systemic disease
  • Delusional infestation (psychiatric)
  • Rapidly progressive symptoms

Routine Referral

  • Symptoms persist >3 months
  • Failed primary care treatment
  • Unexplained chronic itch
  • Need for specialist investigations

Follow-up
Monitoring and ongoing management
‚ñº

üìä Monitoring Response

Short-term (2-4 weeks)

  • Assess adherence to emollient therapy
  • Evaluate antihistamine effectiveness
  • Monitor sleep improvement
  • Check for side effects
  • Review self-care measures

Medium-term (6-12 weeks)

  • Symptom control and QoL assessment
  • Skin condition improvement
  • Need for continued medication
  • Psychological impact evaluation

üîÑ When to Reassess or Escalate

Reassessment Triggers: Lack of improvement, new symptoms, or worsening QoL

Escalation Indicators

  • No improvement after 3 months
  • New systemic symptoms appear
  • Severe psychological distress
  • Development of skin complications
  • Suspicion of underlying disease

Investigation Review

  • Repeat abnormal blood tests
  • Consider additional specialist tests
  • Skin biopsy if indicated
  • Imaging if malignancy suspected

üß† Psychological Support

Screen for Psychiatric Comorbidity

  • Depression screening at each visit
  • Anxiety assessment
  • Impact on sleep quality
  • Social isolation/stigmatization
  • Consider referral to mental health services

Patient Support Resources

  • British Association of Dermatologists leaflets
  • Itch support groups
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Sleep hygiene advice

üìö Patient Education & Prevention

Ongoing Self-Care

  • Maintain regular emollient use
  • Avoid known triggers
  • Continue gentle skincare routine
  • Keep symptom diary

Prevention Strategies

  • Regular moisturising prevents xerosis
  • Avoid harsh soaps and hot water
  • Manage underlying conditions
  • Regular medication review
