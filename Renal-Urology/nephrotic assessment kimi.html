Assessment

Initial evaluation and patient history

‚ñº

Key Diagnostic Criteria Nephrotic Range

Definition: Proteinuria >3.5 g/24 hours (or urine PCR >300 mg/mmol), hypoalbuminaemia <30 g/L, and peripheral oedema. Urine typically contains few cells or casts.

History Taking

  • Principal symptoms: Oedema (starts in legs, may progress to anasarca), foamy urine
  • Red flag symptoms: Rapid deterioration of renal function, severe hypertension, haematuria with red cell casts Urgent Referral
  • Malignancy symptoms: Cough, weight loss, night sweats, change in bowel habit, GI blood loss
  • SLE symptoms: Rash, mouth ulcers, arthralgias
  • Past medical history: Diabetes, malignancy, autoimmune disease, viral infections, drug use
  • Family history: Fabry disease, amyloidosis, other renal diseases
  • Drug history: NSAIDs, pamidronate, gold, penicillamine, Chinese herbs, mercurial skin-lightening creams

Physical Examination

General Findings

  • Peripheral oedema (legs, periorbital)
  • Anasarca in severe cases
  • Muehrcke's lines (white nail banding)
  • Xanthelasmata from hypercholesterolaemia
  • Weight gain from oedema (conceals protein loss)

Disease-Specific Clues

  • SLE: Malar rash, arthritis Caution
  • Amyloidosis: Easy bruising, neuropathy
  • Diabetes: Retinopathy (fundoscopy) Stable
  • Malignancy: Haem-positive stool

Initial Investigations Essential

Urine Tests

  • Spot urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (PCR)
    Diagnostic if >300 mg/mmol
  • Fresh urine microscopy: lipid droplets, oval fat bodies, Maltese crosses, cholesterol crystals
  • Urine protein electrophoresis
  • Few cellular casts (distinguishes from nephritic syndrome)

Blood Tests

  • Renal function (creatinine, eGFR)
  • Serum albumin <30 g/L
  • Lipid profile (elevated total/LDL cholesterol)
  • FBC, thyroid function

Specialized Investigations Targeted

  • Autoimmune screen: ANA, dsDNA, ANCA, complement (C3, C4), cryoglobulins
  • Specific antibodies: Anti-PLA2R antibody (membranous nephropathy)
  • Paraprotein screen: Serum free light chains, urine protein electrophoresis with immunofixation
  • Infectious serologies: Hepatitis B/C, HIV, syphilis (RPR)
  • Genetic panel: Consider in children/young adults with steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome (SRNS)

Imaging & Biopsy Critical

Renal ultrasound: Mandatory before biopsy to assess kidney size and exclude contraindications. Small kidneys increase bleeding risk.
Renal biopsy: Required in most adults. In children, may be deferred if steroid-sensitive (presumed MCD). Contraindications include bleeding diathesis, small chronic kidneys, uncontrolled hypertension.

Common Causes Adult vs Child

  • FSGS: Most common cause of SRNS
  • Renal biopsy often deferred initially

üë®‚Äçü¶∞ Adults

  • Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS): 35%
  • Membranous Nephropathy: 33% (most common in >60 years)
  • MCD: 15%
  • Diabetic Nephropathy: Most common in longstanding diabetes

Differential Diagnosis Overview

Minimal Change Disease Good Prognosis

Normal light microscopy, podocyte effacement on EM. Often steroid-responsive. May be secondary to Hodgkin's lymphoma or NSAID use.

Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) Variable

Primary (idiopathic) vs secondary (HIV, obesity, reflux nephropathy, drugs). Genetic forms in children (NPHS1, NPHS2, LAMB2, WT1).

Membranous Nephropathy Chronic Course

Primary (anti-PLA2R antibodies in 70-80%) vs secondary (malignancy, infections, autoimmune disease). "Spike and dome" appearance on EM.

Diabetic Nephropathy Common

Long-standing diabetes, often with retinopathy. Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules. Rule out non-diabetic renal disease if atypical features.

Key Distinguishing Features

  • Nephritic vs Nephrotic: Nephritic = haematuria, red cell casts, renal dysfunction, hypertension Different Management
  • Secondary causes: Malignancy (especially >60 years), infections (HBV, HCV, syphilis, HIV), autoimmune (SLE)
  • Amyloidosis: AL (light-chain) or AA (inflammatory). Investigate for monoclonal paraprotein.
  • Genetic testing: Consider in SRNS presenting <25 years, especially with family history

Diagnostic Algorithm Stepwise

Step 1: Confirm nephrotic-range proteinuria (PCR >300 mg/mmol) with hypoalbuminaemia
Step 2: Exclude nephritic features (active urine sediment, renal dysfunction)
Step 3: Perform serological workup (autoimmune, infections, paraproteins)
Step 4: Check anti-PLA2R antibodies (may avoid biopsy if positive with preserved renal function)
Step 5: Renal biopsy in adults (except classic diabetic nephropathy)
Step 6: Genetic testing if indicated (pediatric SRNS, family history)

Treatment

Management strategies by underlying cause

‚ñº

General Management All Patients

Oedema Management

  • Salt restriction (<2g/day sodium)
  • Loop diuretics (watch for hypovolaemia)
  • IV albumin + diuretics if severe
  • Monitor weight daily

Proteinuria Reduction

  • ACE inhibitors or ARBs (titrate to max tolerated)
  • Target BP <130/80 mmHg
  • Reduce intraglomerular pressure
  • Monitor potassium and renal function

Specific Immunosuppression

Minimal Change Disease Steroid Responsive

Adults: Prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (max 60mg) for 4-6 weeks, then taper over 2-3 months. Children: 60 mg/m¬≤/day for 4-6 weeks, then alternate-day taper. Consider in children without biopsy if typical features.

FSGS - Primary Steroid Resistant

High-dose steroids may be tried. If resistant: calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus/cyclosporine), mycophenolate mofetil, or rituximab. Plasmapheresis if recurrent post-transplant.

Membranous Nephropathy Risk Stratified

Conservative therapy for low risk. Immunosuppression (steroids + cyclophosphamide or rituximab) for high risk (persistent proteinuria, declining GFR, high anti-PLA2R titres). Monitor antibodies.

Diabetic Nephropathy RAAS Blockade

Optimize glycaemic control (target HbA1c ~7%), maximize RAAS blockade, SGLT2 inhibitors, statins. No immunosuppression.

Supportive Care Essential

  • Lipid management: Statins (target LDL <2.6 mmol/L)
  • Anticoagulation: Consider if albumin <20 g/L with risk factors (immobility, previous thrombosis, membranous nephropathy)
  • Infection prophylaxis: Pneumococcal vaccine, influenza vaccine
  • Encourage exercise to reduce thrombosis risk
  • Dietary: Protein 0.8-1.0 g/kg/day (avoid high protein), salt restriction
  • Monitoring: BP, weight, renal function, electrolytes, albumin, lipids

Complication Management

Thromboembolism Life Threatening

  • Prophylactic anticoagulation if high risk
  • Therapeutic anticoagulation if thrombosis occurs
  • More common in membranous nephropathy

Infection

  • Increased risk due to immunoglobulin loss
  • Prompt treatment of infections
  • Consider prophylactic antibiotics post-splenectomy

Acute Kidney Injury

  • Hypovolaemia from over-diuresis
  • Ischaemic injury or NSAIDs
  • May occur in 20-30% of MCD

When to Refer Nephrology

All adults with new nephrotic syndrome require nephrology referral for biopsy and management. Children may be treated empirically but need referral if steroid-resistant, atypical features, or complications.

Follow-up

Monitoring, relapse prevention, and long-term management

‚ñº

Monitoring Schedule Regular

Early Phase (First 6 months)

  • Clinic visits: Every 2-4 weeks
  • BP, weight, urine dipstick/PCR
  • Renal function, electrolytes monthly
  • Albumin and lipids every 3 months

Stable Phase (>6 months)

  • Clinic visits: Every 3-6 months
  • BP and urine PCR every visit
  • Renal function every 3 months
  • Annual cardiovascular risk assessment

Relapse Monitoring MCD & FSGS

  • High relapse rate: 50-70% in MCD, variable in FSGS
  • Home monitoring: Urine dipsticks for protein weekly during weaning
  • Early signs: Proteinuria, facial/leg swelling, weight gain
  • Action: Prompt treatment with steroids at first sign of relapse
  • Maintenance therapy: Consider in frequent relapsers (steroids, MMF, calcineurin inhibitors)

Long-term Complications Monitor

Cardiovascular Disease

Accelerated atherosclerosis from hyperlipidaemia and hypertension. Annual CV risk assessment, continue statins, strict BP control.

Chronic Kidney Disease Progression

Monitor eGFR trajectory. Optimize RAAS blockade and risk factors. Prepare for renal replacement therapy if eGFR <20 mL/min.

Thrombotic Risk

Lifelong increased risk. Continue prophylactic anticoagulation if indicated. Monitor for DVT/PE symptoms.

Infection Risk

Maintain vaccinations (pneumococcal, influenza). Consider immunoglobulin levels if recurrent infections.

Treatment Withdrawal Remission

Remission criteria: Urine protein <0.2 g/day and stable renal function for >6 months. Taper immunosuppression gradually over months to years. Monitor closely for relapse during and after withdrawal.
  • Steroid withdrawal: Slow taper to avoid adrenal insufficiency
  • Calcineurin inhibitor withdrawal: High relapse risk, taper over >6 months
  • Post-withdrawal: Monthly monitoring for first 6 months

Patient Education Essential

Key Topics

  • Signs of relapse (swelling, foamy urine)
  • Medication adherence (especially steroids)
  • Salt restriction importance
  • Vaccination needs
  • Thrombosis prevention (exercise, hydration)
  • When to seek urgent help

Lifestyle Advice

  • Balanced protein intake (0.8-1.0 g/kg)
  • Low sodium diet (<2g/day)
  • Regular exercise
  • smoking cessation
  • Avoid NSAIDs
  • Weight monitoring

Prognosis Variable

Favorable Outcomes

  • Minimal Change Disease: Good long-term prognosis
  • Steroid-responsive cases: 80% remission
  • Complete remission: Best renal survival

Poor Prognostic Factors

  • FSGS with collapsing variant
  • Persistent heavy proteinuria (>6 months)
  • Renal dysfunction at presentation
  • Hypertension
  • Tubulointerstitial fibrosis on biopsy
