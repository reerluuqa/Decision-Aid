UI in Women (NICE CKS)

Last Revised: Jan 2025
Target: Primary Care / NHS

Suspected Cancer & Urgent Referral

Refer using suspected cancer pathway (within 2 weeks) if:

Age 45+ & Unexplained Visible Haematuria Without UTI, OR persistent/recurrent after successful UTI treatment.
Age 60+ & Unexplained Non-Visible Haematuria AND dysuria or raised white cell count.
Persistent Bladder/Urethral Pain Refer urgently if cancer is suspected (mass, etc).
Detailed History
Core Step
  • Categorize UI: Stress vs Urgency vs Mixed.
  • Voiding symptoms: Straining, incomplete emptying (Overflow?), Haematuria.
  • Associations: Recurrent UTI, Fistula (constant leakage), Urethral diverticulum (post-void dribble).
  • Medical Hx: Neurological conditions (Parkinson's, MS), Diabetes, Meds (Diuretics, ACE-i).
Physical Exam
Core Step
  • General: BMI, Neurological signs, Gait.
  • Abdomen: Palpable bladder mass?
  • Pelvic Exam:
    • Check for Prolapse/Atrophy/Diverticulum.
    • Cough Test: Observe urethral meatus (Leakage = SUI likely).
    • Pelvic Floor Tone: Oxford Grading (0-5).
Investigations
Core Step
Bladder Diary: Minimum 3 days (fluid intake, micturition, voided volume, incontinence episodes).
  • Urine Dipstick: Blood, Glucose, Protein, Leucocytes, Nitrites (All women).
  • MSU Culture: If recurrent UTI symptoms + positive dipstick.
  • Renal Function: Assess if obstruction suspected.
First-Line Lifestyle & Conservative (All Types)
Priority Action

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Weight loss: If BMI > 30.
  • Caffeine/Alcohol: Reduce intake (stimulate detrusor).
  • Fluid intake: Adjust to normal (avoid high or low intake).
  • Physical activity: Encourage general exercise.

Specific Therapies

  • Bladder Training: For Urgency/Mixed (minimum 6 weeks).
  • PFMT: For Stress/Mixed (Supervised, minimum 3 months).
  • Containment: Only as temporary coping strategy or if treatment fails.
Medication Review

Review drugs that may exacerbate UI:

  • Diuretics: Polyuria, frequency, urgency.
  • Alpha-blockers: Relax urethra (Worsen SUI).
  • Antihistamines/ACE-i: Cough or retention.
  • Antidepressants/Sympathomimetics: Detrusor overactivity.
Local Oestrogen

Consider for post-menopausal women with atrophic vaginitis/urethritis.

Adjuvant Treatment

Management: Stress Urinary Incontinence
Definition: Involuntary leakage on effort, exertion, sneezing, or coughing.

Treatment Pathway

Step Intervention Note
1st Line Supervised PFMT Minimum 3 months duration. High intensity (8 contractions, 3x/day).
2nd Line Vaginal Cones / Electrical Stimulation If PFMT ineffective or not suitable.
Specialist Surgery / Mechanical Devices e.g. Mid-urethral slings, Colposuspension, Urethral bulking.
When to refer (Non-urgent): If woman prefers surgical management or conservative management has failed.
Non-Drug Management
  • Bladder Training: Minimum 6 weeks.
  • Lifestyle: Caffeine reduction, fluid modification.
  • Nocturia: Consider desmopressin (if troublesome nocturia/urgency).
Pharmacological Treatment
2nd Line

If bladder training is ineffective or not suitable.

Class / Drug Key Considerations
Antimuscarinics Offer first line. Consider extended release. Watch for dry mouth, constipation, cognitive effects.
Mirabegron Beta-3 agonist. Option if antimuscarinics unsuitable/ineffective. Contraindicated in severe uncontrolled HTN.
Vibegron NEW Beta-3 agonist. New option if antimuscarinics unsuitable/ineffective/not tolerated.
Safety Alerts & Adverse Effects
Mirabegron:
  • Severe HTN (contraindication)
  • Dizziness, Constipation, Headache
Duloxetine:
  • Nausea (common)
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (rare)
  • Stress cardiomyopathy (rare)
Desmopressin:
  • Hyponatraemia risk
  • Fluid restriction advised
Type Definitions
Stress (SUI) Leakage on effort, exertion, sneezing, coughing.
Urgency (UUI) Leakage accompanied by sudden compelling desire to pass urine.
Overactive Bladder (OAB) Urgency, with or without UUI, usually with frequency and nocturia.
Mixed Associated with both urgency and physical stress.
Overflow Chronic retention. Leakage from over-distended bladder.
Continuous Constant loss. Consider fistula or ectopic ureter.
Other Referral Criteria

Refer to Urologist/Urogynaecologist (using clinical judgement):

  • Persistent bladder or urethral pain (if not cancer).
  • Voiding difficulty or chronic urinary retention.
  • Palpable bladder after voiding.
  • Associated faecal incontinence.
  • Suspected neurological disease.
  • Suspected urogenital fistula.
  • History of previous incontinence surgery / pelvic cancer / radiotherapy.
  • Recurrent UTIs.
  • Pelvic mass (clinically benign).
Prognosis & Complications

Many cases can be treated or significantly improved.

Untreated risks:

  • Psychological: Anxiety, depression, social isolation.
  • Physical: Skin breakdown, UTIs, falls.
  • Functional: Sleep disturbance, reduced independence.
