Renal or Ureteric Colic - Acute

Primary Care Management • Adults 16+

Revised: Oct 2024
Immediate Admission

Admit immediately if any of the following are present:

  • Sepsis / Systemic Infection: Fever, rigors, or signs of sepsis.
  • High AKI Risk: Solitary kidney, transplant kidney, pre-existing CKD.
  • Obstruction: Suspected bilateral obstructing stones.
  • Severe Pain: Pain uncontrolled by primary care measures.
  • Pregnancy: If pregnant and symptomatic (refer to O&G/Physician).
Urgent Referral / Imaging

Refer for non-contrast CT KUB within 24 hours if:

  • Suspected renal colic requiring confirmation.
  • Diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Pain is ongoing and not tolerated.
  • Stone is unlikely to pass spontaneously (usually >10mm).
Note: Use Low-dose non-contrast CT (Standard for adults).
Clinical "Red Flag" Warning
Do not rule out renal colic if: Urine dipstick is negative for haematuria. Up to 15% of cases have no microscopic haematuria. Always exclude abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) in men >50 years with first presentation.
History
Classic Presentation
  • Onset: Abrupt, severe, unilateral loin/flank pain.
  • Radiation: To groin, testicle (men), or labia (women).
  • Character: Colicky (comes in waves), patient is restless (cannot sit still).
  • Associated: Nausea, vomiting, haematuria.
  • Severity: Often described as worst pain ever (exceeding childbirth).
Examination
Key Signs
  • General: Restlessness, pacing (differentiates from peritonitis).
  • Abdomen: May be tender, but usually no guarding/rigidity.
  • Systemic: Check for pyrexia (infection).
  • Specific: Loin tenderness often present.
Investigations
Primary Care Tests
  • Urine Dipstick:
    • Haematuria: Supports diagnosis (but absence doesn't exclude).
    • Nitrite/LE: Suggests UTI (Requires urgent admission if obstructed).
  • Pregnancy Test: Essential for women of childbearing age.
Differential Diagnosis (Exclude)
Condition Distinguishing Feature Action
Appendicitis Migratory pain to RIF, anorexia, localized peritonism. Urgent Surgical Referral
Ectopic Pregnancy Amenorrhoea, vaginal bleeding, cervical motion tenderness. Immediate Referral (Gynae)
Ruptured AAA Men >50yr, pulsatile mass, shock, collapsing patient. Emergency 999
Pyelonephritis Systemic fever, constant loin pain, urinary symptoms. Admit if septic
Diverticulitis LLQ pain, fever, older patient, history of diverticula. Refer/Urgent Care
First Line
Analgesia & Symptomatic Relief

1. NSAID (Preferred)

Diclofenac (IM/IV/PR) OR Ibuprofen (Oral).

Effective at reducing pain and inflammation. Use with caution in asthma, CKD, or peptic ulcer disease.

2. Anti-emetic

Give if vomiting or nauseated.

Options: Metoclopramide, Cyclizine, or Ondansetron.

Second Line / Escalation
If Pain Persists
  • Paracetamol: Can be added to NSAID.
  • Opioids: (e.g., Codeine, Tramadol, Morphine). Use with caution; may increase nausea and vomiting. Ensure anti-emetic is co-administered.
Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET)

The role of alpha-blockers (e.g., Tamsulosin) is debated. They are not routinely recommended for everyone but may be considered for larger distal ureteric stones. Discuss with secondary care.

Straining Urine

Advise the patient to strain their urine to retrieve the stone for analysis if possible. This helps guide preventative treatment.

Prognosis & Spontaneous Passage

The likelihood of passing the stone depends on size:

< 5mm ~75% pass
5-10mm ~50-60% pass
> 10mm Unlikely to pass

Average time to passage: 17 days (Range 6-29 days).

Recurrence Risk
  • 5 years: 50% recurrence rate.
  • 10 years: 80% recurrence rate.
  • Highly recurrent disease occurs in >10% of stone formers.
Prevention Advice

Fluids

Drink enough to produce at least 2 liters of urine per day.

Diet

Normal calcium intake is recommended. Low sodium and moderate animal protein intake. Avoid excessive oxalate (spinach, rhubarb, nuts).

Investigations for Recurrence: Check Serum Calcium (to rule out hyperparathyroidism). Urine collection for pH, volume, and crystal analysis.
