Clinical Reference

PSA Testing: Asymptomatic Men

Updated Dec 2025

Based on PCRMP & NICE NG12

Is Patient Symptomatic?

LUTS, Erectile Dysfunction, Visible Haematuria.

Action Required:
  • Clinical Assessment + DRE (if not contraindicated)
  • Refer via NICE NG12 if PSA raised (Age-related thresholds)
  • Do not use screening protocols.

Is Patient Asymptomatic?

Requesting test "While I'm here" or via Campaign.

Action Required:
  • Shared Decision Making (PCRMP).
  • Explain Overdiagnosis risk.
  • No National Screening Programme exists.

NICE NG12 Referral Thresholds

Use these values for both symptomatic & asymptomatic raised results
40-49y > 2.5 mcg/l
50-59y > 3.5 mcg/l
60-69y > 4.5 mcg/l
70-79y > 6.5 mcg/l

Risk Stratification Factors

Ethnicity

1 in 4

Black men have a lifetime risk of 1 in 4, compared to 1 in 8 for White men.

PCRMP allows clinical discretion to test men under 50y if at higher risk. NSC draft guidance (Nov 2025) considers targeted screening for gene variants only (controversial).

Family History

No FH 8%
Father <60y 20%
Brother <60y 25%
>2 Relatives 30%+

Earlier onset (≤50y) common with genetic mutations.

BRCA Mutations

High Risk

Men with BRCA2 have increased risk of aggressive prostate cancer (Abs risk 27% by 75y).

EAU recommends PSA screening from age 40y. IMPACT study suggests BRCA1 carriers have 3x risk of aggressive disease.

The PCRMP Process

When an asymptomatic man asks for a PSA test:

1. Check Eligibility

Age 50+ (or under 50 if higher risk/clinical discretion).

2. Shared Decision Making

Discuss benefits, harms, and limitations of the test. Ensure they understand overdiagnosis.

3. Result Management

If raised: Refer via 2-week wait (Urgent) pathway based on local/NICE thresholds.

Important: Do not proactively raise the issue of PSA testing with asymptomatic men (PCRMP guidance). Only act upon request.

Possible Advantages

  • Picks up cancer before symptoms start.
  • May detect fast-growing cancer early when treatment is effective.
  • Reassurance if result is normal (though not 100% sensitive).

Possible Disadvantages

  • False positives: Raised PSA without cancer.
  • Overdiagnosis: Finding slow cancers that never cause harm.
  • Biopsy risks: Bleeding, infection, sepsis.
  • Treatment side effects: Urinary, bowel, erectile dysfunction.
By the numbers (BMJ Rapid Rec): ~1 in 7 asymptomatic men will have elevated PSA. 66% of men biopsied due to raised PSA do not have cancer.

Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)

Current Status: NICE NG12 still recommends considering DRE for symptomatic men. However, BAUS/PCUK joint statement (June 2025) advises that if PSA is raised, refer for MRI; physical exam is not required prior to referral.

Utility: DRE only palpates posterior wall; most cancer is anterior. A normal DRE does not exclude cancer. DRE is poor as a standalone screening tool (Ann Fam Med 2018).

Do not use DRE to rule out cancer.

Multiparametric MRI (mpMRI)

NICE recommends mpMRI as a first-step investigation before biopsy for men with raised PSA eligible for radical treatment.

Benefits
  • Reduces unnecessary biopsies by >25%.
  • Allows targeted biopsies of suspicious areas.
  • Improves detection of clinically significant cancer.
Impact on Screening

Changes the risk-benefit balance of PSA testing by reducing the harm of biopsies. Future screening trials (e.g., TRANSFORM, GOTEBORG-2) focus on PSA + MRI.

Summary of Key Trials

Trial Outcome (Long Term) Key Takeaway
CAP (UK) No significant diff in mortality at 10y. Non-sig reduction at 15y. Single PSA test not effective population screening.
ERSPC (Europe) 13% reduction in prostate cancer mortality after 23y. Small benefit. 456 men need to screen to prevent 1 death. Increases overdiagnosis.
PLCO (US) No difference in mortality. Annual screening vs usual care showed no benefit.
*Note: None of these trials included modern mpMRI pathways, which may reduce harm profiles.
