Overview

This CKS topic covers the management of women with urinary tract infection (UTI), asymptomatic bacteriuria, recurrent lower UTI, and UTI in association with a long-term (indwelling or intermittent) urinary catheter.

This CKS topic does not cover: prevention of UTI following surgery or instrumentation; secondary care treatment of UTI; 'urethral syndrome' or 'painful bladder syndrome'; upper urinary tract symptoms or acute/chronic pyelonephritis; or UTI in women with impaired renal function. It also does not cover the management of UTI in girls younger than 16 years of age.

Target audience: Healthcare professionals working within the NHS in the UK, and providing first contact or primary healthcare.

Diagnosis

When to Suspect Lower UTI

Women Under 65 Years

Suspect a diagnosis of UTI if there are one or more key symptoms:

Dysuria
New nocturia
Cloudy urine

Note: Symptoms should be in the absence of new-onset vaginal discharge or irritation.

Women Over 65 Years or With Catheter

Consider a diagnosis of UTI if there is:

  • Isolated new-onset dysuria OR
  • Two or more urinary or non-specific symptoms:
    • Fever
    • New frequency or urgency
    • New urinary incontinence

Additional Symptoms to Consider

Frequency
Urgency
Suprapubic pain
Visible hematuria

Management

Treatment Approaches

Uncomplicated UTI

  • 3-day antibiotic course
  • First-line: Nitrofurantoin (avoid in 3rd trimester)
  • Alternatives: Trimethoprim, Pivmecillinam

Pregnant Women

  • 7-day antibiotic course
  • First-line: Cefalexin (twice daily)
  • Avoid nitrofurantoin in 3rd trimester

Catheter-associated UTI

  • Consider removal/replacement if possible
  • Treat only if symptomatic
  • Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria

Recurrent UTI

  • Consider methenamine hippurate
  • Vaginal estrogen for postmenopausal women
  • Refer if cause unknown or recurrent upper UTI

Red Flags (Refer/Urgent)

  • Suspected pyelonephritis
  • Hematuria with no UTI symptoms
  • Persistent symptoms after treatment
  • Structural abnormalities
  • Impaired renal function

Background Information

Definition

A lower urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection of the bladder (also known as 'cystitis'), usually caused by bacteria from the gastrointestinal tract entering the urethra.

Uncomplicated UTI: Caused by typical uropathogens in a non-pregnant woman with no known relevant anatomical or functional abnormalities of the urinary tract and no predisposing comorbidities.

Complicated UTI: With an increased likelihood of complications such as treatment failure, persistent infection, and recurrent infection.

Causes

The most commonly identified causative uropathogen is Escherichia coli, identified in up to 77% of cases.

Less commonly identified organisms include:

  • Proteus mirabilis
  • Pseudomonas species
  • Serratia species
  • Enterococcus species
  • Klebsiella species

NICE Quality Standards

Diagnosis

Women aged under 65 years are diagnosed with a urinary tract infection (UTI) if they have two or more key urinary symptoms and no other excluding causes or warning signs.

Catheter-associated UTI

Adults with indwelling urinary catheters do not have dipstick testing to diagnose UTIs.

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

Non-pregnant women are not prescribed antibiotics to treat asymptomatic bacteriuria.

Treatment Duration

Non-pregnant women with an uncomplicated lower UTI are prescribed a 3-day course of antibiotics, and pregnant women with an uncomplicated lower UTI are prescribed a 7-day course of antibiotics.

Recurrent UTI

Women with a recurrent lower UTI where the cause is unknown or a recurrent upper UTI are referred for specialist advice.
