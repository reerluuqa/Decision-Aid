Clinical Evaluation Key Step
Consider Alport syndrome in: Any child or adult with hematuria, proteinuria, deafness, hypertension, or renal failure

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Gross hematuria - Common in children, often precipitated by infection
  • Hearing impairment - Sensorineural, high-frequency loss
  • Family history of hematuria or renal failure
  • Hypertension - Sign of chronic kidney disease progression
  • Ocular changes - Lenticonus, maculopathy, early-onset cataracts
  • Fatigue & breathlessness - Vague symptoms of renal failure

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors:
  • Family history of Alport syndrome
  • Male relative with renal failure + deafness
  • Maternal history of microscopic hematuria
Other Risk Factors:
  • Microscopic hematuria
  • Family history of thin basement membrane nephropathy
  • Parental consanguinity
Initial Investigations Essential

Basic Tests

  • FBC: Check for anemia, elliptocytosis
  • Metabolic panel: Renal function, electrolytes
  • Fasting lipid panel: Dyslipidemia screening
  • Urinalysis: Hematuria, proteinuria quantification
  • Serum intact PTH: Bone mineral disorder screening

Specialized Tests

  • Audiometry: High-tone sensorineural hearing loss
  • Ophthalmoscopy: Lenticonus, retinal abnormalities
  • Renal ultrasound: Exclude structural abnormalities
  • ECG/Echo: Cardiovascular assessment
When to Suspect High Priority
Strong suspicion if: Hematuria + proteinuria + progressive sensorineural hearing loss + characteristic ocular changes
  • Males with XLAS: 80% present with microscopic/macroscopic hematuria
  • Females with XLAS: >95% have microscopic hematuria, 75% develop proteinuria
  • Autosomal forms: Equal severity in males and females

Diagnosis
‚ñº
Diagnostic Approach Multi-modal
Recommended: Full clinical and family evaluation + molecular or pathological examination

Step 1: Clinical Evaluation

  • Detailed family history
  • Physical examination
  • Audiometry and ophthalmoscopy
  • Screen first-degree relatives

Step 2: Confirmatory Testing

  • Genetic testing (preferred)
  • Renal biopsy (if needed)
  • Skin biopsy (for XLAS)
Diagnostic Criteria Clinical
Classic Alport Syndrome requires: 2 or more of the following criteria
  • Hematuria with/without progression to CKD + family history
  • Progressive sensorineural hearing loss
  • Characteristic ocular changes (lenticonus/maculopathy)
  • Typical ultrastructural changes in GBM
  • Diffuse leiomyomatosis
  • Abnormal type IV collagen distribution in skin/kidney biopsy
Confirmatory Testing Essential

Genetic Testing First-line

  • Next-generation sequencing: COL4A3, COL4A4, COL4A5 genes
  • Benefits: Confirms diagnosis, identifies inheritance pattern, predicts phenotype
  • Indication: ‚â•2 diagnostic criteria or strong clinical suspicion
  • Family screening: Cascade testing for at-risk relatives

Renal Biopsy Selective

  • EM findings: Thinning, thickening, splitting, lamellation of GBM
  • Immunohistochemistry: Loss of type IV collagen chains
  • Indication: When genetic testing unavailable or inconclusive

Skin Biopsy XLAS only

  • Males: Absence of Œ±5(IV) staining in 80%
  • Females: Absent or discontinuous staining in 60%
  • Benefit: Less invasive, faster than genetic testing
Differential Diagnoses Consider

Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy

  • Persistent microscopic hematuria only
  • Family history of hematuria (no renal failure)
  • Normal immunohistochemistry
  • May share COL4A3/A4 mutations

Other Differentials

  • IgA nephropathy: Usually no family history
  • FSGS: Often no hematuria
  • Branchio-oto-renal syndrome: Structural ear defects
  • MYH9 disorders: Macrothrombocytopenia
Family Screening Critical
Early detection of hypertension/proteinuria allows intervention to slow disease progression

XLAS Families

  • All daughters of affected males: 100% risk
  • Sons of carrier females: 50% risk
  • Male siblings: 50% risk, screen early
  • All at-risk relatives: annual urinalysis + BP

ARAS Families

  • Siblings: 25% risk of ARAS
  • Parents: likely carriers with hematuria
  • Offspring: obligate carriers
  • Genetic counseling essential

Treatment
‚ñº
Management Approach Lifelong Care
Core principle: Lifelong nephrology management with ACE inhibition to slow disease progression
  • Manage hypertension, proteinuria, dyslipidemia
  • Target BP: <130/80 mmHg
  • Monitor for extra-renal complications
  • Genetic counseling and family screening
  • Prepare for renal replacement therapy
Pharmacotherapy Critical
Early ACE inhibition delays ESRD and improves survival

Initiation Timing Genotype-specific

Start at diagnosis (age >12-24 months):
  • All males with XLAS
  • All patients with ARAS
  • Before proteinuria appears
Start at microalbuminuria:
  • Females with XLAS
  • All patients with ADAS

Recommended Agents

ACE Inhibitors (First-line)
  • Enalapril: 5-20 mg orally daily
  • Lisinopril: 10-40 mg orally daily
  • Fosinopril: 20 mg orally daily
  • Ramipril (children): Consult specialist
Angiotensin-II Receptor Antagonists (Alternative)
  • Losartan: 50-100 mg orally daily
  • Irbesartan: 150-300 mg orally daily
Aldosterone Antagonists (Adjunct)
  • Spironolactone: Consult specialist
  • May be combined with ACEi/ARB
Monitoring required: Orthostatic hypotension, hyperkalemia, renal function
Renal Replacement Therapy Advanced CKD

When to Consider GFR <20 mL/min

  • Education about dialysis options (hemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis)
  • Refer for transplant evaluation
  • Consider living donor assessment
  • Address patient preferences and support systems

Kidney Transplantation Definitive Treatment

Post-transplant anti-GBM disease: 1-5% risk, usually within 1 year
  • Screen for anti-GBM antibodies regularly
  • Higher risk with loss-of-function mutations
  • Graft loss risk is high if anti-GBM develops
  • Recurrence in second grafts is common

Living Donor Selection Careful Screening

  • Screen relatives for hematuria first
  • Genetic testing to guide selection
  • Non-carriers may be considered
  • XLAS carrier females: donation carries risk, long-term prognosis unknown
  • ARAS carriers typically not considered
Extra-renal Management Multidisciplinary

Sensorineural Hearing Loss

  • Audiology monitoring from age 5 years (males XLAS, all ARAS)
  • Hearing aids for management
  • Education on noise-induced injury prevention
  • Note: Does not improve with transplant

Visual Complications

  • Annual ophthalmology surveillance for children with lenticonus
  • Cataract surgery when indicated
  • Adult complications rare

Diffuse Leiomyomatosis Rare

  • Surgery for symptomatic leiomyomas
  • May involve esophagus, respiratory tract, genital tract
  • Associated with COL4A5-A6 deletions
Emerging Therapies Investigational
  • Dual/triple RAAS blockade: May reduce proteinuria further
  • Ciclosporin: Nephrotoxicity likely outweighs benefits
  • Bardoxolone methyl: FDA/EMA not approved (insufficient benefit-risk data)

Follow-up
‚ñº
Monitoring Protocol Routine
Frequency: At least annually, more frequently as renal function declines

Renal Monitoring

  • Serum creatinine/eGFR
  • Urinalysis (hematuria, proteinuria)
  • Blood pressure
  • Fasting lipid panel
  • Calcium, phosphate, PTH
  • FBC (anemia screening)

Extra-renal Monitoring

  • Audiometry (annually from age 5)
  • Ophthalmology (children with lenticonus)
  • Growth monitoring (children)
  • Depression screening
  • Cardiovascular assessment
Complications to Monitor Vigilance

Chronic Kidney Disease High Risk

  • Anemia, hyperparathyroidism, metabolic acidosis
  • Cardiovascular disease (most common cause of mortality)
  • Protein malnutrition, hyperkalemia
  • Early nephrology referral for RRT preparation

Pregnancy Complications Moderate Risk

Risk factors: Pre-pregnancy serum creatinine ‚â•106 Œºmol/L (‚â•1.2 mg/dL)
  • New or worsening hypertension
  • Pre-eclampsia
  • Fetal and maternal complications
  • Close obstetric-nephrology collaboration

Other Complications

  • Depression: Frequent, screen regularly
  • Hearing loss progression: Irreversible, monitor annually
  • Visual complications: Rare in adults
  • Post-transplant anti-GBM: 1-5% risk
Prognosis Genotype-dependent

X-linked Alport Syndrome (Males)

  • 100% develop CKD - rate varies by mutation
  • Loss-of-function mutations: >90% develop CKD by age 30
  • Early ACE inhibition delays ESRD and improves survival
  • Median age at ESRD: ~25 years (untreated)

X-linked Alport Syndrome (Females)

  • 12% develop CKD before age 40
  • 15-30% risk of renal failure by age 60
  • 30% have some hearing impairment
  • 15% have ocular signs

Autosomal Forms

  • ARAS: Median age at ESRD 22.5 years (range 10-38)
  • ADAS: Mild phenotype, slow progression
  • Digenic: Phenotype between ARAS and ADAS
  • Loss-of-function allele predicts earlier ESRD
Patient Education & Counseling Essential

Key Topics to Discuss

  • Disease course and variable manifestations
  • Importance of BP and proteinuria control
  • Renal replacement therapy options
  • Hearing loss management
  • Risk reduction strategies

Genetic Counseling

  • Reproductive options and prenatal testing
  • Family screening recommendations
  • Donor evaluation for kidney transplantation
  • Support groups and resources

Lifestyle Recommendations

  • Diet: Low-salt, high-fiber, protein 0.8g/kg/day
  • Smoking: Strict avoidance
  • Noise exposure: Minimize to protect hearing
  • BP monitoring: Home monitoring encouraged
