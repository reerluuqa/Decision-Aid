Hyponatraemia Quick Reference

Critical care decision support tool

Severity Classification

Severity Serum Sodium Clinical Priority
Mild 130-135 mmol/L Monitor & investigate
Moderate 125-129 mmol/L Urgent assessment
Severe <125 mmol/L EMERGENCY

Red Flags

IMMEDIATE ADMISSION REQUIRED
  • Acute onset (<48h)
  • Severe symptoms: seizures, coma, vomiting
  • Na <125 mmol/L with symptoms
  • Rapidly falling Na levels

Common Causes

Thiazide diuretics SIADH Heart failure Liver disease Hypothyroidism Adrenal insufficiency Excessive water intake

Primary Care Management

SAFE FOR PRIMARY CARE
Asymptomatic mild hyponatraemia (Na 130-135 mmol/L)
Investigations
  • Repeat U&E within 24h
  • Serum osmolality
  • Urine osmolality & Na
  • TFT, cortisol if indicated
Actions
  • Review medications
  • Stop thiazides
  • Assess fluid status
  • Treat underlying cause

Emergency Actions

Immediate Actions for Severe Hyponatraemia

1. ADMIT IMMEDIATELY
Critical care/HDU if Na <120 mmol/L
2. Hypertonic Saline
3% NaCl if symptomatic
3. Monitor Closely
Check Na every 2-4h initially
4. Correct Slowly
Max 8-10 mmol/L in 24h
WARNING: Rapid correction risks osmotic demyelination syndrome. Target 6-8 mmol/L increase in first 24h.

Complications to Watch

  • Cerebral oedema (acute)
  • Osmotic demyelination (over-rapid correction)
  • Increased falls risk
  • Osteoporosis (chronic)
  • Prolonged hospital stay

Discharge Criteria

  • Na stable >130 mmol/L
  • Underlying cause identified/treated
  • No red flag symptoms
  • GP follow-up arranged
  • Patient education completed
