CRITICAL ALERT: Haemodynamic Instability

Life-threatening bleeding is rare but possible (renal trauma, AV malformations, placenta percreta, severe haemorrhagic cystitis).

ACTION: Aggressive Resuscitation & ICU Referral

Consider Angio-embolisation or Surgery if poor response.

Clot Retention Urgent
  • Clots causing urinary obstruction at bladder outlet.
  • Risk of obstructive renal failure.
  • Intervention: Catheterisation & Irrigation.
Trauma Signs Action Required
  • Male + Pelvic Fracture + Blood at meatus: DO NOT place catheter.
  • Perform Retrograde Urethrogram first.
  • Rupture associated with pelvic fracture in 5%.

Overview & Stats

Definition

Visibly discoloured urine by blood. As little as 1 mL of blood can colour 1 L of urine.

Cancer Risk High Risk
  • Visible haematuria represents a higher-risk group for urological malignancy than non-visible.
  • Men >60y: PPV 22.1%
  • Women >60y: PPV 8.3%
Sensitivity
  • Urothelial carcinoma (bladder): 0.83
  • Ureteric carcinoma: 0.66
  • Renal cell carcinoma: 0.48

Diagnostic Approach

Clinical Assessment

1

History Timing

Initial: Urethra/Prostate/Bladder Neck.
Terminal: Prostate/Bladder Neck.
Total: Bladder or Upper Tract.

2

Associated Symptoms

Painless: Suspect Malignancy (TB, Sickle cell).
Painful: Infection, Stones (flank pain), Cystitis.
Lower Tract Sx: BPH, UTI.

3

Physical Exam

Check for flank tenderness (Pyelo/Mass), suprapubic tenderness (Cystitis), palpable bladder (Retention), or DRE findings (Prostate nodule).

Investigation

1st Line: Urinalysis Must Do
  • WBCs/Nitrites: Infection (Treat & Re-test).
  • RBC Casts/Dysmorphic RBCs: Glomerular (Nephrology consult).
  • Proteinuria >3g/day: Nephrotic range.
Imaging Modality

Gold Standard: CT Urography (CTU).

Use Non-contrast CT for suspected Nephrolithiasis.
Use MRI if contraindications to CT (radiation/contrast).
Special Studies
  • Cystoscopy: Essential for lower tract visualization (Bladder, Urethra).
  • Urine Cytology: For suspected Urothelial carcinoma.

Common Aetiologies

Infection
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
  • Pyelonephritis (Flank pain + fever)
  • TB (Sterile pyuria)
Malignancy High Priority
  • Bladder Cancer (Urothelial Ca)
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma
  • Prostate Cancer (36.4% present with haematuria)
  • Upper tract urothelial carcinoma
Trauma
  • Renal (Blunt/Deceleration)
  • Bladder (Pelvic fracture)
  • Urethral (Blood at meatus)
  • Iatrogenic (Biopsy, Catheter)
Stones
  • Renal/Ureteric calculi
  • Bladder stones
  • Visible haematuria is RARE (mostly microscopic)
Structural
  • BPH (Vascularity)
  • Arteriovenous Malformations
  • Renal Vein Thrombosis
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease
Medical Renal
  • Glomerulonephritis (IgA, SLE)
  • Alport Syndrome
  • Sickle Cell Trait/Disease

Differential Overview

Condition Key Clinical Features Diagnostic Tests
Bladder Cancer Painless haematuria. Risk: Smoking, Age >65. Urine Cytology, CTU, Cystoscopy
BPH Obstructive symptoms (hesitancy, weak stream), nocturia. DRE, PSA, Uroflowmetry
UTI Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain. Urinalysis (Nitrite/Leukocyte), Culture
Kidney Stone Severe flank pain radiating to groin, nausea. Non-contrast CT (Gold Standard), KUB X-ray
Pyelonephritis Flank pain, fever, chills, systemic illness. UA, Culture, Renal US or Contrast CT
Prostate Cancer Obstructive symptoms, weight loss, advanced age. PSA, Transrectal US Biopsy, MRI
Renal Trauma History of blunt/flank trauma or deceleration injury. Contrast CT abdomen (with delayed phase)
Glomerular Disease Proteinuria, casts, oedema, hypertension. UA for casts, Complement levels, Biopsy
