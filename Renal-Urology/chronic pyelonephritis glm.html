ClinRef: Pyelonephritis
CRITICAL: Emphysematous Pyelonephritis (EPN) is a life-threatening necrotizing infection. Immediate stabilization required.
Quick Summary

Chronic Pyelonephritis is a complex renal disorder characterised by chronic tubulointerstitial inflammation and scarring. It is a major cause of End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD).

VUR (Most Common)
Irreversible Damage
Clinical + Imaging Dx
Definition

Inflammation of the kidney resulting from chronic or recurrent infections. Characterised by:

  • Deep segmental cortical scarring
  • Clubbing of pelvic calyces
  • Papillary retraction into scars
Pathophysiology

Damage occurs slowly over time due to chronic inflammation or recurrent UTIs.

  • VUR: Direct route for infection to kidney.
  • Obstruction: Predisposes to infection, causes atrophy.

Diagnostic Approach

Histopathology is definitive but biopsy is not routine. Use clinical history + imaging.

Imaging Modalities
Modality Role Key Findings
CT Abdomen Gold Standard Excludes other causes, visualizes infrastructure. Scarring, obstruction, calculi, gas (in EPN).
Ultrasound Initial test; non-invasive; good for obstruction. Small/irregular kidneys, stones, hydronephrosis.
MRI If allergic to contrast or complex anomalies needed. Superior for pyelonephritis & scarring vs nuclear scan.
VCUG Children Diagnose VUR in children with UTI. Reflux of urine into ureters/kidneys.
Laboratory Tests
  • Urinalysis: Leukocytes, hematuria, proteinuria.
  • Urine Culture: Identify pathogens (e.g., Proteus in XGP, E. coli in EPN).
  • FBC: Anemia (common), Leukocytosis (acute exacerbation).
  • Renal Function: Elevated Creatinine, reduced eGFR.
  • CRP: Marker for severity.
Key History Factors
  • History of VUR
  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Renal Obstruction (Stones, BPH)
  • Prior Renal Surgery
  • Childhood infections
GOAL: Damage is irreversible. Focus on preventing further damage by correcting underlying causes (obstruction, reflux).
General Management

Medical

  • Treat active UTIs if present (culture-guided).
  • Antibiotics generally NOT helpful unless active infection is documented.
  • Tight glucose control (especially for diabetics with EPN).
  • Antihypertensives for late-stage hypertension.

Surgical/Interventional

  • Relieve obstruction (stones, BPH).
  • Correct VUR (in children).
  • Nephrectomy (see specific conditions below).
Xanthogranulomatous Pyelonephritis (XGP)

Action: Surgical intervention is mandatory.

  • Nephrectomy: Usually treatment of choice (Partial for focal disease).
  • Antibiotics: Pre and post-op (Gram-negative cover).
  • Note: Often misdiagnosed as renal cell carcinoma.
Emphysematous Pyelonephritis (EPN)

Action: Medical management + possible surgery.

  • Stabilization: Fluid resuscitation, oxygen.
  • Antibiotics: IV 3rd-gen cephalosporins, carbapenems, etc.
  • Drainage: Percutaneous drainage if stable.
  • Nephrectomy: If worsening sepsis or unstable.
Antibiotic Options (IV)
Primary Options
  • Ceftriaxone (1g IV q24h)
  • Gentamicin (5-7mg/kg IV q24h)
  • Ampicillin/Sulbactam
  • Piperacillin/Tazobactam
  • Imipenem/Cilastatin
  • Ertapenem
Secondary / Resistant
  • Ciprofloxacin (Use with caution)
  • Ceftazidime/Avibactam
  • Meropenem/Vaborbactam
  • Plazomicin
  • Cefiderocol
Patient Risk Checklist

Select factors present to assess risk profile.

  • Diabetes Mellitus (Poorly Controlled)
  • History of VUR
  • Renal Calculi (Staghorn)
  • Obstruction (BPH, Anomaly)
  • Neurogenic Bladder
  • Recurrent UTIs
Immediate Red Flags
  • High Fever + Sepsis signs
  • Gas in renal parenchyma (EPN)
  • Significant Weight Loss
  • Non-functioning kidney
  • Papillary Necrosis

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Symptoms Key Test
Acute Pyelonephritis Sudden onset: Fever, chills, constant dull back pain, nausea. Urinalysis (WBCs), Blood culture.
Renal Calculi Intermittent, sharp, radiating pain. Spiral CT Abdomen.
Renal Cancer Often asymptomatic or similar to XGP. Mass effect. CT/MRI; Often diagnosed post-nephrectomy for XGP.
