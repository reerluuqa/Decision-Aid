Bedwetting (Enuresis)

Clinical Knowledge Summary (NICE Guidelines)

Immediate Action Required (Red Flags)

Consider Child Maltreatment or urgent referral if:

  • Deliberate bedwetting reported by parents/carers.
  • Punishment for bedwetting despite professional advice.
  • Secondary Onset: Wetting started recently (days/weeks) with signs of systemic illness (UTI, Diabetes).
  • Secondary daytime/bed wetting persisting without medical explanation.
  • Spinal signs: Dimples, hairy patch, or sacral abnormalities.
  • Sleep apnoea symptoms (snoring, breathing pauses).
  • Suspicion of Female Genital Mutilation (FGM).

For children under 5 years: Bedwetting is considered normal and often resolves spontaneously.

  • Explain it is not the child's fault.
  • Reassure parents: 5‚Äì10% resolve each year.
  • Fluids: Adequate intake throughout the day. Avoid caffeine.
  • Toilet: Regular toileting, before bed (4-7 times/day).
  • Practical: Waterproof covers. Lifting/waking is short-term only.
  • Avoid: Punishment or restricting fluids as treatment.

üîµ Management: Primary Bedwetting (No Daytime Symptoms)

For children 5+ years: Offer active treatment if motivated.

  • First-line: Enuresis Alarm (High success, low relapse if used correctly).
  • Alternative: Desmopressin (e.g., Demovo¬Æ) - fast acting, consider for short-term needs (sleepovers) or if alarm fails/refused.
  • Lifestyle: Optimize fluid intake (evenly spread), avoid evening caffeine.
  • Monitor: 2-week diary of fluids and wetting episodes helpful.

üü† Action: Refer or Investigate

Daytime Symptoms or Secondary Onset Detected.

  • If Daytime Symptoms: Refer to Paediatrician / Enuresis Clinic.
  • If Secondary Onset: Treat underlying cause (Constipation, UTI, Diabetes) first.
  • Investigations: Urinalysis (if signs of infection/illness), abdominal exam (constipation).
  • Psychosocial: Assess for bullying, abuse, or emotional stressors.

üî¥ URGENT ACTION REQUIRED

Red Flags Present. Follow safeguarding or urgent medical protocols.

  • Refer for suspected Child Maltreatment (if applicable).
  • Urgent referral to Paediatrics if spinal dysraphism suspected.
  • Immediate testing/treatment for Diabetes or UTI if clinically indicated.
  • Refer for Sleep Apnoea assessment.

Management Details

üîî Enuresis Alarms

  • First-line treatment for motivated families.
  • Requires high commitment from child and parents for 4‚Äì6 weeks.
  • Contraindications: Not suitable if deep sleeper cannot wake to alarm, or child shares bedroom (unless waking others is acceptable).
  • Monitor fluid intake; alarm works best without nocturnal polyuria.

üíä Desmopressin (e.g., Demovo¬Æ)

Prescription Option

For Primary Monosymptomatic Enuresis in children over 5.

  • Mechanism: Reduces urine production at night.
  • Use: Good for short-term needs (school trips) or if alarm fails.
  • Administration: Take 1 hour before bed. Fluid restriction 1 hour before and 8 hours after.
  • Safety: Warn about fluid overload. Stop if headache, nausea, or vomiting occurs.

Risk Factors

  • Family history (Genetic link).
  • Male sex (2:1 ratio).
  • ADHD, Autism, Anxiety.
  • Constipation.
  • Developmental delay.

Common Causes

  • Sleep arousal difficulties.
  • Nocturnal Polyuria.
  • Bladder dysfunction (Overactive/Small capacity).
  • Psychological stress (for Secondary).
