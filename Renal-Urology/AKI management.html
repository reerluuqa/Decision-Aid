What is AKI?

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) is a sudden deterioration in kidney function resulting in the inability to maintain fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base balance.

Diagnostic Criteria (KDIGO):
  • Increase in Serum Creatinine by ≥26 µmol/L within 48 hours; OR
  • Increase in Serum Creatinine to ≥1.5 times baseline within the prior 7 days; OR
  • Urine output < 0.5 ml/kg/hr for > 6 hours.
Staging of AKI
Stage Serum Creatinine Urine Output Action
Stage 1 1.5–1.9 x baseline OR ≥26 µmol/L increase < 0.5 ml/kg/h for 6–12 h Investigate & Monitor
Stage 2 2.0–2.9 x baseline < 0.5 ml/kg/h for ≥12 h Urgent Review
Stage 3 ≥3.0 x baseline OR ≥354 µmol/L (acute rise ≥44) < 0.3 ml/kg/h for ≥24 h OR Anuria 12 h Immediate Referral
⚠ Immediate Referral Required

Admit or refer immediately to renal specialist if:

✖ AKI Stage 3
✖ Anuria > 12 hours
✖ K+ < 6.0 mmol/L
✖ Severe Acidosis (pH < 7.1)
✖ Pericarditis / Effusion
✖ Fluid overload / Pulmonary edema
Sick Day Rules (Amber/Green)

Medicines to STOP temporarily during acute illness (dehydration/vomiting):

●
ACEi / ARBs
e.g., Ramipril, Losartan
●
Diuretics
e.g., Furosemide, Indapamide
●
NSAIDs
e.g., Ibuprofen, Naproxen
●
Metformin
If eGFR < 30

Restart: Only when the patient is clinically better (usually eating and drinking normally for 24-48h).

Differential Diagnosis
Prerenal (60-70%)
  • Fluid depletion (vomiting, diarrhea)
  • Sepsis
  • Heart failure
  • Drugs (NSAIDs, ACEi)
Intrinsic (25-40%)
  • ATN (Acute Tubular Necrosis)
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Interstitial Nephritis
  • Rhabdomyolysis
Postrenal (5-10%)
  • Bladder outflow obstruction
  • BPH
  • Stones / Clots
Initial Management Checklist
History & Exam: Fluid balance, drug history, volume status (JVP, sacral edema).
Bloods: U&Es, FBC, CRP, Calcium, Phosphate, CK (if rhabdo suspected), ABG (if acidosis).
Urine Dip: Blood, Protein, Nitrites, Leucocytes. Send for MC&S.
Imaging: Renal Ultrasound if obstruction suspected or no clear cause.

CKD Staging (Reference only):

Clinical Pearls
  • Prevention: Keep patients well hydrated before contrast/contrast scans if risk factors present.
  • Sepsis: The most common cause of AKI in hospital. Treat sepsis early (Sepsis Six).
  • Recovery: Only 50% recover fully. Risk of developing CKD later is high.
  • Hydration: IV fluids are the first line for pre-renal failure, unless contraindicated (e.g., heart failure).
