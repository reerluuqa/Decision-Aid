Undescended Testes

Clinical Decision Support Tool
Revised: Dec 2024
Critical Actions Screening Schedule Assessment Management Pathway Complications
⚠️
URGENT REFERRAL (Within 24h)
If Disorder of Sexual Development (DSD) is suspected OR bilateral impalpable undescended testes are identified at birth.

Screening Timeline

Routine checks for male infants.

  • Within 72 hours of birth
    Initial newborn exam.
  • 6–8 weeks of age
    Primary check. If undescended -> re-examine at 4-5mo.
  • 4–5 months (corrected age)
    Re-assessment. If still undescended -> Refer.

Definitions

True Undescended Never in scrotum. Palpable or non-palpable.
Retractile Moves easily to scrotum & stays there. Not undescended.
Ectopic Outside normal path (femoral, perineal, penile shaft).
Ascending Previously in scrotum, now high. Fibrous remnant.

Assessment

History

  • Term or Pre-term? (Pre-terms often descend later)
  • Family history? (Father or sibling)
  • Previous inguinal hernia surgery?
  • Was testis ever in scrotum?
  • Noticeable lumps in other regions?

Management & Referral Pathway

At Birth

If undescended:

  • Re-examine at 6–8 weeks.
  • If both normally descended, no action.
At 6–8 Weeks

If undescended:

  • Re-examine at 4–5 months.
Red Flag: If bilateral impalpable -> Refer within 48h.

The 4-5 Month Decision Point

If testis remains undescended (corrected age):

Refer to Paediatric Surgery or Urology to be seen by 6 months of age.

Specialist Review Orchidopexy target: 6-12 months
If Retractile:

Advise parents. Annual follow-up needed until after puberty (risk of ascending testis).

Routine Monitoring Patient Education

Older Boy / Adult Presentation

Action: Refer to Paediatric Surgery, Paediatric Urology, or Urology (depending on age).
Advice: Educate on regular testicular self-examination due to increased cancer risk.

Complications & Risks

Fertility

Reduced fertility due to higher temperature causing germ cell damage.

  • Unilateral: Lower fertility rate, similar paternity (~90%).
  • Bilateral: Significantly lower paternity (33–53%).

Testicular Cancer

Cryptorchidism is a significant risk factor.

  • No surgery / Late surgery: 6-fold increased risk.
  • Early surgery (< puberty): ~2-fold increased risk.
  • Orchidopexy in first year is most protective.

Testicular Torsion

Undescended testes are more prone to torsion than scrotal testes.

Inguinal Hernia

Associated inguinal hernia may occur in some boys with undescended testes.
