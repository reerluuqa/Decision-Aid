Itch in Pregnancy — Quick Reference Algorithm

NICE CKS (last revised April 2025) • Primary care one‑pager
Step 1

Initial Assessment

1
Confirm pregnancy and age ≥ 13 years.
2
History: onset & trimester, distribution (palms/soles), nocturnal itch, sleep loss, systemic symptoms (jaundice, malaise, pale stools, dark urine), meds & comorbidities.
3
Examination: rash type/location, excoriations, jaundice.
Step 2

Differentiate by Rash

No Rash — Suspect OC
  • Typical start ≥ 28 wks; worse at night; often palms/soles.
  • ± excoriations; jaundice ~10%.
  • Action: Same‑day maternity referral; serum bile acids + LFTs (pregnancy ranges).
  • If tests normal but symptoms persist → weekly bile acids/LFTs.
Rash — PUPPP
  • Usually 3rd trimester; first pregnancy; multiple gestation ↑ risk.
  • Urticarial papules/plaques on abdominal striae (umbilicus spared) → may spread.
Rash — Atopic eruption
  • Often 1st trimester; personal/family atopy.
  • Eczematous flexural lesions ± papules/nodules.
Rash — Pemphigoid gestationis
  • 2nd–3rd trimester; periumbilical urticated plaques → tense bullae.
  • May flare at delivery; neonatal transient rash ~10%.
Other Causes
  • Non‑pregnancy causes (eczema, scabies, urticaria, infections, drugs, renal/hepatic/endocrine, haematological, psychogenic).
Safety Flags
  • Any suspected OC → same‑day maternity referral.
  • Pemphigoid gestationis → urgent dermatology + obstetrics.
Step 3

Management Overview

Obstetric Cholestasis (no rash)
  • Refer same day to maternity unit.
  • Secondary care: bile acids & LFTs (pregnancy ranges), viral/autoimmune work‑up, urine dip, BP, liver US, fetal surveillance (e.g., CTG).
  • Symptom relief: liberal emollients ± menthol 0.5–1%; sedating antihistamine at night (chlorphenamine 4 mg or promethazine 25 mg; off‑label for pruritus).
  • Self‑care: cooling measures; loose cotton clothing.
  • May be initiated by specialists: ursodeoxycholic acid ± rifampicin, vitamin K; consider elective early delivery.
  • Postpartum: LFTs ≥ 2 weeks; if abnormal, repeat; if abnormal > 8 weeks → specialist. Recurrence risk in future pregnancies 45–90%.
Polymorphic Eruption of Pregnancy (PUPPP)
  • Reassure: unpleasant but no serious risk; lasts 4–6 wks; resolves postpartum.
  • Treat: emollients; moderately potent topical corticosteroids; sedating antihistamine if sleep‑disturbed.
  • Refer dermatology if severe/generalised or poor response → short oral steroids may be indicated.
Atopic Eruption of Pregnancy
  • Reassure: responds well; no serious risk; recurrence common in future pregnancies; infant may have ↑ atopy risk.
  • Treat: emollients; moderately potent topical corticosteroids; sedating antihistamine if needed.
  • Refer dermatology if severe/unresponsive → short oral steroids or phototherapy.
Pemphigoid Gestationis
  • Urgent referral: dermatology and obstetrics.
  • Treat: mild → topical corticosteroids + antihistamines; moderate/severe → systemic corticosteroids in specialist care.
  • Antenatal surveillance due to ↑ risk of preterm birth/reduced fetal growth. Neonatal transient rash ~10%.
Self‑Care (All Patients)
  • Short, cool/tepid baths or showers; avoid hot water; pat dry.
  • Use emollients (incl. as soap substitute); avoid soaps, bubble baths, perfumes.
  • Keep nails short; rub rather than scratch; keep rooms cool, consider humidifying in winter.
  • Wear loose cotton/silk; avoid wool/synthetics.
  • Avoid triggers: spicy food, alcohol, caffeine.
Night‑time Sedating Antihistamines (off‑label for pruritus)
  • Chlorphenamine: 4 mg at bedtime. Advise on sedation; avoid driving/machinery if drowsy.
  • Promethazine: 25 mg at bedtime. Advise on sedation; phenothiazines may potentiate QT prolongation—consider risk factors.
  • Use caution in hepatic/renal impairment, epilepsy, glaucoma, urinary retention, severe CVD/bronchitis/asthma (see local formulary).
Key Notes

Prognosis & Complications (for context)

  • Pregnancy dermatoses with rash (PUPPP, atopic eruption, pemphigoid gestationis) rarely cause serious maternal/infant complications.
  • Obstetric cholestasis: risks include stillbirth (risk increases with bile acids ≥100 μmol/L), iatrogenic prematurity, fetal distress, meconium, vitamin K deficiency. Severity of itch does not correlate well with bile acids.
  • Typical resolution postpartum; OC recurs in 45–90% of future pregnancies; pemphigoid gestationis may recur (50–70%).
