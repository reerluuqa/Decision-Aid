Assessment

Confirm itch, define pattern, map triggers, examine with intent

Clarify the symptom

  • Confirm vulval itch rather than primary pain.
  • Note location, duration, onset, and whether extragenital itch is present.

Pattern clues

  • Acute onset: consider contact dermatitis or candidiasis.
  • Nocturnal itch + perianal itch: consider threadworm.
  • Consider conditions that may involve vagina/skin/oral mucosa.

Triggers & exposures

  • Sexual activity, menses, exercise.
  • Friction, heat, moisture.
  • Hygiene products (soaps, deodorants, perfumes, wipes).
  • Barrier contraception, lubricants, topical treatments.

Associated symptoms & risks

  • Discharge suggests infection (e.g., candidiasis/BV/trichomoniasis).
  • Assess STI risk where appropriate.
  • Ask about sleep disturbance, mood impact, sexual difficulty.

Medical context

  • Atopy/eczema/psoriasis history.
  • Diabetes (increases candidal risk).
  • Systemic illness (renal/hepatic disease, anaemia) if generalized itch.
  • Urinary/faecal incontinence and over-washing patterns.
  • Menopause or breastfeeding-related low oestrogen symptoms.

Examination focus

  • Explain exam, obtain consent, offer chaperone.
  • Inspect colour/texture; look for fissures, excoriation, erosions, ulcers, lichenification, lesions, tumours, atrophy, scarring.
  • Speculum if discharge or to assess vaginal mucosa; take swab if indicated.
  • Check other sites: elbows/knees/nails (psoriasis), skin for eczema, oral mucosa if lichen planus suspected.

Investigations

Often clinical ‚Äî test when findings guide management

When discharge or infection suspected

  • High vaginal swab if discharge, infection signs, or candidiasis suspected.
  • If STI suspected, refer to specialist sexual health services for confirmatory testing.

When itch seems generalized

  • Consider FBC, ferritin, TFTs, renal and liver function, glucose/HbA1c.

Specific condition considerations

  • In possible lichen sclerosus, additional tests may be used to exclude associated autoimmune disease.

Secondary care tools

  • Vulvoscopy, biopsy, and allergy/patch testing when indicated.
Practical rule: Investigate if results will change treatment or urgency.

Management pathways

Known-cause versus unknown-cause structured approach

Known cause ‚Äî primary care actions

  • Contact dermatitis: remove irritants (soaps, fragrances, condoms, lubricants, topical agents). Use simple emollient as soap substitute; consider barriers if incontinence-related.
  • Short course low potency steroid for mild itch; higher potency short course for more severe or lichenified skin where appropriate.
  • Treat co-existing infection with suitable combination topical therapy or oral antibiotics when indicated.
  • Consider sedating antihistamine at night if sleep disruption.
  • Refer for patch testing if avoidance + topical therapy fails.

Known cause ‚Äî conditions needing escalation

  • Lichen sclerosus / lichen planus: refer to confirm diagnosis; specialists typically initiate very potent steroids with longer-term plans.
  • Lichen simplex: identify triggers; potent/very potent steroid short course to break itch-scratch cycle; gradual step-down may be required.
  • Infections/infestations: manage per specific guidelines (candidiasis, BV, herpes, scabies, threadworm, pubic lice, trichomoniasis).

Unknown cause ‚Äî pragmatic stepwise relief

  • Priorities: symptom control, reduce inflammation, restore barrier, prevent/treat secondary infection.
  • Start with regular emollient (apply directly + as soap substitute) and self-care adjustments.
  • Consider night-time antipruritic options if sleep affected.
  • Short trial of low potency topical steroid (e.g., hydrocortisone 1% ointment).
  • Consider combination steroid/antifungal or steroid/antibacterial if co-infection suspected.
  • If persistent despite these measures, refer to dermatology/gynaecology/vulval clinic as per local pathway; continue helpful measures while awaiting review.

Self-care script

High-yield advice patients can act on today

Do

  • Shower rather than bathe when possible.
  • Clean vulval area max once daily with a simple, unscented emollient soap substitute.
  • Dab dry gently or use a cool hairdryer held away from skin.
  • Keep nails short; consider cotton gloves at night if scratching severe.
  • Use cool flannel/gel packs for short-term itch relief.

Avoid

  • Soap or water-only washing that dries skin.
  • Shampoo/bubble bath residue contacting vulval skin.
  • Wet wipes, perfumed pads/liners, OTC vulval ‚Äúsoothing‚Äù products.
  • Antiseptics and abrasive sponges/flannels.
  • Tight or synthetic underwear (e.g., nylon); prefer breathable options.

Additional high-yield avoidance list

  • Fragranced laundry products for underwear.
  • Spermicidally lubricated condoms if symptoms correlate.
  • Coloured/scented toilet tissue.
  • Nail varnish if scratching with varnished nails.
  • Shaving pubic hair during active symptoms.
  • Scratching ‚Äî explain the itch-scratch cycle and how it perpetuates inflammation.
Give written info where available to reinforce consistent behaviour change.

Referral thresholds

When to escalate and how urgently

Routine / standard specialist referral

  • Cause unclear and symptoms persist despite initial measures.
  • Known cause but ongoing symptoms despite appropriate primary care management.
  • Consider dermatology, gynaecology, or dedicated vulval clinic depending on local access.

Possible pre-malignant disease

  • Suspected VIN, lichen sclerosus, or lichen planus should prompt specialist assessment.
  • Urgency depends on clinical concern and local pathways.

Urgent (within 2 weeks)

  • Unexplained vulval lump, ulceration, or bleeding (suspected vulval carcinoma).
  • Unexplained palpable mass in or at the entrance to the vagina.
  • Suspected lymphoma via relevant systemic features.
Document findings clearly and include photos/diagrams if local systems allow.
