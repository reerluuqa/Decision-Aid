Lichen Planus

Clinical Reference Guide

Overview & Epidemiology

What is Lichen Planus?

A pruritic, papular eruption characterized by violaceous color and polygonal shape with fine scale. Most likely a T-cell-mediated immunological disease.

Prevalence

Cutaneous LP: 0.2-1% of adults worldwide
Oral LP: 1-4% of population (more common)

Demographics

• Common in middle age
• Male:Female ratio = 1:1.5
• Can occur in families

Associations

• Primary biliary cirrhosis
• Hepatitis B & C (2x risk)
• Trauma (Köbner's phenomenon)

Quick Reference

Common Sites

Flexor surfaces, genitalia, mucous membranes

Color

Violaceous/purple

Shape

Polygonal, flat-topped

Key Feature

Wickham's striae

Clinical Presentation

Typical Lesion

Intensely itchy 2-5mm red/violet shiny flat-topped papule with white streaks (Wickham's striae)

Common Locations

Front of wrists, flexor forearms, genitals, lumbar region, ankles, shins

Oral Lesions

White slightly raised lesions with trabecular, lacy appearance on cheeks

Palms/Soles

Firm and yellow papules

Nail Involvement

Up to 10% of patients: longitudinal lines, depressions, dystrophy

Scalp

Usually spared, but can cause permanent scarring alopecia

Important Symptoms to Note

  • Blisters may occasionally occur
  • Brown discoloration as papules clear (especially in dark-skinned)
  • Mucosal lesions can be very painful and difficult to treat
  • Oral LP may cause heat sensitivity
  • Esophageal involvement can cause dysphagia and benign strictures

Clinical Variations

Hypertrophic Lichen Planus Common

Very itchy lesions on extensor surfaces, especially ankles. Long-lasting with residual scarring and pigmentation.

Atrophic Lichen Planus Less Common

Few lesions, often resolving annular or hypertrophic lesions.

Erosive/Ulcerative Lichen Planus Mucosal

Found on mucosal surfaces, develops from previous LP sites.

Follicular Lichen Planus (Lichen Planopilaris) Scarring

Keratotic papules merging into plaques. More common in women. May cause scarring alopecia.

Annular Lichen Planus Rare

Purely annular lesions. Papules with atrophic center on male genitalia or buccal mucosa.

Vesicular and Bullous Lichen Planus Rare

Develops from existing LP areas. Common on lower limbs and mouth.

Actinic Lichen Planus Geographic

Seen in subtropical regions. Nummular patches with hyperpigmented center. Avoid phototherapy.

Lichen Planus Pigmentosus Darker Skin

More common in darker skin types. Resembles erythema dyschromicum perstans.

Diagnosis & Differential

Clinical Diagnosis

Usually diagnosed clinically based on characteristic appearance. Histology not often required.

Biopsy Findings

• "Saw-tooth" pattern of epidermal hyperplasia
• Vacuolar alteration of basal layer
• Band-like lymphohistiocytic infiltrate (mainly T cells)
• Reduced melanocytes
• Direct immunofluorescence: IgM deposits

Differential Diagnosis

• Drug eruption (lichenoid)
• Eczema
• Psoriasis
• Candidiasis
• Cutaneous graft-versus-host reaction
• Discoid lupus erythematosus
• Granuloma annulare
• Lichen amyloidosis
• Leukoplakia
• Lichen sclerosus
• Pemphigus
• Sarcoidosis
• Basal cell carcinoma

Management Algorithm

1

Initial Assessment

• May resolve spontaneously within 1 year (skin lesions)
• Mucosal lesions more persistent
• Stop suspected drugs if applicable

2

Symptomatic Treatment

For itching and mild disease:

Moderately potent topical steroids Intralesional steroid injections Sedating antihistamines
3

Local Treatments

For symptomatic cutaneous or mucosal disease:

Potent topical corticosteroids Topical calcineurin inhibitors Tacrolimus ointment Pimecrolimus cream Topical retinoids
4

Systemic Treatment

For widespread or severe disease (1-3 months):

Systemic steroids (prednisolone) Acitretin Hydroxychloroquine Methotrexate Azathioprine Mycophenolate mofetil
5

Special Considerations

• PUVA therapy for pruritus reduction
• Avoid phototherapy in actinic LP
• Sun protection essential for actinic LP
• Long courses of antibiotics (metronidazole, sulfasalazine)
• Antifungals (griseofulvin)
• JAK inhibitors (anecdotal success)

Complications & Red Flags

Malignancy Risk

  • Oral LP: 1.1% risk of oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC)
  • Higher risk in smokers, alcohol-dependent patients, HCV-infected patients
  • Rare carcinoma association with vulval lesions in women

Monitoring Requirements

  • Regular follow-up for oral LP patients
  • Careful monitoring of hypertrophic lesions
  • Watch for persistent ulceration

Other Complications

• Hypertrophic lesions may leave residual hyperpigmentation
• Scarring alopecia from scalp involvement
• Nail destruction in severe cases
• Esophageal strictures from esophageal involvement

Prognosis & Follow-up

Cutaneous Lesions

Spontaneous resolution
12-24 months

Oral Lesions

Chronic course
May never resolve

Hypertrophic Lesions

Longer duration
May scar

Pruritus

Decreases with time

Follow-up Recommendations

• Regular monitoring for oral LP patients (OSCC risk)
• Annual review for persistent lesions
• Patient education on warning signs
• Dermatology referral for atypical or persistent cases
