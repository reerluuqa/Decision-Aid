Acute Cellulitis – One‑Page Clinical Algorithm

Scope: Acute cellulitis in primary care (excludes bites, diabetic foot, persistent/recurrent detailed mgmt) Adults & Children: ≥ 1 month Severity: Eron I–IV framework

1) Diagnosis

  • Typical features: acute red, hot, swollen, tender skin; usually unilateral lower limb.
  • Systemic features may include fever, malaise; check vitals.
  • Exclude mimics: DVT, gout, septic arthritis, contact dermatitis/eczema, insect bite, venous insufficiency, erysipelas.
  • Look for skin breaks (ulcer, wound, tinea pedis, eczema); assess oedema/lymphoedema.
  • Eron severity: I (no systemic toxicity), II (systemically unwell or significant comorbidity), III (marked systemic upset/unstable comorbidity or limb‑threatening), IV (sepsis or life‑threatening e.g., necrotizing fasciitis).
  • Investigations not routine. Consider swab only if broken skin and: penetrating injury, water exposure, or infection acquired outside the UK. In systemic illness/comorbidity: FBC, CRP/ESR, U&Es, LFTs.

2) Admit / Urgent Refer if

  • Suspected necrotizing fasciitis, sepsis, orbital cellulitis, osteomyelitis, or septic arthritis.
  • Severe systemic illness: confusion, tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension.
  • Limb‑threatening infection / severe immunocompromise.
  • Facial or periorbital cellulitis (unless very mild).
  • Spreading infection not responding to oral antibiotics; or unable to take oral therapy.
  • Recurrent cellulitis (≥2 episodes/12 months): consider secondary care advice for prophylaxis.

3) Primary Care Management

  • Mark edges with a single‑use marker to monitor spread.
  • Supportive: elevate limb, encourage fluids, analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen).
  • Avoid compression garments in the acute phase.
  • Skin care: regular emollients to prevent dryness/cracking.
  • Address risk factors: diabetes control, treat eczema/tinea pedis, manage venous disease/oedema, weight management.

5) Follow‑Up (2–3 days)

  • Reassess at 48–72 hours.
  • Consider alternative diagnoses if poor response.
  • Swab if not already done and broken skin with atypical exposures (penetrating injury, water‑borne organisms, outside UK).
  • De‑escalate or switch antibiotics per results if no improvement; use narrow‑spectrum where possible.
  • Refer/seek specialist advice if spreading and not responding to oral therapy.

6) Prevention of Recurrence

  • Manage skin integrity: emollients; treat tinea pedis; manage eczema and ulcers.
  • Risk factor modification: weight management, glycaemic control, manage oedema/venous insufficiency, foot care (esp. diabetes).
  • Recurrent episodes (≥2/12 mo): consider referral for prophylactic antibiotics guidance.
