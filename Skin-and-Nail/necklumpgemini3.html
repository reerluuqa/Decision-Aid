Neck Lump Assessment & Management

Interactive clinical algorithm for the diagnosis and management of neck lumps in adults and children (from 1 month old).

1

Assessment

Red Flags (Urgency Stratification)

High index of suspicion for malignancy if user is 40+ years old or has risk factors (smoking, alcohol).

ACTION: Urgent 2-week wait referral likely required.
  • Timescale: Onset, duration. Persistent (>6 weeks) = higher risk.
  • Change: Rapid growth vs slow.
  • Pain: Tenderness suggests infection/inflammation. Painless suggests malignancy/cyst.
  • Recent illness: URTI, dental issues, travel, insect bites, pets (cats).
  • Family History: Head & neck cancer, endocrine tumors (MEN2).
Palpation (Stand behind patient):
  • Anterior Triangle: Thyroid, submandibular, branchial cyst.
  • Posterior Triangle: Lymph nodes, carotid body tumor.
  • Midline: Thyroglossal cyst, dermoid.
Characteristics:

Size, consistency (hard/rubbery/soft), fixation (mobility), pulsatility, temperature.

Dynamic Tests:
• Swallow: Thyroid lumps/Thyroglossal cysts move UP.
• Tongue Protrusion: Thyroglossal cysts move UP.

Differential Diagnosis

Select a category to view distinguishing features.

Reactive / Infective

Tender, mobile, rapid onset. Associated with URTI, dental issues. Usually subsides 3-6 weeks.

Malignant

Lymphoma: Rubbery, painless, rapid growth.
Metastasis: Hard, fixed, matted.
Virchow's Node: Left supraclavicular (GI/Lung primary).

Moves upwards on swallowing. Can be nodular or diffuse (goitre).

  • Benign: Cysts, colloid nodules, adenomas.
  • Malignant: Solitary nodule, rapid growth, hoarseness, associated lymphadenopathy.

Parotid (80% benign) or Submandibular.

  • Stones (Sialolithiasis): Pain/swelling worse on eating.
  • Tumors: Slow growing, painless (Benign). Rapid, painful, facial nerve palsy (Malignant).
  • Infection (Sialadenitis): Tender, warm, purulent discharge.
Thyroglossal Cyst: Midline. Moves up with tongue protrusion. Usually <20y.
Branchial Cyst: Lateral neck (anterior border of SCM). Fluctuant. Young adults.
Dermoid Cyst: Midline, tethered to skin, painless.
  • Lipoma: Soft, mobile, subcutaneous.
  • Sebaceous Cyst: Punctum visible, attached to skin.
  • Carotid Body Tumor: Pulsatile, mobile side-to-side but not up-down.

Management Scenarios

Scenario: Unexplained Lump

Urgent

2-Week Wait Referral (Head & Neck)

If aged ≥45 with unexplained lump OR any age with persistent unexplained lump.

Investigate

Urgent Ultrasound

Adults ≥25: Urgent US appointment.
Children/Young Adults <25: Very urgent US (within 48hrs).

Emergency

Immediate Admission

Stridor, SVC obstruction signs, dysphagia with aspiration.

Lymphadenopathy

Common
Suspected Infection (Viral/Bacterial):

Treat source (Antibiotics if bacterial). Review in 2-4 weeks.

Unresolved after 2-4 weeks?

Urgent FBC (exclude leukemia).
Urgent CXR (if >40y or supraclavicular).
Refer ENT.

RED FLAG: Generalized nodes + bruising/fatigue = Urgent FBC (48hrs).

Thyroid Lump

Caution
Primary Care Investigation:

Serum TSH/TFTs.

Urgent Cancer Pathway if:
  • Unexplained lump
  • Voice change / Hoarseness
  • Associated lymphadenopathy
  • Painless rapid expansion
Routine Endocrinology:

Abnormal TFTs but non-suspicious nodule.

Salivary Gland

Stones (Calculi):

Refer ENT/MaxFax. Hydration, sialogogues, analgesia pending appointment.

Tumor / Unexplained Swelling:

Urgent ENT Referral (2 weeks).

Skin Lump

Benign (Lipoma/Cyst):

Reassure. No action unless infected/symptomatic.

Infection/Abscess:

Antibiotics. Refer for Incision & Drainage if needed.

RED FLAG: Irregular shape, pigmentation, ulceration, fixation, bleeding -> Urgent Dermatology/Plastic Surgery Referral.

Referral Guidelines Summary

Emergency (Same Day)

  • Stridor / Airway compromise
  • SVC Obstruction
  • Dysphagia with aspiration
  • Kawasaki disease suspected
  • Carotid blow-out risk (post-trauma/surgery)

Urgent (2 Week Wait)

  • Unexplained lump >45y
  • Unexplained persistent lump (any age)
  • Thyroid nodule + Red Flags
  • Unexplained Lymphadenopathy (after FBC/CXR)
  • Salivary gland swelling (persistent)

Routine / Reassure

  • Simple lipoma/cyst
  • Reactive nodes (post-infection)
  • Thyroid cyst (painful bleed)
  • Simple goitre (Normal TFTs)
