Autoimmune Hair Loss

Non-scarring inflammation of hair follicles. Sudden onset, patchy loss.
Sudden Onset T-Cell Mediated
Prevalence 1 in 1000 people
Peak Incidence Children & Young Adults

Poor Prognosis Indicators

Be vigilant if the patient presents with these factors:

Ophiasis pattern (occipital scalp) Nail Dystrophy Onset < 20 yo Atopy History Family History of AA Extensive Body Hair Loss

Management Protocol

Current treatments stimulate hair growth but do not prevent relapse. Choice depends on extent of loss and patient age.

Pharmacological Steps

  • 1st Line: Topical Corticosteroids
    Clobetasol 0.05% (sparingly) twice daily

    Preferred for children or adults refusing injections. Use solution/gel for scalp.

    Watch for: Skin atrophy (temporary)
  • 1st Line (Adults): Intralesional Steroids
    Triamcinolone acetonide 2.5-5mg every 4-6 weeks

    Inject into patch, brows, or beard. Use topical anesthetic first.

    Watch for: Atrophy, folliculitis, pain.
  • Adjunct: Topical Minoxidil
    2% or 5% solution/foam twice daily

    Helps prevent scalp irritation from steroids. Can stop once regrowth achieved.

  • 2nd Line (Rapid Progression): Oral Steroids
    Prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (2 weeks), then taper

    Consult Specialist. Not for long-term use.

Supportive Care (Always Safe)

  • Cosmetic Camouflage: Sprays, pastes, or powders.
  • Wigs: Synthetic or real hair; vacuum fit for Totalis.
  • Psych Support: Essential for distress management.

Specialist Treatment Steps

  • 1st Line: Topical Immunotherapy
    DPCP, DNCB, or SADBE (Weekly application)

    Requires sensitization phase. Adjust concentration to induce mild eczema.

    Side effects: Contact dermatitis, lymphadenopathy, pigment changes.
  • Emerging: Baricitinib (JAK Inhibitor)
    Systemic (Oral)

    FDA/EMA approved for severe AA. Increases hair regrowth vs placebo.

    Adverse: Acne, elevated CK, lipids changes.
  • Fallen out of favor / Disappointing
    Anthralin (staining/irritation), PUVA (cancer risk), Alefacept/Etanercept (ineffective).

Supportive Care (Critical)

In extensive cases, pharmacological success varies. Non-medical support is primary.

  • Prosthetics: High-quality wigs/vacuum prostheses.
  • Counseling: Address significant psychological impact.
  • Patient Groups: National Alopecia Areata Foundation.
