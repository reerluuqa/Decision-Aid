Rosacea Clinical Decision Support Tool

Evidence-based guidance for the diagnosis and management of rosacea in primary care

Red Flags

  • Severe ocular symptoms (pain, vision changes)
  • Rapidly progressive disease
  • Severe inflammatory nodules
  • Poor response to standard therapy

First-Line Management

  • Broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30+)
  • Gentle skin care regimen
  • Topical metronidazole or azelaic acid
  • Avoid known triggers

Key Facts

  • Affects 5.46% of general population
  • More common in women (5.41%)
  • Typically affects ages 30-50
  • Ocular involvement in >50% of cases

What is Rosacea?

Rosacea is a chronic, inflammatory skin condition predominantly affecting the convexities of the centrofacial region (cheeks, chin, nose, and central part of forehead).

Clinical Features:

Diagnostic Features:
  • Phymatous changes
  • Persistent erythema
Major Features:
  • Flushing/transient erythema
  • Papules and pustules
  • Telangiectasia
  • Eye manifestations

Diagnosis & Assessment

Diagnose rosacea if there is at least one 'diagnostic' or two 'major' clinical features present.

Assessment Approach:

History:
  • Onset and duration of symptoms
  • Distribution and extent of involvement
  • Ocular symptoms
  • Psychosocial impact
  • Known trigger factors
  • Previous treatments and response
Examination:
  • Assess facial skin involvement
  • Check for phymatous changes
  • Evaluate erythema and oedema
  • Look for papules, pustules, nodules
  • Assess for ocular manifestations

Clinical Tip

In darker skin phototypes (V and VI), erythema and telangiectasia may be difficult to detect visually. Consider using a dermatoscope for examination.

Management

Management should be based on the presenting clinical phenotype, and may involve more than one treatment approach.

Self-Management Measures:

  • Broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30+)
  • Avoid known triggers
  • Temperature regulation
  • Dietary modifications
  • Gentle skin care regimen
  • Non-oily emollients for dry skin
  • UV protection sunglasses
  • Patient education materials

Pharmacological Treatment:

Topical Treatments

  • Metronidazole 0.75% gel/cream (once or twice daily)
  • Azelaic acid 15% gel (twice daily)
  • Ivermectin 1% cream (once daily)
  • Brimonidine 0.5% gel (once daily for persistent erythema)

Oral Treatments

  • Doxycycline 40mg modified-release (once daily)
  • Doxycycline 100mg (once daily) for inflammatory papules/pustules
  • Erythromycin 250-500mg (twice daily) if doxycycline contraindicated
  • Consider oral isotretinoin for severe or refractory cases (specialist initiation)

Drug Interactions to Consider

  • Rifampicin reduces doxycycline effectiveness
  • Erythromycin interacts with Lomitapide and Corticosteroids
  • Brimonidine gel should not be applied to damaged skin

When to Refer

Urgent Referral Needed:

  • Severe ocular involvement
  • Visual impairment
  • Corneal involvement
  • Rosacea fulminans

Routine Referral Consider:

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Failure to respond to treatment
  • Significant psychosocial impact
  • Phymatous changes requiring surgery
  • Consideration for isotretinoin

Specialist Services

Depending on clinical presentation, consider referral to:

  • Dermatology (for skin manifestations)
  • Ophthalmology (for ocular involvement)
  • Plastic surgery (for phymatous changes)
  • Skin camouflage service
