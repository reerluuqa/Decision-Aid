Cutaneous Larva Migrans

Complications & Red Flags

Monitor for signs of secondary bacterial infection (0-8% of cases) or Loeffler syndrome.

  • ⚠️ Increasing Pain/Warmth
  • ⚠️ Spreading Erythema
  • ⚠️ Pustules/Pus
  • ⚠️ Respiratory Symptoms (Rare)

Diagnosis & History

CLINICAL DIAGNOSIS (NO TESTS NEEDED)

  • Travel: Recent visit to tropics/subtropics (Caribbean, Brazil, Mexico, SE Asia).
  • Exposure: Walking barefoot or lying directly on sand.
  • Appearance: Intensely pruritic, serpiginous (snake-like), raised, erythematous tracks.
  • Progression: Tracks advance few mm to few cm daily.
  • Location: Most common on feet, thighs, buttocks (contact with sand).
  • Onset: Lesions appear 1-5 days after penetration.
Note: Vesiculobullous or papular lesions occur in 10-40% of cases.

Management (Action)

1st Choice: Oral Ivermectin
Dose: 200 micrograms/kg orally as a single dose
Avoid if < 15kg. Off-label use in some regions.
Alternative: Oral Albendazole
Dose: 400 mg orally once daily for 3 days
Avoid in children < 1 year.
Follow Up:
  • Pruritus & eruption usually resolve within 48 hours of therapy.
  • If no response after 1-2 weeks, repeat dose.
  • Pregnancy: Delay treatment or consult specialist (Teratogenicity risk in animals).

Do Not Use

  • Cryotherapy (Liquid Nitrogen) Larvae survive freezing and move ahead of track. Ineffective and painful.
  • Topical Corticosteroids Pruritus is not histamine-related. No benefit.
  • Antihistamines Ineffective for symptom relief.
Prevention:

Wear shoes/sandals. Avoid direct sand contact (use chairs/mattresses).

Differential Diagnosis (Snapshot)

Condition Key Differentiator Differentiating Test
Larva Currens Rapid movement (cm/hour), perianal/abdomen. Stool microscopy (Strongyloides)
Gnathostomiasis Migratory swelling/nodule, raw fish history. Serology
Scabies Worse at night, burrows, interdigital spaces. Skin scraping (Mites)
Cercarial Dermatitis Diffuse maculopapular rash (non-migratory), fresh water exposure. Serology (Schistosoma)
