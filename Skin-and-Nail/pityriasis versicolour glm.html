Based on current selection: Topical treatment recommended

What to ask
Key Points
  • Pityriasis versicolor is not contagious - the yeast is normally present on skin
  • Most common in teenagers and young adults with peak in early twenties
  • More prevalent in hot, humid climates (up to 50% in tropical regions)
What to examine
What to prescribe
For extensive areas:

Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (not licensed for children under 12)

  • Adults and children 12-17 years: Apply once daily for up to 5 days
  • Use as body wash, leave on skin for up to 15 minutes, then rinse
For small areas:

Topical antifungal creams (apply twice daily for 2-3 weeks)

  • Clotrimazole (preferred in pregnancy)
  • Econazole
  • Ketoconazole
  • Terbinafine

What to ask
Key Points

Mild cases with limited involvement typically respond well to topical treatments. Clotrimazole is preferred in pregnancy.

Red Flags
  • No response to appropriate topical treatment after 3-4 weeks
  • Patient is pregnant and symptoms persist
  • Diagnosis remains uncertain
What to ask
Key Points
  • Persisting pigmentary changes don't necessarily indicate treatment failure
  • Consider skin scrapings for microscopy if diagnosis uncertain
  • Oral antifungals may be required for extensive or resistant cases

Alternative Topical Options:
  • Try a different class of topical antifungal
  • Ensure adequate duration of treatment (2-3 weeks minimum)
Oral Antifungal Options:

Itraconazole (not for pregnant/breastfeeding women)

  • Adults: 200 mg once daily for 7 days

Fluconazole (not for pregnant/breastfeeding women)

  • Adults: 50 mg once daily for 2-4 weeks
  • OR 300-400 mg once weekly for 1-3 weeks

When to refer
Red Flags
  • No response to oral antifungal therapy
  • Uncertain diagnosis despite skin scrapings
  • Immunocompromised patient with persistent infection
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding woman not responding to topical therapy
What to ask
Key Points
  • Recurrence is common (up to 80% within 2 years)
  • More frequent in warm, humid climates
  • Prophylactic treatment before sun exposure may be beneficial
What to prescribe
Treatment of Recurrence:

Re-treat each episode as for initial presentation

  • Topical antifungal for localized recurrence
  • Ketoconazole shampoo for extensive recurrence
  • Oral antifungals for severe or resistant cases
Prophylactic Treatment:

Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (children 12+ and adults)

  • Apply once every 1-4 weeks to whole body
  • For prophylaxis before sun exposure: Apply once daily for maximum of 3 days prior to exposure
When to review
When to refer
Red Flags
  • Frequent severe recurrences despite prophylaxis
  • Need for long-term oral antifungal prophylaxis
  • Recurrent infection in immunocompromised patient
When to refer
Immediate Referral Needed:
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Inadequate response to optimal treatment in primary care
  • Children under 12 years old
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women with extensive disease
  • Severe or very extensive disease
  • Immunocompromised patients
Referral Process:
  • Include details of treatments tried and duration
  • Document any skin scraping results
  • Provide relevant medical history (immunosuppression, etc.)
  • Consider photos of lesions if diagnosis uncertain
