Burns & Scalds — One‑Page Algorithm

Primary care quick reference (NICE CKS, last revised Jan 2023). From age ≥1 month (non‑radiation, non‑frostbite).

1) Immediate First Aid

Thermal burns

  • Stop burning (drop/roll/smother), remove non‑adherent clothing & jewellery.
  • Cool with cool/tepid running water for 15–30 min (ideally within 20 min). No ice.
  • Prevent hypothermia (keep warm while cooling large areas).
  • Cover: lay cling film in sheets (don’t wrap) or use clean sheet/clear bag (hand burns).
  • Elevate injured part; avoid topical creams pre‑assessment; give simple analgesia.

Chemical burns

  • Identify agent; remove contaminated clothing; brush off dry agents.
  • Irrigate copiously with water for ~1 hour. Do not neutralise.
  • Arrange immediate A&E

Electrical burns

  • Don’t approach if high‑voltage (≥1000 V). For low‑voltage: isolate power or use non‑conductive object.
  • Arrange immediate A&E

3) Triage & Referral — IMMEDIATE A&E if any apply

Injury factors

  • All complex burns; any full‑thickness.
  • Deep dermal >5% TBSA adult, any in children; circumferential deep dermal.
  • High‑pressure steam; suspected inhalation injury.
  • Involves face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum or flexures (neck, axilla, elbow, knee).

Patient/Context

  • Significant co‑morbidities or high risk of complications.
  • Associated trauma (fracture, head injury, crush, penetrating).
  • Sepsis.
  • Suspected non‑accidental injury (safeguarding).

Also consider A&E

  • Sunburn with heat exhaustion/heat stroke features.
  • Very young child with >10% BSA sunburn blistering.
  • Adults with >15–25% BSA blistering.
Refer/seek burns unit advice if unsure, pain uncontrolled, dressings unmanageable, or not healed <2 weeks.

4) Primary‑Care Management (non‑complex)

Superficial epidermal (e.g., sunburn)

  • Cool bath/shower or cold compresses.
  • Emollient massage (e.g., emulsifying ointment) for itch/dryness.
  • Paracetamol/ibuprofen PRN.
  • Hydration; sun protection education.
  • Urgent review if blisters develop (may indicate dermal injury).

Superficial dermal

  • Wound care by trained clinician; clean & dress; reassess at 48 h, then every 3–5 days until healed.
  • Expect exudate first 72 h; optimise analgesia.
  • Hydration; infection red flags counselling.
  • After healing: daily emollient massage 3–6 mo to reduce hypertrophic scarring; SPF 30–50 or clothing for 1–2 yrs.
  • Refer if not healing as expected by 2 weeks; consider PT/OT for contractures; camouflage services if needed.
Wound care quick guide
  • Clean: lukewarm tap water (clean technique) or 0.9% saline (sterile). Warm fluid to body/room temp; irrigate gently.
  • Debride: preserve blister roof; consider deroofing if >1 cm², likely to rupture, or over joints.
  • Dress: non‑adherent (paraffin gauze, silicone nylon, film, hydrocolloid) + absorbent secondary pad; secure well.
  • Avoid routine: prophylactic antibiotics, antimicrobial dressings/creams (e.g., silver sulfadiazine) absent infection.
  • Analgesia before changes.

5) Infection Management

  • Suspect infection if ↑pain, odour, excessive exudate, fever, or erythema.
  • Take wound swab before antibiotics.
  • Start empirical oral antibiotics (per acute cellulitis guidance/local formulary).
  • Review clinically; if improved but not resolved at 7 days ⇒ consider another course; if poor response ⇒ check swab, change agent, re‑review.
  • No antibiotics if no infection.

7) Safeguarding — Red Flags

  • Absent/unsuitable explanation; non‑mobile patient; pattern not consistent with accidental contact.
  • Implement‑shaped injury; forced‑immersion patterns (glove/stocking, sharply demarcated).
  • Delayed presentation; unrelated adult presenting child; changing history; trigger event (e.g., soiling).
  • Lack of concern/supervision; failure to engage with care.
  • No splash marks; restraint signs; flexion‑crease sparing; doughnut sign; injuries of various ages.

8) Follow‑up & Prognosis

  • Reassess depth at 24–72 h—burns may evolve.
  • Superficial epidermal: heal <7 days (no scar). Superficial dermal: <14 days (no scar).
  • Deep dermal/full thickness: may need surgical management; risk of contracture/scar.
  • Complications: infection/sepsis, TSS (children), inhalation injury, hypothermia, AKI, compartment syndrome.
  • Late: chronic pain/itch, contractures, hypertrophic scarring, psychosocial impact, sleep disturbance.
