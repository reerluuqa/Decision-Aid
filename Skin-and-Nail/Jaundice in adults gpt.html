Jaundice in Adults – Primary Care Quick Reference

Condensed aide‑mémoire for rapid assessment & decisions • Based on NICE CKS (June 2025)

Step 1

Initial Recognition

  • Clinical jaundice evident when serum bilirubin typically > 51 μmol/L.
  • Confirm true jaundice (scleral icterus) vs pseudojaundice (e.g., carotenaemia, tanning products, Addison’s).
Tip
Dark urine + pale stools suggest obstructive jaundice; can be transient in acute hepatic illness.
Step 2

Immediate Red Flags → Same‑Day Admission

  • Hepatic encephalopathy: confusion, altered mental state, ataxia, asterixis.
  • Severe hepatic dysfunction: bruising, purpura, coagulopathy.
  • GI bleed: haematemesis or melaena.
  • Sepsis: fever, hypotension, tachycardia, cyanosis/mottling.
  • Ascending cholangitis: fever + RUQ pain + jaundice (Charcot’s triad).
  • Marked abdominal pain/tenderness or peritonism; persistent vomiting; dehydration.
  • Bilirubin > 100 μmol/L or abnormal INR.
  • Suspected paracetamol overdose.
  • Pregnancy, frailty, or major comorbidity.
Step 3

Focused History

  • Onset, duration, speed, prior episodes.
  • Stool/urine colour; pruritus; systemic symptoms (weight loss, fever, nausea).
  • Pain pattern: none (hepatitis/Gilbert’s) vs RUQ/back (stones, pancreatitis, pancreatic Ca).
  • Alcohol (units/week); features of dependence.
  • Travel; exposures; IVDU; tattoos; transfusions; sexual history.
  • PMH: gallstones, biliary surgery, hepatitis, autoimmune disease, MASLD, thyroid disease.
  • Drugs (Rx/OTC), herbals/supplements, cold/flu preps; paracetamol specifically.
  • Family history: inherited anaemias, haemoglobinopathies, liver disease.
  • Possibility of pregnancy.
Step 4

Examination

  • Vitals for sepsis; hydration status.
  • Stigmata of chronic liver disease: spider naevi, palmar erythema, clubbing, leukonychia, Dupuytren’s, gynecomastia.
  • Hepatic failure: asterixis, confusion/encephalopathy, ascites.
  • Lymph nodes: Troisier’s sign.
  • Masses: hepatomegaly, splenomegaly; palpable gallbladder.
  • RUQ tenderness; Murphy’s sign; generalized tenderness/peritonism.
Step 5

Investigations (if stable – do not delay necessary referrals)

Core bloods: FBC, U&E, LFTs (bilirubin, ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, albumin), clotting (PT/INR), ± amylase.
Serologies/extended (guided by risk): Hep A/B/C, immunoglobulins, autoimmune antibodies, ferritin (± transferrin saturation), α-1 antitrypsin; caeruloplasmin (<40y ± urinary copper); HbA1c; lipids.
Urine: dip for bilirubin (conjugated).
Imaging: abdominal ultrasound (stones, duct dilatation, steatosis, liver lesions, pancreatic mass). Note limitations with body habitus/bowel gas.
Do not delay urgent referral/admission when indicated while awaiting results.
Step 6

Interpret LFT Pattern

  • Isolated ↑ bilirubin → Gilbert’s, haemolysis. Repeat LFTs with split bilirubin ± FBC/retics.
  • Cholestatic (↑ALP ± GGT) → obstruction (stones/stricture/tumour), PBC/PSC, DILI, congestion.
  • Mixed (↑ALT/AST + ↑ALP) → cholangitis, DILI, advanced disease.
  • Hepatitic (↑ALT/AST > ↑ALP) → viral hepatitis, MASLD, alcohol, AIH, DILI.
  • Acute viral hepatitis may give ALT > 1000 U/L.
  • Ischaemia / paracetamol overdose may give ALT > 10,000 U/L.
  • AST:ALT ≥ 2 often alcohol-related. ALT > AST common in chronic viral infection.
  • Albumin ↓ / INR ↑ → significant dysfunction (acute or chronic).
  • ALP confounders: bone disease, vitamin D deficiency.
Step 7

Referral

All unexplained jaundice → immediate referral.
Where available, consider rapid access jaundice clinic/ambulatory unit for painless jaundice.
  • Age ≥ 40 with jaundice → 2‑week suspected cancer pathway (exclude pancreatic cancer).
  • Cholestatic LFT picture → Upper GI / Gastro / Hepatology (urgency per clinical judgement).
  • Hepatitic LFT picture → Urgent Liver clinic / Gastro.
  • Alcohol‑related disease → specialist liver service.
  • Inherited/metabolic (e.g., haemochromatosis, Wilson’s) → Hepatology.
Step 8

Primary Care Management (limited subset)

Gilbert’s syndrome — benign, no treatment; provide reassurance and advice on precipitants (illness, fasting, stress).
Hepatitis A (confirmed) in well patient — supportive care and public health guidance. Admit if systemically unwell, dehydrated or any hepatic decompensation.
