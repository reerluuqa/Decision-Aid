Psoriasis: One-Page Decision Aid

Fast reference for assessment and management of adult psoriasis in primary care: when to treat, what to use, and when to refer.

Immediate Red Flags

Any of the following in a person with psoriasis ➜ same-day specialist / emergency care.

  • Generalised pustular psoriasis (rapid erythema, widespread sterile pustules, systemic illness).
  • Erythrodermic psoriasis (>90% BSA, hot/red skin, fever, malaise, oedema, haemodynamic instability).
  • Severe systemic symptoms: high fever, tachycardia, weight loss, hypothermia or dehydration.
  • Severe or atypical psoriasis as possible HIV indicator condition.

History

  • Onset, course (relapsing/remitting), distribution, symptoms (itch, pain, bleeding, scaling).
  • Triggers: infection, trauma (Koebner), drugs (lithium, antimalarials, β-blockers, NSAIDs, ACE-i), stress, alcohol, smoking.
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, malaise, weight loss.
  • Joint symptoms: stiffness, pain, swelling, dactylitis, enthesitis.
  • Assess with PEST; score ≥3 ➜ rheumatology referral.
  • Psychological impact, DLQI, anxiety/depression, effect on work/relationships.
  • Comorbidities: obesity, metabolic syndrome, IBD, CVD, VTE risk.

Examination

  • Full skin exam: distribution (extensors, scalp, flexures, genital, trunk), lesion type, scale, colour, Auspitz sign.
  • Assess BSA (Rule of Nines). Extensive disease usually >10% BSA.
  • Check nails: pitting, onycholysis, “oil drop”, subungual hyperkeratosis.
  • Joints: swelling, tenderness, spinal and entheseal pain.

2. First-Line for All Patients

General Measures Foundations

  • Daily emollients for dryness, scale and itch; improve penetration of other topicals.
  • Lifestyle: weight loss if overweight, stop smoking, limit alcohol.
  • Address stress, anxiety, depression; signpost to support groups.
  • Reassure: psoriasis is not infectious.

Education & Monitoring Ongoing

  • Explain aim is symptom control; complete clearance may not be achievable.
  • Demonstrate application technique; discuss cosmetic acceptability.
  • Review at ~4 weeks, then as needed (and at least yearly if using potent steroids).
  • Advise to seek urgent review if new unexplained joint pain or swelling.

Check CVD risk at baseline and at least every 5 years, especially with severe disease.

3. Site-Specific Treatment Pathways

Chronic Plaque – Trunk & Limbs

  1. Initial (up to 4 weeks)
    Potent steroid OD + Vit D analogue OD (different times).
  2. If response adequate
    Continue until clear/nearly clear.
    Steroid max 8 weeks per site, then 4-week break (continue Vit D).
  3. If inadequate
    ➜ Stop steroid, use Vit D analogue BD for 8–12 weeks.
  4. If still inadequate
    ➜ Potent steroid BD for 4 weeks or coal tar prep or calcipotriol + betamethasone combo up to 4 weeks (once daily).
  5. Refractory / severe
    Consider short-contact dithranol (usually specialist-guided).

Scalp Psoriasis

  1. First line (4 weeks)
    Potent steroid OD.
  2. If inadequate
    Change formulation (e.g. shampoo/mousse) and add scale removal (salicylic acid, oils, emollients) applied before steroid.
  3. Still inadequate after a further 4 weeks
    ➜ Calcipotriol + betamethasone combo up to 4 weeks.
  4. Still inadequate
    ➜ Very potent steroid BD for 2 weeks or coal tar preparation or refer.
  5. If cannot use steroids (mild–moderate)
    Vit D analogue alone up to 8 weeks → if inadequate, coal tar or refer.

Facial, Flexural & Genital

  • First line: Mild or moderate steroid, limited to 1–2 weeks/month because of atrophy risk.
  • If inadequate or steroid risk high: Topical calcineurin inhibitor (tacrolimus/pimecrolimus) for up to 4 weeks – typically specialist-initiated.

Guttate Psoriasis

  • Often self-limiting (resolves within 3–4 months).
  • Treat with emollients and topical therapies as for trunk/limbs.
  • Refer if severe, persistent, diagnostic uncertainty, or recurrent episodes.

Nail Psoriasis

  • Consider high-potency steroid or Vit D analogue to nail folds/plate.
  • Refer if functionally limiting, severe cosmetic impact, or associated with suspected psoriatic arthritis.

4. When to Refer & Systemic Options

Indications for Dermatology Referral

  • Pustular or erythrodermic psoriasis (same day)
  • Failure of adequate topical therapy despite good adherence.
  • Extensive disease >10% BSA or high DLQI / severe psychological impact.
  • Diagnostic difficulty or atypical presentation.
  • Need for phototherapy or systemic therapy.

Rheumatology Referral

  • PEST score ≥3 or suggestive joint symptoms.

Specialist-Led Therapies

  • Phototherapy: NB-UVB for plaque/guttate; PUVA for selected cases (e.g. palmoplantar, resistant plaques).
  • Systemic non-biologics: methotrexate or ciclosporin (rapid or short-term control; palmoplantar pustulosis; considering conception).
  • Acitretin if methotrexate/ciclosporin not suitable or for pustular forms.
  • Other oral agents (e.g. apremilast, dimethyl fumarate, selected JAK/TYK2 inhibitors) for moderate–severe plaque psoriasis.
  • Biologics (IL / TNF-α inhibitors) – specialist initiation and monitoring only.

5. Monitoring & Follow-Up

  • 4-week review after starting or changing topical therapy: response, adherence, tolerability, impact on life.
  • At least annual review if using multiple intermittent/short courses of potent steroids – check for atrophy, striae and optimise regimen.
  • Assess CVD risk at diagnosis in severe psoriasis and at least every 5 years (BP, lipids, diabetes risk).
  • Advise on VTE risk and preventive measures in severe disease.
  • Annual assessment for psoriatic arthritis symptoms.

6. Quick Drug Safety Reminders

  • Avoid continuous long-term use of potent / very potent topical steroids: risk of irreversible atrophy and unstable psoriasis.
  • Do not use potent steroid courses >8 weeks at one site; very potent steroids for >4 weeks.
  • Beware rebound and possible generalised pustular/erythrodermic flare after abrupt withdrawal of systemic or potent topical steroids.
  • In phototherapy/PUVA, cumulative dose increases risk of dysplastic/neoplastic lesions (esp. SCC) – specialist monitoring required.
  • Systemic therapies (methotrexate, ciclosporin, JAK/TYK2 inhibitors, biologics) require strict lab and safety monitoring under specialist care.
