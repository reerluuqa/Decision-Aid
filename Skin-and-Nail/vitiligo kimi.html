üîç Diagnosis & Assessment
When to Suspect
Asymptomatic depigmented patches (may itch before appearance). Look for chalky-white, well-demarcated, flat lesions.
Key Features
Symmetrical distribution (non-segmental) or dermatomal (segmental). Check for Koebner phenomenon, white hairs, trichrome pattern.
Common Sites
Fingers/wrists, face (periorificial), axillae, groin/genitalia. Mucosal involvement more noticeable in darker skin.
Assessment Steps
  • Classify type: segmental vs non-segmental
  • Estimate extent: Vitiligo Extent Score (VES)
  • Photograph lesions for monitoring
  • Screen for thyroid disease (TSH, antibodies)

üíä Primary Care Management
1
Essential Patient Education & Support
Explain chronic nature, variable treatment response, recurrence risk. Provide resources.
Vitiligo Society - UK charity, support groups
Changing Faces - Skin camouflage services
BAD Patient Leaflet - British Association of Dermatologists
2
Protect Sun Protection
SPF 50 with 4-5 star UVA rating to affected & surrounding skin. Prescribable (endorse 'ACBS').
Examples
Uvistat¬Æ, other high-factor sunscreens
Advice
Avoid sunbeds, consider vitamin D supplementation if avoiding all sun
3
First-Line Topical Treatment
Potent/Very Potent Corticosteroid - once daily (off-label). Avoid periocular area. Review 3-6 months.
Intermittent Regimen
1 week on, 1 week off (especially for face, genitals, flexures)
Alternative
Topical calcineurin inhibitor (tacrolimus/pimecrolimus) for facial lesions
4
Assess Psychosocial Impact
Screen every 3-4 months using PHQ-4/9, GAD7, DLQI, or VIPs scale.
Mild Distress
Provide self-help resources
Moderate-Severe
Refer for CBT (individual/group)
5
Cosmetic Camouflage Options
Refer to skin camouflage service. Prescribable cover creams (endorse 'ACBS').
Prescribable: Covermark¬Æ, Dermacolor¬Æ, Keromask¬Æ, Veil¬Æ
Self-referral: Changing Faces provides application training

üî¨ Differential Diagnosis ‚ñº
Pityriasis Versicolor
Fine scale, pink/hypopigmented macules, often trunk/neck. KOH positive.
Pityriasis Alba
Children, dry patches with ill-defined borders, face/upper limbs
Post-inflammatory Hypopigmentation
History of eczema, psoriasis, lichen planus
Halo Nevus
Benign mole with depigmented halo, often in children/young adults
Chemical Leukoderma
Occupational exposure to phenolic compounds
Segmental Hypopigmentation
Nevus depigmentosus (congenital), tuberous sclerosis (ash-leaf)
üîó Associated Autoimmune Conditions ‚ñº
üîç Screen All Patients With:
Thyroid Disease
Most common association. Check TSH and antithyroid antibodies.
Other Autoimmune Diseases
Addison's, pernicious anemia, SLE, Type 1 DM, alopecia areata, RA, psoriasis, IBD
Family History
20% have first-degree relative with vitiligo; 7-10x increased risk
Personal History
Ask about symptoms of thyroid dysfunction, other autoimmune conditions

Key Prognosis Points

  • Chronic, unpredictable course with periods of activity and stability
  • Spontaneous repigmentation uncommon
  • Face, neck, trunk respond best; lips/distal extremities resistant
  • 44% relapse rate in first year after stopping treatment
  • Segmental vitiligo stabilizes rapidly but can recur after years
