Initial Assessment
‚ñº

Key questions to identify underlying cause:

1

History

  • Location, duration, onset
  • Trigger factors (sex, menses, hygiene)
  • Associated discharge
  • Products used (soaps, perfumes)
  • Medical history (atopy, diabetes)
  • Menopausal status
2

Examination

  • Skin color, texture, lesions
  • Look for lichenification, ulcers
  • Vaginal discharge examination
  • Extragenital skin check
  • Offer chaperone
  • Maintain dignity
3

Investigations

  • High vaginal swab if discharge
  • Blood tests if generalized itch
  • STI screen if risk factors
  • Biopsy in secondary care
  • Consider patch testing

Prescribing Information
‚ñº
üåø
Emollients

First-line for all patients

  • Use as soap substitute
  • Apply directly to vulva regularly
  • Continue even when asymptomatic
  • Protects against irritants
üíâ
Topical Steroids

Short courses only

  • Mild: Hydrocortisone 1% ointment 7-10 days
  • Moderate/Severe: Betamethasone valerate 7-10 days
  • Chronic: Gradual reduction over 3-4 months
  • Ointments preferred over creams
üò¥
Night Itch Control

For sleep disturbance

  • Hydroxyzine: Sedating antihistamine (avoid in elderly)
  • Amitriptyline: 10mg nocte (off-label)
  • Doxepin: 25mg nocte (off-label)

Safety Warnings
  • Hydroxyzine: Contraindicated in QT prolongation
  • Steroids: Avoid in undiagnosed suspicious lesions
  • Aqueous cream: Avoid (increased irritation risk)
  • Always use lowest effective potency
