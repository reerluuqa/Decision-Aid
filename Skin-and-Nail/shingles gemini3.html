Shingles (Herpes Zoster)

Interactive Algorithm | NICE CKS (Rev. May 2025)

Progress

  • Assessment
  • Diagnosis
  • Red Flags
  • Management
  • Pain Control
  • Advice & Prevention

1. Assessment

Step 1

Take a history and examine the patient to confirm features and exclude differentials.

History Checklist
  • Prodrome: Did symptoms (itch, burning, pain, numbness) occur 2-3 days before rash?
  • Pain: Character (burning, shooting, stabbing)? Severity?
  • Systemic: Fever, headache, malaise (occur in ~20%).
  • Risk Factors: Age >50, Immunocompromise (HIV, chemo, steroids), Stress.
Examination Focus
  • Location: Is it dermatomal? Unilateral?
  • Site: Thorax (most common), Head/Neck, Ophthalmic.
  • Morphology: Macules → Papules → Vesicles → Pustules → Crusts.

2. Diagnosis

Step 2

Diagnosis is usually clinical. Investigations are not typically required in primary care.

Typical Clinical Features

Unilateral erythematous maculopapular rash in a dermatomal distribution.

  • Pain often precedes rash.
  • Vesicles form within 1-2 days.
  • Pustulation within 1 week.
  • Crusting after 3-5 days.
  • Healing over 2-4 weeks.

Note: Rash may be atypical in older people (non-vesicular) or immunocompromised (severe/long-lasting).

Differential Diagnosis Caution
  • Herpes Simplex: Recurrent, lack of chronic pain.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Pain and rash simultaneous, exposure history.
  • Insect Bites: Local symptoms, no dermatomal pattern.
  • Impetigo: Honey-colored crusts.
  • Prodromal pain mimics: Cholecystitis, Appendicitis, Renal colic, Migraine.

3. Red Flags & Referral

Urgent

Assess immediately for criteria requiring admission or specialist advice.

Admit or Seek Immediate Specialist Advice if:
  • Ophthalmic Involvement: Hutchinson’s sign (rash on nose tip/side), eye pain, red eye, visual impairment.
  • Severe Immunosuppression: Or immunocompromised children.
  • Serious Complications: Suspected meningitis, encephalitis, myelitis.
  • Severe Presentation: Head/Neck involvement in elderly, widespread/disseminated rash, haemorrhagic lesions.
  • Pregnancy: Seek advice before prescribing antivirals.

If none of the above apply, proceed to Primary Care Management.

4. Antiviral Management

Treat

Goal: Start treatment within 72 hours of rash onset.

Who needs Antivirals?

Offer to:

  • People aged ≥ 50 years.
  • Immunocompromised (non-severe, localized rash).
  • Non-truncal involvement (limbs).
  • Moderate/Severe pain or rash.
  • Ophthalmic zoster (if not referred).

Consider for: Under 50s with trunk/extremity rash based on clinical judgment.

Late presentation (>72h): Consider if new vesicles still forming or high risk of complications.

Prescribing Choices
Drug Adult Dose (7 days)
Aciclovir 800 mg five times daily
Valaciclovir 1000 mg three times daily
Famciclovir 500 mg three times daily
(or 750mg 1-2 times daily)
*Adjust dose for renal impairment. See BNF for specific interactions.

5. Pain Management

Symptomatic

Select analgesia based on pain severity.

Offer:

  • Paracetamol
  • OR NSAID (e.g., Ibuprofen)
  • +/- Weak opioid (e.g., Codeine)

Offer (first-line neuropathic agents):

  • Amitriptyline (start low)
  • Duloxetine
  • Gabapentin
  • Pregabalin

Corticosteroids: Consider (Prednisolone 40-60mg) in first 2 weeks for severe pain in immunocompetent adults (with antiviral), but use caution.

Action:

  • Refer to specialist or pain clinic if oral analgesia is inadequate.
  • Refer if strong opioids (morphine) are being considered.

6. Advice & Prevention

Info
Infection Control
  • Contagious? Yes, fluid from vesicles contains virus. Can cause chickenpox in non-immune contacts.
  • Infectious Period: Until all vesicles have crusted (approx 7 days).
  • Avoid contact with: Pregnant women (if history unknown), immunocompromised, newborn babies.
  • Hygiene: Cover rash, wash hands, avoid sharing towels.
  • Work/School: Exclude only if rash is weeping and cannot be covered.
Follow-up & Referral

Seek advice if:

  • New vesicles form after 7 days of treatment.
  • Signs of bacterial superinfection (fever, spreading redness).
  • Recurrent shingles (2nd episode in immunocompetent or any recurrence in immunocompromised).
Vaccination (Shingrix)

Eligibility (UK National Programme):

  • Aged 70-79.
  • Aged ≥50 if severely immunocompromised.
  • Turning 65 (phased rollout from Sept 2023).

Give 2 doses, 8 weeks apart (up to 6-12 months for immunocompetent).
