Assessment

Key History Questions
Duration & Onset: When did the rash start? How long has it been present?
Itch Severity: "If it's not itchy, it's very unlikely to be eczema" - rate 0-10
Atopic History: Family history of eczema, asthma, hay fever, food allergies
Environmental: Pets, dust mites, smoking at home, recent infections
Sleep & Impact: "Impact on mum, impact on the kids if they're sleeping or not"
Red Flags: Fever, unwell child, painful lesions, rapid spread
Examination Tips
Look for Burrows

Scabies: Check wrists, ankles, waist, genital areas. Use dermoscopy if available - "you see the little windy track"

Scabies until proven otherwise
Satellite Lesions

Candida: "tiny little macules at the periphery" - especially in nappy area, skin folds

Diagnostic clue
Crust Characteristics

Impetigo: "honey-colored crusts, almost cornflake-like" vs weeping eczema

Infection indicator
Skin of Color

"Redness tends to be very difficult to appreciate" - look for scale, pigmentation changes

Papular eczema more common
Urgent Red Flags
IMMEDIATE REFERRAL NEEDED:
  • Eczema Herpeticum: "monomorphic punched lesions on face" + fever/unwell
  • Bullous Impetigo: "looks almost like bullous pemphigoid in kids"
  • Kerion: "boggy swellings with pus, crusting" - DO NOT SEND FOR I&D
  • Infantile Hemangioma: obstructing vision, airway, or ulcerating
  • Severe infection with systemic symptoms

Diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis by Presentation
Itchy Rash on Elbows/Knees?
Consider: Eczema - Especially with atopic family history
Key Features: Itchy, chronic, flexural distribution, improves with steroids
ECZEMA
Almost always itchy
50% genetic
Key Features:
  • Barrier defect with inflammatory component
  • Atopic history (asthma, hay fever, food allergy)
  • Can be dry, scaly, weepy, rough, or bumpy
  • Weeping ≠ infection (common misconception)
SCABIES
Worse at night
Treat whole family
Diagnostic Clues:
  • Burrows/tracks on wrists, ankles, waist
  • Genital nodules (pathognomonic)
  • Family members itching
  • Can affect face in infants
NAPPY RASH
Spares skin folds
Check satellite lesions
Differentiation:
  • Irritant: Spares skin folds, red where nappy contacts
  • Candida: Involves skin folds, satellite lesions at periphery
  • Psoriasis: Involves skin folds, well-demarcated, not necessarily scaly
MOLLUSCUM
Self-limiting
Umbilicated papules
Management:
  • Umbilicated, skin-colored papules
  • "Cut in their eyes" - appear on traumatized skin
  • Reassurance: angry appearance = resolution phase
  • Usually resolves within 12-18 months
IMPETIGO
Honey-colored crusts
Staph/Strep
Types:
  • Non-bullous: "Cornflake-like" yellow crusts
  • Bullous: "Looks like bullous pemphigoid" - urgent referral
  • Risk factors: crowded environment, poor hygiene
TINEA CAPITIS
Must treat aggressively
Oral terbinafine needed
vs Alopecia Areata:
  • Tinea: Scaling, annular patches, broken hairs
  • Alopecia: No scale, not itchy, exclamation mark hairs
  • Progresses to kerion if untreated → scarring alopecia
Investigations
Swab: Only if signs of infection (honey crusts, cellulitic appearance)
Skin Scraping: Scabies burrows, fungal infections
Wood's Lamp: Vitiligo vs Pityriasis Alba (vitiligo fluoresces)
Allergy Testing: "No role unless not growing or clear trigger" - refer to allergy clinic
Warning: "RAST tests open a can of worms" - most show positivity to common allergens that are already known triggers. Avoid routine testing.

Treatment

Emollients & Moisturizers
Key Principles:
  • Quantity: Children need ~1000g/month, adults 250-500g/week
  • Application: "Gently rub into skin, downward stroke along hair growth" to avoid folliculitis
  • Timing: Apply immediately after bathing on damp skin to trap moisture
  • Bathing: Daily baths up to 15 minutes are fine - "nothing too warm"
Creams
Doublebase, Dermol, Cetraben
Patient preference is key
Gels
Adex gel with Niacinamide
For redness and scaling
Ointments
Hydromol, 50/50, White soft paraffin
Very greasy but effective
Soap Substitutes
Same as emollient
Prescribe 3 tubs: 1 for shower, 1 for room, 1 for work
Important: "Avoid Epimax on face/eyelids" - periocular irritation risk. Fine for neck and below.
Topical Steroids - The Ladder
Education First: "Hit it hard and then drop it down" - treat early, use short courses
Severity Assessment: Mild, Moderate, Severe, Body Site
Mild
Hydrocortisone 1%
Face, flexures, children
Moderate
Eumovate (clobetasone)
Trunk, limbs
Potent
Betnovate (betamethasone)
Thick skin, palms/soles
Very Potent
Dermovate (clobetasol)
Short courses only, severe flare
Application Schedule:
  • Acute flare: Once daily for 7-14 days (twice daily if severe)
  • Tapering: Drop to alternate days for 1 week, then twice weekly for 2 weeks
  • Maintenance: "Once or twice a week" for persistent eczema
  • Face: Use mild steroids for 3-5 days, then switch to topical calcineurin inhibitors
Steroid Phobia: "Reassure patients, robust evidence in terms of long-term safety use"
Topical Steroid Withdrawal: "Burning and stinging rather than worsening eczema" - controversial diagnosis, distinct from poorly controlled eczema
Specific Condition Treatments
SCABIES
  • 1st line: Permethrin 5% - apply neck down, leave overnight, wash off next day
  • Repeat: In 7 days to kill newly hatched mites
  • Family: Treat all close contacts simultaneously
  • Environment: Hot wash bedding, clothing, towels; vacuum furniture
  • Itch relief: "Itch can stay for weeks or months" - cetirizine HC, short topical steroid course
Whole family treatment
NAPPY RASH - CANDIDA
  • Barrier: Zinc oxide or barrier cream (e.g., Metanium)
  • Antifungal: Clotrimazole or miconazole cream
  • Combined: Canesten HC (clotrimazole + hydrocortisone) for inflamed cases
  • Hygiene: Keep area dry, frequent nappy changes, fragrance-free wipes
  • Duration: Continue 1 week after clearance
MOLLUSCUM
  • 1st line: Reassurance - "self-limiting condition" 12-18 months
  • If treatment desired: Molludab/Mollutink (potassium hydroxide)
  • Application: Daily up to 14 days or until inflammatory reaction
  • Caution: Risk of post-inflammatory pigmentation especially in skin of color
  • Important: "Angry appearance = resolution phase" - reassure parents
Watchful waiting
IMPETIGO
  • Mild/localized: Topical fusidic acid 3 times daily for 5-7 days
  • Extensive/severe: Oral flucloxacillin for 7 days
  • MRSA risk: Consider topical mupirocin if not responding
  • Hygiene: Avoid sharing towels, wash hands frequently
  • School: Exclude until 48 hours after starting antibiotics
Bullous type = urgent referral
TINEA CAPITIS
  • 1st line: Oral terbinafine (off-license but more effective)
  • Alternative: Griseofulvin (licensed but more side effects)
  • Adjunct: Ketoconazole shampoo twice weekly for family
  • Duration: 4-6 weeks (3 months for kerion)
  • Refer: If uncomfortable prescribing off-license
Aggressive treatment needed
Off-license medication
ALOPECIA AREATA
  • Topical steroids: Dermovate cream once daily for 4 weeks, then twice weekly
  • Intralesional: For resistant patches (specialist procedure)
  • JAK inhibitors: Baricitinib (licensed for >12 years with >50% hair loss)
  • Reassurance: "High likelihood that hair will grow back" - non-scarring
  • Differentiate: Must rule out tinea capitis first
Rule out fungal infection
CRADLE CAP
  • Conservative: Baby shampoo, gentle brushing with soft brush
  • If scaly: Dermax shampoo (salicylic acid) - caution: strong smell
  • If inflamed: Ketoconazole shampoo twice weekly
  • Persistent: Betacap scalp application daily for 10-14 days
  • Alternative: Cocois ointment (apply to dry scalp, leave overnight)
Very common, self-limiting
Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors: Protopic (tacrolimus) - 0.03% for 2+ years, 0.1% for 16+ years. "Very safe to use" but off-license. Use for face/eyelids, maintenance therapy, steroid-sparing.
Advanced Therapies
Bleach Baths

For recurrent "infected eczema" or high Staph aureus colonization:

  • Milton bleach: 150ml in half bath or 300ml in full bath
  • Soak for 15 minutes, twice weekly
  • Avoid face contact
  • Reduces bacterial load, may reduce flares
  • "Anecdotally, some patients love it"
Wet Wraps/Garments

For severe eczema or constant scratching:

  • Brands: Clinifast, Skinnies
  • Occlusive effect: bumps steroid potency by one level
  • Apply moisturizer, wait 30 min, steroid on flare areas, then garment
  • Prevents scratching, breaks itch-scratch cycle
  • Cost: ~£12-13 each, need 6-7 pieces
Expensive
Antihistamines: "No evidence of long-term benefit" - short-term sedative antihistamines for sleep only. Risk of cognitive changes and growth delay with long-term use in children.
Alternative Therapies: Traditional Chinese Medicine may contain immunosuppressants similar to prednisolone or cyclosporine. "Herbal doesn't always mean good" - expensive and unregulated.

Follow-up

Review Timeframes
Acute Flare: Review in 1-2 weeks after starting treatment
Improving? Continue taper vs step up treatment
Maintenance: Review every 3-6 months for stable patients
Refer if: Not responding, frequent flares requiring potent steroids, diagnostic uncertainty
When to Refer
URGENT (Same Day)
  • Eczema herpeticum with systemic symptoms
  • Severe bullous impetigo
  • Kerion (do not send for I&D)
  • Infantile hemangioma obstructing vision/airway
  • Severe infection with fever/malaise
ROUTINE (Community Dermatology)
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Failed primary care treatment (3 months)
  • Recurrent "infected eczema" needing multiple antibiotics
  • Scabies not responding to permethrin
  • Alopecia areata (for intralesional steroids)
  • Off-license treatments (terbinafine <12y, Protopic <2y)
  • Severe acne in pre-teens
SPECIALIST (Secondary Care)
  • Possible skin cancer (2-week wait)
  • Severe bullous disorders
  • Genodermatoses
  • Complex vascular malformations
  • Phototherapy candidates (guttate psoriasis, severe eczema)
Community vs Secondary Care: "Everything can be referred to community dermatology unless 2-week wait needed." Community services can biopsy and manage most conditions. Secondary care for cancer suspicion and complex cases.
Patient Education & Resources
Eczema Society: Provide patient information leaflets
Websites: Eczema Society, BAD (British Association of Dermatologists)
Community Services: Community dermatology, eczema nurse specialists
School: Provide action plans for teachers, ensure emollients available
Education Priorities:
  • Steroid Safety: "It's not a worry if you use it early and treat it quickly"
  • Moisturizer Quantity: Most parents use too little - demonstrate with 500g/week example
  • Trigger Management: Overheating, stress, infection - practical strategies
  • Infection Recognition: When to seek help vs normal eczema weeping
  • Allergy Myths: "Most of the time... regardless of what you remove, eczema will still flare"
Antibiotic Stewardship: "Most of the time, if you treat the eczema flare-up with topical steroids, it will settle down." Only use antibiotics for clear signs of infection (honey crusts, cellulitis).
Special Considerations - Infants
Breastfeeding & Allergy
Key Points:
  • Exclusive breastfeeding rarely causes food allergy
  • If concern: Mum excludes dairy for 6 weeks minimum
  • Reintroduction: If flare occurs, likely related
  • Many foods are pseudo-allergens (tomato, histamine-rich foods)
  • "Education is key" - eczema is primarily genetic
Infant Skin Care
Recommendations:
  • Bathing: Daily baths fine, 15 minutes maximum
  • Temperature: "Nothing too warm" - heat causes dryness and itching
  • Products: Soap substitutes only, fragrance-free baby wipes
  • Drying: Pat dry, don't rub - apply moisturizer immediately
  • Cradle Cap: Baby shampoo, gentle brushing - usually asymptomatic
