Seborrhoeic Dermatitis Management Algorithm

Interactive clinical guidance for assessment, diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up of seborrhoeic dermatitis in adults and infants

Assessment

Conduct a thorough assessment to determine the severity and potential underlying causes:

Key Assessment Points:

  • History taking: Onset, evolution, distribution of symptoms (erythema, scaling, pruritus)
  • Aggravating factors: Environmental, emotional stress, infections
  • Associated features: Failure to thrive in infants, signs of immunosuppression
  • Medical history: HIV, Parkinson's disease, Down's syndrome
  • Medication review: Chlorpromazine, cimetidine, interferon-alpha, methyldopa

Clinical Examination:

  • Typical features in sebaceous gland-rich areas (scalp, face, chest, flexures)
  • Evaluation of lesion characteristics: erythema, greasy scales, symmetry
  • Assessment for signs of secondary infection (crusting, oozing, bleeding)
  • Special attention to skin of color: may present with hypopigmentation

When to Consider Investigations

Caution
  • If diagnosis is uncertain
  • If severe or widespread presentation (especially in young patients)
  • If features suggest alternative diagnosis (tinea, lupus, psoriasis)
  • If signs of underlying immunosuppression (consider HIV testing)

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical based on characteristic findings and distribution:

Clinical Features:

Adults & Adolescents

Stable
  • Well-defined erythematous plaques with greasy yellow/white scales
  • Symmetrical distribution in sebaceous areas: face (nasolabial folds, eyebrows), scalp, chest
  • Pruritus common, especially with scalp involvement
  • In skin of color: hypopigmented scaly patches, erythema may be subtle

Infants (Cradle Cap)

Stable
  • Thick white/yellow greasy scales on scalp (vertex and frontal areas)
  • Salmon-colored plaques on face (forehead, eyebrows, nasolabial folds)
  • Moist, shiny lesions in skin folds (neck, axillae, inguinal)
  • Usually resolves by 8-12 months of age

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Atopic dermatitis: Family history of atopy, extensor involvement in infants
  • Pityriasis versicolor: Hypo/hyperpigmented macules with fine scales on trunk
  • Psoriasis: Thicker plaques with silvery scales, nail changes
  • Tinea capitis: Scalp alopecia with scaling
  • Seborrhoeic-like rashes: Secondary syphilis, drug reactions, nutritional deficiencies

Red Flags for Underlying Conditions

Urgent
  • Severe/widespread presentation in young patients → consider HIV
  • Sudden onset in elderly → consider Parkinson's disease
  • Explosive generalized onset in infants → consider immunodeficiency

Treatment

Treatment is tailored to age, location, and severity of disease:

Infants (0-12 months)

Stable
  • Scalp (cradle cap): Massage with emollient, remove scales with soft brush, wash with gentle shampoo
  • Topical antifungals: Clotrimazole 1% or miconazole 2% cream (2-3 times daily for up to 4 weeks)
  • Topical steroids: Mild hydrocortisone 1% (once/twice daily for up to 2 weeks if significant inflammation)
  • Nappy area: Frequent changes, barrier emollients (zinc & castor oil)

Scalp & Beard (12+ months)

Stable
  • Antifungal shampoo: Ketoconazole 2% (twice weekly for 4 weeks, then maintenance)
  • Keratolytic agents: Salicylic acid before shampooing for scale removal
  • Alternative shampoos: Zinc pyrithione, coal tar, salicylic acid
  • Topical steroids: Betamethasone valerate or mometasone furoate for severe inflammation (short course only)
  • For beard area: Use mild hydrocortisone 1% (not strong steroids due to facial skin thinning)

Face & Body (12+ months)

Stable
  • Topical antifungals: Ketoconazole 2% cream or clotrimazole/miconazole (once/twice daily for 4 weeks)
  • Maintenance: Reduce frequency once controlled (e.g., twice weekly)
  • Topical steroids: Hydrocortisone 1% for flares (max 2 weeks)
  • Eyelid involvement: Daily hygiene measures (see Blepharitis guidance)
  • Children: Ketoconazole cream not licensed - use clotrimazole/miconazole instead

General Advice

Stable
  • Use gentle, soap-free washes and light moisturizers
  • Non-comedogenic makeup if applicable
  • Fruit consumption may help reduce flares
  • Stress management as stress exacerbates flares
  • Chronic condition requiring long-term maintenance in adults

Follow-up & Referral

Monitor response to treatment and identify when specialist referral is needed:

Routine Follow-up:

  • Not usually required for mild cases
  • Advise patients/parents to seek review if:
    • Poor response to treatment
    • Worsening symptoms despite treatment
    • Signs of infection (crusting, oozing, bleeding)

Referral Indications:

Standard Referral

Caution
  • Failure to respond to routine treatment after 4-6 weeks
  • Recurrent symptoms requiring frequent treatment courses
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Eyelid involvement not responding to hygiene measures

Urgent Referral

Urgent
  • Severe or widespread seborrhoeic dermatitis
  • Suspected underlying immunosuppression (e.g., HIV)
  • Suspected alternative serious diagnosis (e.g., histiocytosis in infants)
  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection requiring systemic antibiotics

Prognosis:

  • Infants: Usually self-limiting, resolves by 8-12 months
  • Adults: Chronic condition with remissions and relapses, responds well to treatment
