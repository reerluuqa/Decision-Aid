Step 1

Suspect & Confirm Diagnosis

Discolouration Thickening / hyperkeratosis Onycholysis “Spikes” / distal → proximal

Confirm with lab

  • Microscopy & culture from nail clippings/scrapings (beware ~30% culture false‑negatives).
  • Dermatophyte: either test positive.
  • Candida: both tests positive.
  • Non‑dermatophyte: both tests positive on ≥2 separate samples.

Consider differentials

  • Psoriasis, eczema, lichen planus, trauma/onychogryphosis.
  • Bacterial pigment (e.g., Pseudomonas), periungual warts.
  • Yellow nail syndrome.
  • Rule out melanoma/SCC if atypical pigment or bleeding.
Step 2

Assess Impact & Context

  • Symptoms: pain, difficulty walking/footwear.
  • Psychosocial distress/cosmetic concern.
  • Comorbidity: diabetes, PAD, immunosuppression.
  • Co‑existing tinea pedis / other fungal skin sites.
  • Household contacts affected; exposure risks.
Step 3

Self‑Care (for everyone)

  • Keep nails short & filed; don’t share clippers.
  • Well‑fitting shoes; cotton socks; replace old footwear.
  • Treat co‑existing tinea pedis promptly.
  • Protect feet in communal wet areas.
  • Avoid trauma and prolonged damp exposure.

If asymptomatic / not bothered: no antifungal needed. Set expectations: onychomycosis often recurs.

Step 4A

Topical Therapy

When to use

  • Superficial white disease or very early distal disease.
  • Few nails involved / oral agents contraindicated.
Low cure rates

Amorolfine 5% lacquer

  • Apply once–twice weekly after filing.
  • Duration: 6 mo fingers / 9–12 mo toes.
  • Avoid cosmetic varnish & artificial nails during therapy.
Step 4B

Oral Therapy (culture‑confirmed when possible)

Dermatophyte (first‑line)

  • Terbinafine 250 mg OD
    • Fingers: 6 weeks
    • Toes: 12–24 weeks
  • Alt: Itraconazole pulse 200 mg BD × 1 wk, repeat q3w × 2 pulses (fingers) / 3 pulses (toes).

Candida / Non‑dermatophyte

  • Itraconazole (pulsed as above) Non‑dermatophyte use off‑label
  • Alternative: Terbinafine (off‑label for non‑dermatophyte)

Consider comorbidities, polypharmacy, renal/hepatic status in older adults.

Step 5

Monitoring & Follow‑up

  • Baseline risks & interactions; counsel on hepatic symptoms.
  • Notch nail at baseline or use serial photos to track regrowth.
  • Reassess at 3–6 months.
  • If abnormal nail remains distal to notch → treatment success.
  • If abnormal nail moves proximal to notch → ongoing infection.
Step 6

Treatment Failure & Referral

When treatment seems to fail

  • Treat co‑existing tinea pedis.
  • Repeat microscopy/culture (consider sampling technique).
  • Consider oral + topical combination.

Consider referral

  • Uncertain diagnosis or failed primary‑care therapy.
  • Severe deformity/trauma; podiatry if painful thickened toenails.
  • Child requiring systemic therapy (paediatric derm).
  • Immunocompromised (clinical judgement).
Key Notes

Practice Pearls

  • Toenails affected ≈ 7× more often than fingernails.
  • No spontaneous clearance without antifungals.
  • Complete cure only in ~30–60% even with systemic therapy; recurrence 20–50%.
  • Nail appearance may not normalize despite mycologic cure.
  • Use photos/notch to communicate progress and set expectations.
