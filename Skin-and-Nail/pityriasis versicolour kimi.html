Pityriasis Versicolor Clinical Decision Support

Evidence-based guidance from NICE CKS (Last revised: March 2025) | For patients aged 10+ years

What to Ask (History)

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Age of onset (peak in early twenties)
  • Duration of lesions and progression
  • Symptoms: usually asymptomatic, mild itch possible
  • Previous episodes or treatments
  • Recent travel to warm/humid climates
  • Risk factors: hyperhidrosis, occlusion, immunosuppression
  • Medications: oral contraceptives, corticosteroids
  • Pregnancy status
  • Reassure: NOT contagious, NOT due to poor hygiene

What to Examine

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Distribution: sebum-rich areas (trunk, upper arms, neck, abdomen)
  • Lesion type: round/oval macules and confluent patches
  • Surface: fine powdery scale (may be subtle)
  • Color variations: pink, fawn, red, brown, black, or white
  • Skin type considerations:
    • Light skin: hyperpigmented or hypopigmented (more noticeable when tanned)
    • Dark skin: may present solely with hypopigmented lesions or grey-black macules
  • Wood's lamp: may fluoresce gold-yellow, yellowish-green, or coppery-orange

Key Point

Diagnosis is usually clinical. Laboratory testing is not routinely required.

Differential Diagnosis

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Vitiligo (esp. hypopigmented lesions)
  • Psoriasis (guttate type)
  • Tinea corporis (ring-shaped lesions)
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis (may coexist)
  • Pityriasis rosea (herald patch)
  • Pityriasis alba (children/face)
  • Erythrasma (coral-red fluorescence)
  • Secondary syphilis (palms/soles)
  • Melasma (facial pigmentation)

What to Prescribe (Initial)

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
EXTENSIVE DISEASE:
  • Ketoconazole 2% shampoo: apply once daily for up to 5 days
  • Use as body wash, leave on skin for up to 15 minutes, then rinse
  • Age: 12+ years and adults (not licensed <12 years)
SMALL/LOCALIZED AREAS:
  • Clotrimazole 1% cream (preferred in pregnancy) - twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • OR Econazole 1% cream - twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • OR Ketoconazole 2% cream - twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • OR Terbinafine 1% cream - twice daily for 2-3 weeks

When to Review

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Review in 2-4 weeks to assess response
  • Explain pigmentary changes may take weeks to months to resolve
  • Mycological cure achieved soon after treatment
  • Persistence of pigment changes alone does not indicate treatment failure

Management Approach

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ

Patient Education

  • Not contagious - Malassezia is normal skin flora
  • Not related to poor hygiene
  • High efficacy but recurrence common (especially summer)
  • Discoloration may persist for weeks/months after cure
  • Provide BAD patient leaflet: Pityriasis versicolor

First-Line Treatment

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
TOPICAL ANTIFUNGALS:
  • Clotrimazole 1% cream: apply twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • Econazole 1% cream: apply twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • Ketoconazole 2% cream: apply twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • Terbinafine 1% cream: apply twice daily for 2-3 weeks

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding Considerations

  • Clotrimazole: Preferred in pregnancy
  • Econazole: Manufacturers advise avoidance in pregnancy
  • Terbinafine: Avoid unless clearly necessary
  • Miconazole: Caution in breastfeeding (avoid nipple area)

Follow-up Plan

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Assess clinical response at 2-3 weeks
  • If improved, continue to full course (2-3 weeks total)
  • If no response, consider alternative diagnosis or treatment failure
  • Reassure about pigmentation changes

Assess Treatment Failure

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Confirm adequate adherence to treatment regimen
  • Verify correct application technique and duration
  • Distinguish persistent pigment from active infection
  • Active infection signs: lesions, erythema, pruritus, positive mycology
  • Pigment alone ‚â† treatment failure

Next Steps

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Take skin scrapings for microscopy (see technique below)
  • Consider alternative topical antifungal
  • Consider oral antifungal therapy
  • Refer to dermatology if uncertain

Skin Scraping Technique

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Wipe off any creams before sampling
  • Scrape from advancing edge of patch
  • Collect ~5 mm¬≤ of scales
  • Use folded dark paper squares or commercial fungal pack
  • Alternative: adhesive tape strip to microscope slide
  • Send to lab with clinical details & treatment history

Oral Antifungal Options (Adults, non-pregnant)

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
ITRACONAZOLE: 200 mg once daily for 7 days
FLUCONAZOLE: 50 mg once daily for 2-4 weeks OR 300-400 mg once weekly for 1-3 weeks

Important Contraindications

  • Fluconazole: Acute porphyria, QT-prolonging drugs
  • Fluconazole: Caution in hepatic/renal impairment
  • Itraconazole: Ventricular dysfunction, heart failure history
  • Itraconazole: Avoid in pregnancy

Preventing Recurrence

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ

Recurrence Facts

  • Common: up to 80% within 2 years
  • More frequent in warm, humid climates
  • Most cases: re-treat each episode as initial presentation
PROPHYLAXIS REGIMEN:
  • Ketoconazole 2% shampoo: apply once every 1-4 weeks to whole body
  • Continue during periods of high risk (summer, travel)

Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • For anticipated sun exposure/travel to warm climate
  • Age 12+ years and adults
  • Ketoconazole 2% shampoo: once daily for max 3 days prior to exposure
  • Explain re-pigmentation takes months after treatment

Acute Flare Management

‚ãÆ‚ãÆ
  • Treat active episode as per initial management
  • Review adherence to prophylaxis if already prescribed
  • Consider maintenance therapy if >2 episodes/year
  • Refer if frequent recurrences requiring long-term oral prophylaxis
