Chilblains (Perniosis) — Primary Care Quick Algorithm

Snapshot

Typical: recent cold exposure (12–24h), symmetrical red‑purple acral lesions with itch/burn; self‑limiting in 2–3 weeks if warm.
Primary & Secondary types Acute & Chronic patterns

Algorithm

1

Suspect chilblains

  • Cold exposure within 12–24 hours
  • Symmetrical red‑purple macules/papules/nodules on acral sites (fingers, toes, ears, nose; may blister/ulcerate)
  • Symptoms: itch, burning, tenderness; lesions often resolve in 2–3 weeks if warm
2

Assess

  • History: recurrence, persistence beyond cold season, systemic features (rash, photosensitivity, arthralgia, ulcers, weight loss), recent viral/contact (children)
  • Examine: acral skin, peripheral pulses, screen neuropathy; look for CTD/haematological signs
  • Investigations only if atypical/persistent/severe or secondary cause suspected; discuss/consider dermatology if diagnostic doubt
3

Differentiate

  • Chilblain lupus erythematosus (violaceous plaques over interphalangeal joints; ~20% risk of SLE)
  • Raynaud’s, acrocyanosis, cold urticaria, frostbite, cold panniculitis, livedo
  • Vasculitis / PVD / diabetic foot, granuloma annulare, juvenile spring eruption, “COVID toes”
4

Manage

If idiopathic (typical):
  • Reassure: self‑limiting in 2–3 weeks
  • Self‑care: gradual warming (avoid direct heat), stop smoking, avoid caffeine/decongestants, warm clothing, gloves & socks
  • Do not routinely prescribe drugs
If severe/chronic/persistent despite self‑care:
  • Re‑assess for secondary causes; discuss if diagnosis uncertain
  • Consider modified‑release nifedipine 20–60 mg once daily (off‑label) — titrate slowly; monitor BP; stop if not tolerated/ineffective; continue until healed or for season if prophylaxis needed
  • Seek specialist advice if unsuitable/not tolerated
Do not use nifedipine in children <18y; avoid in pregnancy/breastfeeding unless specialist advises. Use caution with cardiac disease, hypotension, diabetes, hepatic impairment; check interactions (CYP3A4).
5

Refer / Investigate

  • Suspected secondary cause (CTD, haematological malignancy, cryoglobulinaemia)
  • Atypical, ulceration/necrosis, persistent beyond cold season, or diagnostic uncertainty
  • Children or pregnant/breastfeeding patients needing treatment

Complications

  • Excoriation; secondary infection (esp. blistered/scratched lesions)
  • Ulceration, tissue necrosis
  • Scarring; chronic changes (fibrosis, lymphoedema, hyperkeratosis)

Prognosis

  • Idiopathic: self‑limiting; often recurs each winter
  • Secondary: outcomes depend on underlying disorder management

Prevention — Patient advice

  • Keep warm & dry; layer clothing; gloves & thermal socks
  • Warm gradually after exposure (avoid direct heat)
  • Stop smoking; limit caffeine and decongestants
