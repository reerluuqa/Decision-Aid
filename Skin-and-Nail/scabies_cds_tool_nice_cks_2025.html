At a glance

Designed for rapid recognition of escalation triggers and first-line actions
Red flags / urgent escalation

Think crusted scabies

  • Minimal/absent itch with extensive scaly/crusted plaques.
  • People who are immunocompromised or have reduced ability to scratch.
  • Malodorous lesions or widespread involvement including back.
  • High contagion risk for household/care setting.
Hospital admission, isolation and combination therapy may be required; consider underlying immunodeficiency.
Recommended first-line pathway

Classical scabies

  • Two licensed options with similar effectiveness: permethrin 5% cream or oral ivermectin.
  • Ivermectin is typically given as 200 micrograms/kg with a second dose at 7 days to improve effectiveness.
  • Consider ivermectin if pre-existing eczema or skin conditions make topical treatment difficult.
  • Treat all close contacts simultaneously even if asymptomatic.
Itching can persist up to 4 weeks after successful eradication.

Care home/outbreak

This tool does not cover outbreak management in residential/nursing homes — seek local communicable disease control advice.

Infants

Seek specialist advice for treatment in children under 2 months; permethrin is licensed from 2 months.

Pregnancy & breastfeeding

Manufacturer advises ivermectin should be avoided in pregnancy; breastfeeding only if benefits outweigh risk.

Decision engine

A lightweight triage panel to surface the right section fast

1) Suspect scabies?

Key history + exam anchors
  • Intense, generalized itch worse at night.
  • Household/close contacts also itchy.
  • Characteristic papules on waist, periumbilical area, genitalia, breasts, buttocks, axillae, fingers/interdigital spaces, wrists, extensor limbs.
  • Burrows may be subtle; consider ink test/scrapings if needed.

2) Screen for crusted features Do not miss

Hyperinfestation with high transmission
  • Minimal/absent itch.
  • Diffuse plaques with thick scales/crusts.
  • Immunosuppression, neurological disease, learning disability, frailty.
  • Possible secondary infection/odour.

Diagnosis & management

Progressive disclosure for rapid scanning
Clinical diagnosis in 30 seconds History + exam

Most diagnoses are made from history and exam, with consideration of family/close contacts. Microscopy can confirm if appropriate equipment and expertise are available.

History cues

  • Intense generalized itch, often worse at night.
  • Symptoms in reinfestation can start within 1–3 days; primary infestation typically 3–6 weeks.
  • Absence of itch does not exclude scabies (e.g., young babies, neurological conditions).
  • Ask about household/close contact itch, overcrowding, and recent/new sexual partners.

Exam distribution

  • Papules around waist/periumbilical area, genitalia, breasts, buttocks, axillae, fingers/interdigital spaces, wrists, extensor limbs.
  • Head usually spared except in children.
  • Palms/soles can be affected in infants/elderly.
  • Burrows: thin brown-grey line (0.2–1 cm); may be obscured by scratching, infection, eczema.
Suspected crusted scabies Urgent

Crusted scabies is a hyperinfestation with thousands or millions of mites and is highly contagious.

  • Pruritus may be mild or absent due to impaired immune response.
  • Generalized erythematous fissured plaques covered by scales/crusts.
  • Thick yellow-brown verrucous plaques on bony prominences (e.g., finger articulations, elbows, iliac crest).
  • Diffuse non-crusted involvement of the back may occur.
  • Secondary infection can cause malodorous lesions.

Who is at higher risk?

  • People with immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, lymphoma, long-term corticosteroids).
  • Reduced ability to scratch (physical incapacity, skin anaesthesia).
  • Learning difficulties or neurological disorders.
  • Elderly people.

Immediate actions

  • Seek specialist advice; consider hospital admission.
  • Institute isolation and barrier nursing where appropriate.
  • Likely need combination topical + oral ivermectin.
  • Consider work-up for underlying immunodeficiency.
First-line treatment choice Permethrin or ivermectin

For adults and children aged 2 months and over with uncomplicated classical scabies, two licensed regimens with similar effectiveness are available.

When to lean towards ivermectin

  • Pre-existing eczema or other skin conditions that may make topical therapy difficult or painful.
  • Concern about application adherence across the whole body.

When to be cautious with ivermectin

  • Do not prescribe if < 15 kg.
  • Avoid in pregnancy where possible.
  • Breastfeeding only if benefits outweigh potential risk.
  • Contraindicated in severe hepatic impairment.
Permethrin 5% cream — application essentials Topical

The manufacturer advises application once weekly for two doses. Apply to cool, dry skin and wash off after 8–12 hours.

Body coverage by age

  • Adults > 2 years: apply to whole body, with attention to interdigital spaces, under nails, wrists, armpits, external genitalia, breasts, buttocks.
  • Children 2 months–2 years: include scalp, neck, face, ears; avoid around eyes/mouth. Consider mittens.
  • Elderly: include scalp, neck, face, ears; avoid close to eyes.

Practical safety reminders Important

  • Do not apply to broken or secondarily infected skin.
  • Do not apply near eyes or mucous membranes.
  • If hands washed within 8 hours, reapply to hands.
  • Fabric contact risk: clothing/bedding with cream can be ignited by naked flame.
  • Carers should wear gloves.
Oral ivermectin — dosing & cautions Systemic

Recommended dosage is 200 micrograms/kg. Give one oral dose with water on an empty stomach, followed by a second dose after 7 days.

Administration pearls

  • No food within 2 hours before or after (influence of food on absorption is unknown).
  • Children < 6 years who weigh ≥ 15 kg: tablets should be crushed before swallowing.
  • Consider rounding up to closest 3 mg tablet (BASHH advice).

Contraindications & interactions Check

  • Do not prescribe if weight < 15 kg.
  • Contraindicated in severe hepatic impairment.
  • Avoid in pregnancy where possible; caution in breastfeeding.
  • Potentially increases anticoagulant effect of warfarin or acenocoumarol; levamisole increases exposure.
Treat contacts & decontaminate High impact

Who to treat (even if well)

  • All household members.
  • Sexual partners within the past month.
  • Any other close personal contacts.
  • Referral to GUM may be needed for partner notification.

Decontamination steps

  • Wash bedding, clothing, towels at ≥ 60°C and dry hot.
  • Or dry-clean.
  • Or seal in a plastic bag for at least 72 hours.

Prescribing grids

Card-based summaries (not tables) for fast confirmation

Permethrin 5% cream First-line option

  • Contraindications: hypersensitivity to permethrin/pyrethroids/pyrethrins; broken or secondarily infected skin.
  • Cautions: children under 2 years only under medical supervision; not licensed under 2 months.
  • Adverse effects: transient burning/stinging/tingling, erythema, oedema, eczema, rash, pruritus.
  • Interactions: withhold corticosteroids prior to permethrin where possible to avoid exacerbation by reduced immune response.

Oral ivermectin First-line option

  • Dose: 200 micrograms/kg; repeat after 7 days to improve effectiveness.
  • Administration: with water on empty stomach; no food 2 hours before/after.
  • Do not prescribe: weight < 15 kg; severe hepatic impairment.
  • Pregnancy: manufacturer advises avoid; breastfeeding only if benefit outweighs risk.
  • Interactions: increased anticoagulant effect with warfarin/acenocoumarol; levamisole increases exposure.

Approx. permethrin amounts

>12y: up to 30 g (max 60 g). 6–12y: 15 g. 1–5y: 7.5 g. 2 mo–1y: 3.75 g.

Eczema co-management

Treat complications such as eczema and secondary infection alongside scabies therapy.

Symptom expectations

Itch may last up to 4 weeks after successful treatment; do not assume failure too early.

Follow-up, itch & treatment failure

Avoid premature retreatment, spot true resistance
Expected course

Persistent itch ≠ failure

  • Itching may continue for up to 4 weeks after successful eradication.
  • Treat post-scabetic itch with crotamiton 10% cream or, if mites definitely eradicated, hydrocortisone 1%.
  • Night-time sedating antihistamine may help sleep and reduce scratching.
When to re-treat / refer

Action thresholds

  • If symptoms persist beyond 2–4 weeks after last treatment and/or new burrows appear — advise retreatment.
  • After ivermectin, allow a full 4 weeks before considering recovery; do not give additional doses earlier purely for itch/scratching lesions.
  • If resistant scabies suspected after re-treatment: consider combination oral ivermectin + topical therapy and discuss/refer to dermatology.
  • Persistent nodular scabies — refer to dermatology.
