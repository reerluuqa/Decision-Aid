Molluscum Contagiosum

Last Updated: Sep 2022

Critical Referral Required

Urgent Ophthalmology referral for eyelid lesions & Genitourinary referral for anogenital lesions.

Immediate Action Checklist

  • Anogenital lesions: Full sexual health screen (HIV, STIs) in adults. Assess for child abuse in children.
  • Eyelid/ocular lesions: Urgent Ophthalmology referral if red eye or lid-margin involved.
  • Immunocompromised: >50 lesions or facial lesions in adults may indicate HIV.

Definition

Common poxvirus infection of keratinocytes. Generally self-limiting.

Viral
Cutaneous/Mucosal

Key Appearance

  • Pearly, smooth papules.
  • Central dell (umbilicated).
  • Flesh-colored to white.
  • Size: 2-5mm typically.

Transmission

  • Skin-to-skin contact.
  • Fomites (towels, sponges).
  • Sexual contact (adults).
  • Swimming pools.

Epidemiology

  • Children (1-9 yrs): Highest incidence (13-14/1000 person-years).
  • Atopic Dermatitis: Prevalence increases to 20-45%.
  • Tropical Climates: Higher incidence.

Prognosis

  • Self-limiting: Usually resolves in 6-9 months.
  • Persistent: Can last 1-2 years in immunocompetent.
  • Atopic patients: Resolution often takes longer.

Key Diagnostic Factors

Pearly Papule Central Dell Pruritus Erythema

History Clues: Close contact, atopic dermatitis, swimming, recent sexual activity.

Investigations

Clinical diagnosis is standard.

  • Dermoscopy: Crown vessels, central yellow polylobular structure.
  • Biopsy (Rare): Henderson-Patterson bodies (inclusions).
  • HIV Screen: If extensive or facial/anogenital lesions.

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiators
Verruca (Warts) Verrucous (rough) surface. Dermatoglyphics (skin lines) preserved. Paring causes pinpoint bleeding.
Herpes Simplex Usually painful vesicles that ulcerate. Viral swab positive for HSV.
Milia Smaller white cysts, lack central dell. Usually on cheeks/eyes.
Deep Fungal Infection Systemic symptoms (fever), very ill patient. Smear/Culture positive.
Lichen Nitidus Small, shiny, flat-topped papules on flexor surfaces/genitals.

Treatment Decision Helper

Patient Immunocompetent?
Expectant Management (1st Line) Reassurance. Spontaneous resolution within 1-2 years. No treatment needed for most.
Patient Preference / Discomfort?
Active Treatment Cryotherapy or Topical Agents (e.g., Potassium Hydroxide, Salicylic Acid). Note: Imiquimod NOT for children.
Atopic Dermatitis?
Optimize Skin Barrier Step up eczema treatment first (emollients, topical steroids). Molluscum often resolves once AD controlled.

Treatment Options

Method Notes
Cryotherapy In-office. Weekly 6-8 weeks. Painful but effective.
Potassium Hydroxide Topical. Modest benefit. Irritation common.
Curettage Physical removal. Scarring risk. Less traumatic in anogenital area than cautery? No, cautery often preferred for anogenital.
Imiquimod NOT for kids. Limited evidence. Immune modifier.
Podophyllotoxin Often used for anogenital lesions. Specialist guidance needed.

Special Populations

HIV / Immunocompromised

  • Primary: Treat underlying cause (Optimize ART).
  • Secondary: If lesions persist, consider Cidofovir or physical removal.
  • Warning: Watch for Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS).

Anogenital Molluscum

  • Screening: Full STI/HIV panel mandatory.
  • Treatment: Cryotherapy, Cautery, or Podophyllotoxin.
  • Advice: Avoid shaving/waxing area to prevent spread.

Patient Counseling Checklist

Tap items to check them off (local storage).

Condition is self-limiting and benign; no treatment is often needed.
Avoid sexual contact until lesions have fully cleared.
Do not share towels, clothing, or bedding.
Avoid scratching or picking lesions to prevent bacterial spread.
Avoid waxing or shaving the affected area.
Cover lesions with a bandage if participating in contact sports.
