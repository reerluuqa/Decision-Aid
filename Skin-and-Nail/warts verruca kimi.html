Overview & Key Messages

Key Principle

No treatment is often the best first option. Most warts resolve spontaneously, especially in children. Only treat if painful, unsightly, or persistent.

Primary Care Options

  • • Topical salicylic acid (12-26%)
  • • Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen)
  • • Combination therapy
  • • Observation & reassurance

Refer If

  • • Facial warts
  • • Uncertain diagnosis
  • • Immunocompromised
  • • Extensive mosaic warts
  • • Failed primary care treatment
Background Information

Definition

Small, rough growths caused by HPV infection of keratinocytes. Common types include:

  • Common wart (Verruca vulgaris)
  • Plantar wart (Verruca plantaris)
  • Plane wart (Verruca plana)
  • Filiform wart (finger-like)
  • Periungual wart (around nails)

Prevalence

5-30% of school-age children and young adults have warts at any time.

Lifetime prevalence in adults: 41.3%

Peak incidence in adolescence, declines in later adulthood.

Transmission

Spread by direct skin-to-skin contact or via contaminated surfaces.

  • Increased risk if skin barrier damaged
  • Wet/macerated skin
  • Immunosuppression
  • HPV can survive months to years on surfaces

Prognosis

Spontaneous resolution common, especially in children:

  • 25% resolve within 2-3 months
  • 50% within 1 year
  • 90% within 5 years

Adults may have slower resolution (5-10 years)

Complications: Malignant transformation is rare but reported in immunosuppressed individuals. Refer if atypical or non-healing lesions.
Diagnosis
1
Clinical Diagnosis: Typical appearance is usually sufficient. Look for:
  • Firm, rough papules (cauliflower-like)
  • Central dark dots (thrombosed capillaries)
  • Location: hands, feet, face, around nails
2
Confirmatory Examination: If diagnostic doubt, lightly pare the wart to reveal tiny black dots. Caution: bleeding may cause spread
3
Dermatoscope: May be helpful if experienced with the tool.

Differential Diagnosis

Exclude These
  • Molluscum contagiosum (umbilicated papules)
  • Squamous cell carcinoma (non-healing, enlarging)
  • Malignant melanoma (irregular outline/color)
  • Actinic keratosis (sun-exposed areas)
Common Mimics
  • Corns/calluses (no black dots, intact skin markings)
  • Seborrheic keratosis ("stuck-on" appearance)
  • Knuckle pads (over joints)
Refer if diagnosis uncertain or lesions are atypical.
Management Decision Pathway

First Decision: To Treat or Not?

Consider NO TREATMENT if asymptomatic, especially in children. Provide reassurance and advice.

Treat if: Painful, cosmetically unsightly, persistent, or patient requests treatment.

Primary Care Treatment Options

Option 1: Topical Salicylic Acid

First-line for most patients

  • Apply daily for up to 12 weeks
  • Available OTC (12-26% preparations)
  • Soak wart 2-5 minutes before application
  • Protect surrounding skin with paraffin/plaster

Avoid face, intertriginous areas, moles, broken skin

Option 2: Cryotherapy

Requires trained practitioner

  • Liquid nitrogen application
  • Every 2-4 weeks, max 6 treatments
  • 5-30 second freeze (shorter for children/plane warts)
  • Two freeze-thaw cycles per session

Painful, risk of scarring/pigment changes

Special Considerations
Facial Warts: DO NOT treat in primary care → Refer to dermatology
Plantar Warts: Cryotherapy less effective. Regular paring can reduce discomfort.
Young Children (<12): Salicylic acid only. Avoid cryotherapy due to pain.
Plane Warts: Use shorter freeze (5-10s) or weaker salicylic acid (≤17%) to reduce scarring risk.
Treatment Details & Contraindications

Topical Salicylic Acid

Available Preparations
  • Duofilm® (16.7% + lactic acid 15%)
  • Bazuka® (12% or 26%)
  • Occlusal® (26%)
  • Salactol® (16.7% + lactic acid 16.7%)
  • Salatac® gel (12% + lactic acid 4%)
Contraindications
  • Face, intertriginous, or anogenital regions
  • Moles, birthmarks, hair-bearing warts
  • Open wounds, infected or reddened skin
  • Poor circulation (e.g., diabetes, neuropathic feet)
  • Children <2 years
  • Pregnancy/lactation (manufacturer advice varies)

Cryotherapy Contraindications

  • Uncertain diagnosis or suspected malignancy
  • Young children (too painful)
  • Previous adverse reaction to cryotherapy
  • Darker skin (risk of pigment changes)
  • Hair-bearing skin, eyelids, nasal folds
  • Distal extremities with Raynaud's/PVD/neuropathy
  • Periungual sites (risk of nail deformity)
  • Autoimmune or haematological disease

Caution: Use shorter freeze-thaw cycles near tendons or superficial nerves.

Referral to Secondary Care
Refer Urgently to Dermatology if:
1
Facial warts - should not be treated in primary care
2
Diagnostic uncertainty or atypical appearance
3
Immunocompromised patients
4
Extensive disease (e.g., mosaic warts)
5
Failed primary care treatment - both salicylic acid and cryotherapy tried without success

Secondary Care Options

Physical Ablation
  • Surgery, laser therapy
  • Microwave therapy
  • Photodynamic therapy
Medical Treatments
  • Topical podophyllin, retinoids
  • Intralesional bleomycin
  • Topical immunomodulators (imiquimod, diphenylcyclopropenone)
  • Intralesional immunotherapy (candida, MMR vaccine)
Patient Advice & Prevention

Key Messages for Patients

  • Warts are not harmful and usually resolve without treatment
  • They are contagious but transmission risk is low
  • Children should not be excluded from school/sports/swimming

Reducing Transmission Risk

1
Swimming: Cover warts with waterproof plaster. Swimming socks discouraged
2
Communal areas: Wear flip-flops in communal showers
3
Personal items: Do not share shoes, socks, or towels

Preventing Personal Spread (Auto-inoculation)

  • Avoid picking, scratching, or biting warts
  • Keep feet dry; change socks daily
  • Do not use emery boards/pumice stones on normal skin
  • Dispose of skin filings hygienically
