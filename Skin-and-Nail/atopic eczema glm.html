Definition & Key Features

Clinical Definition

Chronic inflammatory skin condition characterized by dry, pruritic skin with episodic flares and remissions. In severe cases, disease activity may be continuous.

Natural History

Most frequently presents in early childhood (before 5 years). About 65% clear by age 7 and 74% by 16 years, though relapses may occur.

Atopic Association

Often associated with personal or family history of atopic conditions: asthma, allergic rhinitis (hay fever), and food allergy.

Diagnostic Criteria (NICE)

Essential Criterion:

Itchy skin (or parental report of scratching) - diagnosis is unlikely without itch

Plus 3 or more of the following:

  • Visible flexural eczema (or cheeks/extensor areas in children ≤18 months)
  • Personal history of flexural eczema (or cheeks/extensor areas in children ≤18 months)
  • Personal history of dry skin in last 12 months
  • Personal history of asthma/allergic rhinitis (or in first-degree relative for children <4 years)
  • Onset before age 2 years (not used for children <4 years)

Important Note:

In children of Asian, black Caribbean, and black African ethnic groups, eczema can affect extensor surfaces rather than flexures, and discoid or follicular patterns may be more common.

Assessment

Severity

Classify as mild, moderate, or severe based on physical symptoms and extent of skin involvement.

Impact

Assess both physical symptoms and psychological/social impact on the patient and family.

Pattern

Document age of onset, distribution, triggers, and response to previous treatments.

Management Approach

Emollients

First-line for all patients. Prescribe sufficient quantities (250-500g weekly). Use regularly, not just during flares.

Topical Corticosteroids

Stepwise approach based on severity. Apply emollients first, wait 30 minutes, then apply corticosteroids.

Trigger Management

Identify and avoid triggers: soap/detergents, animal dander, house-dust mites, temperature changes, rough clothing, pollen, certain foods, stress.

Patient Education

Provide information on condition, treatment options, and correct use of therapies. Involve parents/caregivers in management plan.

Referral Criteria

Refer for Specialist Dermatological Advice:

  • Uncontrolled or unresponsive atopic eczema
  • Recurring infections
  • Psychosocial problems related to eczema
  • Infants/young children with moderate/severe eczema not controlled by optimal treatment

URGENT REFERRAL (Same Day)

Suspected eczema herpeticum (grouped vesicles, punched-out erosions, fever, malaise). Start systemic aciclovir before referral.

Complications to Watch For

Bacterial Infection

Staphylococcus aureus - worsening eczema with increased redness, oozing, crusting

Eczema Herpeticum

Medical emergency - grouped vesicles, punched-out erosions, fever, malaise

Fungal Infections

More common in people with atopic eczema

Psychosocial Impact

Depression, sleep disturbance, behavioral problems in children

Asthma

30-50% of children with eczema develop asthma

Allergic Rhinitis

30-80% of children with eczema develop hay fever
