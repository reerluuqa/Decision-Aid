Psoriasis

Based on NICE CKS Guidelines | Last Revised: Dec 2024

Overview & Diagnosis

Psoriasis is a systemic, immune-mediated, inflammatory skin disease with a chronic relapsing-remitting course. It may involve nails and joints (psoriatic arthritis).

‚ö†Ô∏è Drug Triggers

Psoriasis may be provoked or exacerbated by certain medications. Latency can be weeks to months.

  • Lithium
  • Beta-blockers
  • Antimalarials (e.g., chloroquine)
  • ACE Inhibitors
  • NSAIDs
  • Withdrawal of oral/potent topical corticosteroids (rebound effect)

Assessment

  • Assess Severity: Use the Physician's Global Assessment (PGA) and DLQI (Dermatology Life Quality Index).
  • Check for Complications: Psoriatic arthritis (use PEST tool), CVD risk (every 5 years), depression/anxiety.
  • Classify Type: Chronic plaque (most common), Guttate, Flexural, Pustular, Erythrodermic.

üö® Medical Emergencies

Immediate Action Required

Erythrodermic Psoriasis (>90% BSA, fever, malaise, tachycardia) and Generalized Pustular Psoriasis are life-threatening medical emergencies.

Action: Arrange immediate same-day specialist dermatology assessment.

General Management Principles

‚úÖ Core Advice

  • Offer topical treatment first-line to all patients.
  • Lifestyle: Advise on smoking cessation, alcohol limits, and weight loss (obesity worsens severity).
  • Emollients: Prescribe generously (e.g., 500g/week). Apply frequently to reduce scaling and itch. Wait 15-30 mins between emollient and steroid application.

Safety: Topical Corticosteroids

Continuous use of potent steroids can cause instability and skin atrophy.

  • Limit Use: Do not use potent steroids >8 weeks (or very potent >4 weeks) at one site continuously.
  • Treatment Break: Implement a 4-week break after a course. Continue Vitamin D analogues during this break.
  • Review: Review patients requiring multiple courses at least annually.
  • Fire Risk: Advise patients that paraffin-based emollients and treatments on clothing/bedding are a severe fire hazard.

Referral Criteria

Refer if extensive (>10% BSA), moderate-severe (PGA), significant impact on life, or resistant to topical agents.

Scenario: Scalp Psoriasis

Treatment Pathway

  1. Removal of Scale: If scale is thick, use salicylic acid, coconut oil, or tar shampoo before applying active treatment.
  2. Step 1: Offer Potent Corticosteroid (once daily) for 4 weeks. (Lotions/Gels/Mousses preferred for hair).
  3. Step 2 (if failure): Offer Combined Product (Calcipotriol/Betamethasone) once daily for 4 weeks.
  4. Step 3 (if failure): Vitamin D alone, Coal Tar, or Referral.

Note: Coal tar shampoos alone are not recommended for severe scalp psoriasis.

Scenario: Face, Flexural & Genital

‚ö†Ô∏è Thin Skin Warning

The face and flexures are highly susceptible to steroid atrophy. Do not use potent/very potent steroids continuously.

Recommended Treatment

  • First Line: Mild or Moderate potency topical corticosteroid (short term: 1-2 weeks per month max).
  • Second Line (Specialist supervision): Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (e.g., Tacrolimus, Pimecrolimus) - off-label use.

Guttate & Nail Psoriasis

üíß Guttate Psoriasis

  • Often self-limiting (resolves in 3-4 months). Triggered by Streptococcal infection.
  • Mild: Emollients, Coal Tar, or weak steroids/Vitamin D.
  • Severe/Widespread: Urgent referral for Phototherapy (UVB).
  • Do not use antibiotics solely for the skin lesions unless there is active infection.

üíÖ Nail Psoriasis

  • Keep nails short. Avoid manicures/trauma.
  • Difficult to treat topically. Improvements lag behind skin treatment.
  • Refer if functional impairment or cosmetic distress (may require systemic therapy).

Specialist Management & Referral

When to Refer (Routine/Urgent)

  • Diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Extensive disease (>10% BSA).
  • Treatment failure with topical agents.
  • Significant impact on physical/psychological wellbeing (high DLQI).
  • Suspected Psoriatic Arthritis: Urgent referral to Rheumatology.

Specialist Options
