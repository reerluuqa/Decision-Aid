Varicose Veins

Clinical Quick Reference Guide for Primary Care

URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED
Sudden onset, rapid deterioration, or signs of complications require immediate vascular referral

Definition & Overview

  • Varicose veins are dilated, tortuous subcutaneous veins >3mm in diameter
  • Affect 20-30% of adults; more common in women (3:1 ratio)
  • Caused by venous reflux due to incompetent valves
  • Part of spectrum of CVD including telangiectasias and reticular veins

Risk Factors

  • Age - risk increases with age
  • Female gender - hormonal influences, pregnancy
  • Family history - strong genetic component
  • Obesity - increased venous pressure
  • Prolonged standing - occupational risk
  • Previous DVT - post-thrombotic syndrome

Red Flags - Urgent Action Required

Sudden Onset / Rapid Deterioration

New onset varicose veins with rapid progression - may indicate underlying pathology

Thrombophlebitis

Tender, indurated cord with erythema - superficial vein thrombosis

Leg Ulceration

Active or healed venous leg ulcer (C6 or C5) - refer to vascular/ulcer clinic

Significant Bleeding

Active bleeding or recurrent hemorrhage from varices - emergency referral

Clinical Assessment

  • History: Leg pain, heaviness, swelling, itching, previous DVT
  • Examination: Trendelenburg test, Perthes test, Doppler assessment
  • Doppler US: Mandatory for all patients being considered for treatment
  • ABPI: Check before compression therapy (target >0.8)

Conservative Management

  • Compression therapy: Class 2 (23-32mmHg) stockings as first-line
  • Leg elevation: Above heart level for 30 mins, 3-4x daily
  • Exercise: Walking, calf exercises improve venous return
  • Weight management: Reduce BMI if overweight/obese
  • Avoid prolonged standing: Regular movement breaks

Referral Criteria (NICE NG168 / SIGN 162)

Urgent (2-week wait)

Venous ulceration, significant skin changes (C4-C6)

Routine

Symptomatic varicose veins (C2-C3), failed conservative management

Cosmetic Only

Telangiectasias/C1 - usually private referral

Interventional Treatments

Treatment Success Notes
Endothermal ablation 95% First-line for saphenous reflux
Ultrasound-guided foam sclerotherapy 85% Good for tortuous veins
High ligation & stripping 90% Traditional gold standard
Ambulatory phlebectomy 90% For large varicosities
Radiofrequency ablation 93% Alternative to laser

Post-Procedure Care

  • Compression: Continue stockings 1-4 weeks post-procedure
  • Early mobilization: Resume normal activities same day
  • Pain management: NSAIDs as required
  • Follow-up: Usually 6 weeks for duplex scan if indicated
  • Return to work: Typically 1-3 days for sedentary jobs
