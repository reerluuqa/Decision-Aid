üìã Overview & Definition

üìù

Definition

Inflammatory process of hair follicles

Folliculitis is an inflammatory process involving any part of the hair follicle, most commonly secondary to infection. Manifests as erythematous papules or pustules around hair follicles.

üí°
Non-infectious causes exist (e.g., eosinophilic folliculitis, folliculitis decalvans) - always consider clinical context.

Population patterns

üî¨ Classification by Etiology

Types and subtypes of folliculitis organized by causative agent

Bacterial

Staphylococcus aureus

Most common cause. Can be superficial (impetigo of Bockhart) or deep (sycosis barbae).

Erythematous papules/pustules in beard region
MSSA: dicloxacillin, cefalexin, flucloxacillin
MRSA: clindamycin, TMP-SMX, minocycline, linezolid
Bacterial

Gram-Negative Folliculitis

Associated with long-term oral antibiotic use for acne. Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Proteus.

Small pustules on cheeks, chin, perinasal region
Stop current antibiotics, use benzoyl peroxide
Consider isotretinoin for recalcitrant cases
Bacterial

Hot Tub (Pseudomonas)

Pseudomonas aeruginosa folliculitis. 2-3 days after hot tub exposure.

Large erythematous papules/pustules on trunk
Areas occluded by swimwear affected
Usually self-limited; ciprofloxacin if severe
Fungal

Dermatophytic (Tinea)

Tinea capitis, tinea barbae, Majocchi granuloma. Trichophyton species most common.

Tinea capitis: scaling, broken hairs, kerion
Tinea barbae: submaxillary area in male farmers
Treat: oral terbinafine or itraconazole
Fungal

Malassezia (Pityrosporum)

Malassezia furfur. Young adults, tropical climates, immunosuppressed.

Monomorphic intensely pruritic papules
Upper back, upper arms, chest
Treat: topical or oral antifungals
Viral

Herpes Simplex

HSV-1 or HSV-2 folliculitis. Beard region in men, spread by shaving.

Vesicles and ulcerations in beard area
PCR or Tzanck smear for diagnosis
Treat: aciclovir, valaciclovir, famciclovir
Parasitic

Demodex Folliculitis

Demodex folliculorum mite. Resembles rosacea or acne.

Erythema with papules/pustules on face
Perifollicular scaling may be present
Treat: topical permethrin or oral ivermectin
Inflammatory

Eosinophilic Pustular (Ofuji)

Recurrent intensely pruritic papules/pustules. Explosive onset.

Face, back, arms; annular lesions possible
Lesions resolve in 7-10 days, recur in 3-4 weeks
1st line: oral indometacin
Inflammatory

HIV-Associated Eosinophilic

HIV patients with CD4 <300 cells/mm¬≥. Chronic intensely pruritic.

Upper torso and head involvement
ART may lead to resolution
Symptomatic: antihistamines, topical steroids
Drug

Drug-Induced

Corticosteroids, lithium, isoniazid, EGFR inhibitors, halogens.

Acute onset within 2 weeks of new drug
Shoulders, torso, upper arms affected
1st: stop offending drug; 2nd: topical tretinoin
Inflammatory

Folliculitis Decalvans

Rare scalp disorder. Vertex and occipital regions. Tufted hairs.

Painful recurrent purulent follicular exudation
Scarring alopecia with inflamed margins
Dermatology referral required
Inflammatory

Eosinophilic (Infancy)

Scalp pustules/vesicles with erythema and crusting. Self-limiting.

Infants <1 year, male predominance
Recurs in cycles of 3 months to 5 years
Benign course; treat symptoms only

What to look for
üî¥

Erythematous papules, pustules, or cysts around hair follicle ostia. Hair shaft may be visible extending from lesion.
üìç

Any hair-bearing area: scalp, face (beard), axillae, groin, buttocks. Check areas of occlusion.

Diagnostic Tests

When to order and what to expect
Test Indication Expected Result
Bacterial Swab Recurrent/persistent cases. Unroof pustule, swab contents. Gram-positive cocci ‚Üí S. aureus. Guides antibiotic selection.
Nasal/Groin Swab Recurrent S. aureus folliculitis. Check for colonization. MRSA carrier state identification.
Viral PCR/Tzanck Suspected HSV folliculitis. Vesicles/ulcerations present. HSV DNA or multinucleated giant cells on Tzanck.

Fungal Culture Unresponsive to bacterial treatment. Identify species. Trichophyton, Malassezia, Candida species.
Skin Biopsy Persistent/unusual cases. Suspect eosinophilic folliculitis. Neutrophils ‚Üí bacterial/fungal. Lymphocytes ‚Üí viral. Eosinophils ‚Üí eosinophilic type.
CD4 Count HIV patient with folliculitis. Check for AIDS-associated. CD4 <300 cells/mm¬≥ suggests HIV-associated EF.

Clinical Diagnosis Principle
Most cases are diagnosed clinically based on history of lesions in hair-bearing areas and characteristic physical exam findings. Laboratory tests are reserved for recurrent, persistent, or atypical presentations.

‚öñÔ∏è Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that mimic folliculitis

üîç

Comparison Table

Key distinguishing features
Condition Distinguishing Signs/Symptoms Key Diagnostic Test
Pseudofolliculitis Barbae Hair re-entering skin. More common in men of African descent (Fitzpatrick 5-6). Beard area and anterior neck. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. May cause hypertrophic scars. Clinical - curved hair shaft re-entering epidermis on histology
Acne Vulgaris Affects pilosebaceous unit. Both comedones and inflammatory papules. Face, chest, shoulders, back. Typically adolescent onset but can persist. Clinical - comedone presence distinguishes from folliculitis
Acne Rosacea Age >30 years. Central face (nose, cheeks, glabella, chin). May have telangiectasia, papules, pustules, phymatous changes, or ocular involvement. Clinical - distribution and associated features
Alopecia Mucinosa Follicular papules with alopecia. Some cases present with only well-circumscribed alopecia patch. Follicular mucinosis. Skin biopsy - mucin deposition in outer root sheath
Tinea Capitis Scalp scaling, brittle hair breaking off, or kerion. May mimic folliculitis on scalp. KOH prep, fungal culture, Wood's lamp
Perioral Dermatitis Papules and pustules around mouth, nose, eyes. Often steroid-induced. Spares the vermillion border. Clinical - characteristic distribution
Milia Small, firm, white papules. Not inflammatory. Trapped keratin, not infection. Clinical - non-inflammatory appearance
Keratosis Pilaris Follicular hyperkeratosis. Rough, bumpy skin. Usually asymptomatic or mild roughness. Clinical - characteristic texture, distribution on extensor surfaces
üí°
Key Differentiator
Folliculitis is defined by localization AROUND hair follicles with erythema, papules, or pustules. Conditions like acne have lesions WITHIN the pilosebaceous unit (comedones). Pseudofolliculitis barbae shows hair shaft re-entry.
