Critical information for inflammatory skin conditions with visual urgency indicators. Red for emergencies, green for safe actions, blue for general information.

RED FLAG ALERTS: Erythroderma (>90% skin involvement), failure to respond to appropriate therapy, progressive symptoms, systemic symptoms, recurrent severe infections, unusual distribution.

Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

Common
Overview

Chronic, relapsing-remitting course with epidermal barrier defect. 20% children, 3% adults. DH2 immune dysregulation.

Red Flags
  • Failure to respond to appropriate therapy
  • Progressive erythroderma (>90% skin involvement)
  • Recurrent severe infections
  • Systemic symptoms
  • Consider cutaneous T-cell lymphoma if unresponsive
Management
Severity Treatment
Mild Emollients, mild steroids (1% hydrocortisone for face/flexures)
Moderate Moderate potency steroids (clobetasone), topical calcineurin inhibitors
Severe Potent steroids, phototherapy, systemic immunosuppressants, biologics (dupilumab)

Psoriasis

CVS Risk
Overview

Hyperproliferative disorder of keratinocytes. Associated with cardiovascular risk, metabolic syndrome, psoriatic arthritis.

Red Flags & Referral
  • Erythrodermic psoriasis - medical emergency
  • Generalized pustular psoriasis
  • Failure to respond to topical therapy
  • Arthritis symptoms (use PEST score ≥2 → rheumatology)
Management
  • Topical: Corticosteroids + vitamin D analogs (calcipotriol)
  • Moderate: Narrowband UVB phototherapy
  • Severe: Systemic (methotrexate, cyclosporin, acitretin*) or biologics
  • *Acitretin: Teratogenic, avoid in women of childbearing age

Acne Vulgaris

Common
Overview

Four pathogenic factors: follicle hyperkeratinization, sebum production, inflammation, C. acnes proliferation.

Assessment
  • Distribution: face, neck, chest, back
  • Scarring assessment: atrophic/hypertrophic/keloids
  • Psychosocial impact
  • Trigger identification
Management
Type Treatment
Comedonal Topical retinoids (adapalene) or benzoyl peroxide
Inflammatory Topical retinoid + BP ± topical antibiotic (limit 8-12 weeks)
Moderate-Severe Oral tetracyclines + topical therapy
Nodulocystic Isotretinoin* (secondary care)

*Isotretinoin: Pregnancy prevention program, monitor LFTs/lipids, photosensitivity

Urticaria & Angioedema

Acute
Classification
  • Acute: < 6 weeks duration
  • Chronic: ≥ 6 weeks duration
  • Pathophysiology: Mast cell degranulation (histamine release)
  • Triggers rare in CSU despite patient beliefs
Emergency: Angioedema
  • Swelling of eyelids, lips, tongue, pharynx
  • Potential airway compromise
  • Urgent assessment required
Management
  • First-line: Antihistamines (non-sedating)
  • Severe acute: Short course oral corticosteroids (0.5-0.75 mg/kg)
  • Refractory chronic: Omalizumab (biologic)
  • Pregnancy: Cyclosporin considered safe

Scabies

Infectious
Key Presentation
  • Intense pruritus worse at night
  • Burrows in web spaces, wrists, ankles, genitals, nipples
  • Norwegian scabies: crusted, highly contagious form
  • Commonly misdiagnosed as psoriasis/eczema
Management
  • First-line: Permethrin 5% cream
  • Day 1: Apply all over body, leave for 8-12 hours
  • Day 7: Repeat application
  • Treat all close contacts simultaneously
  • Decontaminate clothing/bedding
  • Resistant cases: Add oral ivermectin 200μg/kg
Note on Post-Scabietic Itch

Can persist 2-6 weeks after successful treatment. Differentiate from active infection (burrows present).

Skin of Color Considerations

Important
Key Differences
  • Erythema: Less apparent; may appear purplish/dark
  • Post-inflammatory: Hyper/hypopigmentation common, can last months-years
  • Follicular disorders: Pseudofolliculitis barbae more common
  • Keloid scarring: Higher risk
Steroid Caution

Avoid unnecessary high-potency steroids to reduce risk of atrophy, telangiectasia, and pigment changes.

Management Tips
  • Reassure about post-inflammatory changes - slow to resolve
  • Pseudofolliculitis: Avoid close shaving, single-blade razors, shave with hair growth
  • Consider topical retinoids, glycolic/salicylic acid for ingrown hairs

Immediate Referral Indicators

  • Erythroderma (>90% skin involvement)
  • Acute blistering disorders (SJS/TEN)
  • Pustular psoriasis
  • Eczema herpeticum (HSV infection in eczema)
  • Systemic symptoms with skin findings
  • Suspected cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (unresponsive eczema/psoriasis)

Topical Steroid Potency

Potency Examples Use
Mild Hydrocortisone 1% Face, flexures, genitals
Moderate Clobetasone (Eumovate) Trunk, limbs
Potent Betamethasone valerate Localized areas, short courses
Very Potent Clobetasol propionate (Dermovate) Resistant areas, supervised use

Clinical Pearls

  • Tinea incognito: Fungal infection masked by steroid use
  • Koebner phenomenon: Trauma-induced lesions (psoriasis, lichen planus)
  • Auspitz sign: Pinpoint bleeding when scale removed (psoriasis)
  • Norwegian scabies: Crusted, highly contagious form in immunocompromised
  • Herald patch: Solitary oval plaque preceding pityriasis rosea
  • Topical antibiotic resistance: Limit to 8-12 weeks, combine with benzoyl peroxide
