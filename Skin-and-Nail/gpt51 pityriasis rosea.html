2. Diagnosis ‚Äì is this pityriasis rosea?

Visual pattern recognition

Classic pityriasis rosea
Most cases
  • ‚Ä¢ Herald patch in up to ~90%: 2‚Äì5 cm oval erythematous plaque with collarette of scale and central clearing on trunk, thigh, upper arm or neck.
  • ‚Ä¢ Generalised eruption a few days‚Äì3 weeks later: multiple 0.5‚Äì1 cm oval, slightly raised, pink-red or hypopigmented lesions with fine peripheral scaling.
  • ‚Ä¢ Distribution: symmetrical, mainly trunk and proximal limbs. Lesions align along cleavage lines ‚Üí ‚ÄúChristmas tree‚Äù pattern on back/chest.
  • ‚Ä¢ Palms and soles typically spared; lesions are not vesicular.
  • ‚Ä¢ Itch variable (often mild‚Äìmoderate); possible prodromal malaise, headache, URTI-like symptoms.

Atypical patterns & skin tone considerations
Look closely
  • ‚Ä¢ Hypopigmented lesions more common in dark-skinned people; look for collarette scale and classic distribution.
  • ‚Ä¢ Face & scalp involvement more frequent in children; oral lesions in ~25% (ill-defined erythematous patches, erosions, petechiae or bullae).
  • ‚Ä¢ Variants (around 20%): inverse (face/axillae/groin), few large patches in flexures (Vidal type), larger plaques, papular/purpuric/pustular/vesicular forms.
  • ‚Ä¢ Recurrent cases (3‚Äì4%) may lack herald patch or show fewer/smaller lesions.

Guttate psoriasis Very common confusion
‚Ä∫
  • ‚Ä¢ Numerous 2 mm‚Äì1 cm pink/red scaly papules on trunk & proximal limbs; often post-streptococcal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Plaques may be thicker; look for other psoriasis stigmata (nails, scalp).
  • ‚Ä¢ No herald patch; distribution more generalised and not along cleavage lines.
Tinea corporis / pityriasis versicolor Consider fungal causes
‚Ä∫
  • ‚Ä¢ Tinea corporis ‚Äì annular, expanding plaques with active scaly edge and central clearing; often fewer, larger lesions.
  • ‚Ä¢ Pityriasis versicolor ‚Äì multiple macules on trunk/upper arms with fine scale; colour fawn, pink, red, brown or almost white.
  • ‚Ä¢ Mycology / empiric antifungals if clinical suspicion high or single persistent lesion.

‚Ä∫
  • ‚Ä¢ Implicated drugs include ACE-inhibitors, beta-blockers, clonidine, gold, interferon, isotretinoin, lamotrigine, metronidazole, NSAIDs, omeprazole, terbinafine and some vaccines (BCG, hepatitis B, pneumococcal).
  • ‚Ä¢ Usually no herald patch; lesions more violaceous; itch often more severe; eosinophilia may be present.
  • ‚Ä¢ Review need for culprit drug; seek specialist advice before stopping disease-modifying agents.

‚Ä∫
  • ‚Ä¢ Acute HIV ‚Äì symmetrical erythematous maculopapular rash, including face, trunk, limbs, palms and soles ¬± fever, sore throat, lymphadenopathy.

Core explanation & safety netting
Key action
  • ‚Ä¢ Explain that pityriasis rosea is an acute, self-limiting rash mainly affecting young adults and children.
  • ‚Ä¢ Rash may appear to worsen initially, with new crops for up to 6 weeks; most fade within 2‚Äì6 weeks, occasional cases up to 3‚Äì5 months.
  • ‚Ä¢ It is not contagious.
  • ‚Ä¢ After resolution, residual darker or lighter patches may persist for several months but there is no scarring.
  • ‚Ä¢ Recurrence is uncommon (~3‚Äì4%) and usually milder.
  • ‚Ä¢ Provide written info (e.g. local leaflet or national dermatology resources) and give clear safety-netting: seek review if fever, new systemic symptoms, blistering, mucosal involvement or if rash persists >3 months.
üíä
Symptomatic treatment for itch
Treat if bothersome
  • ‚Ä¢ Emollients liberally and frequently. Prefer ointments/creams suited to skin type. i Remember MHRA advice: paraffin or paraffin-free emollients on clothing/bedding can pose a fire risk ‚Äì advise not to smoke or go near naked flames.
  • ‚Ä¢ Topical corticosteroid once‚Äìtwice daily for up to 4 weeks if itch is moderate‚Äìsevere: mildly potent (e.g. hydrocortisone 1%) or moderately potent (e.g. clobetasone 0.05% or betamethasone valerate 0.025%) depending on severity and body site.
  • ‚Ä¢ Oral sedating antihistamine (e.g. chlorphenamine) at night can be considered off-label if itch disturbs sleep; stop if no benefit after 2 weeks.
  • ‚Ä¢ Tailor choices to age, comorbidities, pregnancy/breast-feeding and concomitant drugs; check BNF/eMC for dosing and cautions.

Pityriasis rosea in first trimester pregnancy
Context-sensitive
  • ‚Ä¢ Reassure that pityriasis rosea is usually self-limiting and in most cases there is no harm to the pregnancy.
  • ‚Ä¢ Evidence on adverse outcomes (miscarriage, preterm delivery, neonatal issues) is limited and inconclusive; risk appears low in uncomplicated cases.
  • ‚Ä¢ If diagnosis is uncertain, disease is extensive or there are obstetric concerns, discuss with dermatology/obstetrics. Specialist team may consider oral antiviral therapy where appropriate.
  • ‚Ä¢ Manage itch as above, choosing agents with established safety profiles in pregnancy and lowest effective potency/dose.

When to refer to dermatology
Referral triggers
  • ‚Ä¢ Uncertain diagnosis after considering differentials or atypical morphology/distribution.
  • ‚Ä¢ Suspected atypical pityriasis rosea variant or overlap with other dermatoses (e.g. pityriasis lichenoides, lichen planus, subacute lupus).
  • ‚Ä¢ Extensive disease or severe itch not controlled with primary-care measures.
  • ‚Ä¢ Rash persists >3 months despite typical features and conservative management.
  • ‚Ä¢ Pregnancy with extensive rash, systemic symptoms or diagnostic uncertainty (liaise with obstetrics as well).
