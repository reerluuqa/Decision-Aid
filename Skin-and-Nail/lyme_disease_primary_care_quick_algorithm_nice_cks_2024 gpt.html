At-a-glance
  • Erythema migrans → Diagnose & treat No tests
  • No EM → Test strategy based on symptom duration
  • Consider empirical antibiotics if high clinical suspicion
  • Children <18y: discuss with specialist unless isolated EM
Red flags – Refer/Discuss urgently
  • Neurological: facial palsy, meningitis, radiculopathy, encephalitis
  • Cardiac: heart block, pericarditis
  • Arthritis: inflammatory oligoarthritis
  • Derm: acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans, lymphocytoma
  • Diagnostic uncertainty or non‑response after two courses
Prevention & Bite Advice
  • Paths over long grass; daily tick checks
  • DEET repellents; cover limbs; light fabrics
  • Remove ticks promptly (fine tweezers, steady upward pull)
  • No antibiotic prophylaxis after bite; monitor 30 days
Diagnosis – When to suspect EM pathognomonic Consider w/o recalled bite

1) Erythema migrans (EM) present?

Expanding erythema with clear edge; usually >5 cm; often not itchy/painful; 1–36 days after bite; may show central clearing.

If EM → Diagnose clinically & start antibiotics

2) No EM

Consider Lyme with compatible symptoms (fever/sweats, malaise, myalgia/arthralgia, headache, neck pain, paraesthesia, cognitive issues) or focal features (neuro, cardiac, arthritis, ocular, skin).

Do not exclude due to absent bite history.

3) Differentiate from mimics

Cellulitis, tinea, erythema multiforme, insect bite reaction (<48 h, itchy, <5 cm), urticaria, spider bite, STARI (US).

Jarisch–Herxheimer reaction

Fever/chills/myalgia within 1–12 h of starting antibiotics (up to 48 h). Self‑limiting. Continue antibiotics if no allergy (e.g., no urticaria/angioedema). Treat symptoms supportively.

Ongoing symptoms – what next? ReassessSecond course?
  • Worse during therapy? – Consider Jarisch–Herxheimer vs allergy.
  • Post‑course no improvement/worse:
    • Review history; verify adherence; consider reinfection.
    • If possible treatment failure → give second course using an alternative agent.
    • Persistent symptoms after two completed courses → do not offer long‑term antibiotics; discuss with or refer to specialist.
    • Explain that symptoms may take months to resolve; some reflect prior organ damage; no test confirms active infection.
Tick bite management & prevention No prophylaxisPrompt removal

Tick attached?

Remove with fine‑tipped tweezers close to skin; pull upwards firmly without twisting. Clean skin; hand hygiene.

Post‑bite

Do not give prophylactic antibiotics. Monitor for 30 days for EM or systemic symptoms; advise early review if they occur.

Prevention

Stay on paths; avoid long grass; DEET repellents; cover limbs; light fabrics; daily tick checks (incl. scalp/skin folds; children’s head/neck); check pets & clothing.

Key messages Clinic quick wins
  • EM present → treat now (no lab tests).
  • High suspicion → don’t wait – start antibiotics.
  • Testing guided by duration; repeat ELISA in 4–6 weeks if early negative & still suspicious.
  • Refer/discuss focal organ involvement or persistent symptoms after 2 courses.
  • No prophylaxis after tick bite; emphasise self‑monitoring.
