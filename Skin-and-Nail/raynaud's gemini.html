Raynaud's Phenomenon (RP)

Assessment & Definition

Initial presentation and history taking
Definition & Epidemiology General Info

Definition: Vasospasm causing digits to change color to white (pallor) from lack of blood flow, usually triggered by cold temperatures [cite: 23]. The pallor must be well-demarcated [cite: 13].

  • Triphasic Response: White (pallor) → Blue (deoxygenation) → Red (reperfusion) [cite: 12, 24].
  • Prevalence: Affects 3% to 5% of the population [cite: 10, 34].
  • Risk Factors: Female sex, cold climates, family history [cite: 37, 42].
Key History Questions
  • Location: Fingertips or toe tips are most common. Rarely ears/nose [cite: 132, 79].
  • Triggers: Cold exposure, stress, vibration tools, drugs (beta-blockers, migraine therapies, stimulants) [cite: 23, 60, 64].
  • Systemic Review: Check for connective tissue disease (CTD) symptoms: inflammatory arthritis, rash, dry eyes/mouth, skin tightness, dysphagia [cite: 154].
  • Occupational: Use of vibration tools (e.g., jackhammers) [cite: 61].
Physical Examination
  • Pulse: Check peripheral pulses and blood pressure in both arms (rule out large-vessel narrowing) [cite: 170, 171].
  • Allen Test: Check for arterial occlusion [cite: 173].
  • Skin Exam: Look for sclerodactyly, telangiectasia, digital ulcers, or pitting scars [cite: 179].
  • Nailfold Capillaries: Use ophthalmoscope/dermatoscope. Dilated capillaries (looking like red pen marks) suggest Secondary RP [cite: 239].

Diagnosis & Classification

Distinguishing Primary vs. Secondary RP
Primary (Idiopathic) RP Stable

Dysregulation of thermoregulation without an underlying cause. Usually benign [cite: 82, 85].

  • Age of Onset: Usually adolescence; rare after age 40 [cite: 165].
  • Symmetry: Attacks are usually symmetric.
  • Tissue Integrity: No ulcers, gangrene, or tissue damage [cite: 85].
  • Labs: Normal ANA and ESR [cite: 273, 276].

Diagnosis is clinical if patient is young with no signs of CTD.

Secondary RP Referral/Workup Needed

Associated with underlying conditions (e.g., Systemic Sclerosis, SLE, Sj√∂gren's, Rheumatoid Arthritis) [cite: 50, 52].

  • Age of Onset: Often >40 years [cite: 152].
  • Clinical Signs: Digital ulcers, digital pits, puffy fingers, sclerodactyly, or asymmetric attacks [cite: 177, 249].
  • Labs: Positive ANA (esp. centromere/nucleolar patterns), elevated ESR/CRP [cite: 274, 275].
  • Capillaroscopy: Abnormal/dilated capillaries at nailbed [cite: 221].
Caution: Presence of digital ulceration generally excludes Primary RP diagnosis [cite: 227]. Refer to rheumatologist.
Investigation Plan

If Secondary RP is suspected or age >40:

  • Blood Tests: FBC, ESR or CRP, Antinuclear Antibody (ANA) with titre/pattern [cite: 219].
  • Urinalysis: Check for renal involvement [cite: 219].
  • Imaging: Chest X-ray only if cervical rib suspected (rare) [cite: 230].

Management Algorithm

Stepwise approach from lifestyle to surgery
Step 1: Lifestyle Measures (All Patients) First Line

Many Primary RP cases do not need drugs [cite: 14].

  • Thermoregulation: Keep warm (body and hands), use hand warmers/gloves [cite: 14, 394].
  • Habits: Smoking cessation is crucial [cite: 14, 369]. Avoid stress. Regular exercise.
  • Avoid Triggers: Avoid caffeine, sympathomimetics (decongestants), and vibration tools [cite: 369, 396].
Step 2: Pharmacological (Mild/Primary) If Lifestyle Fails

Calcium-Channel Blockers (CCBs) - First Line Drug:

  • Nifedipine: 30-60 mg extended-release once daily (or 10mg IR tid) [cite: 409].
  • Alternatives: Amlodipine, Felodipine, or non-dihydropyridine (Diltiazem) if intolerant [cite: 411].
  • Side Effects: Flushing, headache, ankle edema, hypotension [cite: 301].

Second/Third Line Alternatives:

  • Angiotensin-II receptor antagonists (e.g., Losartan) [cite: 306].
  • SSRIs (e.g., Fluoxetine) [cite: 16].
  • Topical Nitrates (e.g., GTN ointment) [cite: 464].
Step 3: Severe Secondary / Digital Ulcers Urgent / Specialist
Indication: Critical ischemia, threatened digit, or active ulcers [cite: 311].
  • PDE-5 Inhibitors: Sildenafil, Tadalafil. Reduces frequency/severity of attacks [cite: 338, 342].
  • Prostacyclins: IV Iloprost (or Epoprostenol). Highly effective for healing ulcers [cite: 311, 314].
  • Endothelin Receptor Antagonist: Bosentan (prevents new ulcers in systemic sclerosis, does not heal existing ones) [cite: 362, 363].
  • Antibiotics: If ulcers are infected (Staph aureus/Pseudomonas common) [cite: 358].
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs or opioids depending on severity [cite: 354, 403].
Step 4: Surgical Intervention Last Resort
  • Digital Sympathectomy: Effective for severe RP failing drug therapy. Can improve pain and healing [cite: 322].
  • Debridement/Amputation: Required for necrotic tissue/gangrene [cite: 320].

Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring and complications
Monitoring & Prognosis
  • Primary RP: Symptoms often mild or disappear. Non-progressive [cite: 85, 531].
  • Secondary Risk: ~13% of initially "primary" cases develop underlying CTD. Watch for nailfold changes and positive ANA [cite: 532, 222].
  • Referral: Refer to rheumatologist if Secondary RP suspected, or complications (ulcers) present [cite: 226, 298].
Complications Watch List
  • Digital Ulcers: Common in Scleroderma. 60% heal by 6 months [cite: 518].
  • Gangrene/Necrosis: Result of prolonged ischemia. Requires urgent attention [cite: 516].
  • Infection: Cellulitis or Osteomyelitis from infected ulcers [cite: 525].
