Parvovirus B19 Infection

Primary Care
Updated: Feb 2022
All Ages
Red Flags Management Pregnancy

Critical Red Flags URGENT

Pregnant Woman with Suspected Infection

Immediate virology/microbiology contact required. Fetal hydrops risk 3-11% at 9-20 weeks.

Severe Anemia Symptoms

Breathlessness, lightheadedness, lethargy, confusion. Risk of aplastic crisis in sickle cell/thalassemia.

Fetal Hydrops / Mirror Syndrome

Maternal edema reflecting fetal hydrops. Emergency obstetric admission required.

Neurological Complications

Encephalitis, meningitis, stroke, peripheral neuropathy in immunocompromised.

Immediate Actions

Contact Specialists

Virology/Microbiology/ID for all pregnant exposures/infections

Fetal Medicine Referral

URGENT referral if confirmed infection in pregnancy

Emergency Bloods

FBC + reticulocytes for anemia symptoms in at-risk patients

Define Exposure

Significant contact = same room ≥15min or face-to-face

Management Scenarios

Children & Adults (Non-Pregnant) SELF-LIMITING

Suspected Infection

Diagnosis clinical; no labs needed for healthy patients
Supportive care: paracetamol/NSAIDs
Child not infectious once rash appears → no school exclusion
Inform school/nursery to manage at-risk contacts

Possible Exposure

Significant contact defined as ≥15 min same room or face-to-face
Avoid contact with pregnant women & immunocompromised
Infectious period: up to 10 days before rash (including day of onset)
Household exposure highest risk; occupational risk low
Pregnant Women HIGH RISK
Critical Action

Contact local virology/microbiology/ID department IMMEDIATELY for all suspected or confirmed cases in pregnancy. Do not delay initial blood testing.

Suspected Infection

Urgent bloods: Parvovirus B19 IgM, Rubella (regardless of status)
IgM positive in first 20 weeks: Confirm with PCR or booking IgG
IgM negative: Excludes infection in past 4 weeks only
Supportive care: paracetamol. Not contagious once rash appears

Possible Exposure

Test IgG/IgM ASAP after contact
IgG+/IgM-: Past infection, immune → reassure
IgG-/IgM-: Susceptible → repeat test 1 month post-exposure
Avoid contact with other pregnant women until status known
Confirmed Infection in Pregnancy

Immediate Actions:

  • Urgent referral to fetal medicine
  • Risk of transmission ~30%
  • Specialist fetal monitoring arranged

Specialist Monitoring:

  • Serial ultrasound + Doppler from ~4 weeks post-exposure
  • Detect fetal anemia, hydrops, heart failure
  • Consider fetal blood sampling + intrauterine transfusion if hydrops
High-Risk Complications CRITICAL

Pregnancy Complications

Non-immune hydrops fetalis: 3-11% risk at 9-20 weeks
Fetal death: Overall 5-10% loss rate; most in 2nd trimester
Mirror syndrome: Maternal edema reflecting fetal hydrops (rare)

Hematological Complications

Transient aplastic crisis: Sickle cell, thalassemia, spherocytosis
Pure red cell aplasia: Immunocompromised (leukemia, HIV, transplant)
Neurological: Encephalitis, meningitis, stroke, neuropathy
Key Clinical Points HIGH-YIELD

Epidemiology

  • Endemic: 50-60% adults, 60-70% women of childbearing age seropositive
  • Seasonal outbreaks every 3-4 years (late winter/early spring)
  • 1-3% susceptible pregnant women infected annually (≥10% in epidemics)
  • Transmission: respiratory droplets, vertical (30% transmission rate), rare transfusion

Clinical Features

  • Incubation: 14-21 days; Infectious: up to 10 days before rash
  • Children: Slapped cheek → lacy reticular rash (1-3 weeks, may recur)
  • Adults: Polyarthropathy (50%), less specific rash
  • 25-50% asymptomatic infections
Universal Screening Not Recommended

No vaccine or prophylaxis available. Management is based on exposure and symptom-based testing.
