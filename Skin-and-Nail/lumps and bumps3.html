Melanoma
Urgent (2WW)
Key Features
  • 80% appear de novo (new lesion)
  • ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving
  • Ugly duckling sign (different from other moles)
  • Rapid change in size, shape, color, or border
  • Nodular melanoma: Firm, dark papule/nodule, aggressive
Red Flags
  • New mole in adult >50 years
  • Rapid growth over weeks/months
  • Irregular borders or multiple colors
  • Bleeding, ulceration, or itching
  • Nail changes with Hutchinson's sign
Management
  • Immediate referral on 2-week wait pathway
  • Full skin examination recommended
  • Dermoscopy if available (look for streaks, blue-grey structures)
  • Document ABCDE characteristics
  • Ask about sunbed use, blistering sunburns, family history
IMMEDIATE ACTION: Refer via 2WW pathway
Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)
Urgent (2WW)
Key Features
  • Rapid growth over months (vs. years for BCC)
  • Firm, tender nodule or ulcer
  • Common on sun-exposed sites
  • High risk in immunosuppressed (200x risk in transplant patients)
  • May arise from pre-existing AK or Bowen's
Red Flags
  • Rapid growth (3-6 months)
  • Tenderness (suggests perineural invasion)
  • Non-healing ulcer >6 weeks
  • Immunosuppressed patient
  • History of phototherapy or chronic inflammation
Management
  • Urgent referral on 2WW pathway
  • Full skin check (high risk of other skin cancers)
  • Document size, location, growth rate
  • Assess for lymphadenopathy
  • Higher suspicion if on lip, ear, or scar site
IMMEDIATE ACTION: Refer via 2WW pathway
Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)
Routine Referral
Key Features
  • Slow-growing (years), "bleeds, scabs, never heals"
  • Pearly nodule with telangiectasia
  • Most common skin cancer (70% of cases)
  • Non-metastasizing but locally destructive
  • 80% risk of another BCC within 5 years
Red Flags
  • High-risk locations: eyelids, nose, lips
  • Rapid growth or ulceration
  • Recurrent lesion
  • Immunosuppressed patient
  • Large size (>1cm on face, >2cm on trunk)
Management
  • Routine referral (urgent if high-risk location)
  • Consider topical 5-FU for superficial BCC (6 weeks)
  • Offer full skin check (high recurrence rate)
  • Document with dermoscopy if available (arborizing vessels)
  • Excision with histology for confirmation
ACTION: Routine referral (urgent if high-risk site)
Bowen's Disease (SCC in situ)
Routine Referral
Key Features
  • Slow-growing, scaly, erythematous patch
  • Common on lower legs in elderly women
  • Full-thickness dysplasia (vs. partial in AK)
  • Can resemble eczema or psoriasis
  • Small risk of progression to invasive SCC
Red Flags
  • Rapid growth or nodular change
  • Bleeding or ulceration
  • Immunosuppressed patient
  • Multiple or large lesions
Management
  • Topical 5-FU for 4 weeks (can take breaks if too irritated)
  • Consider cryotherapy (risk of ulceration on legs)
  • Refer if diagnosis uncertain or treatment fails
  • Review at 3 months post-treatment
  • Dermoscopy: glomerular vessels, scale
ACTION: Topical 5-FU 4 weeks or refer
Actinic Keratosis (AK)
Routine Referral
Key Features
  • Rough, scaly patches on sun-exposed skin
  • 25% self-resolve, 75% persist, small risk of SCC transformation
  • "Field change" - multiple lesions indicate high sun damage
  • Dermoscopy: strawberry pattern, rosettes
  • Common in elderly, outdoor workers
Red Flags
  • Induration or nodular change (suspicious for SCC)
  • Rapid growth or ulceration
  • 10+ AKs = higher SCC risk
  • Immunosuppressed patient
Management
  • Sun protection and moisturizers for mild cases
  • Topical 5-FU (4% or 5%) for 28 days
  • Consider diclofenac gel or imiquimod
  • Field treatment for multiple lesions (split area into quadrants)
  • Refer if widespread, uncertain diagnosis, or treatment fails
ACTION: Sun protection + topical 5-FU if needed
Pyogenic Granuloma
Urgent (2WW if uncertain)
Key Features
  • Rapidly growing, bleeding red nodule
  • Common on fingers, during pregnancy, post-trauma
  • Friable, bleeds easily
  • Key differential: amelanotic melanoma
  • Hormonal association (contraception, pregnancy)
Red Flags
  • Rapid growth over weeks
  • Bleeding with minimal trauma
  • Uncertain diagnosis (vs. amelanotic melanoma)
  • Recurrence after previous treatment
Management
  • If confident: curettage & cautery with histology
  • ALWAYS send for histology (rule out melanoma)
  • Refer if uncertain or rapid growing
  • Pregnancy-related lesions may resolve postpartum
  • Consider shave excision if pedunculated
Always send for histology - key differential is amelanotic melanoma
ACTION: Excise with histology or refer if uncertain
Seborrheic Keratosis
Benign / Self-care
Key Features
  • "Stuck-on", waxy, verrucous appearance
  • Multiple lesions common in middle-aged/elderly
  • Itchy, may catch on clothing
  • Dermoscopy: milia-like cysts, comedone openings, brain-like pattern
  • Benign - no malignant potential
Management
  • Reassure - benign lesion
  • No treatment needed unless symptomatic
  • Cryotherapy or curettage for cosmetic reasons
  • Key skill: differentiate from melanoma (ugly duckling sign)
  • Refer only if diagnosis uncertain
Most common benign lesion referred to dermatology - learn to recognize to avoid unnecessary referrals
ACTION: Reassure and monitor unless symptomatic
Warts & Verrucae
Benign / Self-care
Key Features
  • Common warts: firm, rough papillae on hands/fingers
  • Plantar warts: black dots (bleeding points), loss of skin lines
  • Filiform: facial/neck, stalk-like projections
  • Anogenital: refer to GUM clinic, STI screen needed
  • Persistent, often resolve spontaneously (months-years)
Red Flags
  • Anogenital warts (safeguarding concerns)
  • Immunosuppressed patient
  • Rapid growth or change
  • Elderly with new anogenital warts (could be latent HPV)
Management
  • Salicylic acid (Salatac, Wartex) daily for up to 12 weeks
  • Soak in warm water, file down, apply treatment
  • Use disposable files to avoid reinfection
  • Cryotherapy if available (weekly x 4, not always commissioned)
  • Set expectations: slow response, high recurrence
ACTION: First-line topical salicylic acid, manage expectations
Skin Tags (Acrochordons)
Benign / Self-care
Key Features
  • Soft, fleshy, pedunculated lesions in skin folds
  • Common in axillae, neck, groin
  • Associated with friction, obesity, pregnancy
  • May become inflamed if twisted or traumatized
  • Benign with no malignant potential
Management
  • No treatment needed if asymptomatic
  • Snip excision or cryotherapy for cosmetic reasons
  • Home remedy: cotton string tied at base for pedunculated tags
  • If doing minor surgery, no need to send for histology
  • Reassure about benign nature
ACTION: No treatment needed unless symptomatic/cosmetic concern
Keratoacanthoma
Urgent (2WW)
Key Features
  • Rapidly growing "volcano-like" nodule over weeks
  • Central keratin crust/crater
  • May spontaneously regress but cannot be reliably distinguished from SCC
  • Common on sun-exposed sites
  • Often history of recent trauma
Red Flags
  • Rapid growth (weeks)
  • Large size (>2cm)
  • Immunosuppressed patient
  • Recurrent lesion
Management
  • ALWAYS refer on 2WW pathway (cannot distinguish from SCC clinically)
  • Urgent excision for histology
  • Do not assume spontaneous regression
  • Complete excision needed for diagnosis
IMMEDIATE ACTION: Refer via 2WW pathway

Clinical Tip: When in doubt, refer or seek advice via A&G. Always send excised lesions for histology. Dermoscopy significantly improves diagnostic accuracy.
