Areas of skin (or hair) depigmentation that are typically asymptomatic. Note: itch may occur before lesions appear.

üìç Distribution & Extent

Common Sites (affected first):

  • Fingers and hands
  • Wrists
  • Face (periorbital, perioral)
  • Axillae
  • Groin and genitalia
  • Mucosal areas (prominent in darker skin)

Key Features:

  • Often bilateral & symmetrical
  • May be unilateral in early disease or segmental type
  • Koebner phenomenon at trauma/friction sites
  • Hair roots can be affected ‚Üí white hair
  • Individual lesions enlarge over time without treatment

Active Disease Indicators

üí° Clinical Pearl

Halo naevi may be present and are associated with vitiligo. These are benign moles surrounded by a halo of depigmentation.

Take digital photographs as a benchmark to monitor disease progression and treatment effectiveness.

üíä Primary Care Management

üí°

Management Principles

Treatment depends on: skin phenotype, subtype, lesion extent & distribution, activity, age, comorbidities, and patient preference.

For Fitzpatrick types I-II: May only need camouflage cosmetics and sunscreens.

‚úÖ First-Line Treatment
‚Ñû Topical Corticosteroid
  • Potent or very potent topical corticosteroid
  • Apply once daily
  • Avoid periocular area
  • Review every 3-6 months

üìã Patient Education

  • Avoid friction and trauma (reduces Koebner phenomenon)
  • Repigmentation rates are variable
  • Treatment may not stop spread
  • Recurrence common after successful treatment (44% relapse in first year)
  • Face, neck, trunk respond best; lips and extremities more resistant

üë∂ Congenital
Albinism ‚ñº

Generalized depigmentation from birth, includes eyes (strabismus, nystagmus)

Naevus anaemicus ‚ñº

Hypopigmented patches due to localized vasoconstriction

Naevus depigmentosus ‚ñº

Segmental hypopigmentation present from birth/first year

Piebaldism ‚ñº

Autosomal dominant. White forelock, midline and shin depigmentation from birth

Tuberous sclerosis ‚ñº

Ash-leaf macules, angiofibromas, seizures, developmental delay

üî• Post-inflammatory
Chemical leukoderma ‚ñº

From phenolic compounds in adhesives, dyes, disinfectants, rubber etc.

Pityriasis alba ‚ñº

Children. Dry, hypopigmented patches with diffuse borders. May have subtle scaling. Face/arms.

Post-inflammatory ‚ñº

Following eczema, psoriasis, lichen planus, scleroderma, lupus, syphilis

Lichen sclerosus ‚ñº

Itchy, white atrophic plaques. Genital/perianal.

Morphoea ‚ñº

Localized thickening of dermis. Induration present.

ü¶† Infective
Pityriasis versicolor ‚ñº

Yeast infection. Small (<1cm), round, pale macules with fine scale. Neck, trunk, chest.

Progressive macular hypomelanosis ‚ñº

Circular hypopigmented patches on trunk. More common in darker skin.

Tuberculoid leprosy ‚ñº

Raised borders. Loss of sensation. Thickened nerves.

üî¨ Neoplastic
‚ö†Ô∏è

Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma

Hypopigmented patches with 'wrinkled' appearance, dryness, scaling. Buttocks common site.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Melanoma-associated depigmentation

üìã Other
  • Drug-induced - corticosteroids, chloroquine, fluphenazine, imiquimod
  • Halo naevus - mole with depigmented halo
  • Idiopathic guttate hypomelanosis - 1-3mm macules on limbs in elderly
  • Sarcoidosis - hypopigmented macules over granulomas

üí¨ Key Messages for Patients
  • Vitiligo is a chronic condition that can be managed
  • It is NOT contagious
  • Avoiding friction/trauma may help prevent new lesions
  • Treatment can help but results vary
  • Spontaneous repigmentation can occur
  • Sun protection is important
  • Camouflage products are available on prescription
  • Psychological support is available if needed
  • Regular monitoring appointments are recommended
  • Thyroid screening is important
