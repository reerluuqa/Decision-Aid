Background Information

Definition, prevalence, transmission, prognosis

Cutaneous warts are small, rough growths caused by infection of keratinocytes with human papillomavirus (HPV). They can appear anywhere on the skin but are commonly seen on the hands and feet.

A verruca (plantar wart) is specifically a wart on the sole of the foot.

Common wart (Verruca vulgaris)

Firm, raised papules with rough cauliflower-like surface. Common on knuckles, knees, fingers.

Plantar wart (Verruca plantaris)

On soles of feet. Often have central dark dots (thrombosed capillaries). May be painful.

Periungual wart

Around fingernails or toenails. Can be painful and disturb nail growth.

Plane/Flat wart (Verruca plana)

Round, flat-topped, skin-coloured or greyish-yellow. Common on face, backs of hands, shins.

Filiform wart

Finger-like appearance, may have a stalk. Most often on face or neck.

Mosaic warts

Occur when palmar or plantar warts coalesce into larger plaques.

Mode of transmission: Direct skin-to-skin contact, or indirectly via contaminated surfaces (e.g., pool floors, showers).

Risk factors for developing warts:

  • Damaged epithelial barrier
  • Skin conditions causing breaks in skin
  • Wet and/or macerated skin
  • Contact with rough surfaces (microtrauma)
  • Nail biting or picking at warts (autoinoculation)
  • Occupation involving meat/fish handling
  • Frequent water immersion
  • Immunosuppression
‚è±Ô∏è

Incubation period: Few weeks to more than a year

HPV survival: Months to years on contaminated surfaces

üìä Natural Resolution Rates

Children:

  • ~25% resolve within 2-3 months
  • ~50% clear within 1 year
  • ~66% clear by 2 years
  • ~90% resolve within 5 years

Adults: Resolution tends to be slower - warts may persist 5-10 years.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Immunosuppressed patients: Warts may be large, extensive, and resistant to treatment.

  • Spread from picking at the wart
  • Spread to contacts
  • Local infection
  • Skin pigmentation changes and scarring (from treatments)
  • Psychosocial impact (embarrassment, teasing)
  • Nail dystrophy from periungual warts
  • Pain from plantar warts affecting walking
üî¥

Rare but important:

Malignant changes (squamous cell carcinoma) reported in immunosuppressed patients. Consider if lesions change appearance.

How to Diagnose

Clinical features and examination

‚ñº
‚úÖ

Diagnosis is clinical

Warts are diagnosed from their typical appearance. In most cases, no further investigations are required.

Clinical Features by Type

‚úã Common Warts

  • Firm, raised papules
  • Rough, cauliflower-like surface
  • Common on knuckles, knees, fingers
  • Usually asymptomatic but may be tender

ü¶∂ Plantar/Palmar Warts

  • On soles of feet or palms
  • Central dark dots (thrombosed capillaries)
  • Often painful
  • May coalesce into mosaic warts

üíÖ Periungual Warts

  • Around fingernails or toenails
  • Can be painful
  • May disturb nail growth
  • Nail biting is a risk factor

üü° Plane/Filiform Warts

  • Plane: Round, flat-topped, skin-coloured
  • Common on face, hands, shins
  • Filiform: Finger-like, may have stalk
  • More common on face/neck
üîç If Diagnostic Doubt
  • Light paring of a wart will reveal tiny black dots (thrombosed capillaries)
  • Dermoscopy may be helpful for experienced clinicians
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Be aware: bleeding from paring may lead to warts spreading

Refer if lesions are atypical

Arrange specialist assessment if the lesion does not have typical wart features.

Differential Diagnosis

What else might it be?

‚ñº

Molluscum contagiosum

Umbilicated pinkish or pearly white smooth papules, usually in clusters.

Corns/Calluses

Over friction/pressure areas. Smooth surface, intact skin markings, no black dots on paring.

Actinic keratosis

White/yellow flat or thickened papules on sun-exposed areas. Pre-cancerous.

Seborrheic keratosis

Grey-brown scaly plaques, "stuck-on" appearance, smooth or rough surface.

Bowen's disease

Non-healing, enlarging hyperkeratotic papules becoming inflamed and tender.

‚ö†Ô∏è Squamous cell carcinoma

Enlarging, non-healing hyperkeratotic papules, inflamed and tender.

‚ö†Ô∏è Malignant melanoma

Irregular outline and colour, may itch or bleed. Requires urgent referral.

Lichen planus

Usually widespread, itchy rash with purple, polygonal papules.

Management

Treatment decisions and options

‚ñº

ü§î Should I Treat?

üí°

For most people, there is a strong case for NOT treating

  • Warts usually don't cause symptoms
  • Most resolve spontaneously
  • Treatment may be prolonged with adverse effects
  • Children: warts more likely to resolve, treatment may not be tolerated

üîπ Consider treatment if:

  • The wart is painful (e.g., on soles of feet or near nails)
  • The wart is cosmetically unsightly (e.g., on hands or face)
  • The person requests treatment and the wart is persisting

‚ö†Ô∏è Facial warts - DO NOT treat in primary care

For Non-Facial Warts - Choose Treatment

Consider patient age, preference, and what has been tried already.

When to Refer

Secondary care referral criteria

‚ñº
üö® Consider Dermatology Referral If:
  • Facial wart requiring treatment
  • Diagnosis is uncertain
  • Patient is immunocompromised
  • Extensive areas affected (e.g., mosaic warts of hands/feet)
  • Persistent warts unresponsive to both salicylic acid AND cryotherapy

üè• Secondary Care Treatment Options

  • Microwave therapy
  • Photodynamic treatment

Antimitotic Treatments

Immunomodulatory

  • Topical sensitizers (DPCP, SADBE)

Other Options

  • 5-fluorouracil
