1) Initial Assessment

Gather focused history and examine to differentiate normal vs abnormal bruising and to surface red flags for bleeding disorders or safeguarding concerns.

  • History: mechanism/trauma, timing, progression; medications (anticoagulants, antiplatelets, NSAIDs, SSRIs, steroids), alcohol; systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats), diet/malnutrition, thyroid/liver/renal disease; family history of bleeding disorders.
  • Bleeding pattern red flags: mucocutaneous bleeding (epistaxis, gingival, menorrhagia); immediate bleeding after minor trauma ‚Üí platelet disorder; delayed (e.g., ~24h) post‚Äëprocedure bleeding ‚Üí coagulation factor disorder.
  • Children: bruising uncommon in non‚Äëmobile infants; document distribution/size/shape; consider accidental vs non‚Äëaccidental patterns.
  • Exam highlights: petechiae, palpable purpura (vasculitis), pallor/lymphadenopathy/hepatosplenomegaly (malignancy), stigmata of liver disease; joint swelling/tenderness (haemarthrosis); periorbital bruising (child: consider neuroblastoma).

2) Immediate Actions

  • Admit if significant active bleeding not controlled by simple measures.
  • Stabilise as needed; apply local pressure; review medications and anticoagulation status.

Safeguarding

High suspicion in children, elderly, or vulnerable adults when any of the following are present:

  • Non‚Äëmobile child with bruises; clusters; patterned injuries (hand/implement/ligature); atypical sites (ears, neck, trunk, abdomen, buttocks, genitalia); inconsistent history; presentation delay.
  • Action If suspected non‚Äëaccidental injury ‚Üí immediate referral to the appropriate agency (social care/police/NSPCC) and document concerns.

  • Same‚Äëday urgent if suspected leukaemia: unexplained petechiae, hepatosplenomegaly, or FBC suggestive of leukaemia.
  • Urgent (‚â§48h) if periorbital bruising plus abdominal mass/enlarged organ ‚Üí consider neuroblastoma.
  • Otherwise: liaise with paediatrics/paediatric haematology for venepuncture and further work‚Äëup.

Adults

  • Immediate specialist assessment if FBC suggests leukaemia.
  • Haematology referral if thrombocytopenia, abnormal coagulation, or persistent clinical suspicion despite normal initial labs (urgency per judgement).

4) Primary Care Management

  • Simple bruising (no underlying disorder): reassurance; simple analgesia (paracetamol preferred).
  • Warfarin with prolonged PT/raised INR: adjust per local protocol or liaise with anticoagulation/haematology services.
  • Drug‚Äëinduced thrombocytopenia suspected: stop culprit if safe/appropriate and monitor for resolution.

5) Investigations (guided by clinical judgement)

  • Adults with unexplained bruising/bleeding: very urgent FBC + film (‚â§48h), platelet count, coagulation (PT, aPTT, INR if on warfarin); LFTs, renal, and consider thyroid tests.
  • Children/young people: offer very urgent FBC + film (‚â§48h). Note: mild platelet/coagulation disorders can have normal screening labs.
