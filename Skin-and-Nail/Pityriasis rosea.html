What is Pityriasis Rosea?

Pityriasis rosea is an acute self-limiting skin rash mainly affecting young adults. It is characterized by distinctive, scaly, erythematous lesions, and in up to 90% of people, a herald patch typically appears within a few days to 3 weeks before a more generalized rash.

Causes

The cause remains uncertain. Evidence is strongest for a viral aetiology with herpesvirus-like particles found in 71% of pityriasis rosea lesions. Reactivation of human herpes virus (HHV)-6 and HHV-7 after an asymptomatic primary infection have been suggested as possible causes.

Prognosis

  • Skin lesions can continue to appear up to 6 weeks after initial eruption
  • Rash usually resolves within 2 weeks to a few months, but may persist up to 5 months
  • After lesions disappear, temporary hyperpigmentation or hypopigmentation may occur
  • No scarring occurs
  • Recurrence occurs in 3-4% of people after months to years

Diagnostic Tip

Suspect pityriasis rosea when a herald patch (2-5 cm) appears 1-3 weeks before generalized rash with "Christmas tree" pattern on trunk.

Key Diagnostic Features

Appearance of Lesions
  • Multiple, discrete, pink-red ("salmon colored")
  • Hypopigmented lesions more common in dark-skinned people
  • Slightly raised, circular/oval, 0.5-1 cm diameter
  • Slightly scaly with peripheral "collarette" scaling
  • Not vesicular, not on palmar/plantar surfaces
Distribution Pattern
  • Usually symmetrical rash
  • Most lesions on trunk, base of neck, proximal limbs
  • "Christmas tree" pattern on upper chest/back along cleavage lines
  • Face/scalp involvement common in children (30% facial in black children)
  • Oral lesions in ~25% of cases (more common in dark-skinned people)
Herald Patch
  • Single larger lesion (2-5 cm) appearing before generalized rash
  • Appears 1-21 days before widespread rash
  • Sharply defined, round/oval, erythematous
  • Slightly elevated "collarette" of scale at margin
  • Usually on trunk, thigh, upper arm, or neck
  • May be absent in 10% of cases

Atypical Presentations (20% of cases)

  • Inverse pityriasis rosea - face, axillae, groin predominantly affected
  • Pityriasis circinata et marginata of Vidal - few large patches in axillae/inguinal region
  • Pityriasis gigantean - larger lesions
  • Pustular, purpuric, haemorrhagic, vesicular, papular, or urticarial lesions

Patient Reassurance is Key

Explain that the rash may worsen before resolving, with new lesions appearing for up to 6 weeks. Reassure that it's self-limiting, not contagious, and usually resolves within 2-6 weeks without treatment.

Management Steps

Step 1: Information & Reassurance
  • Explain rash may worsen before resolving (new lesions up to 6 weeks)
  • Reassure: usually fades within 2-6 weeks without treatment
  • Not contagious
  • Post-inflammatory pigment changes may persist for months
  • Recurrence rare (3-4%)
  • Provide patient information leaflet from BAD website
Step 2: Symptomatic Treatment for Itch
  • Emollients - Apply regularly to soothe skin
  • Oral antihistamine (off-label) - Consider sedating type (e.g., chlorphenamine) at night if itching affects sleep
  • Topical corticosteroids - Mildly-potent (hydrocortisone 1%) or moderately-potent (betamethasone valerate 0.025%) once/twice daily for up to 4 weeks
Step 3: Referral Criteria
  • Diagnosis uncertain
  • Atypical form suspected
  • Extensive disease or severe itch not controlled by primary care treatment
  • Rash persists longer than 3 months

Most Commonly Confused With

Guttate psoriasis is the condition most frequently confused with pityriasis rosea. Also consider drug reactions, secondary syphilis, and tinea corporis.

Key Differential Diagnoses

Common Conditions
  • Guttate psoriasis - Small, round/oval pink/red scaly papules
  • Discoid eczema - Intensely itchy, coin-shaped plaques, often vesicular/crusted
  • Drug reactions - No herald patch, violet-red lesions, severe pruritus
  • Tinea corporis - Expanding annular patches with clear center, active scaly edge
Systemic/Severe Conditions to Rule Out
  • HIV seroconversion - Symmetrical maculopapular rash affecting palms/soles
  • Secondary syphilis - Non-itchy maculopapular rash, may involve palms/soles
  • Lyme disease (erythema migrans) - Single expanding lesion (≥5 cm) after tick bite
  • Pityriasis lichenoides - Red-brown papules evolving to vesicles/pustules

Emollient Safety Alert

Advise patients using paraffin or paraffin-free emollients not to smoke or go near naked flames. Clothing/bedding with dried emollient residue may be easily ignited.

Prescribing Options

For Itch: Topical Corticosteroids
  • Mildly-potent - Hydrocortisone 1% ointment/cream
  • Moderately-potent - Betamethasone valerate 0.025% or clobetasone 0.05% ointment/cream
  • Apply once or twice daily for up to 4 weeks
  • Choice depends on severity of itch
For Sleep Disturbance: Oral Antihistamines (off-label)
  • Consider sedating oral antihistamine at night if itching affects sleep
  • Example: Chlorphenamine
  • Discontinue if no relief after 2 weeks
  • Check contraindications and cautions (see CKS topic on Itch - widespread)
Supportive: Emollients
  • Regular use to soothe skin and relieve dryness
  • Paraffin-based emollients: Fire hazard warning
  • See CKS topic on Eczema - atopic for prescribing details

Immediate Referral Indicators

Refer urgently if secondary syphilis or HIV seroconversion is suspected. In pregnancy, discuss with secondary care immediately.

Red Flag Criteria

  • Pregnancy in first trimester - Associated with adverse outcomes (miscarriage, premature delivery, neonatal hypotonia)
  • No herald patch + severe pruritus - Consider drug reaction
  • Involvement of palms/soles - Consider secondary syphilis or HIV
  • Rapidly expanding single lesion - Consider erythema migrans (Lyme disease)
  • Systemic symptoms - Fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy with rash
  • Oral lesions with systemic symptoms - May indicate more serious condition
  • Atypical lesions - Pustular, haemorrhagic, or vesicular forms

When to Refer to Secondary Care

  • Diagnosis uncertain - Needs dermatology evaluation
  • Atypical form suspected - Inverse, gigantean, or other variants
  • Extensive disease - Widespread or severe involvement
  • Severe itch uncontrolled - Not responding to primary care treatment
  • Persistence >3 months - Beyond expected resolution time
  • Pregnancy concerns - First trimester or obstetric concerns
