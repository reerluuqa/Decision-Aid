Rosacea Management Algorithm

BMJ Best Practice Clinical Decision Support Tool

Last updated: March 21, 2025

Assessment & History

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Flushing: Early feature, trigger-induced vasodilation
  • Erythema: Persistent centrofacial redness (may be subtle in dark skin)
  • Papules & Pustules: Dome-shaped lesions, no comedones
  • Telangiectasia: Prominent superficial capillaries
  • Ocular Manifestations: Foreign body sensation, lid margin telangiectasia
  • Phymatous Changes: Rhinophyma (more common in men)

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors
  • Fitzpatrick skin type I or II
  • Family history (46% genetic contribution)
  • Sun/UV exposure
  • Hot baths/showers
  • Emotional stress
Moderate Risk Factors
  • Spicy foods
  • Alcohol consumption
  • Heavy exercise
  • Certain medications (amiodarone, steroids)
  • Harsh skincare products

Investigations

Test Indication Expected Result
Clinical Diagnosis Typical presentation No tests necessary
Skin Biopsy Atypical features, exclude other conditions Non-specific findings, may show Demodex mites
ANA Titre Suspect connective tissue disease Negative in rosacea
Key Point: Rosacea is a clinical diagnosis. Consider biopsy only if sarcoidosis, lupus, or other mimics are suspected.

Diagnosis & Phenotypes

Diagnostic Criteria (ROSCO 2019)

Diagnostic Phenotype

Presence of ONE of the following:

  • Fixed centrofacial erythema
  • Phymatous changes (rhinophyma)

Major Phenotypes

Presence of TWO or more:

  • Papules and pustules
  • Flushing
  • Telangiectasia
  • Ocular manifestations

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Features Badge
Acne Vulgaris Comedones present, younger age, no flushing Clinical
Seborrheic Dermatitis Greasy yellow scales, seborrheic distribution Clinical
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus ANA positive, malar rash, systemic features Test
Steroid-Induced Rosacea History of topical steroid use, atrophy, striae History
Carcinoid Syndrome Flushing with diarrhea, elevated 5-HIAA Test

Clinical Stages

1
Flushing (Pre-rosacea)

Transient erythema, trigger-induced

2
Persistent Erythema

Unremitting redness, may be subtle in dark skin

3
Papules & Pustules

Inflammatory lesions, no comedones

4
Telangiectasia & Phymatous Changes

Prominent vessels, sebaceous hyperplasia

Treatment Algorithm

Universal Measures for All Patients

Lifestyle & Skincare
  • Trigger identification through diary keeping
  • Gentle emollient cleansers, avoid harsh products (SLS, menthol)
  • Daily sunscreen (zinc oxide/titanium dioxide)
  • Yellow/green-tinted cosmetics for erythema camouflage

Phenotype-Specific Management

Fixed Centrofacial Erythema

1
Topical Alpha Agonists

Brimonidine 0.33% once daily OR Oxymetazoline 1% once daily

Most effective for persistent erythema

2
Laser Treatment Adjunct

Pulsed-dye, KTP, Nd:YAG, or intense pulsed light

Papules & Pustules

1
First-Line Topical Therapy

Azelaic acid 15% twice daily OR
Ivermectin 1% once daily OR
Metronidazole 0.75-1% twice/once daily

2
Systemic Therapy If Refractory

Doxycycline 40mg delayed-release once daily OR
Tetracycline 250-500mg twice daily

3
Refractory Cases Specialist

Isotretinoin 0.5-1mg/kg/day

Teratogenic - strict pregnancy prevention required

Phymatous Changes

1
Early Disease

Isotretinoin may improve soft changes

2
Established Fibrosis Surgical

Ablative lasers, CO₂ laser, electrosurgery, or dermabrasion

Ocular Manifestations

First-Line Measures
  • Warm compresses & lid hygiene
  • Omega-3 fatty acid supplements
  • Artificial tears/liposomal sprays
  • Avoid eye makeup, smoke, air conditioning
1
Topical Therapy

Azithromycin 1% ophthalmic OR
Ciclosporin drops

2
Systemic Therapy

Doxycycline 40mg once daily

Refer to Ophthalmology if:
  • Symptoms persist >12 months despite treatment
  • Red flags: reduced vision, pain on eye movement, nocturnal pain

Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring Requirements

Mild Disease

  • Review annually or biennially
  • Medication refills as needed
  • No significant worsening expected

Moderate-Severe Disease

  • Monthly to quarterly reviews
  • Treatment adjustments
  • Regular procedures (laser/surgery)

Specific Monitoring

Isotretinoin Therapy Essential

Monthly: Pregnancy tests (premenopausal women)
Regular: Full blood count, lipids, liver function tests

Ocular Rosacea

Every 3-6 months: Visual acuity, slit lamp exam
Immediate: If red flag symptoms develop

Prognosis

Variable Course
  • Mild disease: Controlled with trigger avoidance, minimal treatment
  • Moderate disease: Satisfactory response to topical/systemic therapy
  • Severe/refractory: May require multiple modalities, periodic flare-ups
  • Phymatous: Permanent changes if untreated; surgical correction possible
  • Ocular: Can lead to vision impairment if untreated

Rosacea is a lifelong condition with remissions and flare-ups. Emphasize realistic expectations with patients.

Patient Education Points

  • Chronic condition with no cure, but controllable
  • Strict sun protection is essential
  • Telangiectasia may appear more prominent after erythema treatment
  • Isotretinoin: mandatory contraception and pregnancy testing
  • Report ocular symptoms promptly
