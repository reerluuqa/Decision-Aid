Clinical Overview

Definition

Superficial Vein Thrombosis (SVT) is thrombus formation in a superficial vein with associated inflammation of surrounding tissue. Previously considered benign, but carries significant risk of DVT/PE.

Also known as superficial thrombophlebitis. Most commonly affects the Great Saphenous Vein (GSV) and its tributaries.

Key Facts

  • 80% of patients have pre-existing varicose veins
  • More common in women (50-70% of cases)
  • Incidence increases with age
  • GSV involved in 60-80% of cases

Diagnostic Approach

Clinical Signs

  • Painful, tender superficial vein
  • Palpable "cord-like" mass
  • Linear erythema along vein
  • Warmth over affected area
  • Localised oedema

Symptoms develop over hours to days, resolve in days to weeks.

Ultrasound Assessment

  1. Confirm SVT ‚Äî Non-compressible vein
  2. Exclude DVT ‚Äî Deep system check
  3. Measure distance ‚Äî To SFJ/SPJ (critical)
  4. Measure length ‚Äî Extent of thrombus
  5. Check for propagation ‚Äî Serial scanning

Sensitivity >95% for DVT diagnosis.

Investigations

  • Duplex ultrasonography (mandatory)
  • Full blood count
  • Coagulation screen
  • Urea & electrolytes
  • D-dimer (if DVT suspected)
  • Thrombophilia screen (if recurrent/idiopathic)
  • Malignancy workup (if indicated)
  • CRP/ESR (if inflammatory cause)

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

Up to 80% of SVT patients have pre-existing varicose veins. Venous stasis in dilated veins is the primary pathogenic factor.

Factor V Leiden (most common), prothrombin mutation, protein C/S deficiency, antithrombin deficiency, antiphospholipid antibodies.

Patient Categories & Management

Criteria

  • Thrombus < 5cm in length
  • > 3cm from SFJ/SPJ
  • Only tributary varicosities
  • No high-risk features

Management

NSAIDs: Oral or topical for symptom relief

Compression: Class I-II stockings

Ambulation: Encourage walking

Monitor for propagation. Consider anticoagulation if high-risk features present.

Criteria

  • Thrombus ‚â• 5cm in length
  • Within axial superficial vein (GSV/SSV/AASV)
  • > 3cm but ‚â§ 5cm from SFJ/SPJ
  • Multiple risk factors for VTE

Management

Anticoagulation: 45 days prophylactic dose

  • Fondaparinux 2.5mg SC OD (first-line)
  • Intermediate-dose LMWH
  • Rivaroxaban 10mg PO OD (if available)

ESVS 2023: Consider 3 months for high-risk features (extensive SVT, axial involvement, multiple sites).

When Anticoagulation Contraindicated

  • Active bleeding
  • Severe thrombocytopenia
  • Recent major surgery
  • High bleeding risk

Management

Surgical ligation of SFJ or SPJ

  • Refer to vascular surgery
  • Consider IVC filter if extensive propagation risk
  • Restart anticoagulation when contraindication resolves

Pregnancy Considerations

  • Anticoagulate ALL pregnant women with SVT
  • Persistent thrombotic risk throughout pregnancy
  • Continue for at least 6 weeks postnatal

Management

LMWH: Prophylactic or intermediate dose

  • Avoid DOACs and warfarin in pregnancy
  • Compression stockings + ambulation
  • Consider ligation if anticoagulation truly contraindicated

Safety Netting - Return Advice

Advise patients to return immediately if:

  • Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
  • Chest pain or tightness
  • Coughing up blood
  • Sudden collapse or fainting
  • Leg becomes pale, cold or significantly more swollen
  • Redness spreads rapidly beyond original area
  • Severe pain not controlled by analgesia
