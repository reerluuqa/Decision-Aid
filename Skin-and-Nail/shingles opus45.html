Clinical diagnosis & differential considerations

‚ñº
‚úÖ Clinical Diagnosis Primary Care
‚ñº
‚ÑπÔ∏è

Diagnose on Clinical Features

Shingles is diagnosed on the basis of typical clinical features. Investigations are not usually required in primary care.

üî¨ When Investigations May Be Needed

If there is diagnostic uncertainty, refer or seek specialist advice. Viral culture, PCR, or direct fluorescent antibody testing may be used in secondary care.

üìã Key Diagnostic Features
‚ñº
  • Unilateral distribution (does not cross midline)
  • Dermatomal pattern (follows single nerve root)
  • Prodromal pain preceding rash by 2-3 days
  • Vesicular rash on erythematous base
  • T1-L2 dermatomes most commonly affected in immunocompetent people
‚ö†Ô∏è Atypical Presentations

Older people: Rash may not be vesicular
Immunocompromised: Rash may be severe or long-lasting
Rare: Zoster sine herpete (pain without rash)

‚ñº

Admit to Hospital or Seek Immediate Specialist Advice If:

  • Serious complications suspected (meningitis, encephalitis, myelitis)
  • Ophthalmic distribution of trigeminal nerve, especially with:
    • Hutchinson's sign (rash on tip, side, or root of nose)
    • Eye pain or photophobia
    • Reduced corneal sensitivity
    • Visual impairment
    • Unexplained red eye
  • Head and neck affected, especially in elderly
  • Haemorrhagic/necrotic lesions, multisegmental involvement
  • Aberrant vesicles/satellite lesions, mucosal involvement
  • Generalized herpes zoster
  • Severely immunosuppressed patient
  • Immunocompromised child with shingles
  • Signs of visceral or CNS involvement

Step 4: Follow-up & Prevention

Patient advice, monitoring & vaccination

‚ñº
üìã Patient Information & Advice Essential
‚ñº
‚ö°

Infectivity

People with active lesions can transmit varicella-zoster virus to susceptible people (who haven't had chickenpox) to cause chickenpox. Infectious until all vesicles have crusted over (approximately 7 days after rash onset).

Advise Patients To:

  • Avoid contact with high-risk people (immunocompromised, newborns, elderly, pregnant women without chickenpox immunity) until rash crusts over
  • Avoid sharing clothes and towels
  • Avoid touching or scratching the rash
  • Wash hands frequently
  • Wear loose-fitting clothes to reduce irritation
  • Cover lesions not under clothes while rash is weeping
  • Avoid topical antibiotics and adhesive dressings
  • Keep rash clean and dry to reduce bacterial superinfection risk
  • Avoid work/school/daycare if rash is weeping and cannot be covered
üå°Ô∏è When to Seek Medical Advice

If temperature increases, as this may indicate bacterial superinfection

‚ö†Ô∏è Complications to Monitor Awareness
‚ñº
  • Post-herpetic neuralgia ‚Äî pain persisting >90 days after rash onset (most common complication)
  • Cardiovascular ‚Äî increased risk of stroke, TIA, MI
  • CNS complications ‚Äî encephalitis, meningoencephalitis, myelitis
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus ‚Äî keratitis, corneal ulceration, blindness
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome ‚Äî facial paralysis, hearing/vestibular symptoms
  • Skin changes ‚Äî scarring, pigmentation changes
  • Superinfection ‚Äî cellulitis, rarely necrotizing fasciitis, sepsis
  • Systemic dissemination ‚Äî pneumonia, hepatitis (especially in severely immunocompromised)
