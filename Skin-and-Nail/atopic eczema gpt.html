Key Practice Points

  • Step up for flares, step down once controlled.
  • Continue topical steroids 48h after control of a flare.
  • Face/flexures: prefer mild; max 5 days if moderate needed.
  • Emollients: liberal & frequent, even when clear.
  • Suspect eczema herpeticum → same-day specialist/admit.
  • Secondary bacterial infection: often no antibiotics if not systemically unwell.

Patient Education

Flare recognition, infection signs, trigger avoidance, correct use of emollients, and psychosocial impact.

Assessment

Severity & Infection Check

CategoryDefinition
Mild Dry skin with infrequent itch ± small areas of redness.
Moderate Dry skin, frequent itching and redness ± excoriation/localised thickening.
Severe Widespread dry skin, incessant itch and redness ± bleeding/oozing/cracking/pigment change.
Infected Weeping, crusts, pustules, fever/malaise or rapid worsening. Consider secondary bacterial infection.
Psychosocial impact: assess effect on daily activities, sleep, and mood at each visit; consider validated tools (IDQoL, CDLQI, DFI).

Foundations

General Measures & Advice

  • Emollients: use generously and frequently, including when skin is clear (steroid-sparing).
  • Trigger avoidance: soaps/detergents (use substitutes), heat, synthetic fabrics; keep rooms cool.
  • Do not alter diet unless advised by specialist. Encourage evidence-based therapy.
  • Complementary therapies are not recommended; potential harms and contaminants.
  • Educate on flare recognition, infected eczema signs, and eczema herpeticum (emergency).
Escalation

Referral & Follow-up

  • Urgent / same-day: suspected eczema herpeticum; uncontrolled severe eczema despite optimal topical therapy; infected eczema not responding within 2 weeks.
  • Routine dermatology: diagnostic uncertainty, ≥1–2 flares/month despite treatment, recurrent infections, non-responsive facial eczema, need for bandaging guidance, suspected contact allergy, significant psychosocial impact.
  • Allergy/Dietitian: suspected cow’s milk protein allergy in infants (consider 6–8 week trial of hydrolyzed/amino-acid formula in <6 months if uncontrolled by emollient + mild steroid; not goat/sheep/partially hydrolyzed formulas; consider soya ≥6 months with advice).
  • Clinical Psychology: quality of life impaired despite skin control.
  • Reviews: emollients annually; maintenance steroids 3–6 months; non-sedating antihistamines every 3 months.
Face & Flexures: Prefer mild steroids; if moderate needed, limit to ≤5 days.
Maintenance: Consider intermittent/step-down steroids on frequent-flare areas (not face/genitals/axillae).
Children: Avoid potent steroids in <12 months; avoid very potent in any child without specialist advice.
