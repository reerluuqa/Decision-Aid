Lichen Sclerosus Management

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm

Based on BMJ Best Practice Guidelines (Sep 2024) | Last updated: Dec 2024

Assessment & History

Adult Women

Key Symptoms
  • Pruritus (especially nocturnal)
  • Pain/burning/discomfort
  • Dyspareunia (68% of patients)
  • Tearing with intercourse (63%)
  • Dysuria
Examination Findings
  • White, atrophic plaques
  • Purpura/ecchymoses (pathognomonic)
  • Crinkly/fine wrinkling
  • Scarring: labial fusion, clitoral hood fusion
  • Narrowing of introitus

Adult Men

Key Symptoms
  • Itching/burning of penis
  • Tight foreskin (phimosis)
  • Pain with erections
  • Changes in urinary stream
  • Dysuria
Examination Findings
  • White, atrophic patches on glans/corona
  • Purpura
  • Meatal stenosis
  • Phimosis/adhesions
  • Almost exclusive to uncircumcised men

Children & Adolescents

Girls: Similar to adult women + constipation, painful defecation, fear of voiding
Boys: Often asymptomatic, present with phimosis (12-100% incidence)

Risk Factors Important

Strong Risk Factors
  • Female sex (3-10x more common)
  • Uncircumcised state (men)
  • Autoimmune diseases (thyroid disorders)
Moderate Risk Factors
  • Family history (8-12% have first-degree relative)
  • Obesity/metabolic syndrome
  • Urinary incontinence (men)
Red Flags
  • Rapidly enlarging plaque, ulcer, or nodule (suspect SCC)
  • Non-healing hyperkeratotic areas despite treatment
  • Suspicious for child abuse (must evaluate carefully)

Diagnosis & Investigations

Clinical Diagnosis

Usually Clinical
Characteristic history and examination findings are usually sufficient. No tests are typically necessary.

When to Biopsy Critical

  • Concern for malignancy - rapid change, ulceration, nodule
  • Failure of initial treatment after 8-12 weeks
  • Diagnostic uncertainty or atypical presentation
  • Non-specific findings (only erythema/fissures without classic features)

Note: Biopsy generally NOT taken in children unless refractory or diagnostic doubt. Refer to pediatric specialist.

Histology

Typical Findings:
  • Atrophic epidermis with effaced rete ridges
  • Dense lymphocytic band in dermis
  • Collagen homogenization
  • CD34 expression absent (helps differentiate from lichen planus)

Key Differentials Important

Condition Distinguishing Features Diagnostic Test
Vitiligo No scarring, smooth texture, asymptomatic Wood's lamp, biopsy (Fontana-Masson)
Lichen Planus Erosions, Wickham's striae, vaginal involvement Biopsy (sawtooth rete ridges, CD34 present)
Candidiasis Bright red, satellite papules, discharge KOH prep, culture
Psoriasis No architectural changes, extensor surfaces Biopsy (psoriasiform hyperplasia)
Morphea More indurated, extragenital Biopsy (deeper involvement)
Diagnostic Delay

Average 4-year delay between symptom onset and diagnosis. 66% initially receive alternative diagnosis (49% misdiagnosed as yeast infection).

Treatment Algorithm

First-Line Treatment Standard

Very-High Potency Topical Corticosteroids
Options: Clobetasol 0.05%, Ulobetasol 0.05%, Betamethasone dipropionate augmented 0.05%
Dosing: Twice daily for up to 2 weeks (max 50g/week)
Formulation: Ointment preferred (less stinging, better penetration)
Initial Course: Daily treatment for 2-4 weeks, then taper to alternate days for 2-3 months total. Most improve within weeks but continue until signs resolve.

Lifestyle & Supportive Care

Avoid Irritants
  • No fragranced products
  • Clean with water only (or gentle soap)
  • No internal cleansers (women)
Emollients
  • White petrolatum recommended
  • Use as soap substitute
  • Don't apply simultaneously with steroids

Men & Boys

Circumcision

Early circumcision is often CURATIVE. Consider trial of topical steroids first, but circumcision is definitive treatment for refractory cases or urethral involvement.

Refractory Disease Second-Line

Definition: Persistent signs/symptoms after 8-12 weeks of appropriate therapy

Women/Girls Options:
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus)
  • Intralesional triamcinolone
  • Oral retinoids (isotretinoin, acitretin) Teratogenic
  • Methotrexate
Men/Boys Options:
  • Circumcision (usually curative)
  • Frenuloplasty + triamcinolone
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
  • Systemic therapy (retinoids, methotrexate)
Calcineurin Inhibitors

Theoretical malignancy risk (not established). Limit to affected areas, avoid long-term use when possible. Not recommended in pregnancy/breastfeeding or children <2 years.

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

Safe Options
  • Topical corticosteroids: Safe in limited amounts (<50g/week)
  • Intravaginal estrogen: For breastfeeding-related dryness
  • Symptoms often improve during pregnancy

Avoid: Oral retinoids (teratogenic), topical calcineurin inhibitors (use only if benefits outweigh risks)

Dosing Guidance

Fingertip Unit (FTU) for Anogenital Area:
  • Adult female: Lentil or half pea-sized amount
  • Apply to affected area(s) only
  • Demonstrate using mirror/photographs
  • Address steroid phobia with reassurance

Follow-up & Monitoring

Surveillance Schedule

Initial Phase:
  • Review every 1-3 months until stable
  • Assess treatment response
  • Adjust therapy as needed
Maintenance:
  • Every 6-12 months once stable
  • Monitor for malignant transformation
  • Assess scarring/functional impact

Maintenance Therapy Recommended

Indication: Once disease control achieved, consider maintenance to prevent flares and reduce scarring/SCC risk.
Regimen: Minimum potency/frequency needed to control symptoms
• Very-high potency: 3x weekly OR
• High-potency: Daily OR
• Lower potency: As needed

Duration: Lifelong for anogenital LS. Extragenital LS usually doesn't require maintenance unless symptomatic.

Malignancy Surveillance Critical

Risk: ~5% of women with anogenital LS develop SCC

Risk minimized by good disease control and maintenance therapy. One study showed 0 SCC cases in treatment-compliant patients vs 7 cases in non-compliant patients.

Suspicious Features (Biopsy):
  • Non-healing hyperkeratotic areas
  • Erosions or ulcerations
  • Firm papules/nodules
  • Texture/color change persisting despite therapy

Complications Preventable

High Risk:
  • Vulvar architectural changes/scarring
  • Urinary retention/incontinence
Medium Risk:
  • Penile structural changes (phimosis)
  • Dyspareunia/sexual dysfunction (59% of women)
Malignancy:
  • SCC, dVIN, PeIN
  • 800-fold increased risk in men

When to Refer

Referral Indications:
  • Diagnostic uncertainty or atypical features
  • Refractory disease after 8-12 weeks
  • Functional impairment (sexual, urinary)
  • Suspicion for malignancy
  • Psychological distress
  • Pediatric cases (specialist input)
  • Extragenital disease requiring specialist management

Quality of Life

Psychosocial Impact: Anxiety, depression, reduced self-esteem, sexual dysfunction, difficulty walking/sitting. Screen and refer for psychological support as needed.
Support Resources: Lichen Sclerosus Support Network, ISSVD. Provide education materials and reassure about chronic but manageable nature.

Special Populations

Children: May have more chronic course than previously thought. 58% of girls still symptomatic after menarche, 26% have persistent signs. Long-term follow-up essential.

Autoimmune Screening: Consider screening for thyroid disease and other autoimmune conditions given strong association.

This algorithm is for educational purposes and does not replace clinical judgment. Always consult current guidelines and specialist opinion when needed.
