Assessment of Pustular Rash

⚠️ LIFE-THREATENING CONDITIONS - IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

Generalised Pustular Psoriasis (Von Zumbusch)

Sudden eruption within hours of sterile pustules on erythematous base. Can be fatal. Immediate specialist referral required.

DRESS Syndrome

Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms. 10% mortality rate from liver damage. Stop all non-essential drugs immediately.

Disseminated Candidiasis

40% mortality in premature infants. Risk factors: broad-spectrum antibiotics, corticosteroids, prematurity. Urgent antifungals required.

Neonatal HSV Disseminated

Systemic involvement in newborns. Viraemia spreads to multiple organs. Immediate IV aciclovir required.

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS

ABCDE Survey

Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure

IV Access + Fluids

Establish IV access, consider fluid resuscitation

Septic Workup

Blood cultures, lactate, CBC, CRP, consider LP

Isolation

Contact precautions for suspected infections

KEY HISTORY QUESTIONS
  • Onset & Duration

    Minutes/hours = fire ants, GPP. Days = scabies, candidiasis. Weeks = DRESS, syphilis.

  • Drug History

    New medications in past 8 weeks? AGEP (antibiotics, terbinafine), DRESS (anticonvulsants, allopurinol), EGFR inhibitors.

  • Systemic Symptoms

    Fever, malaise, arthralgias? Consider GPP, DRESS, disseminated infection.

  • Exposure History

    Contaminated water (Pseudomonas), animals (Orf), sexual contacts (syphilis), travel (mpox).

  • Past Medical History

    Psoriasis, IBD, immunosuppression, atopy, endocrine disorders.

  • Family History

    Psoriasis in relatives suggests GPP. Sick contacts suggest scabies, viral infections.

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION
  • Distribution Pattern

    Follicular = folliculitis, acne. Flexural = APF. Acral = IA, ACH. Facial = rosacea, perioral dermatitis.

  • Lesion Characteristics

    Size, arrangement, presence of crusting, Nikolsky sign, mucosal involvement.

  • Systemic Signs

    Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, joint swelling, oral/genital ulcers.

  • Vital Signs

    Fever, tachycardia, hypotension (sepsis), tachypnea.

  • Special Signs

    Behçet's: oral + genital ulcers + uveitis. Reactive arthritis: keratoderma blennorrhagicum.

ONSET TIMELINE
MINUTES
Fire ant bites - immediate burning/itching, sterile pustule develops within 20 minutes, lasts several days
HOURS
Generalised Pustular Psoriasis - sudden eruption within hours of sterile pustules
DAYS
Infantile scabies - generalized pruritus, characteristic burrows
Congenital candidiasis - presents at birth or within first days
AGEP - sudden eruption 1-2 weeks post-drug exposure
WEEKS
DRESS - onset within 8 weeks of drug initiation
Secondary syphilis - lesions 2-12 weeks after primary chancre
Corticosteroid-induced eruption - weeks to months after starting steroids
VARIABLE
HSV - primary (2-20 days incubation) or recurrent (triggered by stress, UV, fever)
Orf - incubation <4 weeks, resolves 6 weeks after onset
DRUG ASSOCIATIONS

AGEP (Acute Generalised Exanthematous Pustulosis)

Typically occurs within 1-2 weeks of drug exposure. Rash lasts 6-31 days followed by desquamation.

  • Antibiotics: aminopenicillins, macrolides, quinolones, sulphonamides
  • Antifungals: terbinafine
  • Cardiovascular: diltiazem, calcium channel blockers
  • Analgesics: paracetamol (acetaminophen)
  • Hormonal: progesterone preparations
  • Other: mercury

DRESS Syndrome

Onset within 3-8 weeks. 10% mortality, mainly from liver failure.

  • Anticonvulsants: carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, lamotrigine
  • Allopurinol (most common non-anticonvulsant cause)
  • Antibiotics: sulphonamides, dapsone, minocycline
  • Anti-inflammatories: NSAIDs
  • Cardiovascular: captopril, calcium channel blockers, mexiletine
  • Other: gold salts, sulfasalazine, zonisamide, fluoxetine

EGFR Inhibitor Rash

Sterile follicular and pustular rash appearing 1-3 weeks after starting treatment. Can be a marker of therapeutic efficacy.

  • Drugs: cetuximab, erlotinib, gefitinib
  • Onset: Weeks 1-3, peaks weeks 3-5
  • Resolution: 4 weeks after drug withdrawal
  • Frequency: 60-80% of treated patients

Drug-Induced Psoriasis/GPP

Can trigger GPP or exacerbate existing psoriasis.

  • Withdrawal: systemic corticosteroids (most common trigger)
  • Other triggers: coal tar, iodides, minocycline
  • In pregnancy: impetigo herpetiformis (GPP variant)
TREATMENT GUIDELINES
EMERGENCY TREATMENTS

Generalised Pustular Psoriasis

Treatment: Acitretin (with or without phototherapy), Ciclosporin, or Methotrexate. Initiate promptly under specialist guidance.

DRESS Syndrome

Treatment: Stop all non-essential drugs immediately. Corticosteroids for severe symptoms. Supportive care. Monitor for hepatitis (can evolve for weeks).

Disseminated Candidiasis

Treatment: Early antifungal treatment critical. Options: Amphotericin B, Itraconazole, Fluconazole, Voriconazole, Caspofungin. Do not delay.

Neonatal HSV Disseminated

Treatment: Immediate IV Aciclovir. Foscarnet for Aciclovir-resistant HSV. Urgent treatment essential.

Impetigo Herpetiformis

Treatment: Systemic steroids. Requires joint obstetric and dermatology management. Fetal morbidity risk - close monitoring required.

INFECTIOUS TREATMENTS
  • Impetigo

    Topical: mupirocin 2% ointment. Oral: flucloxacillin 500mg QDS for 7 days (or erythromycin if penicillin allergic).

  • Folliculitis (Bacterial)

    Topical antibiotics (mupirocin, clindamycin), warm compresses. Oral antibiotics if extensive.

  • Pseudomonas Folliculitis

    Usually self-limiting. Consider ciprofloxacin if severe or in immunocompromised.

  • Secondary Syphilis

    Benzathine penicillin G 2.4MU IM single dose (or 3 weekly doses for neurosyphilis).

  • HSV (Immunocompetent)

    Oral aciclovir 400mg TDS for 5-10 days (or valacyclovir 1g BD).

  • HSV (Immunocompromised/Severe)

    IV aciclovir 5-10mg/kg every 8 hours for 7-14 days.

  • Candidal Infection (Localized)

    Topical azoles (clotrimazole, miconazole). Oral fluconazole if extensive.

  • Dermatophytosis (Tinea)

    Topical: terbinafine, clotrimazole. Oral: terbinafine 250mg daily for 2-6 weeks.

  • Scabies

    Permethrin 5% cream (apply neck to feet, wash off after 8-14 hours). Oral ivermectin if needed.

  • Orf

    Self-limiting (6 weeks). Supportive care. Usually no specific antiviral treatment needed.

SPECIAL POPULATIONS & RISK FACTORS
NEONATES & INFANTS
  • Invasive Candidiasis Risk Factors

    Prematurity (especially <1000g), vaginal delivery, broad-spectrum antibiotics, postnatal corticosteroids, parenteral nutrition, central venous catheters, necrotising enterocolitis.

  • Congenital vs Neonatal Candidiasis

    Congenital: presents at birth/first hours (in utero acquisition). Neonatal: develops after first few days (acquired during/after delivery).

  • Neonatal HSV Risk

    Most infections from HSV-1. Disseminated disease risk in immunocompromised and premature infants.

  • Infantile Scabies

    Can affect entire body including head/scalp (unlike adults). Look for burrows on palms/soles. Family members often affected.

  • Erythema Toxicum Neonatorum

    50-70% of healthy term infants, self-limiting within weeks, benign condition.

  • Neonatal Cephalic Pustulosis

    Face/scalp, onset 2-3 weeks, resolves spontaneously, differentiated from infantile acne by lack of comedones.

IMMUNOCOMPROMISED HOSTS
  • Eosinophilic Folliculitis (Ofuji's Disease)

    Associated with HIV/AIDS and other immunosuppression. Characterised by eosinophilic spongiosis and pustulosis.

  • Disseminated HSV

    Systemic involvement with viraemia, spread to multiple organs. Requires immediate IV aciclovir.

  • Gram-Negative Folliculitis

    After prolonged tetracycline use for acne. Consider if no improvement after 3-6 months.

  • Herpes Zoster/HSV Folliculitis

    Rare manifestation, consider in folliculitis refractory to antibiotics/antifungals.

  • Pityrosporum Folliculitis

    Associated with immunosuppression and chemotherapy. More prevalent in hot/humid climates.

  • Recalcitrant Candida

    May indicate underlying immunodeficiency (congenital or acquired).
