Recognition

1) When to suspect fungal scalp infection

Clinical features vary with organism, hair invasion type, and inflammatory response.

Non‑inflammatory

  • Diffuse scale/itch.
  • Single/multiple asymmetrical patchy alopecia.
  • Black dots (broken hair stubs within follicles) — typical with Trichophyton tonsurans.

Inflammatory

  • Erythema, scattered pustules, crusting.
  • Painful boggy mass with thick crust (kerion).
  • Post‑auricular/cervical lymphadenopathy; risk of scarring alopecia.
Epidemiology
Mostly pre‑pubertal children (peak 3–7y); higher prevalence in Afro‑Caribbean children.
Transmission
Close contact or fomites (hats, combs). Consider pets (esp. Microsporum canis).
Kerion
Suspected kerion ⇒ urgent dermatology referral.
History · Exam · Labs

2) Assessment & Sampling

History

  • Duration; itch/pain; prior OTC/antifungals.
  • Contacts with similar scalp changes; school cohort.
  • Animal exposure; household pets.
  • Comorbidities incl. immunosuppression.

Examination

  • Extent/severity, kerion?
  • Eyebrows/eyelashes involved?
  • Check for dermatophyte at other sites (nails, body, feet).

Sampling (microscopy & culture)

  • Wipe off creams; scrape edge of lesions for hairs/scale with blunt scalpel.
  • Collect ≥5 mm² of scale/hair into folded dark paper; label well.
  • Keep at room temp (do not refrigerate).
  • Request form: treatments used, animal contacts, travel.
  • Turnaround: microscopy 1–2 days; culture 2–3 weeks.
  • If sampling intolerable or carrier suspected: scalp brush/cytobrush for culture.
  • Dermoscopy can aid diagnosis/monitoring (if trained).
Rule‑outs

3) Differentials

  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis (greasy scale, little hair loss; face/chest involvement).
  • Atopic eczema (itch/scale; hair loss less common).
  • Alopecia areata (sudden discrete patches; minimal scale; “exclamation mark” hairs).
  • Traction alopecia (marginal/temporal thinning related to hairstyles).
  • Trichotillomania (irregular, incomplete hair loss; broken hairs of varying length; brows/lashes may be involved).
  • Psoriasis (silver scale plaques; nails/other sites).
  • Discoid lupus erythematosus (scaly plaques; pigment change; scarring alopecia).
  • Lichen planus (very itchy vertex; perifollicular erythema/scale; scarring patches).
  • Folliculitis or scalp abscess (pustules; tender collection; painful hair pluck).
Action Pathway

4) Treatment Algorithm (at a glance)

Suspected tinea capitis

  • Initiate self‑care / transmission reduction (see below).
  • Take scalp scrapings/hairs for microscopy & culture.
  • Kerion? → Urgent dermatology referral
▼

Start systemic therapy (if strong suspicion or confirmed)

  • Adults: start oral antifungal if confirmed or highly suggestive.
  • Children: start in primary care if confident; otherwise seek paediatric derm advice.
  • Empiric choice (pending culture):
    • Urban (T. tonsurans likely) → Terbinafine × 4 weeks.
    • Rural (Microsporum likely) → Griseofulvin × 4–8 weeks.
  • Add ketoconazole shampoo 2–3× weekly for 2–4 weeks to reduce transmission.
    Selenium sulfide shampoo discontinued
▼

Culture result guides therapy

  • Trichophyton → continue/switch to terbinafine.
  • Microsporum → continue/switch to griseofulvin.
  • If intolerance/contraindication → consider itraconazole (off‑label) × 4 weeks.
▼

Review 4–8 weeks after completing oral therapy

  • Hair regrowth ⇒ likely clearance.
  • Persistent/recurrent signs ⇒ assess adherence, re‑exposure, interactions, immunosuppression, alternative diagnosis; repeat microscopy/culture.
  • If improving but culture positive → consider extend treatment 2–4 weeks, then reassess.
  • Manage complications (secondary infection, Id reaction).
School/Nursery: attend as normal once appropriate treatment is started.
Dosing · Cautions · Interactions

5) Prescribing at a Glance (Systemic)

Terbinafine (tablets only)

Age/WeightDose · Duration
1–17 y, 10–19 kg62.5 mg once daily × 4 weeks
1–17 y, 20–39 kg125 mg once daily × 4 weeks
≥40 kg or Adults250 mg once daily × 4 weeks
Contraindications/Cautions
  • Hepatic disease: avoid; monitor LFTs; stop if deranged.
  • Severe renal impairment (avoid; limited data).
  • Autoimmune disease (LE‑like effect), psoriasis (may worsen).
Key adverse effects
  • GI upset, headache; taste disturbance.
  • Rash/urticaria; rare SJS/TEN; alopecia.
  • Rare: hepatotoxicity, blood dyscrasias — counsel red‑flag symptoms.
Terbinafine — notable interactions
  • ↓ Analgesia with codeine.
  • Rifampicin ↓ terbinafine levels.
  • Tamoxifen — avoid (reduced active metabolite).
  • ↑ Levels of: flecainide/mexiletine/propafenone, SSRIs (paroxetine/sertraline), TCAs, β‑blockers, aripiprazole/risperidone, dextromethorphan, MAOI‑B (selegiline), tramadol (↑ AEs), etc.
  • Azoles (fluconazole/ketoconazole) & amiodarone may ↑ terbinafine levels.

Griseofulvin

AgeUsual Dose · Duration
1 month–11 y10 mg/kg daily (max 500 mg); up to 20 mg/kg in severe; reduce dose when response. Once daily or divided.
12–17 y500 mg daily; up to 1 g if severe; reduce when response. Once daily or divided.
Adults1000 mg OD or 500 mg BD × 4–8 weeks
Contraindications/Cautions
  • Acute porphyria; severe liver disease; SLE (risk flare).
Key adverse effects
  • GI upset — take after high‑fat meal to ↑ absorption & ↓ AEs.
  • Hepatic LFT changes, cholestasis, hepatitis.
  • Neuro (headache, dizziness), photosensitivity, rash (incl. TEN).
  • May impair skilled tasks; alcohol enhances effects.
Griseofulvin — notable interactions
  • Alcohol: possible disulfiram‑like reaction.
  • Hormonal contraception: efficacy may be reduced — avoid COC/POP/implant/ulipristal during & 28 days after; if COC preferred, consider ≥50 µg EE with continuous/tricycling regimen + 4‑day break.
  • Warfarin: ↓ effect — monitor INR, adjust dose.
  • Enzyme inducers (phenobarbital, primidone) ↓ griseofulvin levels.

Itraconazole (off‑label)

AgeDose · Duration
1–17 y3–5 mg/kg OD (max 200 mg) × 4 weeks
Adults100 mg OD × 4 weeks
Contraindications/Cautions
  • Acute porphyria.
  • Heart failure or ventricular dysfunction (negative inotrope).
  • High HF risk (e.g., CCBs), hepatic disease (monitor), renal impairment, immunocompromise.
  • Multiple significant CYP3A4 interactions (see examples).
Key adverse effects
  • GI upset; rare hepatotoxicity; headache/dizziness; rash, alopecia; oedema; menstrual disorders.
Itraconazole — notable interactions (selected)
  • Levels ↓ by: carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, rifampicin/rifabutin; St John’s wort (avoid).
  • Levels ↑ by: macrolides (clarithromycin/erythromycin), HIV protease inhibitors.
  • Increases levels of many agents: aliskiren; antipsychotics (aripiprazole, quetiapine [contraindicated], risperidone); PDE‑5 inhibitors (avoid avanafil; ↓ dose sildenafil/vardenafil); benzodiazepines (alprazolam, triazolam, midazolam); CCBs (amlodipine, verapamil); colchicine; corticosteroids (avoid budesonide/dexamethasone); digoxin (often ↓ by 50–75%); domperidone (avoid); eplerenone (contraind.); ergot alkaloids (contraind.); ivabradine (contraind.); oral anticoagulants; pimozide/quinidine/ranolazine (QT/torsades risk; generally contraind.).
Adjuncts

6) Topical Adjuncts (off‑label for tinea capitis)

Ketoconazole 2% shampoo

  • Licensed ≥12 y (for dandruff/seb derm); use 2–3× weekly for 2–4 wks to reduce spore shedding.
  • Avoid eye/mucosal contact. No major drug interactions reported.

Imidazole creams (younger children)

  • Clotrimazole 1% or Miconazole 2% or Econazole 1% — apply daily to affected areas (e.g., 1 week for transmission reduction alongside systemic therapy).
  • Avoid eye/mucosal contact.
Important
  • Selenium sulfide shampoo — no longer available.
  • Clotrimazole ↔ tacrolimus interaction (↑ tacrolimus levels).
  • Miconazole/econazole topical: clinically significant anticoagulant interactions are rare, but consider monitoring if on oral anticoagulants.

Self‑care & Transmission Reduction

  • Soften crusts (moistened dressings) then gently tease away.
  • Discard/disinfect hats, scarves, hairbrushes, combs, pillows, blankets, scissors.
  • Do not share towels; frequent washing.
  • Parents/carers inspect scalps of siblings/household regularly.
  • Suspect pet source? Arrange veterinary assessment/treatment.

School/Nursery

  • Attend as normal once appropriate treatment started.
Monitor & Manage

7) Complications

  • Secondary bacterial infection (see cellulitis/impetigo guidance).
  • Kerion → painful, boggy mass; risk scarring alopecia; urgent referral.
  • Dermatophytid (Id) reaction — pruritic papular/vesicular eruption (ears, trunk/limbs), often soon after starting oral therapy. Continue antifungal; consider topical corticosteroid for symptoms.
  • Skin pigmentation changes; rare erythema nodosum.
Review & Refer

8) Follow‑up & Referral

Review

  • Reassess 4–8 weeks after completing oral therapy.
  • Hair regrowth suggests clearance.
  • Persistent/recurrent: check adherence, drug interactions, multiple organisms, reinfection from contacts, immunocompromise, alternative dx; repeat mycology.
  • Ongoing improvement but positive culture: consider extend current therapy 2–4 weeks.

Refer to Dermatology

  • Urgent: suspected kerion.
  • Child needs systemic therapy and primary care lacks experience.
  • Severe/extensive or recurrent disease.
  • Uncertain diagnosis or treatment failure.
  • Severe Id reaction not settling in primary care.
  • Immunocompromised host (per clinical judgement).
Public Health

9) Management of Contacts

  • Known contact of a confirmed case → arrange scalp culture (brush method acceptable) to identify infection or carriers.
  • Symptomatic + confirmed: self‑care + oral antifungal.
  • Asymptomatic carrier: seek dermatology advice for management/follow‑up.
    • Heavy growth/high spore count → may need oral therapy.
    • Low spore load → topical ketoconazole shampoo may suffice.
Notes

Additional Notes

Common UK organisms
T. tonsurans (anthropophilic) and M. canis (zoophilic).
Prognosis
Generally excellent with effective therapy; risk of complications without access to care or with comorbidities.

This quick reference is intended for clinicians as an aide‑mémoire in primary care. Consider local microbiology/dermatology pathways where applicable.
