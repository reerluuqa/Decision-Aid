When to Suspect Post-Herpetic Neuralgia

Suspect in patients with chronic pain following a shingles rash:

Pain characteristics: Aching, burning, itching, lancinating, or sharp pain in a dermatomal distribution
Timing: Pain persisting ≥3 months after onset or healing of shingles rash
Clinical note: Patient may not remember or associate current pain with previous shingles episode

Examination Findings

  • Deficits in thermal, tactile, pinprick, or vibration sensation
  • Areas of anaesthesia
  • Hyperalgesia (excessive pain to noxious stimuli)
  • Allodynia (pain from non-painful stimuli like light touch)

Critical Red Flags

Warning Signs

  • Pain spreading beyond dermatomal area
  • New neurological deficits
  • Fever or signs of infection
  • Rapid progression of symptoms
  • Immunocompromised status
  • Ophthalmic involvement (requires urgent referral)

Important Considerations

  • Consider comorbidities: diabetes, respiratory disease, autoimmune conditions
  • Age >60 years significantly increases risk
  • Severe acute zoster pain predicts PHN development
  • Prodromal pain before rash increases risk

Immediate Action Required

Ophthalmic involvement (V1 distribution) requires urgent same-day ophthalmology referral to prevent vision loss.

  • Look for: Pain around eye, forehead, or nose
  • Check for: Nasal vesicles (Hutchinson's sign)
  • Assess: Visual acuity, corneal sensation, ocular motility

Management Pathway

First-Line Approach

Start with patient education and conservative measures before escalating to pharmacotherapy:

  • Provide written information on PHN nature and prognosis
  • Recommend cotton/silk clothing to reduce irritation
  • Suggest protective coverings for sensitive areas
  • Consider cold packs (unless allodynia worsens)
  • Assess impact on daily activities and mood

Pharmacological Management

Mild pain: Paracetamol ± codeine (though often insufficient alone)
Moderate-severe pain: Neuropathic agents (amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, pregabalin)
Localized pain with allodynia: Lidocaine plasters (especially in elderly)
Rescue therapy: Tramadol for acute exacerbations (avoid long-term use)

Prescribing Guidance

Amitriptyline

  • Start: 10mg nocte
  • Titrate: Increase by 10mg weekly
  • Max: 75mg daily
  • Caution: Anticholinergic effects, falls risk in elderly

Duloxetine

  • Start: 30mg daily
  • Titrate: After 1-2 weeks if needed
  • Max: 60mg daily
  • Caution: Nausea, hypertension, hepatic impairment

Gabapentin

  • Start: 300mg nocte
  • Titrate: Increase by 300mg every 3-7 days
  • Max: 3600mg daily
  • CONTROLLED DRUG

Pregabalin

  • Start: 75mg nocte
  • Titrate: Increase to 150mg after 3-7 days
  • Max: 600mg daily
  • CONTROLLED DRUG

Lidocaine Plasters

  • Apply: Once daily for up to 12 hours
  • Max: 3 plasters simultaneously
  • Duration: Reassess after 2-4 weeks
  • Caution: Hypersensitivity to amide anesthetics

Important Updates

Capsaicin cream is currently unavailable until at least June 2026. Do not prescribe.

NSAIDs are not recommended - no evidence of efficacy in PHN.

Follow-up Protocol

Initial Follow-up (1-2 weeks)

  • Assess titration progress and tolerability
  • Address medication side effects
  • Reinforce self-management strategies
  • Provide pain diary template

Ongoing Management

  • Titrate to maximum tolerated dose if pain uncontrolled
  • If well-controlled: Consider gradual dose reduction
  • Reassess lidocaine plaster need monthly
  • Monitor for mood changes (anxiety/depression)
Prognosis: Up to 50% experience pain >1 year; satisfactory relief in <50% of cases

Referral Criteria

Refer to Specialist When:

  • Pain significantly limits daily activities
  • Severe, uncontrolled pain despite optimized therapy
  • Primary care options exhausted
  • Complex comorbidities affecting management
  • Patient requests psychological support (CBT)

Specialist Options

Neurologist

  • Complex neuropathic pain
  • Atypical presentations
  • Diagnostic uncertainty

Pain Specialist

  • Capsaicin 8% patch
  • Nerve blocks/injections
  • Neuromodulation
  • Multidisciplinary approach
