Adult psoriasis in primary care

For adults ≥18 years with chronic plaque, guttate, nail and localized pustular psoriasis in non-specialist settings.

Last major evidence review: 2022–2024 First-contact clinicians & GPs Based on UK primary care guidance
Pustular / erythrodermic → same-day specialist assessment
Document BSA, PGA and impact Aim: symptom control, minimal burden

Overview Definition, patterns and expectations

Background
Systemic, immune-mediated skin disease

Chronic relapsing course with skin, nail and possible joint involvement. Not infectious; strong genetic and environmental components.

  • Well-demarcated erythematous plaques with scale, often on extensor surfaces and scalp.
  • Forms include chronic plaque, scalp, flexural, guttate, pustular, erythrodermic and nail disease.
  • Onset can occur at any age, with peaks in early adulthood and later mid-life.
Goals of care
Pragmatic endpoints
  • Control symptoms (itch, pain, scaling) and visible plaques.
  • Minimise psychosocial impact on relationships, work and daily activities.
  • Detect psoriatic arthritis and comorbidities early; manage cardiovascular risk.

Assessment & tools History, examination, patient- and clinician-rated measures

Structured history
Key questions
  • Sites involved, symptom burden (itch, pain, bleeding, scaling), flares/remissions.
  • Triggers: infection (esp. recent strep throat), drugs, trauma, stress, alcohol, smoking, pregnancy/post-partum.
  • Impact on mood, sleep, relationships, work; screen for anxiety/depression.
  • Joint symptoms: stiffness, swelling, dactylitis, inflammatory back pain, heel pain.
Examination
Whole-skin check
  • Inspect scalp, trunk, flexures, genitalia, nails and joints where possible.
  • Describe distribution, plaque size/number, colour, scale, fissuring.
  • Estimate body surface area (BSA) involvement (e.g. Rule of Nines or “palm ≈1%”).
  • Look for signs of systemic illness (fever, tachycardia, oedema, weight loss).
Assessment tools
Simple scores for clinic
  • Static Physician’s Global Assessment (PGA 0–5) for clinician-rated severity.
  • Patient Global Assessment and Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI) to assess impact.
  • Psoriasis Epidemiology Screening Tool (PEST): ≥3/5 → consider rheumatology referral.
Quick reference – bedside screening
PEST ≥3 → psoriatic arthritis likely Ask about early morning stiffness & dactylitis Annual joint screening in all psoriasis DLQI >10 → major life impact

Red flags – same-day action Medical emergencies and urgent situations

Medical emergency
Generalised pustular psoriasis

Rapid onset tender erythema with sterile pustules, often with systemic upset.

  • Widespread erythema with crops of non-follicular pustules that may coalesce.
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, malaise, tachycardia, weight loss.
  • Can occur on background of plaque psoriasis or de novo.
  • Action: immediate same-day specialist dermatology assessment (medical emergency).
Medical emergency
Erythrodermic psoriasis

Diffuse erythema involving >90% BSA; risk of thermoregulation and fluid imbalance issues.

  • Almost total body redness and scaling, warm skin, possible oedema and lymphadenopathy.
  • Complications: heart failure, hypothermia, dehydration, malabsorption.
  • Can be triggered by infection, phototherapy, irritants or steroid withdrawal.
  • Action: same-day hospital/dermatology assessment.
Indicator condition
Severe or atypical psoriasis
  • Consider HIV testing in severe, treatment-resistant or atypical presentations.
  • Check for systemic symptoms and opportunistic infections.

Standard care – trunk & limbs Topical management for chronic plaque psoriasis

Emollients & education
Foundation of therapy
  • Regular emollients to reduce dryness, itch and scaling.
  • Ointments for thick plaques; creams/gels for large areas; lotions/foams for scalp or hair-bearing sites.
  • Explain chronic relapsing nature; aim for control not cure.
  • Discuss triggers, lifestyle (smoking, alcohol, weight) and self-management plans.
Topical drug treatment
First-line combinations
  • Potent topical corticosteroid plus vitamin D analogue (e.g. once daily each at different times).
  • Consider salicylic acid keratolytics if heavy scale limits penetration.
  • Second-line: vitamin D analogue alone, short courses of potent steroid twice daily, or coal tar preparations.
  • Avoid very potent steroids in primary care except on specialist advice.
Review & safety
Follow-up intervals
  • Initial review ~4 weeks after starting or changing topical therapy.
  • Check response, adherence, application technique and adverse effects (especially steroid atrophy).
  • Avoid continuous use of potent steroids on one site beyond ~8 weeks; use “treatment breaks”.
  • Yearly review for those needing frequent steroid courses to reassess severity and joint symptoms.
Quick reference – topical strategy
Start: potent steroid + vit D Review at 4 weeks Limit potent steroid to ≤8 weeks/site Use emollients throughout Consider coal tar if vit D/steroid sub-optimal

Good response & monitoring What “doing well” looks like

Good response
Features of good control
  • PGA improved to “mild” or “nearly clear”; BSA substantially reduced.
  • DLQI low (e.g. ≤5) and patient reports minimal interference with life.
  • Topical regimen acceptable and sustainable; minimal steroid exposure.
Follow-up
Ongoing review
  • Plan proactive but intermittent use of topicals to prevent flares.
  • Annual review: joint screening, cardiovascular risk, weight, mood.
  • Reinforce self-management and when to seek urgent help (e.g. rapid worsening, systemic symptoms).

Referral & shared care When to involve dermatology or rheumatology

Dermatology
Routine / urgent referral
  • Same-day: generalised pustular or erythrodermic psoriasis.
  • Urgent: rapidly progressive, unstable disease, significant impact despite optimised topicals.
  • Routine: diagnostic uncertainty, extensive disease (e.g. large BSA), or need for phototherapy/systemic therapy.
Rheumatology
Suspected psoriatic arthritis
  • PEST ≥3, inflammatory joint pain/swelling, dactylitis or enthesitis.
  • Back pain with inflammatory features or spinal stiffness.
  • Consider joint imaging and inflammatory markers as per local pathways.

Comorbidities & risk modification Cardio-metabolic and psychological health

Metabolic & vascular
Screening and prevention
  • Assess weight, BP, lipids and diabetes risk; psoriasis (esp. severe) is linked with metabolic syndrome and CVD.
  • Offer cardiovascular risk assessment at diagnosis of severe disease and at least every 5 years in adults.
  • Support smoking cessation, exercise, alcohol moderation and healthy diet.
Mental health
Psychological impact
  • Actively ask about low mood, anxiety, body image and social withdrawal.
  • Consider screening tools for depression/anxiety and referral to psychological support.
  • Acknowledge stigma and visible nature of disease; signpost support groups.
Quick reference – associated conditions
Psoriatic arthritis (up to ~30%) Metabolic syndrome / NAFLD IHD & VTE risk IBD, coeliac disease (subset) Non-melanoma skin cancer & lymphoma (rare) Ocular inflammation (uveitis, dry eye, etc.)
