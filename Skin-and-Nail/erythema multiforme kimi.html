Erythema Multiforme (EM)

Updated: Oct 2024
Source: BMJ Best Practice
CRITICAL EXCLUSION: Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) & Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN).
Check for: Nikolsky sign (+), Atypical targets, Epidermal necrosis >10% BSA. These are NOT EM.
Clinical Identification
  • Target Lesions: Bull's eye appearance. 3 zones: Outer erythema, normal ring, central blister/erosion.
  • Distribution: Symmetrical, acral (dorsal hands, extensor extremities). Palms/soles common.
  • Onset: Acute (24-48 hrs), self-limiting (1-2 weeks).
  • Skin Area: Typically <10% Body Surface Area (BSA).
"Typical targets + Minimal mucosal disease = EM"

Risk Factors
  • Recurrence: Prior episode (Strong risk).
  • Infection: HSV recurrence, Mycoplasma pneumonia.
  • Weak Factors: Cancer/Lymphoma, HIV, certain vaccines (HPV, COVID-19).
Key Exam Findings
Target Lesions
Clustered Vesicles (if HSV)
Red Tympanic Membranes (Mycoplasma)
Mucosal Erosions
Common Causes (>90%)

Infectious Triggers:

  • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): The #1 cause. Look for cold sores.
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae: Look for respiratory symptoms.

Other Infections: Hep B/C, EBV, Influenza, Histoplasmosis, Orf, Syphilis.

Drug Induced (<10%)

Review recent medication starts.

  • Antibiotics, NSAIDs, Anticonvulsants.
  • Sulfonamides, Allopurinol.
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors, TNF-alpha inhibitors.
  • Barium contrast, Vaccines (rare).
Diagnostic Investigations
1st Line (if cause unclear):
  • FBC (CBC), Electrolytes.
  • HSV Serology (PCR if lesions active).
  • Cold Agglutinins / Mycoplasma titres.
  • Chest X-ray (if Mycoplasma suspected).
Specialized/Second Line:
  • Biopsy (H&E): If diagnosis uncertain. Monocytic infiltrate, satellite cell necrosis.
  • Direct Immunofluorescence: If H&E inconclusive.
  • Dermatologist Referral: If SJS/TEN suspected.
Key Differentials
Condition Distinguishing Feature
Urticaria Itchy, resolves < 24hrs, no central blistering.
SJS/TEN Atypical targets, painful epidermal necrosis, sloughing, >10% BSA.
Fixed Drug Eruption Recurs at same site; usually 1 or few lesions, not symmetric.
Subacute Cutaneous Lupus Anti-Ro/SSA positive, photosensitive, no target lesions.
Management Strategy
  • Supportive Care: Hydration, wound care.
  • Treat Cause: Antivirals for HSV, Antibiotics for Mycoplasma.
  • Remove Trigger: Stop offending drug.
  • Anti-inflammatory: Topical or Systemic Steroids.
EM Minor Plan
  • Skin Care: Clean with soap/water bid. Apply topical emollients.
  • Dressings: Sterile covers for open lesions.
  • Pain: Paracetamol or NSAIDs.
  • Inflammation: Topical corticosteroids (Triamcinolone 0.1% or Clobetasol 0.05%).
  • Antiviral: If HSV trigger present.
EM Major Plan
  • Fluids: IV if volume depleted (poor intake).
  • Mucosal Care: Topical lidocaine 2% swish & spit for mouth pain. Magic mouthwash.
  • Urine: Catheterization if urethra blocked.
  • Inflammation: Oral Prednisolone 40-60mg daily OR IV Methylprednisolone.
  • Infection: Aggressively treat HSV or Mycoplasma.
Recurrent Prevention (HSV-associated)

Suppressive therapy with antivirals can be started. Initiate daily or twice-daily Valaciclovir at first sign of outbreak, or continuous daily dose for 6 months.

Valaciclovir (Preferred)
Herpes Labialis: 2g orally twice daily for 1 day.
Genital: 500mg BD for 5 days.
Aciclovir
Labialis: 200mg orally five times daily for 5 days.
Genital (Initial): 400mg TDS for 7-10 days.
Genital (Recurrence): 400mg TDS for 5 days.

Labialis: 1500mg orally once daily for 1 day.

500mg orally day 1, then 250mg daily for 4 days.
OR 500mg IV daily for 7-10 days.

100mg orally every 12 hours for 14 days.

40-60 mg orally once daily for 5-10 days.
