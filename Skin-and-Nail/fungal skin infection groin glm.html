Fungal Skin Infection - Body and Groin

Clinical Guidance for Healthcare Professionals

Last Revised: July 2023

Suspicion & Diagnosis

Body (Tinea Corporis)

  • Annular (ring-shaped) red/pink patches (1-5 cm)
  • Active scaly advancing edge with central clearing
  • Asymmetrical distribution

Groin (Tinea Cruris)

  • Red-brown plaques in inguinal folds and proximal thighs
  • Penis and scrotum usually spared
  • Uniform scale without central clearing in moist areas

Risk Factors

Hot/humid environments Tight-fitting clothing Obesity Hyperhidrosis Immunocompromised states

When to Test

  • Severe/extensive disease
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Atypical appearance

Sample Collection

  • Scrape from advancing edge of lesion
  • Collect 5mm² skin flakes
  • Store at room temperature (do not refrigerate)

Management Algorithm

Clinical Decision Pathway

1

Mild, Non-extensive Disease (All Ages)

Self-care measures: Loose cotton clothing, daily washing/drying, avoid scratching, frequent washing of towels/linens

Topical antifungal: Terbinafine cream (first-line) or Clotrimazole/Miconazole/Econazole cream

If marked inflammation: Add hydrocortisone 1% cream once daily (max 7 days) - never use corticosteroids alone

2

Severe/Extensive Disease

Adults: Oral terbinafine (first-line) or oral itraconazole/griseofulvin. Start treatment while awaiting mycology results if high clinical suspicion

Children: Refer to pediatric dermatologist

3

Follow-up & Referral

Follow-up: Review if inadequate response. Consider non-adherence, inappropriate corticosteroid use, drug resistance, reinfection, immunocompromise, or alternative diagnosis

Refer to Dermatology: Severe/extensive disease in children, uncertain diagnosis, treatment failure/frequent recurrences, immunocompromised patient
