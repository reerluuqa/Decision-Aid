Fungal Skin Infection - Foot

Clinical Guidance for Diagnosis and Management of Tinea Pedis (Athlete's Foot)

Definition & Types

Fungal infection of the foot (athlete's foot or tinea pedis) is a superficial skin infection of the feet and toes, predominantly caused by dermatophytes.

Subtypes of Fungal Foot Infection

  • Interdigital (most common): White or red, fissured, scaling skin or macerated areas between the toes. Typically affects the lateral interdigital space between the fourth and fifth toes.
  • Moccasin or dry-type: A more diffuse, chronic presentation causing scaling, erythema, and hyperkeratosis of the sole and lateral aspect of the foot. The dorsal surface is usually unaffected.
  • Vesicobullous (least common): An inflammatory variant with hard, tense, small (1–5 mm) vesicles, blisters, bullae, and pustules on an erythematous base, mainly on the arches and soles of the feet.

Common causative organisms: Trichophyton rubrum (about 80% of infections), Trichophyton interdigitale (about 15%), and Epidermophyton floccosum.

Risk Factors

  • Hot, humid climates or working in high-temperature environments
  • Wearing occlusive footwear (common in athletes, miners, and soldiers)
  • Hyperhidrosis (excessive sweating)
  • Walking on contaminated surfaces (communal showers, swimming pools, saunas)
  • Immunocompromised states (may lead to severe, resistant, or extensive disease)

Prevalence

  • Prevalence increases with age
  • Common in adolescents (mean onset at 15 years), but rare in pre-pubertal children
  • Most common form of fungal skin infection in the post-puberty period
  • Higher risk in men than women
  • Most common between 31-60 years
  • Up to 70% of the population may be affected at some point in their life

Complications

  • Secondary bacterial infection - immunocompromised people are at increased risk
  • Recurrent cellulitis of the lower leg - fungal foot infection may impair the skin barrier and act as a portal of entry for bacteria
  • Fungal infection of the hand (tinea manuum) - may develop as a result of scratching the affected foot, resulting in 'two feet-one hand' syndrome
  • Dermatophytid (id) reaction - a reactive phenomenon causing a disseminated and itchy, papular or vesicular eruption, commonly affecting around the outer helix of the ear
  • Tinea incognito - inappropriate use of topical corticosteroids can lead to spread of fungal infection and change in lesion morphology

Prognosis

Prognosis is generally excellent but infection often relapses after successful treatment in susceptible people, and may become a chronic problem.

Diagnosis

When to Suspect Fungal Foot Infection

The diagnosis should be made on the basis of clinical features, which allows classification into different sub-types.

There may be a history of scaly, itchy, or painful skin of the feet. On examination:

  • Interdigital type (most common): White or red, fissured, scaling skin or macerated areas between the toes
  • Moccasin or dry-type: Scaling, erythema, and hyperkeratosis of the sole and lateral aspect of the foot
  • Vesicobullous type: Hard, tense, small vesicles, blisters, bullae, and pustules on an erythematous base, mainly on the arches and soles

Assessment

If fungal foot infection is suspected on the basis of clinical features:

  1. Ask about:
    • Nature, site, and duration of symptoms
    • Previous treatments (including over-the-counter preparations)
    • Family or close contacts affected
    • Co-morbidities such as underlying causes of immunosuppression
  2. Examine to assess:
    • Pattern, extent, and severity of infection
    • Any associated inflammation
    • Associated fungal infection at other sites (nails, groin, hands)
  3. Consider skin sampling for fungal microscopy and culture if:
    • Severe or extensive disease in adults
    • Diagnosis is uncertain or atypical presentation

Note: Wood's light examination is not needed to aid diagnosis in primary care.

Differential Diagnosis

Other conditions that may present similarly to fungal foot infection include:

  • Allergic or irritant contact dermatitis
  • Atopic eczema
  • Pompholyx eczema
  • Localized (palmoplantar) pustular psoriasis
  • Plantar keratosis
  • Pitted keratolysis
  • Gram-negative bacterial infection, cellulitis, or impetigo
  • Candida skin infection

Management

Self-Care Advice (All Patients)

  • Wear well-fitting, non-occlusive footwear that keeps the feet cool and dry
  • Consider replacing old footwear which could be contaminated with fungal spores
  • Maintain good foot hygiene by wearing a different pair of shoes every 2–3 days
  • Wear cotton, absorbent socks
  • Avoid scratching affected skin, as this may spread the infection to other sites
  • After washing the feet, dry them thoroughly, especially between the toes
  • Do not share towels and wash them frequently, to reduce the risk of transmission
  • Wear protective footwear when using communal bathing places, locker rooms, and gymnasiums
  • If a child is affected, it is not necessary to exclude them from school or nursery

Treatment Options

Mild, Non-Extensive Disease

First-line: Topical antifungal cream

  • Terbinafine 1% cream (children >12 years): Apply thinly to affected area once or twice a day for up to 7 days
  • Clotrimazole 1% cream: Apply to affected area 2–3 times a day and continue for at least 4 weeks
  • Miconazole 2% cream: Apply to affected area twice a day for 2–6 weeks
  • Alternatives: Undecenoic acid cream or tolnaftate preparations

With marked inflammation: Add hydrocortisone 1% cream once daily for a maximum of 7 days

Severe or Extensive Disease

Adults: Consider oral antifungal treatment if:

  • Positive skin sample fungal microscopy or culture result
  • Strong clinical suspicion of fungal foot infection before mycology results are back
  • Negative mycology result but clinical features are very suggestive of infection

First-line oral antifungal: Terbinafine

Alternatives: Oral itraconazole or oral griseofulvin (if terbinafine not tolerated or contraindicated)

Children: Arrange referral to a paediatric dermatologist

Follow-Up and Referral

  • Arrange review if there is an inadequate response to initial treatment
  • For persistent infection after topical treatment in adults:
    • Consider and manage underlying causes (non-adherence, drug resistance, reinfection, immunocompromise)
    • Arrange skin sampling if not already done
    • Consider oral antifungal treatment
  • Arrange referral to dermatology if:
    • Severe or extensive disease, or topical treatment unsuccessful in a child
    • Diagnosis is uncertain
    • Treatment in primary care is unsuccessful
    • Person is immunocompromised (urgency based on clinical judgment)

Prescribing Information

Topical Antifungals

Terbinafine 1% cream

Application: Apply thinly to affected area once or twice a day for up to 7 days

Age: Children above 12 years of age

Clotrimazole 1% cream

Application: Apply to affected area 2–3 times a day and continue for at least 4 weeks

Dosage tip: A strip of cream about half a centimetre long is enough to treat an area about the size of the hand

Miconazole 2% cream

Application: Apply to affected area twice a day for 2–6 weeks depending on the severity

Oral Antifungals

Oral Terbinafine

First-line for severe/extensive disease in adults

Considerations: Check liver function before starting; monitor for drug interactions

Oral Itraconazole

Alternative if terbinafine not tolerated or contraindicated

Considerations: Multiple drug interactions; check contraindications

Oral Griseofulvin

Alternative if terbinafine and itraconazole not suitable

Considerations: Longer treatment course; potential side effects
