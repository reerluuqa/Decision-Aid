Overview

This CKS topic covers the diagnosis and management of Lyme disease (Lyme borreliosis) in primary care. It does not cover management of Lyme disease with focal symptoms in secondary care, or post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome.

Goals

  • Recognize features of possible Lyme disease in primary care
  • Treat people diagnosed with Lyme disease with antibiotics in primary care
  • Refer appropriately to a specialist
  • Provide appropriate advice to people who have been bitten by a tick
  • Provide appropriate advice for prevention of tick bites

NICE Quality Standards

  • People presenting with erythema migrans are diagnosed and treated based on clinical assessment, without laboratory testing
  • People with suspected Lyme disease without erythema migrans who have a negative ELISA test within 4 weeks of symptoms should have the test repeated 4-6 weeks later if still suspected
  • People with Lyme disease receive initial antibiotic treatment, with antibiotic, dosage and duration determined by their symptoms
  • Local authorities organize health promotion activities to raise public awareness about prevention

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis: Make a clinical diagnosis of Lyme disease in people with erythema migrans. No laboratory testing is required.

Erythema Migrans Features

  • Spreading erythema with well-defined edge
  • Usually round or oval, but may be triangular or linear
  • Red, purple or bluish-red in color
  • Usually flat, but may be palpable
  • May have central punctum, spot, vesicle or pustule
  • May have clearing behind leading edge (bulls-eye appearance)
  • At the site of a tick bite
  • Often on legs, flexor creases (knees, axillae, groins)
  • Around waistband, under breasts
  • Near straps (which impede tick progress)
  • In children: at hairline or upper parts of body
  • Usually visible 1-2 weeks after tick bite (range: 1 day to 36 days)
  • Usually expands over days to weeks
  • Diameter typically >5cm (around 15cm average, up to 70cm)
  • Lasts for several weeks if untreated
  • Rash is usually not itchy, hot, or painful
  • In Europe, ~1/3 experience flu-like symptoms
  • Fever, headache, tiredness, nausea, vomiting
  • Arthralgia (joint pain) and myalgia (muscle pain)
  • Flu-like symptoms more common in the US

When to Suspect Lyme Disease

Consider Lyme disease in people presenting with several of the following non-specific symptoms:
  • Cognitive impairment (memory problems, difficulty concentrating)
  • Fatigue
  • Fever and sweats
  • Headache
  • Malaise
  • Migratory joint or muscle aches and pain
  • Neck pain or stiffness
  • Paraesthesia
  • Swollen glands
Consider Lyme disease in people presenting with focal symptoms affecting one or more organ systems:
  • Eye symptoms: uveitis or keratitis
  • Inflammatory arthritis: affecting one or more joints, may be fluctuating and migratory
  • Cardiac problems: heart block or pericarditis
  • Neurological symptoms: facial palsy, meningitis, radiculopathy, encephalitis
  • Skin rashes: acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans or lymphocytoma

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Distinguishing Features
Tick bite hypersensitivity reaction Develops within 48 hours of tick detachment, usually <5cm, itchy, may have urticarial appearance, disappears within 24-48 hours
Hypersensitivity to insect bite Develops rapidly, often itchy, usually smaller than erythema migrans
Cellulitis Usually painful, hot, and tender, may have systemic signs of infection, associated with break in skin
Fungal infections Usually more slowly progressive, may have associated scaling, different distribution pattern

Management

Treatment Algorithm

WITH Erythema Migrans
Treat immediately (no testing needed)
  • Adults: Doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 21 days
  • Children (<12): Amoxicillin 50mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses for 21 days
  • Pregnant: Amoxicillin as above
WITHOUT Erythema Migrans (but clinical suspicion)
  1. Initial testing: ELISA
  2. If negative but symptoms <4 weeks: repeat ELISA in 4-6 weeks if still suspected
  3. If positive: confirm with immunoblot test
  4. If confirmed positive: treat based on symptoms (same regimens as above)
  5. Consider specialist referral for focal symptoms
Tick Bite Without Symptoms
No prophylactic antibiotics recommended
  • Advise on tick removal and monitoring for symptoms
  • Prompt proper removal reduces transmission risk

Referral Guidelines

Urgent specialist referral for:
  • Neurological symptoms (except isolated facial palsy)
  • Cardiac symptoms
  • Severe arthritis
  • Eye involvement
  • Pregnant women with Lyme disease

Prevention Advice

  • Use insect repellent (DEET)
  • Wear protective clothing in wooded/grassy areas
  • Check for ticks after outdoor activities
  • Prompt proper tick removal (grasp with fine tweezers close to skin)

Background Information

Lyme disease, or Lyme borreliosis, is an infection caused by a group of spiral-shaped bacteria (spirochetes) which are transmitted to humans following a bite from an infected tick.

Lyme disease is caused by a group of bacteria termed Borrelia burgdorferis sensu lato (sl). Several geno-species exist, with B. afzelii and B. garinii being the two major European pathogens, while B. burgdorferi ss is the dominant species in North America.

All species are transmitted to humans by Ixodes ticks. The longer an infected tick is attached, the more likely it is to transmit infection.

The true incidence of Lyme disease in the UK is unknown. UKHSA estimates 2,000-3,000 new confirmed cases each year in England and Wales.

There were 1,156 laboratory confirmed cases in England and Wales in 2021, with a mean annual rate per 100,000 population of 1.94.

Highest risk areas in the UK are the south of England or the Scottish highlands, but cases occur in every region.

Most people recover completely with appropriate treatment. Antibiotic treatment in early Lyme disease is highly effective and reduces the risk of further symptoms developing.

Evidence suggests complete response rates over 90% for the treatment of erythema migrans.

A small minority of people will have persisting symptoms of fatigue, pain, or joint or muscle aches longer term after appropriate treatment (post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome).
