Key pillars

Compression (if safe) • Treat complications • Prevent recurrence

Start compression early: Apply by trained staff once arterial disease is excluded (ABPI in normal range). Offer strongest tolerated and review skin in 24–48 h.
Look out for red flags: rapidly worsening ulcer, severe pain, systemic illness, atypical site/appearance, suspected malignancy, or ABPI outside normal range.

1) Initial Assessment

History • Exam • Investigations • Differentials

Targeted history

  • Ulcer duration; venous symptoms (aching, heaviness, swelling, itch worse later in day, relieved by elevation).
  • Risk factors: immobility, obesity, limited ankle range, prior ulcer/DVT/varicose veins, trauma, pregnancy, meds (e.g., nicorandil, steroids, NSAIDs, anticoagulants).
  • Comorbidities delaying healing: diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis; allergies (latex/lanolin).
  • Impact on function/QoL.

Examination

  • Ulcer: site (gaiter), sloping edges, record size/depth, bed (granulation/slough), exudate, infection signs.
  • Skin: hyperpigmentation, venous eczema, lipodermatosclerosis, atrophie blanche.
  • Legs: pitting oedema, varicose veins, ankle mobility.
  • Exclude arterial disease: hair loss, pallor, cold, cap refill >4 s, peripheral pulses; check for AAA.

Investigations

  • Doppler ABPI both legs to exclude arterial insufficiency.
  • FBC, CRP/ESR, Urea/Creatinine, Albumin, HbA1c as indicated.
  • Swab only if clinical infection (after cleaning, from deep within ulcer).
Key differentials
  • Peripheral arterial disease (punched‑out edges, ischaemic signs).
  • Neuropathic/diabetic ulcers (pressure points, callus; clean edges/base).
  • Vasculitic ulcers; RA‑related; systemic vasculitis features.
  • Malignancy (rolled/everted edges; non‑healing/unusual appearance).
  • Pressure/drug‑induced ulcers; tropical/infective causes.

2) Primary Care Management

Initiate if no immediate referral is needed

Compression therapy

  • Apply by trained staff; confirm ABPI within normal range.
  • High‑compression multilayer bandaging recommended; hosiery can be as effective in selected patients.
  • Offer strongest tolerated; address comfort/adherence; reassess skin at 24–48 h.
  • Use caution in moderate–severe heart failure (risk of fluid overload).

Clean & dress

  • Wash with tap water; dry carefully. Debride slough/necrosis if needed.
  • Simple non‑adherent primary dressing; tailor to exudate, depth, location.
  • Do not use antimicrobial/antiseptic dressings routinely.
  • Once compressed, change less often (often weekly) — avoid over‑ or under‑saturation.

Adjuncts & complications

  • Pentoxifylline 400 mg TDS up to 6 months (off‑label; per local policy).
  • Pain: paracetamol ± codeine; avoid NSAIDs; pre‑medicate before dressing changes.
  • Oedema: reinforce compression use; elevate legs (≥30 min, 3–4×/day); exclude HF/CKD and drug causes.
  • Eczema: emollients; treat flares with topical corticosteroid if no infection; consider more frequent bandage changes to apply topicals.

Patient advice (quick)

Keep mobile; walk Elevate when resting Footwear to avoid trauma Daily skin checks Emollients (avoid allergens) Weight mgmt • Stop smoking

Remove compression and seek urgent review if numbness/tingling, severe pain, or dusky toes.

3) Infection Management

Treat cellulitis/clinical infection promptly
Clinical infection signs: cellulitis (pain, warmth, swelling, erythema), fever, increased pain, rapidly expanding ulcer, malodour, ↑ exudate. Take swab (deep, after cleaning) if infected.

4) Referral Triggers

Escalate without delay where indicated

Immediate / urgent

  • Sepsis, necrotising fasciitis, osteomyelitis, spreading infection not responding to orals, lymphangitis.
  • Rapid deterioration; severe/uncontrolled pain.
  • Atypical site/appearance; suspected malignancy (urgent skin cancer pathway).

Specialist assessment

  • Diagnostic uncertainty (vascular/dermatology).
  • ABPI outside normal range (suspected arterial/mixed disease).
  • Poor ankle mobility/reduced joint function/falls history.
  • Recurrent ulcer; suspected iliac vein stenosis (consider CT venography/IVUS).
  • No/delayed healing after 2 weeks of appropriate compression therapy.

5) Follow‑Up

Monitor healing, adherence, and complications
  • Review weekly ×2 after starting primary care treatment; extend interval if healing.
  • Track size/depth, granulation, exudate, pain/oedema; compare to baseline (consider photos/acetate tracing).
  • Check compression and lifestyle adherence; troubleshoot comfort/heat/practical barriers.
  • Watch for dressing issues (maceration, dermatitis) and compression issues (pressure damage, arterial symptoms). Remove bandage if colour/temp change or increased pain; seek advice if no improvement.
  • Re‑measure ABPI with deterioration/recurrence or sudden change; follow local policy.

6) Prevention of Recurrence

Compression stockings + lifestyle + consider surgery

Compression hosiery

  • Offer below‑knee graduated compression stockings after healing; strongest tolerated class.
  • Replace every 3–6 months; re‑measure leg at replacement.
  • Follow‑up Dopplers ideally every 6–12 months (or earlier if indicated).

Further measures

  • Reinforce mobility, elevation, skin care, footwear, weight management, smoking cessation, alcohol within guidance, balanced diet.
  • For chronic ulcer + superficial venous reflux → consider referral for superficial venous surgery to reduce recurrence.
