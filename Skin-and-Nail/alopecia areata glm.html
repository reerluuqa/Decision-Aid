Alopecia Areata Clinical Algorithm

A concise reference guide for fast decision making in clinical practice

Definition & Presentation

  • Chronic inflammatory condition causing sudden non-scarring hair loss
  • Typically patchy, circular/oval areas; most commonly scalp or beard
  • Nail changes in 10-15% of cases
  • Can range from mild patchy to totalis/universalis

Assessment

History:

  • Onset, duration, triggers (stress)
  • Impact on quality of life
  • Family history of alopecia
  • Associated autoimmune disease

Examination:

  • Pattern and extent of hair loss
  • Look for regrowth
  • Check for exclamation mark hairs
  • Assess nail changes

Investigations (if uncertain):

  • FBC, ferritin, thyroid function
  • Fungal scrapings if tinea suspected

Management Approach

Suspected Alopecia Areata

Evidence of regrowth

No treatment needed

Limited, stable, non-conspicuous

Watchful waiting

No regrowth, patient doesn't want treatment

No treatment

No regrowth, patient wants treatment

Adult: Trial potent topical corticosteroid* for 3 months
Child: Refer to specialist
*Not for facial areas (beard/eyebrows)

When to Refer

  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Child, pregnant or breastfeeding woman
  • No response to primary care treatment
  • Patient declines topical corticosteroids but wants treatment
  • Severe disease (>50% scalp involvement)
  • Significant psychological distress

Specialist Treatments

  • Intralesional corticosteroids
  • Topical minoxidil
  • Contact immunotherapy
  • Systemic treatments: JAK inhibitors (baricitinib, ritlecitinib) for severe cases

Patient Advice

  • Sun protection for affected areas
  • Support groups: Alopecia UK (www.alopecia.org.uk)
  • Cosmetic options: hairstyling, hairpieces, wigs (NHS funding may be available)
  • Realistic expectations: spontaneous remission possible but unpredictable
  • Regrown hair initially fine/depigmented before returning to normal color

Prognostic Factors

  • Poorer prognosis with:
  • Childhood onset
  • Family history
  • Extensive alopecia
  • Ophiasis pattern
  • Nail changes
  • Atopy/autoimmune disease
