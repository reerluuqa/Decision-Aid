Definition & Cause

Ectoparasite infestation by Sarcoptes scabiei (mite). Burrows into stratum corneum.

Vector 0.3‚Äì0.5 mm mite Spread Direct skin-to-skin (Prolonged)

Epidemiology
  • Endemic in overcrowded/poverty areas.
  • Peaks in colder seasons (mite survival + crowding).
  • High risk: Age <15 or >65, Institutionalized.
RED FLAG: Crusted (Norwegian) Scabies

Highly Contagious. Contains thousands to millions of mites.

Who: Immunosuppressed (HIV, HTLV-1, malignancy), Neurological disorders (Down's), Institutionalized.

Signs: Hyperkeratotic plaques on palms/soles, dystrophic nails, minimal pruritus (immune impaired).

ACTIONS:

  • Contact Isolation
  • Combination Therapy
  • Treat Contacts
  • Environmental Clean

Investigations

Ectoparasite Prep (1st Line)

Skin scraping (liquid paraffin, not KOH). Visualize mite/eggs. Specificity 100% if seen.

Dermatoscopy

"Delta wing jet" sign (triangular head with burrow).

Treatment Algorithm
1st Line Permethrin Topical 5% Apply neck down to soles. Wash off after 8-14 hours. Repeat 7 days if needed. Most effective. Low toxicity. Safe for pregnant/lactating women.
OR Ivermectin Oral 200 micrograms/kg orally as a single dose. Repeat dose in 14 days. Use for poor compliance or mass outbreaks.

Urea Cream (20-40%) Apply twice daily (except night of permethrin) to reduce hyperkeratosis and aid penetration.

Pregnant / Lactating

Preferred Permethrin 5% Standard application (neck down). B2 category. Considered safe.
Alternative Sulfur 6% in Petroleum

Infants & Children

Infants < 2 months
Sulfur 6% in Petroleum Apply to head, neck, and body. Leave on 24h, repeat for 3 consecutive nights.
Children 2mo - 5y
Preferred Permethrin 5% Scalp, hairline, neck down to soles.
Key Differentials
Condition Differentiating Feature
Impetigo Golden-yellow crusts. No burrows. Usually face/hands.
Atopic Dermatitis History of flexural involvement. No burrows. Not specifically worse at night.
Contact Dermatitis History of exposure. Scaly patches without burrows.
Flea Bites Pet exposure. Grouped papules, usually lower extremities only. No burrows.
Bullous Pemphigoid Tense bullae. Biopsy required for differentiation.
Follow Up
  • Timeframe: Re-evaluate in 4 weeks.
  • If failed: Check treatment compliance, resistance, or re-infestation from contacts.
  • If crusted: Monitor skin for clearance of scales.
Complications
Post-scabetic Pruritus (Common) Immune reaction lasting up to 1 month. Treat with topical steroids/antihistamines.
Secondary Infection Strep/Staph from scratching. Risk of post-streptococcal GN/RHD in children.
