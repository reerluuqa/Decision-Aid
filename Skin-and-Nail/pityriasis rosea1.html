â–¶ Key Diagnostic Features Essential
Herald Patch (80% of cases):
  • Largest lesion, appears first (usually on trunk)
  • Pink erythematous oval patch/plaque up to 10cm
  • Raised border with free edge inside (collarette) and central scale
  • Precedes main eruption by 7-10 days (range: up to 84 days)
  • Multiple herald patches seen in ~2% of cases

Secondary Eruption:

  • Smaller papulosquamous oval lesions
  • Long axes aligned along Langer lines creating "fir-tree" pattern
  • V-distribution over chest/back, circumferential over shoulders/hips
  • Primarily affects trunk and upper extremities
  • Face, palms, soles involved more often in children

Pruritus:

  • Present in 75% of patients
  • Moderate to severe in 50% of cases
  • Absent in 25% of patients
â–¶ Case History Example

28-year-old woman presents with 2-week history of itchy rash over chest and back.

  • Began as single large oval lesion on abdomen (herald patch)
  • Did not improve with OTC antifungal creams
  • Erupted into many smaller lesions after 1 week
  • Recalled "cold-like" symptoms the week before first lesion appeared

Classic presentation consistent with pityriasis rosea

â–¶ Prodromal Symptoms (5% of cases)
  • Fever
  • Malaise
  • Headache
  • Arthralgia
â–¶ Risk Factors
Weak Associations:
  • Age 10-35 years (most common)
  • Female sex (M:F ratio 1:1.43)
  • Immunosuppression (associated with recurrences)

Recurrence Rate: 2.8% of cases, often in immunosuppressed individuals

Diagnosis & Investigations
â–¼
Clinical Diagnosis: Diagnosis is usually based on clinical history and findings. Additional investigations may be necessary for atypical cases.
â–¶ When to Perform Skin Biopsy Atypical Cases

Indications:

  • Atypical or confusing clinical presentation
  • To exclude other diagnoses
  • Palmoplantar involvement

Histopathological Features:

  • Superficial perivascular lymphohistiocytic infiltrate
  • Focal spongiosis
  • Focal parakeratosis in mounds
  • Dyskeratotic keratinocytes
  • Papillary dermal edema
  • Possible dermal eosinophils
  • Extravasated red blood cells
â–¶ Laboratory Investigations Selective
Antistreptolysin O titres

To exclude streptococcal infection and/or guttate psoriasis if infectious symptoms present

KOH preparation or fungal culture

To exclude fungal infection (Tinea) if only few lesions present

RPR/VDRL serology

For syphilis if atypical lesions, palmoplantar involvement, or high-risk patients

â–¶ Differential Diagnoses Critical
Secondary Syphilis Must Exclude
  • Palmoplantar involvement common
  • Random lesion orientation
  • Possible history of primary genital ulcer
  • Test: RPR/VDRL positive
Guttate Psoriasis Consider
  • Follows streptococcal infection
  • Small erythematous papules with micaceous white scale
  • No particular orientation
  • Test: ASO titres positive, skin biopsy shows regular acanthosis
Tinea Corporis Consider
  • Usually single or few lesions
  • May resemble herald patch
  • No prodromal symptoms, no subsequent eruption
  • Test: Fungal culture positive, hyphae on KOH
Drug Eruption Consider
  • Rash 5-20 days after initiating medication
  • Protracted course, no herald patch
  • Lesions bright red or violet, severe pruritus
  • Test: Increased dermal eosinophils on biopsy
Other differentials to consider: Nummular eczema, pruritic urticarial papules of pregnancy, pityriasis lichenoides chronica, pityriasis versicolor

Treatment Management
â–¼
Key Principle: Most cases are self-limiting and require no treatment. Management focuses on symptom control and patient reassurance.

Treatment Algorithm Overview

Step 1: Mild-to-Moderate Symptoms First-line
  • Reassurance + emollients
  • Oral antihistamines
  • Low-to-mid-potency topical corticosteroids (if itching interferes with daily activities)
Step 2: Severe or Refractory Symptoms Second-line
  • Doxepin and/or higher-potency topical corticosteroids
  • Narrow-band UVB (adjunct)
Step 3: Refractory to All Other Treatments Reserve
  • Systemic corticosteroids (controversial)
â–¶ First-Line: Reassurance & Emollients All Patients
Patient Education:
  • Benign, self-limiting condition (resolves in 2-12 weeks)
  • Not contagious
  • Unlikely to recur (2.8% recurrence rate)
  • May rarely persist up to 5 months

Emollients:

  • Topical emollients may diminish appearance of scale
  • Use regularly for symptomatic relief
â–¶ First-Line: Oral Antihistamines For Pruritus
Loratadine Non-sedating

Dose: 10 mg orally once daily

Preferred for daytime use

Fexofenadine Non-sedating

Dose: 180 mg orally once daily

Alternative daytime option

Cetirizine Non-sedating

Dose: 10 mg orally once daily

Alternative daytime option

Hydroxyzine Sedating

Dose: 10-25 mg orally once daily at night

Use at night for sleep disturbance from pruritus

â–¶ Adjunct: Topical Corticosteroids Selective Use
Hydrocortisone 2.5%

Dose: Apply sparingly to affected area(s) twice daily for 2 weeks

Use when itching interferes with daily activities

Contraindications & Monitoring:
  • Avoid prolonged use (>2 weeks)
  • Monitor for skin atrophy, telangiectasia
  • Potential for pigmentary alteration
  • Risk of systemic absorption with extensive use
â–¶ Second-Line: Severe/Refractory Cases Specialist Input
Doxepin Limited Evidence

Dose: 10 mg orally once daily at night OR 5% cream up to 4x daily

Tricyclic antidepressant with antipruritic properties

Triamcinolone 0.1% High Potency

Dose: Apply sparingly twice daily for 2 weeks

For severe symptoms unresponsive to hydrocortisone

Narrow-band UVB Adjunct Therapy

Mechanism: Cross-linking DNA to prevent synthesis, inhibiting inflammatory cells

Note: Requires significant patient commitment. No good evidence for shortening disease duration.

Adverse Effects: Phototoxicity, small theoretical increased skin cancer risk with repeated therapy

â–¶ Third-Line: Systemic Corticosteroids Controversial
Reserve for:
  • Patients in severe discomfort
  • Refractory to topical corticosteroids and antihistamines
Prednisolone

Dose: 1 mg/kg/day (maximum 60 mg/day) orally for 4 days, then gradual taper over 2 weeks

May help symptoms but will NOT shorten disease course or improve appearance

Controversy: Some physicians do not advocate systemic steroid use in PR due to lack of disease-modifying effect and potential side effects.
â–¶ Emerging Therapies Limited Evidence
Aciclovir

Limited evidence of visible rash improvement vs placebo. Effective dose not determined, limiting recommendation.

Erythromycin

Moderate-quality evidence from one small RCT suggests decreased pruritus vs placebo. Unclear if improves rash.

Follow-up & Prognosis
â–¼
Prognosis: Excellent. Most cases resolve completely in several weeks. Recurrences occur in 2.8%, often in immunosuppressed patients.
â–¶ Monitoring Schedule Routine
  • Primary care or dermatology follow-up: Monitor resolution of lesions and symptom control
  • Return evaluation: If no improvement by 4 weeks after diagnosis
  • Re-assess: If atypical features develop or diagnosis uncertain
