Vitiligo Clinical Guidance

Evidence-based guidance for diagnosis and management in primary care

Overview

Definition

Vitiligo is an acquired, chronic, depigmentation disorder, characterized by selective loss of functional melanocytes resulting in white patches on the skin and sometimes the hair.

Prevalence

  • Affects 0.5-2% of the population worldwide
  • Childhood onset common (30% of cases)
  • 50% present before age 20
  • 70-80% present before age 30
  • Even distribution between sexes

Types of Vitiligo

Non-segmental

More common (85-90%), symmetrical distribution

Segmental

Less common, unilateral, dermatomal distribution

Mixed

Segmental and non-segmental forms coexist

Key Risk Factors

  • Family history of vitiligo (20% of cases)
  • Personal/family history of autoimmune diseases
  • Thyroid disease (most common)
  • Skin trauma (Koebner phenomenon)
  • Environmental exposure to chemicals

Diagnosis

When to Suspect Vitiligo

Typical Lesion Characteristics:

  • Colour: Initially pale/hypopigmented, becoming chalky white
  • Margins: Well-demarcated, occasionally with inflammation at advancing edge
  • On palpation: Flat and non-scaly
  • Progression: Usually enlarge over time without treatment

Common Distribution:

  • Fingers and wrists
  • Axillae, groin, and genitalia
  • Skin around eyes and mouth
  • Mucosal areas (especially in darker skin)
  • May exhibit Koebner phenomenon

Differential Diagnosis

Congenital:
  • Albinism (generalized depigmentation with ocular abnormalities)
  • Naevus anaemicus (localized vasoconstriction)
  • Naevus depigmentosus (segmental hypopigmentation)
  • Piebaldism (white forelock, midline depigmentation)
  • Tuberous sclerosis (ash-leaf shaped macules)
Post-inflammatory:
  • Chemical/occupational depigmentation
  • Lichen sclerosus (itchy white atrophic plaques)
  • Morphoea (localized thickening of dermis)
  • Pityriasis alba (hypopigmented, dry patches in children)
  • Hypopigmentation following inflammatory skin conditions
Infective:
  • Pityriasis versicolor (superficial yeast infection)
  • Progressive macular hypomelanosis
  • Tuberculoid leprosy (sharply defined patches with sensory loss)
Neoplastic:
  • Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (mycosis fungoides)
  • Melanoma-associated depigmentation
Other:
  • Drug-induced depigmentation
  • Halo naevus (benign mole with depigmented halo)
  • Idiopathic guttate hypomelanosis
  • Sarcoidosis

Assessment

Key Assessment Components

Assessment Area Key Points
History Age of onset, speed of onset, duration, lesion activity, triggers, family history, autoimmune disease history
Impact Quality of life, psychosocial impact, anxiety, depression
Skin Type Fitzpatrick skin type (affects treatment options)
Examination Type (segmental/non-segmental), location, extent, Koebner's phenomenon, halo nevi
Documentation Estimate affected body surface area using Vitiligo Extent Score (VES), consider digital photographs
Investigations Screen for antithyroid antibodies and thyroid function in all patients

Fitzpatrick Skin Types

Type I

Always burns, never tans

Type II

Burns easily, tans poorly

Type III

Tans after initial burn

Type IV

Burns minimally, tans easily

Type V

Rarely burns, tans darkly

Type VI

Never burns, always tans darkly

Management in Primary Care

Recommended Management Approach

Provide Information and Support

Explain the condition, prognosis, and treatment options. Provide information on support organizations like the Vitiligo Society (www.vitiligosociety.org).

Address Triggers

Advise avoiding friction and trauma which may trigger new depigmentation (Koebner phenomenon).

Sun Protection

Recommend appropriate sunscreens to protect depigmented skin from sunburn and potentially reduce contrast between affected and unaffected skin.

Camouflage Options

Discuss cosmetic camouflage options and refer to skin camouflage services if needed.

Psychosocial Support

Screen for and address psychosocial impact, including anxiety and depression. Consider referral to mental health services if needed.

Treatment Options in Primary Care

Topical Treatments

  • Topical corticosteroids: For localized vitiligo, particularly on the face and neck. Use potent or very potent preparations for limited periods (usually 4-6 weeks).
  • Calcineurin inhibitors: Tacrolimus or pimecrolimus may be considered for sensitive areas like the face and eyelids.

Phototherapy

Referral for phototherapy (narrowband UVB) may be appropriate for widespread or rapidly progressing vitiligo, particularly in darker skin types.

Important Considerations

  • Treatment response varies significantly between individuals
  • Treatments may not stop the spread of vitiligo
  • Recurrence is common after successful initial treatment (about 44% in the first year)
  • The face, neck, trunk, and mid-extremities respond best to therapy
  • The lips and distal extremities are more resistant to treatment

Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral Required Red Flag

  • Rapid progression of vitiligo
  • Significant psychosocial impact
  • Suspected alternative diagnosis

Routine Referral Consider

  • Failure to respond to appropriate primary care treatments
  • Widespread disease affecting large body surface area
  • Segmental vitiligo (may benefit from early specialist intervention)
  • Vitiligo affecting sensitive areas (eyes, lips, genitals)
  • Need for specialist treatments (phototherapy, surgical options)
  • Diagnostic uncertainty

Referral to Other Services

  • Skin camouflage service: For cosmetic camouflage advice and products
  • Mental health services: For significant anxiety, depression, or body image issues
  • Endocrinology: If thyroid dysfunction or other autoimmune conditions identified
