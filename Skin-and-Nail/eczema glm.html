Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

Chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition. Quick-scan reference.

Acute Severe Exacerbation: Consider Systemic Corticosteroids as a short-term bridge. Watch for infection (Staph aureus).
Definition

Inflammatory skin condition characterised by dry, pruritic skin with a chronic relapsing course.

Age < 5 (Common) Relapsing
First-Line Tx
  • Emollients: Essential daily skin care.
  • Topical Corticosteroids: Anti-inflammatory.
Epidemiology

15-20% Children (Industrialized)

1-3% Adults

Clinical Features by Age
Phase Key Signs
Infantile
< 2 years		 Cheeks, forehead, scalp. Extensor surfaces. Vesicular, oedema, weeping.
Childhood
2y - Puberty		 Flexures (antecubital, popliteal fossae, wrists, ankles). Lichenification.
Adult
Puberty+		 Flexures, neck, upper back, hands/feet. Dry, lichenified plaques.
Key Risk Factors
  • Genetic: Filaggrin gene mutations (Strongest known risk).
  • Family History: Asthma, Allergic Rhinitis, Eczema.
  • Demographic: Higher in urban areas, higher socioeconomic status (in industrialized nations).
  • Comorbidities: Food allergies (Egg, Milk, Nuts).

Diagnosis

Primarily clinical. History and physical examination are sufficient.

Clinical Diagnosis: No routine laboratory work necessary.
Quick Check: UK Working Party Criteria Interactive

Must have Itchy Skin PLUS at least 3 of the following:

History Taking
  • Time of onset, pattern, severity
  • Response to previous treatments
  • Trigger factors (Irritants/Allergens)
  • Impact on life (Sleep, School)
  • Dietary history & manipulations
Physical Exam
  • Acute: Erythema, scaling, papules, vesicles.
  • Chronic: Lichenification, thickening.
  • Signs: Xerosis (dryness), excoriations, hypopigmentation/hyperpigmentation.
  • Associated: Keratosis pilaris (extensor arms).
When to Investigate (Food Allergy)

Do NOT test routinely. Only if:

  • Immediate reaction to food history.
  • Infant/young child with Moderate-Severe eczema unresponsive to optimal management.
  • Associated with gut dysmotility or failure to thrive.

Use Skin-prick or IgE only if history suggests specific allergy (e.g., egg).

Management

Stepwise approach. Educate patient is priority.

Patient Education: Explain basic skin care, trigger avoidance, and the 'control not cure' mindset.
Stepwise Treatment
1

Emollients (All Patients)

Rehydrate & restore barrier. Use liberally and frequently.

2

Topical Corticosteroids (TCS)

First-line anti-inflammatory. Potency depends on site/age.

3

Second-Line Topicals

Calcineurin inhibitors (Tacrolimus), PDE4 inhibitors (Crisaborole), JAK inhibitors (Ruxolitinib). Use if unresponsive or for sensitive areas.

4

Phototherapy / Systemic

UVB light. Systemic Immunosuppressants, JAK inhibitors, or Biologics (Dupilumab) for severe resistant cases.

Trigger Management
Trigger Type Action
Irritants (Soaps, detergents) Avoid. Use soap substitutes.
Infections (S. aureus) Treat infection promptly (Antibiotics if indicated).
Contact Allergens Patch testing if refractory.
Inhalant (Pollen, Dust mite) Consider if seasonal flares / head/neck distribution.
Systemic Corticosteroids (Caution)

Repeated courses NOT advocated. Reserve exclusively for acute, severe exacerbations as a short-term bridge to other steroid-sparing therapies.

Aetiology & Pathophysiology

Skin Barrier Defect

Impairment leads to increased sensitisation to cutaneous antigens.

Filaggrin Gene Mutation

Null mutations impair epidermal differentiation, causing breaks in the barrier.

Immune Dysregulation

Genetic polymorphisms affect IgE receptors and cytokine genes.

Acute Phase

Th2 mediated response. Over-expression of IL-4, IL-5, IL-13. Increased IgE & eosinophils.

Chronic Phase

Th1 response predominates (IL-12). Th17/IL-17 also increased. Leads to lichenification.

Environmental
  • Urbanization / Smaller families
  • Hard water
  • Vitamin E deficiency
  • Autumn/Winter birth (N. Hemisphere)
Scoring Tools

EASI (Eczema Area and Severity Index): Used in secondary care/biologics assessment.

SCORAD: < 25 Mild, > 50 Severe.

Differential Diagnosis

Key distinguishing features.

Condition Differentiating Signs/Symptoms Differentiating Tests
Seborrhoeic Dermatitis Greasy scale, NOT pruritic. Nappy area often affected (unlike eczema). Clinical exam.
Irritant Contact Dermatitis Common in nappy area/extensors. Less pruritic than eczema. Elimination of irritants improves it.
Allergic Contact Dermatitis Asymmetrical lesions. Specific allergen trigger. Patch testing positive.
Scabies Severe night pruritus. Burrows visible. Family members also affected. Microscopy (mites, eggs).
Psoriasis Well-circumscribed, silver scale, extensor surfaces. Nail pitting (smaller pits). Clinical. Skin biopsy if uncertain.
Mycosis Fungoides Plaques in random distribution. Older age of onset (avg >50). Skin biopsy + Flow cytometry.
