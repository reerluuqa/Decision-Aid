Atopic Eczema
Inflammatory
Clinical Features
  • Chronic, relapsing, remitting course.
  • Distribution: Flexural (antecubital/popliteal fossae), face/neck in adults.
  • Symptoms: Pruritus (itch) is cardinal. Excoriations common.
  • Comorbidities: Asthma, allergic rhinitis ("Atopic March").
Skin of Color: Erythema may appear purple, dark brown, or violaceous. Look for lichenification (silvery-white scales) and hyperpigmentation.

Red Flags: Refer Urgently

Eczema Herpeticum: Fever, punched-out erosions, viral lesions (Emergency).
Erythroderma: >90% body surface area involvement.
Failure to respond: to appropriate therapy after systemic/biologics (consider Cutaneous T-cell Lymphoma).

1. Baselines: Emollient Therapy (Paraffin-based). Soap substitutes. Educate "Fingertip Unit".
2. Flare Treatment:
  • Face/Flexures: Mild Potency (1% Hydrocortisone).
  • Trunk/Limbs: Moderate (Clobetasone) or Potent (Betamethasone/Mometasone) for short courses (1-2 weeks).
  • Steroid Sparing: Calcineurin inhibitors (Tacrolimus 0.1%/Pimecrolimus) for sensitive areas.
3. Refractory (Step-up): Phototherapy ‚Üí Immunosuppressants (Cyclosporin/Methotrexate) ‚Üí Biologics (Dupilumab).
Psoriasis
Inflammatory
Clinical Features
  • Well-demarcated, silvery-scaled plaques.
  • Auspitz Sign: Pinpoint bleeding on scale removal.
  • Nail Changes: Pitting, oil-drop spots, onycholysis (Screen for arthritis).
  • Guttate: Raindrop appearance, post-streptococcal (self-limiting).
Systemic Risks

Cardiovascular risk, Metabolic Syndrome, Psoriatic Arthritis (PEST score screening), IBD association.

Skin of Color: Plaques appear more violaceous or greyish. Scale may be thicker. Diagnostic delay common.
1. First Line (Topical): Vitamin D Analogues + Corticosteroid combinations (e.g., Dovobet, Enstilar). Scalp: Keratolytics (Salicylic acid) + Coconut oil.
2. Systemic Indications: PASI >10, DLQI >10, or Arthritis.
3. Agents:
Methotrexate / Cyclosporin
Acitretin (Teratogenic - avoid in women of childbearing age).
Biologics (IL-17, IL-23 inhibitors).
Acne Vulgaris
Inflammatory
Pathophysiology

1. Follicular hyperkeratinization ‚Üí 2. Sebum production ‚Üí 3. C. acnes inflammation ‚Üí 4. Rupture.

Types
  • Comedonal (Black/White heads)
  • Inflammatory (Papules/Pustules)
  • Nodulocystic (Severe, scarring)
1. Mild/Comedonal: Topical Retinoid (Adapalene) ¬± Benzoyl Peroxide (BP). Warn about initial irritation (start 3-4 spots).
2. Moderate/Inflammatory: Add Topical Antibiotic (Clindamycin/Lymecycline) to BP/Retinoid. Limit antibiotics to 8-12 weeks.

Scabies
Infectious
Key Signs
  • Intense pruritus (worse at night).
  • Burrows: Web spaces, wrists, ankles, genitals (S-shaped tracks).
  • Crusted (Norwegian) Scabies: Hyperkeratotic scales, high mite load (immunocompromised).
Rx Permethrin 5%: Apply Day 1 & Day 7 (whole body, neck down). Treat all household contacts simultaneously.
Resistant? Use Oral Ivermectin (200mcg/kg). Crucial for Crusted Scabies.
Advice Wash clothes/linen on hot day (50¬∞C+). Post-scabetic itch can last 2-6 weeks (does not mean active infection if burrows gone).
Hidradenitis Suppurativa
Inflammatory
Features

Chronic, painful abscesses, sinus tracts, scarring. Apocrine gland areas (axilla, groin, inframammary).

Hurley Stages
Stage 1Abscesses, no tracts/scarring
Stage 2Recurrent abscesses + tracts + scarring
Stage 3Diffuse involvement, interconnected sinuses
Lifestyle Weight loss (GLP-1 inhibitors showing promise), Smoking cessation.
Stage 1/2
  • Topical: Clindamycin lotion.
  • Antibiotics: Tetracyclines (Lymecycline/Doxycycline).
  • Hormonal: COCP or Spironolactone (females).
Stage 3/Refractory Biologics (Adalimumab), Surgery.
Rosacea
Inflammatory
Subtypes
  • Erythematotelangiectatic (Flushing/Redness).
  • Papulopustular (Acne-like).
  • Phymatous (Rhinophyma - thickened skin).
  • Ocular (Dry/gritty eyes).
Skin of Color: Erythema harder to see; look for thickening, edema, and sensation (burning/stinging).
1. Trigger Avoidance: Sun, heat, spicy food, alcohol.
2. Topicals: Metronidazole, Azelaic acid, or Ivermectin (Soolantra).
3. Systemic: Oral Doxycycline (Sub-antimicrobial dose). Brimonidine for flushing redness.
Contact Dermatitis
Inflammatory
Types
  • Irritant (ICD): Cumulative wet work (detergents, healthcare workers). Burning sensation.
  • Allergic (ACD): Type 4 Hypersensitivity. Nickel, Fragrances, PPD (hair dye), Rubber accelerators.
1. Identify & Eliminate: Take detailed occupational/hobby history.
2. Protection: Barrier creams, cotton liners, gloves (avoid latex if allergic).
3. Referral: Patch testing if ACD suspected and trigger unknown.
Tinea Infections
Infectious
Presentations
  • Corporis/Cruris/Pedis: Annular (ring-shaped), scaly, advancing edge.
  • Tinea Incognito: Modified appearance due to inappropriate topical steroid use (less distinct margins).
  • Capitis: Scalp scaling/alopecia (requires oral tx).

Treat the Household

If treating Tinea Capitis, check and treat all family members to prevent reinfection.

1. Skin (Limited): Topical Terbinafine, Clotrimazole, or Miconazole (1-2 weeks).
2. Resistant / Nail / Scalp: Oral Terbinafine or Itraconazole. Monitor LFTs.
Seborrheic Dermatitis
Inflammatory
Clinical Features
  • "Yeasts-driven" (Malassezia).
  • Erythema + Greasy Yellow Scale.
  • Sites: Scalp, eyebrows, glabella, nasolabial folds, presternal chest.
Scalp: Ketoconazole shampoo (twice weekly), Coal tar, Steroid lotions.
Face/Body: Ketoconazole 2% cream, mild topical steroids, or Calcineurin inhibitors.
Maintenance: Use antifungal shampoo weekly to prevent recurrence.
Urticaria & Angioedema
Inflammatory
Classification
  • Acute: < 6 weeks.
  • Chronic Spontaneous (CSU): > 6 weeks. Often autoimmune (IgG vs IgE receptor).
Skin of Color: Wheals may be harder to see; focus on palpation (raised) and patient description of sensation.
1. First Line: Non-sedating antihistamines (standard dose).
2. Refractory: Increase antihistamine dose up to 4x standard.
3. Biologic: Omalizumab (Anti-IgE) for CSU.
Severe: Short course oral steroids.
Pityriasis Rosea
Inflammatory
Features
  • Herald Patch: Solitary oval plaque (2-5cm), precedes generalized rash by 1-2 weeks.
  • Distribution: "Christmas tree" pattern along skin lines.
  • Course: Self-limiting (6-8 weeks).
