Diagnosis

How should I diagnose warts and verrucae?

Warts are diagnosed from their typical appearance:

  • Common warts — firm, raised papules with a rough surface that resembles a cauliflower (common on knuckles, knees, and fingers) — usually asymptomatic but may be tender.
  • Periungual warts are common warts around the nails that can be painful and disturb nail growth — nail biting is a risk factor.
  • Plane warts — usually round, flat-topped, and skin-coloured or greyish-yellow (common on the face, backs of hands, and shins).
  • Filiform warts — finger-like appearance and may have a stalk (more common on the face and neck).
  • Palmar and plantar warts — grow on the palms and the soles of the feet (verrucae). They often have central dark dots (thrombosed capillaries) and may be painful.
  • Mosaic warts — occur when palmar or plantar warts coalesce into larger plaques on the hands and feet.

Diagnosis is usually clinical and in most people, further investigations are not required. If there is diagnostic doubt, light paring of a wart will reveal tiny black dots on the surface (thrombosed capillaries).

Management

Clinical Insight: For most people there is a strong case for not treating warts or verrucae. Cutaneous warts do not usually cause symptoms and in most cases resolve spontaneously.
1

Decision to Treat

Consider treatment if:

  • The wart is painful (for example on the soles of the feet or near the nails)
  • The wart is cosmetically unsightly (for example on the hands or face)
  • The person requests treatment and the wart is persisting

For children, warts are more likely to resolve spontaneously and treatment may be uncomfortable or not tolerated.

2

Initial Management

Provide general advice to all people presenting with warts or verrucae:

  • Cover the wart with a waterproof plaster when swimming
  • Avoid going barefoot in public places
  • Avoid sharing shoes, socks, or towels
  • Avoid picking or scratching lesions
  • Keep feet dry and change socks or tights daily
3

Treatment Options

For non-facial warts in adults and older children:

  • Topical salicylic acid (12–26%) applied daily for up to 12 weeks
  • Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen (every 2–4 weeks, up to six treatments)
  • Combination therapy with salicylic acid and cryotherapy

For younger children, recommend treatment with topical salicylic acid applied daily for up to 12 weeks. Do not use cryotherapy in younger children.

4

Referral Criteria

Consider referral to a dermatologist if:

  • The person has a facial wart
  • The diagnosis is uncertain
  • The person is immunocompromised
  • The person has areas of skin that are extensively affected
  • The person is troubled by symptoms from persistent warts which are unresponsive to both topical salicylic acid and cryotherapy

Treatment Options

Topical Salicylic Acid

Available preparations include:

  • Duofilm® (salicylic acid 16.7% plus lactic acid 15%)
  • Bazuka® gel (salicylic acid 12% plus lactic acid 4%)
  • Bazuka® extra strength gel (salicylic acid 26%)
  • Occlusal® (salicylic acid 26%)
  • Salactol® (salicylic acid 16.7% plus lactic acid 16.7%)
  • Salatac® gel (salicylic acid 12% plus lactic acid 4%)

Apply once a day, at night. Soak the affected area in warm water for 2–5 minutes before application.

Cryotherapy

Liquid nitrogen cryotherapy:

  • Usually carried out every 2–4 weeks until the wart is gone
  • Maximum of six treatments
  • Only suitable for some older children who are likely to tolerate this treatment
  • A shorter freeze (5–10 seconds) is recommended for plane warts

May cause pain, blistering, infection, scarring, and depigmentation.

Secondary Care Treatment Options

Physical Ablation

  • Surgery
  • Laser
  • Microwave therapy
  • Photodynamic treatment

Antimitotic Treatments

  • Topical podophyllin
  • Topical or oral retinoids
  • Intralesional bleomycin

Immunomodulatory Treatments

  • Topical sensitizers (diphenylcyclopropenone, squaric acid dibutyl ester)
  • Topical imiquimod 5%
  • Intralesional immunotherapy agents

Other Options

  • Virucidal treatments (formaldehyde, glutaraldehyde)
  • Topical chemotherapy agents (5-fluorouracil)
  • Cantharidin (blistering agent)
Patient Advice

General Information

  • Although warts can be cosmetically unsightly, they are not harmful
  • Usually, they do not cause symptoms and resolve without treatment
  • Warts are contagious, but the risk of transmission is thought to be low

To Reduce Transmission

  • Cover the wart with a waterproof plaster when swimming
  • Avoid going barefoot in public places
  • Avoid sharing shoes, socks, or towels

To Limit Personal Spread

  • Avoid picking or scratching lesions
  • Avoid biting nails or sucking fingers that have warts
  • Keep feet dry and change socks or tights daily
  • Dispose of any skin filings hygienically

Activity Participation

  • Children with warts or verrucae should not be excluded from activities
  • Sports and swimming can continue with precautions
  • Take care to minimize transmission

Prescribing Information

Contraindications for Topical Salicylic Acid

Topical salicylic acid should not be used on:

  • The face
  • Intertriginous or anogenital regions
  • Moles or birthmarks
  • Mucous membranes
  • Warts with hair growing out of them, red edges, or an unusual colour
  • Open wounds, irritated or reddened skin, or any area that is infected
  • Areas of poor healing, such as neuropathic feet, or in those with impaired blood circulation
  • Large areas of skin
  • Children under the age of 2 years
Adverse Effects

Topical salicylic acid therapy may cause:

  • Irritation of the skin
  • Contact dermatitis
  • Erythema
  • Burning sensation

Cryotherapy may cause:

  • Pain at the time of application
  • Blistering
  • Infection
  • Scarring
  • Depigmentation

Background Information

Cutaneous warts are small, rough growths caused by infection of keratinocytes with human papillomavirus (HPV). They can appear anywhere on the skin but are commonly seen on the hands and feet.

A verruca (also known as a plantar wart) is a wart on the sole of the foot.

Cutaneous warts may be classified as:

  • Common wart (Verruca vulgaris)
  • Periungual wart — wart around the fingernails or toenails
  • Plantar wart — wart on the sole of the foot (Verruca plantaris)
  • Flat wart or plane wart (Verruca plana)
  • Filiform wart — wart that presents as a cluster of fine fronds emerging from a narrow pedicle base
  • Anogenital wart — wart around the anus or genital area (Condylomata acuminata)

Cutaneous warts are common and can occur at any age, but are unusual in infants and very young children.

Incidence is thought to increase during school years reaching a peak in adolescence and early adulthood before declining in later adulthood.

An estimated 5–30% of school-age children and young adults have warts.

A cross-sectional study of the prevalence of skin disease in adults (n = 12,377) from five European countries identified a lifetime prevalence of warts of 41.3% (95% CI 40.4 to 42.2); 7.3% (95% CI 6.9 to 7.8) of the study population had an active manifestation at the time of interview.

Warts are usually spread by direct skin-to-skin contact, or indirectly via contact with contaminated floors or surfaces.

Risk of developing cutaneous warts is increased if:

  • The epithelial barrier is damaged
  • There are skin conditions causing breaks in the skin
  • Skin is wet and/or macerated
  • There is contact with rough surfaces causing microtrauma to the skin
  • Shaving may spread warts over the beard area
  • New warts may develop along sites of trauma (Koebner phenomenon)
  • An existing wart is scratched, picked or knocked
  • Occupation involves regular direct handling of meat or fish
  • There is frequent immersion in water
  • The person is immunosuppressed

The incubation period for warts is thought to range from a few weeks to more than a year.

Warts may clear spontaneously at any time or persist for years. Prognosis varies from person to person and resolution is usually more rapid in children.

In children:

  • Around a quarter of warts resolve spontaneously within 2-3 months
  • Half clear within a year
  • Two-thirds by 2 years
  • 90% within 5 years

Spontaneous resolution in adults tends to be slower, and warts may persist for 5-10 years.

The Human papillomavirus (HPV) type may influence the likelihood of spontaneous regression.

For people with severe immunosuppression warts may be large, extensive, and resistant to treatment.

Complications may include:

  • Spread caused by picking at the wart
  • Spread to contacts
  • Local infection
  • Changes in skin pigmentation and scarring following destructive treatments
  • Psychosocial impact - teasing at school, embarrassment, unsightly appearance
  • Exclusion from swimming lessons
  • Nail dystrophy and destruction from periungual warts
  • Pain from extensive plantar warts may interfere with walking or sporting activities
  • Malignant changes are thought to be rare, but have been reported in immunosuppressed people
