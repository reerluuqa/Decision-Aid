Red Flag Symptoms
  • Nikolsky sign positive (epidermal detachment)
  • >30% BSA involvement (suspect TEN)
  • Extensive mucosal involvement
  • Signs of sepsis (fever, tachycardia, hypotension)
  • Neurological symptoms (headache, confusion)
  • Immunocompromised status with widespread lesions
üö®

CRITICAL EMERGENCIES - Immediate Action Required

These conditions require immediate recognition, stabilization, and specialist consultation. Do not delay treatment.

ü¶†
Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)
LIFE THREATENING
  • Drug-induced epidermal necrosis (>30% BSA)
  • High mortality (25-35%)
  • Immediate discontinuation of causative drug
  • Transfer to burn ICU/ICU
  • IVIG and wound care urgently needed
ü¶†
Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)
LIFE THREATENING
  • <10% BSA epidermal detachment
  • Severe mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital)
  • Often drug-induced (antibiotics, anticonvulsants, NSAIDs)
  • Admit to hospital for supportive care
  • Ophthalmology consult for ocular involvement
üë∂
Neonatal HSV Infection
HIGH RISK
  • Significant morbidity and mortality
  • Disseminated disease possible (skin, CNS, viscera)
  • Immediate IV acyclovir required
  • LP for CSF analysis if CNS involvement suspected
  • Isolate infant, contact infection control
ü¶†
Disseminated VZV/HSV (Immunocompromised)
HIGH RISK
  • High morbidity and mortality in immunocompromised
  • Widespread cutaneous lesions + visceral involvement
  • Immediate antiviral therapy required
  • Monitor for pneumonia, hepatitis, encephalitis
  • Acyclovir IV at high dose
üö® Emergency Action

For suspected SJS/TEN: Stop all non-essential medications immediately, consult dermatology/burn unit, calculate BSA detachment, initiate supportive care.

‚ö†Ô∏è Urgent Action

For neonatal HSV: Start IV acyclovir (20 mg/kg/dose q8h) within 24 hours of symptom onset to improve outcomes.

Genetic Conditions
  • Epidermolysis Bullosa - Mechanical fragility, blistering from minor trauma. subtypes: EBS (intraepidermal), JEB (junctional), DEB (dystrophic)
  • Incontinentia Pigmenti - X-linked dominant, vesicular ‚Üí verrucous ‚Üí pigmented stages
  • Bullous Ichthyosiform Erythroderma - Congenital, epidermolytic hyperkeratosis

Metabolic Causes
  • Porphyria Cutanea Tarda - Fragile skin, photosensitivity, milia, heme enzyme deficiency
  • Diabetic Bullae (Bullosis Diabeticorum) - Painless, acral, in long-standing diabetes
  • Nutritional Deficiencies - Zinc (acrodermatitis enteropathica), niacin (pellagra), biotin
  • Amyloidosis - Primary systemic, waxy papules, purpura
üíä
Drug-Induced
  • SJS/TEN - Allopurinol, anticonvulsants, antibiotics, NSAIDs
  • Drug-induced Pemphigus - Penicillamine, captopril, ACE inhibitors
  • Linear IgA Disease - Vancomycin (most common)
  • Bullous Pemphigoid - Immune checkpoint inhibitors, furosemide

Other Causes
  • Friction Blisters - Pressure/friction sites, hot/moist environments
  • Burns/Frostbite - Thermal injury with vesicle formation
  • Coma Bullae - Pressure sites in comatose patients
  • Miliaria - Sweat duct occlusion, heat/humidity
  • Eczematous Dermatitis - Acute allergic contact, dyshidrotic eczema

Diagnostic Approach

Follow a systematic approach: History ‚Üí Physical ‚Üí Targeted Investigations ‚Üí Biopsy when indicated.

üìã History Taking Checklist

  • Onset & Duration - Acute vs chronic, evolution of lesions
  • Distribution - Localized vs generalized, specific patterns
  • Symptoms - Pruritus, pain, burning, paresthesia
  • Systemic Symptoms - Fever, malaise, arthralgia
  • Medical History - Diabetes, malignancy, autoimmune disease
  • Medications - New drugs, timing of initiation
  • Allergies - Drug, contact, environmental
  • Family History - Similar conditions, genetic disorders
  • Exposure History - Travel, contacts, arthropod bites
  • Immunocompromised? - HIV, chemotherapy, steroids

‚úÖ Supportive Care - All Patients

Wound care: Non-adherent dressings, gentle cleansing, topical antibiotics for secondary infection. Pain management: Acetaminophen, consider opioids for severe pain. Monitor for infection, fluid/electrolyte imbalance.

üö® SJS/TEN Management

Immediate drug withdrawal, admit to burn unit/ICU, IVIG (1g/kg/day x 3 days), wound care with non-adherent dressings, fluid/electrolyte management, temperature regulation, eye care with ophthalmology.

‚ö†Ô∏è SSSS Management

IV anti-staphylococcal antibiotics (nafcillin, oxacillin, or vancomycin if MRSA), supportive care, fluid management, warm compresses, antipyretics. Prognosis excellent with treatment.

üìû When to Consult

  • Dermatology - All blistering diseases, suspected autoimmune, unclear diagnosis
  • ICU/Burn Unit - SJS/TEN, extensive SSSS
  • Ophthalmology - Ocular involvement (SJS, pemphigoid, PV)
  • ID Consult - Disseminated infection, neonatal HSV, immunocompromised host
  • Pediatrics - Neonatal disease, genetic disorders
  • Oncology - Paraneoplastic pemphigus, suspicion of underlying malignancy
