Molluscum Contagiosum Clinical Guidelines

Last revised: March 2022

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is predominantly clinical based on characteristic lesion appearance:

Quick Reference: Diagnostic Features

  • Smooth-surfaced, firm, dome-shaped papules
  • Flesh-colored or pearly white with central umbilication
  • Usually 2-5 mm in diameter (can be larger in immunocompromised)
  • Commonly 1-30 lesions, occurring in clusters
  • In children: trunk, flexures, anogenital areas
  • In adults: genitalia, pubis, thighs, lower abdomen

Differential Diagnosis

  • Common warts (verruca vulgaris)
  • Acne vulgaris
  • Folliculitis
  • Condyloma acuminatum
  • Basal cell carcinoma (atypical presentations)

Red Flags & Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral Required

  • HIV patients with extensive molluscum lesions → HIV specialist
  • Eyelid-margin or ocular lesions with red eye → Ophthalmology

Consider Referral

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Known immunocompromised patient
  • Extensive and painful lesions
  • Adults with anogenital lesions → GUM clinic for STI screening

Quick Reference: Children with Anogenital Lesions

Important: In children, lesions are commonly seen in anogenital areas. Referral for suspected sexual abuse should ONLY be arranged if there is other evidence to suggest this.

Lesions in children may result from autoinoculation or non-sexual contact.

Management: Non-Genital Molluscum

Primary Care Approach

  • Reassure that condition is self-limiting (usually resolves within 18 months)
  • Treatment not usually required in immunocompetent patients
  • General advice to avoid spread of infection
  • Manage symptoms/complications as needed

Patient Advice

  • Avoid sharing towels, clothing, bedding
  • Don't scratch or squeeze lesions
  • No exclusion from school/gym/swimming
  • Cover lesions with waterproof bandages before swimming

Quick Reference: Treatment Options (Limited Evidence)

No single treatment has been shown to be convincingly effective in immunocompetent people:

  • Imiquimod 5% cream
  • Podophyllotoxin 0.5% (off-label)
  • Cryotherapy

Note: Evidence for efficacy is limited. Treatment may be considered for cosmetic reasons or if lesions are problematic.

Management: Anogenital Molluscum

Adults

  • Refer to GUM clinic for STI screening (including HIV)
  • Avoid shaving/waxing genital regions
  • Use condoms (partial protection only)
  • Consider cryotherapy, podophyllotoxin 0.5% (off-label), or imiquimod 5% cream

Children

  • Anogenital lesions common in children
  • Sexual abuse referral ONLY with other evidence
  • Conservative management usually appropriate
  • Same general advice as non-genital molluscum

Background & Risk Factors

Risk Factors

  • Close contact with infected person
  • Atopic dermatitis (prevalence 20-45% vs 5-12%)
  • Immunocompromise (HIV, transplants, immunosuppressive therapy)
  • Warm climates
  • Swimming

Epidemiology

  • Most common in children 1-14 years
  • Peak incidence: preschool children (1-4 years)
  • No significant gender or ethnic differences
  • In adults: often sexually transmitted (MCV-2 subtype)

Quick Reference: Complications

  • Emotional/psychological distress (cosmetic appearance)
  • Pruritus & erythema (30% of cases)
  • Scarring (7%, rarely permanent)
  • Bacterial superinfection
  • Ocular complications (lid margin lesions)
  • Hypersensitivity reaction (molluscum dermatitis, 10%)

Treatment Options

Pharmacological

  • Imiquimod 5% cream - Immune response modifier
  • Podophyllotoxin 0.5% - Antimitotic (off-label for molluscum)
  • Potassium hydroxide - Topical keratolytic
  • Salicylic acid - Topical keratolytic

Physical Interventions

  • Cryotherapy - Liquid nitrogen application
  • Curettage - Surgical removal
  • Laser therapy - For extensive or resistant cases
  • Cantharidin - Topical blistering agent

Key Messages for Patients

  • Benign, self-limiting condition
  • Usually resolves within 6-18 months
  • Treatment often not necessary
  • Good hygiene prevents spread
  • No need to exclude from activities
