Bullous Pemphigoid

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm

Based on BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Clinical evaluation and risk stratification

Key Diagnostic Factors
Age 60-90 years Common

Mean onset 65 years, prevalence increases dramatically >90 years

Pruritus Common

May precede lesions by 3-4 weeks, variable intensity

Tense blisters Common

On normal or erythematous skin, flexor surfaces favored

High-Risk Features Urgent
Age ≥86 years High Mortality

2-6x increased mortality risk compared to general population

Generalized disease Severe

Extensive skin involvement with poor general condition

Low Karnofsky score Poor Prognosis

Independently predicts death at 6 months

Clinical Presentation

Prodromal phase: Pruritus with eczematous or urticarial lesions for weeks to months

Bullous stage: Tense vesicles/bullae on normal/erythematous skin, clear serous fluid (may become bloody)

Distribution: Symmetrical, favors flexural aspects of extremities, lower trunk, abdomen

Oral lesions: Present in up to 30% of patients (usually minor)

History & Exam Checklist
  • Onset and evolution of lesions
  • Neurological/cardiovascular comorbidities
  • Complete skin examination (head-to-toe)
  • Oral mucosa examination (up to 30% involvement)
  • Assessment of disease extent (localized vs widespread)

Diagnosis

Confirmatory investigations and differential diagnosis

1st Line Investigations

Skin Biopsy for Histopathology

  • Excise entire blister with adjacent normal tissue if possible
  • Biopsy newly formed blister without trauma to tissue
  • Findings: Sub-epidermal blister with eosinophil-rich dermal infiltrate

Direct Immunofluorescence (BEST TEST)

  • Biopsy peri-lesional uninvolved skin
  • Specimen handling: snap-freeze or place in Michel's media
  • Positive result: Linear band of IgG and/or C3 at basement membrane zone

Indirect Immunofluorescence

  • Performed on patient serum if DIF positive
  • Positive result: Antibodies to bullous pemphigoid antigens
  • Positive in up to 96% of patients (may be lower if prior immunosuppression)
Additional Tests
ELISA for BP Antigens

Highly specific/sensitive if immunofluorescence non-diagnostic

Immunoblotting

75% react with BP230, 50% with BP180

Immunoprecipitation

More sensitive than immunoblotting (uses native protein)

Diagnostic Criteria Summary
  • Prodromal pruritus with eczematous/urticarial lesions
  • Tense bullae on normal/erythematous skin
  • Sub-epidermal blister with eosinophils on histology
  • Linear IgG/C3 at basement membrane zone on DIF
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Differentiating Features Diagnostic Test
Pemphigus Vulgaris Erosive lesions, no tense bullae, more oral lesions Suprabasilar IgG/C3 on DIF
Epidermolysis Bullosa Acquisita Trauma-induced blisters, scarring Salt-split skin test (IgG on dermal side)
Linear IgA Dermatosis Bimodal age, "string of pearls" appearance Linear IgA deposition on DIF
Dermatitis Herpetiformis Extensor surface involvement, no oral lesions Granular IgA in dermal papillae

Treatment

Evidence-based management algorithm

Treatment Algorithm Overview

Localized Disease Mild

Lesions limited to small body area

Widespread Disease Moderate-Severe

Extensive involvement requiring systemic therapy

Treatment-Resistant Refractory

Inadequate response to initial therapy

Localized Disease Treatment
High-Potency Topical Corticosteroids
Clobetasol 0.05% apply sparingly twice daily for up to 2 weeks
Topical Tacrolimus (Alternative)
0.1% apply sparingly twice daily for up to 2 weeks

Useful for limited disease without skin atrophy risk

Note: Avoid widespread application due to systemic absorption risk

Widespread Disease - Systemic Corticosteroids
Prednisolone First-line
0.5-2 mg/kg/day orally

Taper: Maintain until new blister formation ceases, then taper slowly over 6-9 months
Note: Starting doses >0.75 mg/kg/day provide no additional benefit

Adjunctive Therapy (Corticosteroid-sparing)

Antibiotic + Nicotinamide Combination
Tetracycline 250-500mg QID OR Doxycycline 50-100mg daily/bid
PLUS Nicotinamide 500-2000mg once daily at bedtime

Allows faster corticosteroid taper (over several months)

Ciclosporin
2.5-4 mg/kg/day orally in 2 divided doses

Evidence conflicting; mainly beneficial with concomitant oral corticosteroids

Contraindication to Corticosteroids
Dapsone
50mg once daily (increase to 2 mg/kg/day in children)

Check G6PD deficiency first (especially in black males ~10% prevalence)

Methotrexate (if concomitant psoriasis)
7.5-25mg once weekly + Folic acid 1mg daily (except MTX day)
Azathioprine
50-200mg once daily (max 2.5 mg/kg/day)
Treatment-Resistant Disease Refractory

If inadequate response to initial therapy:

Add Immunosuppressant
Azathioprine 50-200mg/day
OR Mycophenolate 500-1000mg bid
OR Cyclophosphamide 2-3 mg/kg/day
Severe Cases
Plasmapheresis OR IVIG 400mg/kg/day × 5 days
AND/OR Rituximab (consult specialist)
Childhood Bullous Pemphigoid

Usually self-limited with appropriate therapy:

Mild Cases
Topical or oral corticosteroids
Moderate-Severe
Prednisolone 0.5-2 mg/kg/day
OR Erythromycin 30-50 mg/kg/day + Nicotinamide
Symptom Control
Sedating Antihistamines
Hydroxyzine 25mg q6-8h (max 100mg/day)
OR Diphenhydramine 25-50mg q4-6h

Effective for pruritus control in most patients

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications and prognosis

Monitoring Protocol
Frequency

Every 1-2 months during dose reduction, then as needed

Assessment

Clinical criteria (not immunofluorescence) for dose adjustments

Relapse Risk

~50% relapse during dose-reduction period

Long-term monitoring: For patients on immunosuppressive regimens, appropriate interval monitoring is required

Complications High Risk
Complication Timeframe Likelihood Prevention/Management
Corticosteroid-induced osteoporosis Long-term High Bisphosphonates (alendronate/risedronate) for high-risk patients
Secondary infection Variable High Monitor denuded skin areas, prompt treatment
Death First year Low Risk 6-41%, mainly from infection/sepsis
Prognosis
Mortality Rate

6-41% in first year (higher in Europe: 19-41% vs US: 6-12%)

Remission

Most achieve clinical remission with appropriate treatment

Prognostic Factors

Age ≥86, poor general condition, female sex, generalized disease

Course: Chronic disease with spontaneous exacerbations and remissions

Supportive Measures
  • Gastric protection: Consider for all patients on systemic corticosteroids
  • Bone protection: Post-menopausal women, men >50, or prednisolone ≥7.5mg/day for ≥3 months
  • Wound care: Appropriate dressings for erosions to prevent secondary infection
