Melanoma Assessment & Management Guide

A comprehensive clinical reference for healthcare professionals

Last revised: July 2022

Patient Assessment

History

  • When was the lesion first noticed?
  • Any changes in size, shape, or color?
  • Any ulceration, itching, or bleeding?
  • Associated symptoms (cough, weight loss, fatigue, night sweats, headache)?
  • Personal or family history of melanoma?
  • Risk factors: sunburn history, tanning bed use, skin type, number of moles

Physical Examination

  • Examine in good light with/without magnification
  • Check entire skin surface including scalp and mucous membranes
  • Palpate regional lymph nodes if lesion suspicious
  • Look for "Ugly Duckling sign" (lesions different from surrounding moles)

Score ≥3 = Suspicious - Refer Urgently

Clinical Types of Melanoma

Superficial Spreading (60-70%)

Most common type, often stays within epidermis for long periods, usually spreads horizontally. Found on sun-exposed sites.

Nodular (15-30%)

Atypical nodule that may ulcerate and bleed easily. Can penetrate deep within skin within months of appearance.

Lentigo Maligna (5-15%)

Irregularly-shaped brown macule growing slowly. Most common in people over 60 on sun-damaged skin.

Acral Lentiginous (5-10%)

Most common on soles of feet, palms, and nail bed. More common in darker skin types and people over 40.

Risk Factors for Melanoma

Personal history of skin cancer or atypical naevi
Family history of melanoma
Pale skin that burns easily (Fitzpatrick Type I-II)
Red or light-coloured hair
Light coloured eyes (blue, green)
History of sunburn, especially in childhood
Use of tanning beds (10+ sessions)
Increasing age
Outdoor occupation
Immunosuppression
Genetic syndromes (xeroderma pigmentosum)
Large number of moles or large congenital naevi

Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral (2-week suspected cancer pathway)

  • Weighted 7-point checklist score ≥3
  • Dermoscopy suggests melanoma
  • Nail changes (new pigmented line, lesion under nail)
  • New persistent skin condition with unclear diagnosis
  • Any doubt about the lesion or recent change
  • Biopsy confirmed malignant melanoma

Consider Urgent Referral

  • Lesion suggesting nodular melanoma
  • Any major feature in 7-point checklist
  • Any ABCDE features

Routine Referral

  • Giant congenital pigmented hairy naevi (≥20cm)
  • Family history of ≥3 melanoma cases

Information to Include with Referral

  • Detailed description of the lesion
  • Duration and any changes noted
  • 7-point checklist score
  • Patient's risk factors
  • Any relevant medical history
  • If biopsy performed: copy of pathology report with tumor thickness and excision margin details

Management in Primary Care

DO NOT

  • Biopsy suspected melanoma in primary care
  • Excise suspected melanoma in primary care
  • Delay referral if suspicious features present

DO

  • Refer urgently if suspicious
  • Provide prevention advice to high-risk patients
  • Document findings thoroughly
  • Take clear photographs if possible (with consent)
  • Examine entire skin surface

Documentation

  • Size, shape, color, and borders of the lesion
  • Any changes noted by patient or clinician
  • 7-point checklist score
  • Risk factors assessment
  • Referral details and urgency
  • Patient education provided
  • Follow-up plan

Prevention Advice for High-Risk Patients

Regular skin self-examination
Sun protection (SPF 30+, protective clothing)
Avoid tanning beds
Avoid peak sun hours (10am-4pm)
Wear wide-brimmed hats
Wear UV-protective sunglasses
Protect children from sun exposure
Regular skin checks by healthcare professional
Take photos of moles for comparison
Prompt reporting of new/changing lesions

Patient Education

  • Teach ABCDE rule for melanoma detection:
    • Asymmetry
    • Border irregularity
    • Color variation
    • Diameter >6mm
    • Evolving (changing)
  • Explain importance of early detection
  • Provide written materials for reference
  • Discuss appropriate sun protection measures
  • Address patient concerns and questions
