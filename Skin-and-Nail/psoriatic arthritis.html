URGENT: High-Risk Presentations

Polyarticular involvement, dactylitis, or elevated ESR/CRP require prompt escalation to DMARDs within 3 months

Red Flags & Warning Signs

URGENT

Severe PsA Criteria

  • Erosive disease on imaging
  • Elevated ESR/CRP attributable to PsA
  • Joint deformities with functional impairment
  • Dactylitis - high-risk presentation
  • Rapidly progressive disease
  • Function-limiting disease at few sites

Medication Contraindications

TNF Inhibitors - AVOID if:

Congestive heart failure, previous serious/recurrent infections, demyelinating disease

IL-17 Inhibitors - AVOID if:

Concomitant inflammatory bowel disease (lack of evidence for effectiveness)

Systemic Corticosteroids - AVOID if possible

Risk of post-exposure psoriasis flare; increased CV risk even at low doses (<5mg/day)

JAK Inhibitors - FDA Warnings

Increased risk of death, blood clots, MI, cancer. Reserve after TNF inhibitor failure.

Clinical Presentation

DIAGNOSIS

Pattern Classification (Moll & Wright)

DIP Arthritis Asymmetrical Oligoarthritis Symmetrical Polyarthritis Arthritis Mutilans Psoriatic Spondylitis

Arthritis Mutilans: Severe destructive form with osteolysis ("pencil-in-cup" deformity)

Key Symptoms

  • Morning stiffness >30 minutes
  • Morning first-step foot pain
  • Lower extremity predominance early
  • Ray pattern (individual digit) vs row pattern

Dactylitis

Uniform swelling of entire digit ("sausage digit")

Key Points

• Most common in 3rd/4th toes (40-50% of patients)
• Majority swelling is in flexor tenosynovium
• Not seen in RA
• High-risk feature for progression

Enthesitis

Inflammation at tendon/ligament-bone attachment sites (30-50% of patients)

Common Sites

• Heel: Plantar fascia, Achilles tendon
• Elbow: Epicondyles
• Hip: Lateral (trochanteric)

Spinal Involvement

  • Cervical spine most severely affected
  • Asymmetrical sacroiliitis characteristic
  • 25-33% have isolated spondylitis without sacroiliitis

Skin & Nail Manifestations

Skin: Red, scaly plaques on elbows, knees, scalp

Nail: Pitting or onycholysis in 80-90% of PsA patients

Inspection Tips

Check scalp, intergluteal fold, perineum, periumbilical regions in patients without known psoriasis

Treatment Algorithm

TREAT-TO-TARGET

Treat-to-Target Timeline

1

Initial Assessment

Evaluate disease activity using DAPSA, PsAJAI, or CPDAI composite measures. Set treatment target.

2

3-Month Review

DMARDs should show ≥50% improvement in signs and symptoms. If not, escalate therapy.

3

6-Month Target

Achieve clinical remission of musculoskeletal and extra-articular manifestations.

Peripheral Arthritis Treatment

Step 1: NSAIDs + Corticosteroids (Adjunct)

NSAIDs: Effective for pain/stiffness. Use continuously, not PRN.

US Guidelines: Failed if 2+ NSAIDs at max dose for 1 month with incomplete response for 2 months.

Intra-articular steroids: Preferred for mono/oligoarthritis. Avoid long-acting in superficial joints (risk of atrophy).

Step 2: csDMARDs (First-Line)

Methotrexate: Preferred (once-weekly dosing, rapid onset, good for psoriasis). Trial for 3 months. Requires folic acid.

Alternatives: Sulfasalazine, Leflunomide (less effective for skin).

Alternative if inadequate response: Switch to different csDMARD.

Step 3: Biologics / Targeted DMARDs

TNF inhibitors: First-line biologic option (etanercept, adalimumab, infliximab, golimumab, certolizumab)

IL-17 inhibitors: Secukinumab, Ixekizumab (after TNF or if TNF contraindicated + severe psoriasis)

IL-23 inhibitors: Guselkumab, Risankizumab (after csDMARD + biologic failure)

JAK inhibitors: Tofacitinib, Upadacitinib (after TNF failure - FDA warning)

Apremilast: PDE-4 inhibitor, first-line or after csDMARD failure

Axial Disease Treatment

First-Line

NSAIDs continuously for up to 12 weeks (some relief by 4 weeks)

Second-Line

TNF inhibitors or IL-17 inhibitors if NSAIDs fail

Special Situations

• Recurrent uveitis/IBD: Monoclonal anti-TNF over etanercept
• Severe psoriasis/contraindications: IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab)
• IL-12/23 & IL-23 inhibitors: Not effective for axial disease

Monitoring Requirements

FOLLOW-UP

Clinical Assessment (Every Visit)

  • Peripheral joint count: Swollen + tender
  • Enthesitis count: Tender entheses
  • Skin assessment: PASI or BSA
  • Patient global VAS: Disease activity + pain
  • Physical function: HAQ, SF-36, or PROMIS
  • Spine mobility: Modified Schober, cervical rotation

Laboratory & Imaging

Initial Investigations

• RF + Anti-CCP (distinguish from RA)
• ESR or CRP (inflammation markers)
• Lipid profile, fasting glucose, uric acid (metabolic screening)
• X-ray hands/feet (40% show damage)

Biologic Pre-Treatment

• Chest X-ray
• TB screening (PPD or IGRA)
• Hepatitis B/C status

Comorbidities & Risk Factors

SCREENING

Strong Risk Factors

Psoriasis

20% of psoriasis patients have PsA; rises to 25% in moderate-severe disease

Family History

40x increased risk with 1st-degree relative with PsA

Associated Conditions

Metabolic Syndrome Cardiovascular Disease Uveitis (8%) Inflammatory Bowel Disease Depression Obesity Diabetes NAFLD

Screening Recommendations

BP, lipid profile, fasting glucose, weight/BMI at every visit

Extra-Articular Manifestations

Manifestation Prevalence
Uveitis 8%
Crohn's Disease Increased risk
Ulcerative Colitis Increased risk

Special Populations

HIV+ Patients

Significantly higher PsA risk. A Zambian study: 96% of new PsA diagnoses were HIV+ (vs 30% general population)

Diabetes & Methotrexate

Increased risk of liver toxicity and fatty liver disease
