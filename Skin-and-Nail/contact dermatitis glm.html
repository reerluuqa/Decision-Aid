Contact Dermatitis – Quick Reference Algorithm

Condensed aide-mémoire for rapid decision-making in primary care (all ages from 1 month).

NICE CKS (Jan 2024) Light theme Printable
Use alongside local formulary guidance. Consider patch testing / dermatology referral if diagnosis remains unclear.
1

Suspect diagnosis

  • Acute: erythema, vesicles; Chronic: dryness, fissuring, lichenification.
  • Irritant: burning/stinging, localized, improves quickly with avoidance.
  • Allergic: pruritus, delayed (24–72h), may spread beyond contact site.
Hands Face/Eyelids Ears/Scalp Neck Axillae Anogenital Lower legs Feet
2

Rule out differentials

  • Atopic/seborrhoeic dermatitis
  • Infections (tinea, impetigo/cellulitis, HSV/VZV), scabies
  • Urticaria, psoriasis, photosensitivity, lupus, drug eruption
  • Consider safeguarding in children if non-accidental injury suspected
3

Focused history

  • Timing vs. exposures, initial site → spread, intermittent vs. persistent.
  • Occupational & household/recreational exposures (detergents, water, PPE, metals, cosmetics, meds, plants, cement, glues).
  • Improves on weekends/holidays? Past atopy/dermatitis; family history.
4

Targeted examination

  • Distribution suggests trigger (e.g., hands→wet work; neck→nickel; eyelids→nail varnish/cosmetics; scalp→hair dye; feet→rubber/leather/adhesives).
  • Look for infection: heat, discharge, systemic features.
5

Investigations

Patch testing = gold standard if diagnosis/trigger uncertain → refer dermatology.

6

Core management

  • Avoidance of trigger (expect 8–12 weeks to improve).
  • If unavoidable: substitute products, rinse promptly, use cotton-lined gloves; avoid chemical/water build-up under jewellery.
  • Skin care: liberal emollients; soap substitutes; avoid aqueous cream.
  • Safety: fire risk advice with paraffin-based emollients.
7

Pharmacological

  • Topical corticosteroids by age, site, severity & extent.
  • Assess & treat secondary infection (impetigo/cellulitis) when suspected.
8

Referral criteria

  • Severe, chronic, recurrent, or treatment-resistant cases.
  • Previously stable disease now uncontrolled.
  • Suspected allergy to OTC/prescribed topical agents.
  • Unclear/atypical presentation; diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Suspected occupational dermatitis → discuss workplace controls.
9

Occupational considerations

  • When work-related dermatitis confirmed: employer must assess risk and control exposure (duty of care; adapt duties/PPE as appropriate).
  • Employer has legal duty to report to the Health & Safety Executive (HSE).
