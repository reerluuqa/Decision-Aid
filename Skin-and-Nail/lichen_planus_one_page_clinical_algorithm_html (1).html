Lichen Planus — Rapid Clinical Algorithm

Mnemonic: 6 P’s — Pruritic • Purple • Polygonal • Planar • Papules • Plaques
Resolution: cutaneous often 12–24 mo
Oral SCC risk: ~1.1% → surveillance

1) Initial Assessment

Typical presentation

  • Intensely pruritic, flat‑topped, violaceous 2–5 mm papules ± Wickham’s striae
  • Sites: flexor wrists/forearms, shins/ankles (hypertrophic common), lumbar, genitalia, oral mucosa
  • Occasional blisters; post‑inflammatory hyperpigmentation when resolving

Ask / examine

  • Extent: skin, oral, genital, scalp, nails; itch severity & duration
  • Triggers: new drugs; trauma (Köbner); HBV/HCV risks; family/autoimmune history

Variants

  • Hypertrophic; Erosive/ulcerative (mucosa); Follicular / lichen planopilaris (scarring alopecia)
  • Annular; Atrophic; Vesicular/Bullous; LP pemphigoides (rare)
  • Actinic (sun‑exposed; avoid phototherapy); LP pigmentosus

Associations

  • Hepatitis B/C (↑ prevalence)
  • Primary biliary cholangitis
  • Autoimmune / family tendency
  • Oral LP: heat sensitivity; historical amalgam link

2) Differentials

  • Lichenoid drug eruption (thiazides, antimalarials, gold)
  • Psoriasis, eczema
  • Discoid lupus erythematosus
  • Lichen sclerosus, leukoplakia, pemphigus
  • GvHD, sarcoidosis, cutaneous lymphoma

3) Investigations

  • Clinical Dx usually sufficient
  • Biopsy if atypical → saw‑tooth hyperplasia; band‑like lymphocytic infiltrate at DEJ; globular IgM on DIF
  • HBsAg & HCV screen where risk factors or oral/genital LP
  • If drug suspected: stop/replace where safe and review

4) Stepwise Management

Local / Symptomatic (first‑line)

  • Potent topical corticosteroids (incl. steroid tape)
  • Calcineurin inhibitors for mucosa: tacrolimus ointment / pimecrolimus cream
  • Topical retinoids for refractory lesions
  • Intralesional steroids for localized hypertrophic plaques
  • Antihistamines (consider sedating at night)

Systemic (extensive / resistant / severe local)

  • Prednisolone short course (1–3 months) ± adjunct
  • Consider: acitretin; hydroxychloroquine; methotrexate; azathioprine; mycophenolate mofetil
  • Phototherapy (NB‑UVB/PUVA); avoid in actinic LP
  • Anecdotal: metronidazole, sulfasalazine, griseofulvin, JAK inhibitors

5) Follow‑up & Prognosis

  • Cutaneous LP: often resolves in 12–24 months (hypertrophic may persist)
  • Mucosal/genital LP: chronic/relapsing; pruritus tends to lessen
  • Oral/genital surveillance 1–2×/year where indicated

Red Flags (urgent)

  • Erosive/ulcerative oral or genital disease
  • Scarring alopecia (lichen planopilaris)
  • Nail destruction / severe dystrophy
  • Suspicious oral/vulval lesion → biopsy (OSCC risk ~1.1%)

One‑Glance Algorithm

  1. Suspect LP → pruritic violaceous flat papules ± Wickham’s; check classic sites
  2. Rule out mimics → drug, psoriasis/eczema, DLE, LS, leukoplakia, pemphigus, GvHD
  3. Confirm if needed → biopsy atypical; screen HBV/HCV when indicated
  4. Treat → potent topical ± calcineurin; intralesional for hypertrophic; escalate to short prednisolone ± systemic; phototherapy (not actinic)
  5. Monitor → symptom relief, lesion regression; schedule oral/genital surveillance; act on red flags
