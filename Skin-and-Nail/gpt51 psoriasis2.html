1. First contact ‚Äì assess
Confirm psoriasis, assess impact, and rule out emergencies.
Clinic starting point ‚ñ∂

1A. Confirm pattern

Consider: plaque, scalp, facial, flexural/genital, guttate, nail, pustular, erythrodermic.

1B. Red flags ‚Äì same-day emergency dermatology

  • Generalized pustular psoriasis ‚Äì rapidly developing widespread erythema with lakes of sterile pustules, systemic illness (fever, malaise, tachycardia, weight loss).
  • Erythrodermic psoriasis ‚Äì > 90% BSA, diffuse erythema, often warm to touch with systemic features (fever, lymphadenopathy, oedema, haemodynamic compromise).

1C. Focused history

  • Symptoms ‚Äì itch, soreness, burning, bleeding, scaling; pattern of flares and remissions.
  • Triggers ‚Äì infections (particularly streptococcal), trauma (Koebner), drugs, stress, alcohol, smoking, pregnancy-related change.
  • Comorbidities ‚Äì obesity, metabolic syndrome, IBD, cardiovascular disease.
  • Impact ‚Äì work, sleep, daily activities, relationships; use DLQI/PGA if helpful.
  • Treatments tried ‚Äì prescription and OTC; response and tolerability.

1D. Screen for psoriatic arthritis

  • Ask about inflammatory joint pain, stiffness, swelling, dactylitis, enthesitis (Achilles/plantar fascia), inflammatory back pain.
  • Use PEST tool where available ‚Äì score ‚â• 3 ‚Üí rheumatology referral.

1E. Examination

  • Inspect entire skin where possible: record type, distribution, size/shape, scale, colour, and BSA (Rule of 9s).
  • Look for Auspitz sign (pinpoint bleeding after scale removal) and post-inflammatory colour change.
  • Check nails (pitting, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis, ‚Äúoil drop‚Äù sign) and joints for swelling/tenderness.
2. General principles ‚Äì all psoriasis types
Set expectations, support self-management, and address risk factors.
Applies to everyone ‚ñ∂
Chronic relapsing‚Äìremitting Not infectious Control > cure
  • Explain disease course ‚Äì chronic relapsing condition; goal is good symptom control, not permanent cure.
  • Reassure ‚Äì psoriasis is not contagious.
  • Emollients for all ‚Äì regular use to reduce scaling and itch; ointments for thicker plaques.
  • Address modifiable factors ‚Äì smoking cessation, healthy alcohol intake, weight reduction in overweight/obesity, optimise CVD risk factors.
  • Mental health ‚Äì screen for distress, anxiety and depression; offer appropriate support/referral.
  • Follow-up ‚Äì routine review ~4 weeks after starting or changing treatment.
3. Chronic plaque psoriasis ‚Äì trunk & limbs
Topical-first, with clear steps for escalation.
Plaque disease ‚ñ∂

3A. First-line topical therapy

  • Potent topical corticosteroid once daily up to 4 weeks, OR
  • Vitamin D analogue once or twice daily (if steroid-sparing preferred), OR
  • Combination potent steroid + vitamin D once daily (often convenient).

Choose formulation: creams/gels for larger areas; ointments for thick, scaly plaques.

3B. Review at 4 weeks

  • Good response ‚Äì continue until clear or nearly clear, then:
    • Avoid potent steroid use > 8 weeks at one site without a steroid-free interval.
    • Consider maintenance with vitamin D analogue and intermittent short steroid courses.
  • Poor or partial response:
    • Check adherence and application technique.
    • Consider changing vehicle (cream ‚Üí ointment/gel), or alternating potent steroid with vitamin D.
    • Add keratolytic (e.g. salicylic acid) if scale is heavy.

3C. If still inadequate after appropriate topical trials

  • Assess BSA, PGA, QoL impact, and joint symptoms.
  • Refer to dermatology for consideration of phototherapy or systemic/biologic therapy (see ‚ÄúWhen to refer‚Äù).
4. Scalp psoriasis
Vehicle choice is key ‚Äì think solutions, gels, foams, or shampoos.
Hair-bearing area ‚ñ∂

4A. First-line

  • Potent topical corticosteroid (e.g. solution, foam, mousse, shampoo) once daily up to 4 weeks.
  • Use scalp emollients/keratolytics (e.g. salicylic, oils, emollient preparations) to lift thick scale before applying steroid.

4B. If inadequate at 4 weeks

  • Check adherence and technique, switch to an alternative formulation if necessary.
  • Consider:
    • Different potent steroid formulation, and/or
    • Adding or optimising keratolytic use.
    • Combined potent steroid + vitamin D, or vitamin D alone (mild disease or steroid intolerance).

4C. Ongoing failure

  • For adults, a very-potent steroid may be used short-term (up to 2 weeks) in selected cases.
  • Consider coal tar-based scalp treatments.
  • Refer to dermatology if resistant beyond ~8 weeks, extensive, or with major impact on QoL.
5. Facial, flexural & genital psoriasis
High-risk sites ‚Äì use lower-potency and non-steroid options.
Sensitive areas ‚ñ∂

5A. General

  • Confirm diagnosis (consider seborrhoeic dermatitis, candidal intertrigo, eczema, etc.).
  • Explain chronicity and non-infectious nature; address embarrassment, sexual and relationship impact.

5B. Topical treatment (short-course, low-risk)

  • Mild-potency topical steroid short-term, carefully limited by duration and area, or
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors (e.g. tacrolimus/pimecrolimus where locally recommended) as steroid-sparing options.

5C. If difficult to control

  • Re-evaluate diagnosis and triggers (friction, moisture, infections).
  • Refer to dermatology for advice on longer-term regimens and adjunctive therapies.
6. Guttate psoriasis
Often post-streptococcal and self-limiting.
Acute droplet lesions ‚ñ∂

6A. Typical features

  • Sudden eruption of numerous small (< 1 cm) ‚Äúdrop-like‚Äù scaly papules, mainly trunk and proximal limbs.
  • Often follows streptococcal upper respiratory tract infection or other infections (including reported post-COVID cases).

6B. Management

  • Explain that disease often resolves over 3‚Äì4 months.
  • Emollients and mild‚Äìmoderate topical steroids for itch and inflammation as needed.
  • Consider throat swab and treatment of streptococcal infection if clinically indicated.
  • Refer to dermatology if:
    • Severe or extensive disease,
    • No improvement or progression after several months, or
    • Diagnostic uncertainty.
7. Nail psoriasis
Often slow to respond; consider functional and cosmetic impact.
Nail involvement ‚ñ∂

7A. Patient advice

  • Improvement typically lags behind skin/joint disease; months are often needed.
  • Keep nails short; avoid trauma, manicure of cuticles, and prosthetic nails.
  • Non-acetone varnish may be used to camouflage pitting/oncholysis (avoid harsh removers).

7B. When to treat / refer

  • Mild disease, little impact ‚Äì may need no specific treatment beyond general nail care advice.
  • Moderate‚Äìsevere disease:
    • Consider alternative diagnoses (e.g. fungal nail infection) and investigate if needed.
    • Refer to dermatology for options such as intralesional, systemic, or biologic therapy where appropriate.
8. Pustular psoriasis
Differentiate localized from generalized ‚Äì one is an emergency.
High-risk subtype ‚ñ∂

8A. Generalized pustular psoriasis

  • Systemically unwell with widespread erythema and coalescing sterile pustules.
  • Immediate same-day dermatology assessment and hospital management.

8B. Localized (palmoplantar) pustular psoriasis

  • Pustules and erythema confined to palms/soles, often in smokers.
  • Offer support; provide information resources.
  • Refer to dermatology ‚Äì urgency according to severity and functional limitation.
9. When to refer
Clear referral triggers for dermatology and rheumatology.
Escalation criteria ‚ñ∂

9A. Immediate same-day dermatology

  • Suspected generalized pustular psoriasis or erythrodermic psoriasis.

9B. Urgent rheumatology

  • Suspected psoriatic arthritis (new inflammatory joint pain, dactylitis, enthesitis, axial symptoms).
  • PEST ‚â• 3 or any clear clinical suspicion.

9C. Routine dermatology referral

  • Uncertain diagnosis ‚Äì atypical morphology, distribution, or failure to behave as expected.
  • Extensive psoriasis ‚Äì e.g. > 10% BSA or at least moderate on PGA.
  • Failure or intolerance of appropriate topical therapy in primary care.
  • Significant impact on physical, psychological, or social wellbeing (even with limited BSA).
  • Challenging sites ‚Äì face, genitals, flexures, palms/soles, nails with major functional or cosmetic impact.
  • Need for specialist treatments ‚Äì phototherapy, systemic agents, biologics.
10. Follow-up & monitoring
Plan reviews, safety checks, and comorbidity assessments.
Safety & long-term care ‚ñ∂

10A. Short-term

  • Review at ~4 weeks after initiating or changing topical therapy:
    • Assess severity (e.g. PGA), BSA, symptom relief, and impact on life.
    • Check adherence, ease of use, and any local steroid side effects (atrophy, telangiectasia, striae).

10B. Ongoing follow-up

  • Review at intervals guided by activity and treatment ‚Äì often every 3‚Äì6 months in primary care for stable mild disease.
  • In people needing repeated potent steroid courses, review at least annually to:
    • Optimise regimens and check for adverse cutaneous effects.
    • Reassess need for referral if disease control remains suboptimal.

10C. Comorbidity risk assessment

  • Assess cardiovascular risk (lipids, blood pressure, diabetes risk) at diagnosis and at least every 5 years in adults with severe psoriasis.
  • Discuss venous thromboembolism risk, particularly in severe disease or with additional risk factors.
  • Screen annually for psoriatic arthritis symptoms in people with established psoriasis.

üìå Practical tips

Use vehicle and potency appropriate to site and thickness, and limit potent steroids at any one site to short repeated courses with clear breaks.

  • Ointments for thick plaques; solutions/foams/shampoos for hair-bearing areas.
  • Involve patients in choosing formulations they can realistically apply.
  • Record BSA and a simple global score (e.g. PGA) to track response.
