Alopecia Areata – Fast Decision Aid

Clean, one-page reference for rapid recognition, assessment, management in primary care, and referral triggers. Built from the provided comprehensive guidance.

Key Messages

CourseRelapsing–remitting; many remit spontaneously
Primary RxPotent topical corticosteroid (3 months)
Red FlagsSevere >50%, children, pregnancy, diagnostic doubt
PsychosocialAssess & support every time

Severity Guide

  • Mild Patchy, <25% scalp
  • Moderate 25–50% scalp
  • Severe >50% scalp

Severity informs prognosis and referral urgency.

Algorithm

1) Suspect Alopecia Areata
  • Sudden, patchy, well-circumscribed loss (scalp/beard/brows/lashes).
  • Smooth skin with follicular openings; ± exclamation mark hairs.
  • Nail changes: pitting, ridging, onycholysis, leukonychia.

Exclude mimics: tinea capitis, trichotillomania, traction, androgenetic alopecia, telogen/anagen effluvium, cicatricial alopecias, syphilis.

2) Assess
  • Psychological impact, stressors, support network, prior episodes, treatments.
  • Pattern & extent (mild/moderate/severe) and other sites (brows/lashes/body hair).
  • Look for regrowth (fine/depigmented hairs). Consider pull test if shedding active.
  • Investigate only if atypical: FBC, ferritin, TFTs, fungal microscopy/culture.
3) Primary Care Management
  • Education & reassurance: spontaneous regrowth common in limited disease; total/universal uncommon.
  • Psychological support: consider counselling/CBT; CAMHS if child with psychosocial change.
  • If regrowth present: no treatment needed.
  • Mild, stable, not distressing: watchful waiting with review.
  • Treatment desired: trial potent/very potent topical corticosteroid (e.g., clobetasol 0.05%, betamethasone 0.1%, fluocinolone 0.025%) for 3 months (off‑label). Avoid face/brows.
  • Counsel effects: response often ≥3 months; early hairs may be fine/white; possible atrophy, telangiectasia, folliculitis, systemic absorption.
  • Cosmetic options: hairstyling, keratin fibres/camouflage sprays, brow dermatography, false lashes, wigs/hairpieces (NHS eligibility varies).
4) Refer / Seek Specialist Advice
  • Diagnostic uncertainty (consider trichoscopy/biopsy).
  • Children, pregnant or breastfeeding.
  • Severe disease (>50% scalp) or rapid progression.
  • Failed/declined topical steroid but wants treatment.
  • Any patient requesting treatments beyond primary care.
  • Significant psychological distress despite support.
5) Specialist Management (Overview)
  • Intralesional corticosteroids (patchy sites incl. brows) — temporary regrowth; risks: pain, atrophy, hypopigmentation.
  • Oral corticosteroids (continuous/pulsed) — mixed evidence; relapse on cessation; weigh adverse effects.
  • Topical immunotherapy (DPCP/SADBE) — for extensive patchy disease in specialist centres.
  • Systemic immunosuppressants: ciclosporin, methotrexate — limited evidence; monitor toxicity.
  • JAK inhibitors for severe AA (e.g., baricitinib, ritlecitinib) — dermatology‑led; counsel risks per safety alerts.
  • PUVA / dithranol / minoxidil — limited or inconsistent benefit; rarely used.

Differential Diagnosis — At a Glance

  • Tinea capitis — scale/erythema, lymphadenopathy; confirm with microscopy/culture.
  • Trichotillomania — incomplete/irregular patches, hair of variable length.
  • Traction alopecia — margins/temporal thinning with styling history.
  • Androgenetic alopecia — patterned miniaturisation; women with diffuse crown thinning.
  • Telogen effluvium — diffuse shedding post‑stress/illness/PP period.
  • Cicatricial alopecias — scarring inflammatory disorders (DLE, lichen planus, etc.).
  • Secondary syphilis — moth‑eaten alopecia; test if risk.

Prognosis

  • Unpredictable; relapsing–remitting common.
  • Spontaneous remission within a year in many with limited patches.
  • Worse outlook: childhood onset, family history, longstanding/extensive disease, ophiasis, nail involvement, atopy/other autoimmune disease.

Consultation Tips

  • Document % scalp involvement and sites (scalp/brows/lashes/body); photograph with consent for follow‑up.
  • Safety‑net: discuss course, triggers, when to return (rapid spread, distress, new sites).
  • If prescribing potent topical steroids: set expectations, advise on application technique and breaks, avoid face/brows.
  • Offer written info/support (e.g., patient groups), and discuss cosmetic options early.
