Seborrheic Keratosis

Benign
Visual Cue "Stuck on" appearance, waxy, warty. Can look like a brain (cerebriform pattern).
  • Key: If confident in diagnosis, DO NOT refer.
  • Dermoscopy: Comedo-like openings, milia-like cysts.
  • Management: Reassurance. Cosmetic removal if private.

Skin Tags (Acrochordon)

Benign
Visual Cue Soft fleshy lumps on skin folds (neck, axilla). Often due to friction.
  • Management: If asymptomatic, no treatment needed.
  • Tip: Thread ligation (tie cotton string at base) for pedunculated ones.

Warts (Verrucae)

Benign
Visual Cue Firm, rough papules. Plantar warts lose skin lines and have bleeding dots.
  • Treatment: Salicylic acid + soak + file (disposable file!).
  • NHS: Cryotherapy generally not commissioned unless painful/symptomatic.
  • Duration: Can take 3-6 months to resolve.

Molluscum Contagiosum

Benign
Visual Cue Shiny pearly papules, often umbilicated. Common in children.
  • Inflammation: If angry/crusty, body is clearing it. Do not treat with antibiotics (e.g. Fucidin).
  • Treatment: Usually none needed. Can try Podophyllotoxin if persistent (max 2 weeks).

Pyogenic Granuloma

Caution
Visual Cue Rapidly growing, red, fleshy, bleeds easily.
  • Danger: Differential is Amelanotic Melanoma.
  • Action: If treated in community (curettage/cautery), MUST send for histology.
  • Risk Factors: Pregnancy, trauma, hormones.

Actinic Keratosis (AK)

Pre-Malignant
Visual Cue Red, scaly macules on sun-exposed sites (scalp, face). "Strawberry pattern" on dermoscopy.
  • Risk: High cumulative sun exposure increases SCC risk.
  • Management: 5-Fluorouracil (Efudix) or generic 5-FU once daily for 4 weeks.
  • Warning: Will look angry/crusty during treatment (normal reaction).

Bowen's Disease

SCC In Situ
Visual Cue Persistent scaly patch, sharp border, often on lower legs. Glomerular vessels on dermoscopy.
  • Definition: Full thickness dysplasia (vs AK partial).
  • Treatment: 5-FU for 4 weeks.
  • Caution: High risk of ulceration on lower legs. Stop if too inflamed.

Basal Cell Carcinoma

Malignant
Visual Cue Pearl roll edges, telangiectasia. History: "Bleeds, scabs, never heals."
  • Types: Nodular (classic), Superficial (scaly patch).
  • Growth: Slow (years). 80% get another within 5 years.
  • Management: Excision is gold standard. Superficial BCC can be treated with topical (Imiquimod/5-FU) for 6 weeks.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Malignant
Visual Cue Rapidly growing nodule, keratin plug, ulcerated. Can look like a volcano.
  • Key differentiator vs BCC: Grows fast (months vs years). TENDERNESS is a red flag (perineural invasion).
  • Risk: Immunosuppression (Transplant patients x200 risk).
  • Action: Refer 2WW (Urgent).

Keratoacanthoma

High Risk
Visual Cue Volcano-like lesion with central keratin plug. Grows rapidly over weeks.
  • Problem: Cannot differentiate clinically from well-differentiated SCC without histology.
  • Action: Treat as SCC. Excise or Refer 2WW.

Malignant Melanoma

Critical
Visual Cue Ugly Duckling sign. New changing mole. Nodular = Aggressive.
  • Demographics: 80% de novo (new lesions). Intermittent sun exposure/childhood sunburn.
  • Types: Nodular (aggressive, vertical growth), Superficial Spreading, Acral (nailbed), Lentigo Maligna (face).
  • Nail Melanoma: Hutchinson sign (pigment on nail fold). Wide band (>3mm) with variation.
