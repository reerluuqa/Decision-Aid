Assessment

Comprehensive history and examination of inflamed skin conditions

‚ñº

üìùHistory Taking

Temporal Pattern & Triggers:

  • Document onset, evolution, and duration
  • Precipitating factors (new products, exposures)
  • Current medications (many cause skin reactions)
  • Recent infections, psychological/social stressors

Symptom Characterization:

  • Pruritus: Itch severity, impact on sleep
  • Pain/Burning: Location and quality
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, malaise (red flag)

Personal & Environmental Factors:

  • Family history of atopy (eczema, asthma, allergic rhinitis)
  • Occupational exposures (wet work, chemicals)
  • Hobbies, recent travel, pet exposure
  • Previous treatments and adherence

üéØDistribution Patterns & Special Signs

Distribution: Flexural, extensor, acral (hands/feet), photodistributed

Surface Changes: Scaly, crust, lichenified (thickened), excoriations (scratch marks)

Configuration: Annular (ring-shaped), linear, follicular, dermatomal

Special Signs:

  • Blanching vs Non-blanching: Press glass test
  • Koebner phenomenon: Lesions appear at trauma sites
  • Auspitz sign: Pinpoint bleeding after scale removal (psoriasis)

‚ö†Ô∏èRed Flags URGENT

Immediate Referral Indicators:

  • Erythroderma (>90% skin involvement)
  • Generalized pustular psoriasis
  • Severe drug eruptions (SJS/TEN)
  • Eczema herpeticum (HSV infection)
  • Angioedema with airway compromise
  • Severe systemic symptoms

üå°Ô∏è Atopic Eczema PC Manage

Criteria: Pruritus + erythema + excoriations + lichenification

Distribution: Flexures (antecubital/popliteal fossa) in adults

Key Features: Chronic relapsing course, personal/family atopy

Comorbidities: Asthma, allergic rhinitis, infection risk

‚ò†Ô∏è Contact Dermatitis Caution

Irritant: Burning sensation, hands most common (wet work)

Allergic: Type IV delayed hypersensitivity

Common Allergens: Nickel, fragrances, preservatives, rubber

Occupations: Healthcare workers, hairdressers, cleaners

üß¥ Seborrheic Dermatitis PC Manage

Distribution: Scalp, eyebrows, glabella, nasolabial folds, chest

Appearance: Erythema with greasy yellow scale

Pathophysiology: Yeast-driven (Malassezia)

Often Asymptomatic: Mild itch or irritation

üî¥ Psoriasis PC Manage

Appearance: Well-demarcated plaques with silvery scale

Auspitz Sign: Pinpoint bleeding after scale removal

Koebner Phenomenon: Trauma-induced lesions

Types: Chronic plaque (most common), guttate, inverse, pustular

Comorbidities: CV risk, metabolic syndrome, psoriatic arthritis

üéØ Urticaria & Angioedema Caution

Acute: < 6 weeks duration

Chronic: > 6 weeks (CSU - Chronic Spontaneous Urticaria)

Pathophysiology: Mast cell degranulation ‚Üí histamine release

Angioedema: Deep dermal edema (eyelids, lips, tongue, pharynx)

‚ö†Ô∏è Airway involvement = EMERGENCY

ü¶† Tinea Infections Caution

Tinea Corporis: Body ringworm, annular lesions

Tinea Incognito: Modified by topical steroids

Tinea Capitis: Scalp infection, requires systemic therapy

Onychomycosis: Nail infection, often resistant to topicals

üêõ Scabies Caution

Key Symptom: Intense nocturnal pruritus

Burrows: Web spaces, wrists, ankles, genitals, nipples

‚ö†Ô∏è Highly contagious: Treat contacts & decontaminate environment

Norwegian Scabies: Crusted, hyperkeratotic (immunocompromised)

üî¨ Other Differentials Caution

Cutaneous T-cell Lymphoma: If not responding to treatment

Pityriasis Rosea: Herald patch ‚Üí Christmas tree distribution

Dermatitis Herpetiformis: Pruritic papulovesicles, associated with coeliac

Lichen Planus: Violaceous, polygonal papules

üéØ Key Diagnostic Principle

If condition not responding to appropriate therapy after 6-8 months (or sooner if worsening), reconsider diagnosis and have low threshold for biopsy to rule out cutaneous T-cell lymphoma.

Treatment

Evidence-based management strategies

‚ñº

üõ°Ô∏èGeneral Principles - All Conditions

1. Emollient Therapy
  • Paraffin-based ointments as baseline (e.g., 50:50 white soft paraffin/liquid paraffin)
  • Apply liberally and frequently (4-6 times daily)
  • Use soap substitutes for washing
  • Continue even when skin clear
2. Trigger Identification & Avoidance
  • Wool, synthetic fibers, fragrances
  • Excessive sweating, heat
  • Psychological stressors
  • Irritant avoidance (detergents, solvents)
3. Patient Education
  • Fingertip Unit (FTU): Amount for application
  • How to step-down treatment
  • When to seek medical review
  • Expectations for chronic conditions

üåøEczema Management

Step 1: Topical Corticosteroids
  • Mild (1% hydrocortisone): Face, flexures, genitals
  • Moderate (clobetasone butyrate): Trunk, limbs
  • Potent (betamethasone valerate): Thicker plaques, palms/soles
  • Very Potent (clobetasol propionate): Short courses only, severe flares
  • Apply once daily to active lesions until controlled (1-2 weeks)
  • Then reduce frequency (alternate days ‚Üí weekends)
Step 2: Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors
  • Tacrolimus 0.1% (adults): Face, neck, flexures
  • Tacrolimus 0.03% (children <5y):
  • Pimecrolimus: Milder, less irritating
  • Steroid-sparing agents, safe for long-term use
  • Common: transient burning/stinging initially
Step 3: Advanced Therapies
  • Wet Wraps: For moderate-severe flares
  • Antihistamines: For itch (sedating at night)
  • Phototherapy: Narrowband UVB
  • Systemic: Cyclosporine, methotrexate, azathioprine
  • Biologics: Dupilumab, tralokinumab (refractory cases)

üßºSeborrheic Dermatitis

Scalp Treatment
  • Anti-fungal Shampoo: Ketoconazole 2% twice weekly
  • Alternative: Selenium sulfide, zinc pyrithione
  • Steroid Solutions: Betamethasone for inflammation (short course)
  • Keratolytics: Salicylic acid, coal tar preparations
Facial/Body Treatment
  • Ketoconazole 2% Cream: Daily application
  • Low-potency Steroids: Hydrocortisone 1% for inflammation
  • Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors: For maintenance
  • Chronic Management: Weekly anti-fungal shampoo for prevention

üéØPsoriasis Management

Topical Therapies
  • Vitamin D Analogs: Calcipotriol, calcitriol
  • Combined Steroid + Vitamin D: Dovobet/Daivobet gel/cream
  • Scalp: Keratolytics (salicylic acid), coconut oil for scale removal
  • Nails: Calcipotriol solution
Escalation Pathway
  • Phototherapy: Narrowband UVB (failed topicals)
  • Systemic: Methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin
  • Biologics: Anti-TNF, IL-17, IL-23 inhibitors
  • Acitretin: Teratogenic (avoid pregnancy 2-3 years)

ü¶†Infections & Infestations

Bacterial Folliculitis
  • Topical: Antiseptic washes (chlorhexidine, benzoyl peroxide)
  • Systemic: Flucloxacillin (if widespread)
  • Prevention: Avoid occlusion, friction
Tinea Infections
  • Corporis/Cruris: Terbinafine 1% cream BD for 1-2 weeks
  • Resistant: Oral terbinafine 250mg daily (monitor LFTs)
  • Capitis: Griseofulvin (licensed), or terbinafine/itraconazole
  • Treat Contacts: Especially for tinea capitis (Microsporum)
Scabies HIGHLY CONTAGIOUS
  • First-line: Permethrin 5% cream
  • Application: Whole body (day 1 & day 7)
  • Contacts: Treat all household members simultaneously
  • Environment: Wash bedding/clothing >50¬∞C
  • Post-scabetic itch: Can persist 2-6 weeks (not treatment failure)
  • Resistant/Norwegian: Oral ivermectin 200mcg/kg

üíâUrticaria & Angioedema

First-line: Non-sedating Antihistamines
  • Cetirizine 10mg daily, loratadine 10mg daily, fexofenadine 180mg daily
  • Can increase dose up to 4x standard (under guidance)
Short Courses (Severe Flares)
  • Oral corticosteroids 0.5mg/kg (0.75mg/kg if inadequate response)
  • Maximum 3-5 days to avoid rebound
Refractory Chronic Urticaria
  • Omalizumab: Anti-IgE monoclonal antibody
  • Cyclosporine: Safe in pregnancy (unlike omalizumab)

üî∫ When to Escalate to Secondary Care

  • Failure to respond to appropriate topical therapy after 6-8 weeks
  • Severe/widespread disease requiring phototherapy or systemic therapy
  • Diagnostic uncertainty (consider biopsy)
  • Severe infections requiring specialist management
  • Pregnancy with severe disease (needs specialist input)
  • Before initiating biologics or systemic immunosuppressants

üéØReferral Criteria URGENT

üî¥ Immediate/Same Day

  • Erythroderma (>90% skin involvement)
  • Generalized pustular psoriasis
  • Eczema herpeticum (suspected HSV)
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/TEN
  • Severe angioedema with airway compromise
  • Septicemia from skin infection

üü° Urgent (<2 weeks)

  • Severe flare not responding to potent steroids
  • Significant impact on quality of life
  • Severe facial/genital involvement
  • Diagnostic uncertainty (need biopsy)
  • Suspected cutaneous T-cell lymphoma
  • Severe scalp psoriasis

üü¢ Routine (6-12 weeks)

  • Failure to respond to appropriate therapy
  • Persistent disease despite optimal primary care
  • Consideration for systemic therapy/phototherapy
  • Need for patch testing (contact dermatitis)
  • Moderate chronic stable disease

üé®Skin of Color Considerations

Key Differences in Presentation
  • Erythema: May appear as purplish-dark tinge or grey
  • Post-inflammatory Hyperpigmentation: Very common, takes months-years to fade
  • Psoriasis: Silvery-white scale more prominent
  • Follicular Disorders: Pseudofolliculitis barbae common in black men
  • Corticosteroid Caution: Higher risk of pigment changes
Management Adjustments
  • Reassure about post-inflammatory pigmentation
  • Avoid unnecessary high-potency steroids
  • Consider earlier referral for cosmetic concerns
  • Photograph documentation essential
