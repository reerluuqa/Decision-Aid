Urgent Red Flags - Refer Within 2 Weeks

  • Unexplained vulval lump, ulceration, or bleeding
  • Unexplained palpable mass in or at vaginal entrance
  • Persistent abnormal area that is thick, warty, or ulcerated
  • Suspected lymphoma or vulval carcinoma

First-Line Management Approach

  • Start with emollients and self-care advice
  • Identify and remove irritants
  • Consider short course of topical corticosteroids
  • Address underlying cause if identified

How to Assess a Patient with Pruritus Vulvae

Key History Questions:

  • Duration & onset: Acute vs chronic symptoms
  • Location: Vulval only or generalized itch?
  • Nocturnal itching: Consider threadworm
  • Associated symptoms: Discharge, pain, dysuria
  • Hygiene practices: Soaps, wipes, douches, etc.
  • Medications: Topical treatments, systemic drugs
  • Medical history: Diabetes, skin conditions, menopause
  • Impact assessment: Sleep, relationships, mental health

Physical Examination:

  • Examine anogenital region with proper chaperoning
  • Assess skin: color, texture, lesions, lichenification
  • Check for vaginal discharge (consider speculum if needed)
  • Examine extragenital sites: oral mucosa, elbows, knees, nails

Management Strategies

Known Causes

  • Infections: Treat candidiasis, BV, trichomoniasis per guidelines
  • Dermatitis: Stop irritants, use emollients, short-term topical steroids
  • Hormonal: Consider vaginal estrogen for atrophic vaginitis
  • Pregnancy: Increased risk of candidiasis
  • Incontinence: Barrier creams, manage underlying cause

Unknown Causes

  • Start with emollients as soap substitute
  • Consider sedating antihistamine (hydroxyzine) or low-dose TCA (amitriptyline) at night
  • Trial of hydrocortisone 1% ointment for 1-2 weeks
  • If persistent despite treatment, refer to specialist

Self-Care Advice for Patients

Recommended

  • Shower instead of baths
  • Use plain emollient as soap substitute (max once daily)
  • Apply cool compresses for itch relief
  • Keep nails short to minimize skin damage
  • Wear loose, cotton underwear

Avoid

  • Soap, bubble baths, shampoos on vulval area
  • Wet wipes, feminine hygiene sprays
  • Scented toilet paper, sanitary products
  • Tight clothing, synthetic fabrics
  • Scratching (explain itch-scratch cycle)

Referral Guidance

  • Urgent (2 weeks): Suspected malignancy, VIN
  • Non-urgent: Persistent symptoms despite treatment
  • Suspected contact dermatitis needing patch testing
  • Suspected lichen sclerosus or lichen planus
  • Architectural changes or scarring

Prescribing Information

Topical Corticosteroids

Hydrocortisone 1% ointment for 7-10 days (mild)

Potency guide Use ointments (not creams) for better penetration and less irritation

Antihistamines

Hydroxyzine 10-25mg at night

Contraindications Avoid in elderly and those with QT prolongation risk

Tricyclic Antidepressants

Amitriptyline 10mg or doxepin 25mg at night

Off-label use Used for nocturnal itch and sleep disturbance

Emollients

Regular application as soap substitute

Barrier protection Protects against urine, menstrual blood, and irritants

Key Complications

  • Squamous cell carcinoma risk in lichen sclerosus (<5%)
  • Lichen simplex chronicus from itch-scratch cycle
  • Psychological impact: anxiety, depression, relationship issues
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Secondary bacterial infection of excoriated skin
