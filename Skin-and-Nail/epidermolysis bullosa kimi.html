Epidermolysis Bullosa Management Algorithm

BMJ Best Practice | Last updated: Sep 26, 2023

Rare genetic skin disorder requiring multidisciplinary care

Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk stratification

Risk Assessment

Risk Factors

STRONG

Family history of EB

Only known risk factor. AD forms usually have parental history. AR forms have carrier parents.

Consanguinity

Increased risk for autosomal recessive forms

Key Diagnostic Factors

Mechanical fragility of skin (Nikolsky's sign)
Recurrent blisters and erosions
Poorly healing wounds
Onset at birth or early infancy

Clues to EB Sub-types

EB Simplex

No milia/scarring, localized or generalized

Junctional EB

Enamel hypoplasia, granulation tissue

Dystrophic EB

Milia, scarring, dystrophic nails

Physical Examination Tips

Apply gentle traction to normal-appearing skin to test for Nikolsky's sign. Examine for pseudosyndactyly, oral cavity involvement, and nail changes.

Diagnosis

Confirmatory investigations and classification

Specialist Referral

Consultant Referral

All patients with suspected EB, especially newborns, must be evaluated by a dermatologist experienced in EB. Where diagnosis is uncertain, refer to a specialized EB center or academic dermatology center.

Diagnostic Investigations

PREFERRED

Immunofluorescence Antigen Mapping

97% concordance with EM. Determines exact level of cleavage and distinguishes sub-types. Performed on cryopreserved skin biopsy.

  • • Absent type VII collagen = severe RDEB
  • • Absent laminin-332 = severe JEB
  • • Absent plectin = EBS-MD
NOW ROUTINE

DNA Mutational Analysis

Gene panels using targeted or whole-exome sequencing. Confirms type and mode of transmission. Can be performed as first-line in specialized centers.

Transmission Electron Microscopy

Direct visualization of ultra-structural level of cleavage. More expensive and labor-intensive. Use when IFM unavailable.

Not Recommended

Routine light microscopy is not recommended due to low sensitivity and specificity in distinguishing EB types.

Differential Diagnoses

Condition Differentiating Features Test
Bullous impetigo Localized, honey-colored crusts, not mechanically fragile Culture shows S. aureus
Staphylococcal scalded skin Superficial cleavage, no blisters Biopsy shows subcorneal splitting
Immunobullous diseases Lack mechanical fragility, family history negative Direct immunofluorescence positive

Treatment

Management strategies and interventions

Emergency

Acute Management

URGENT

Tracheolaryngeal Obstruction

Occurs in ~50% of JEB-severe children. Hoarse cry demands immediate ENT evaluation.

Tracheostomy recommended if airway compromised
URGENT

Systemic Infection

Usually bacterial sepsis in neonates/infants with severe EB sub-types.

IV broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately

Suspicion of Squamous Cell Carcinoma

In RDEB/JEB patients: wounds lasting longer than usual or presence of nodules. Requires full-thickness biopsy and wide excision if confirmed.

Ongoing Wound Care

Dressings

Clean padded dressings to high-traction areas. Non-adherent/weakly adherent sterile dressings for open wounds. Individualize based on sub-type, location, and cost.

Blister Management

Sterile drainage of blisters to enhance healing and reduce pain. Leave roof intact when possible.

Topical Antimicrobials

Polymyxin B/bacitracin ointment under dressings. Silver sulfadiazine for gram-positive infections. Mupirocin for confirmed S. aureus.

Lifestyle Modifications

Avoid mechanical trauma while encouraging normal activities. Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and psychological support recommended.

Nutritional Support

Protein and carbohydrate-enriched supplements. Vitamin D and C supplementation common. Gastrostomy feeding for severe JEB/RDEB. Modified feeding nipples for oral erosions.

EB Sub-type Specific Treatments

EB Simplex (EBS)
  • • Aluminum chloride (20%): Astringent for palms/soles with hyperhidrosis
  • • Tetracycline (250-500mg BD): Anti-inflammatory, reduces blistering (age >8 years)
Emerging Therapies
  • • Beremagene geperpavec: Topical gene therapy for DEB (FDA approved)
  • • Birch triterpene gel: Promotes wound healing (EMA approved)
  • • Ex vivo gene therapy: Autologous keratinocyte transplantation
  • • Stem cell transplantation: Experimental for RDEB

Follow-up

Monitoring schedule and long-term complications

Routine

Monitoring Schedule

  • • First 1-2 years: Every 6 months for generalized EB (monitor for infection, failure to thrive)
  • • Junctional EB: Close monitoring first 6 years for tracheolaryngeal stenosis
  • • RDEB adolescents: Yearly full-body skin exams for SCC screening

Prognosis by Sub-type

EB Simplex (EBS)

Excellent prognosis, except EBS-severe with early infant mortality risk

Dominant DEB

Similar to EBS, life-long scarring but generally good outcomes

Junctional EB (severe)

Poor prognosis - ~50% die by 1 year, most by 25 years

Recessive DEB (severe)

Major SCC concern - 90% develop SCC by age 50, 87% die from metastasis
