Chilblains Management Algorithm

Quick reference guide for primary care management of chilblains

‚Ñπ Definition & Clinical Features

What are Chilblains?

  • Cold-induced inflammatory lesions
  • Affect acral skin (fingers, toes, nose, ears)
  • Also known as perniosis

Clinical Appearance

  • Red-purple papules, plaques or nodules
  • Painful or itchy
  • Appear 12-24 hours after cold exposure
  • Often symmetrical (both hands/feet)

Classification

  • Primary: Idiopathic, no underlying condition
  • Secondary: Associated with underlying disorders
  • Acute: Resolve in 2-3 weeks
  • Chronic: Persistent with repeated exposure

üîç Key Differential Diagnoses

Connective Tissue Disorders

  • Lupus erythematosus (chilblain lupus)
  • Sarcoidosis (lupus pernio)

Haematological Conditions

  • Myelodysplastic syndrome
  • Chronic myelomonocytic leukaemia
  • Cryoglobulinaemia

Vascular Conditions

  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Acrocyanosis
  • Frostbite
  • Livedo reticularis

Other Skin Conditions

  • Cold contact urticaria
  • Cold panniculitis
  • Granuloma annulare
  • Juvenile spring eruption

COVID-Related

  • "COVID toes": Chilblain-like lesions without cold exposure
  • Commonly in children/young adults
  • Associated with COVID-19 infection or contact

Idiopathic chilblains management:
  • Reassurance (self-limiting, resolves in 2-3 weeks)
  • Self-care advice:
    • Dry and gradually warm skin (avoid direct heat)
    • Smoking cessation
    • Avoid cold, damp conditions
    • Wear warm clothing (gloves, socks)
    • Avoid caffeine and decongestants

üíä Nifedipine Prescribing Considerations

Contraindications

  • Hypersensitivity to nifedipine/dihydropyridines
  • Unstable angina, cardiogenic shock
  • Significant aortic stenosis
  • Recent myocardial infarction (<1 month)
  • Pregnancy, breastfeeding
  • Children <18 years
  • Concurrent rifampicin

Cautions

  • Heart failure, poor cardiac reserve
  • Hypotension
  • Elderly (lower doses may be needed)
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • GI obstruction or inflammatory bowel disease
  • Hepatic impairment

Adverse Effects

  • Common: Headache, dizziness, peripheral oedema, flushing
  • Uncommon: Palpitations, hypotension, GI disturbance
  • Rare: Mood disturbances, depression, hyperglycaemia

üö© Red Flags for Referral

Refer to specialist if:

  • Suspected underlying connective tissue or haematological disorder
  • Severe ulceration or necrosis
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • No response to conservative measures in children
  • No response to conservative measures in pregnant/breastfeeding women
