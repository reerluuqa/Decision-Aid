Lumps & Bumps Management

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm for Skin Lesion Assessment and Management

Assessment

Initial evaluation and history taking for skin lesions

History Taking

Duration and Progression

Key question: "How long has the lesion been present?"

  • Urgent Rapid growth (<6 months) - consider SCC, melanoma, pyogenic granuloma
  • Caution Gradual changes (6-24 months) - consider BCC, AK, Bowen's
  • Stable Longstanding (>2 years) - likely benign (SK, skin tags, milia)

Symptoms

  • Bleeding/scabbing: Suggests BCC, SCC, pyogenic granuloma
  • Tenderness: Suggests SCC (possible perineural invasion)
  • Itching: Less specific, can occur in multiple conditions

Risk Factors

  • UV Exposure: Chronic (outdoor work) → BCC/SCC; Intermittent sunburn → Melanoma
  • Skin Type: Type 1-2 higher risk; However melanoma more common in type 2-3
  • Immunosuppression: 200x risk SCC, 20-60x BCC, 8-10x melanoma
  • Family/Personal History: Previous skin cancer increases risk
  • Sunbed Use: Significant melanoma risk factor

Tip: Ask about blistering sunburns in childhood

Physical Examination

Key Features to Assess

  • Site: Sun-exposed areas higher risk for malignancy
  • Size: Measure in mm/cm
  • Appearance:
    • Pigmented: Consider melanocytic lesions
    • Non-pigmented: Consider BCC, SCC, AK
    • Vascular: Consider pyogenic granuloma, cherry angioma
  • Texture:
    • Rough/warty: Warts, SK, AK
    • Smooth: Milia, cysts, BCC
    • Friable: Pyogenic granuloma
  • Surrounding skin: Sun damage signs (lentigines, wrinkles)

Dermoscopy (if available)

Primary care goal: Benign vs concerning features

Benign patterns: Symmetrical, uniform network, no atypical features
Concerning features: Asymmetry, multiple colors, irregular borders, streaks

Tip: If unsure, always refer. Better to over-refer than miss malignancy.

Red Flags Urgent Referral

Mandatory 2WW Referral Criteria

  • Suspected melanoma (following ABCDE/EFG criteria)
  • Suspected SCC (rapid growth, tenderness, induration)
  • Keratoacanthoma (volcano-like rapid growth)
  • Lesion on eyelid/lip/nose with cosmetic concern
  • Any lesion with uncertain diagnosis that cannot be ruled out as malignancy

Diagnosis

Differentiate benign, premalignant, and malignant lesions

Benign Lesions Primary Care Management

Seborrheic Keratosis (SK)

Features: "Stuck-on" appearance, warty/velvety, variable color

Dermoscopy: Comedo-like openings, milia-like cysts, cerebriform pattern

Management: Reassurance, cosmetic removal if requested (private)

Key point: If confident it's SK → no referral needed

Skin Tags

Features: Soft, pedunculated, skin-colored, common in friction areas

Management: Reassurance, cotton string method, or minor surgery if symptomatic

Warts

Types: Common (fingers), plantar (feet), filiform (face/neck), genital

Management: Salicylic acid 12-16wks, cryotherapy (if commissioned), set expectations (3-6 months)

Note: Most treatments not NHS commissioned for asymptomatic warts

Other Common Benign Lesions

  • Milia: Small white cysts, childhood or sun-damaged skin
  • Sebaceous Hyperplasia: Yellowish papules with central umbilication, T-zone
  • Cherry Angioma: Bright red papules, blanches on pressure
  • Dermatofibroma: Firm nodule, dimple sign positive

Premalignant Lesions Monitor/Treat

Actinic Keratosis (AK)

Features: Red, scaly macules on sun-exposed sites

Risk: 25% resolve spontaneously, small risk SCC transformation

Management options:

  • Watchful waiting + sun protection (if few, elderly, asymptomatic)
  • 5-Fluorouracil 4% (Tolak) daily x 4 weeks
  • Cryotherapy (field change may need multiple sessions)
  • Imiquimod or diclofenac gel alternatives

Warning: Treat lower leg lesions carefully → risk ulceration

Bowen's Disease (SCC in situ)

Features: Persistent scaly plaque, often on lower legs

Dermoscopy: Polymorphous vessels (dots/coils), symmetrical distribution

Management: 5-FU daily x 4 weeks OR cryotherapy (care on legs)

Follow-up: Review in 3 months post-treatment

Malignant Lesions Urgent Referral

Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)

Features: Slow-growing, pearly/rolled edge, telangiectasia, ulceration

History: "Bleeds, scabs, never heals" over months-years

Types:

  • Nodular: Most common, pearly papule/nodule
  • Superficial: Scaly patch, fine raised edge on stretch

Referral: Routine unless high-risk site (eyelid, nose, lip) or cosmetic concern

Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)

Features: Rapid growth (<6 months), tender, indurated, may be ulcerated

Risk factors: Immunosuppression (organ transplant), chronic inflammation, UV exposure

Warning: Tenderness suggests perineural invasion

Referral: 2WW Pathway

Melanoma

Key facts: 80% arise de novo (new lesion), 20% from existing mole

ABCDE Rule:

  • Asymmetry
  • Border irregularity
  • Color variation
  • Diameter >6mm
  • Evolution/elevation

EFG (nodular): Elevated, Firm, Growing

Ugly duckling sign: Lesion that stands out as different from others

Referral: 2WW Pathway

Keratoacanthoma

Features: Rapidly growing (weeks), volcano-like with central keratin plug

Management: 2WW Pathway (cannot differentiate from SCC without histology)

Diagnostic Uncertainty

When to Refer for Diagnosis

  • Any pigmented lesion you're unsure about
  • Lesions not fitting classic benign patterns
  • Atypical presentation in high-risk patients (immunosuppressed)
  • Lesions on special sites (nails, mucosa, face)

Remember: "When in doubt, refer out"

Treatment

Management options based on diagnosis

Primary Care Treatments Suitable for GP Management

Topical Treatments

  • Salicylic acid (15-40%): For warts
    • Soak 5 mins, file with disposable emery board
    • Apply daily for 12 weeks
    • Warn about surrounding skin irritation
  • 5-Fluorouracil 4% (Tolak): For AK and Bowen's
    • Apply once daily for 28 days
    • Warn about significant inflammatory reaction
    • Can take 2-day breaks if too inflamed
    • Avoid topical steroids during treatment
  • Imiquimod 5%: For AK (off-label)
    • 3x weekly for 4 weeks, rest 4 weeks, repeat

Cryotherapy If commissioned

  • Warts: Weekly for 4 sessions (40-50% success)
  • AK: Single or repeated treatment
  • Bowen's: Only if confident and careful (risk ulceration)
  • Contraindications: Lower leg in elderly (poor healing)

Minor Surgery

  • Pyogenic granuloma: Curettage + cautery, ALWAYS send for histology
  • Skin tags: Snip excision, cryotherapy, or ligature
  • Sebaceous cyst: Excision if inflamed/infected

Secondary Care Treatments

Dermatology/Community Clinic

  • Photodynamic therapy for extensive AK
  • Curettage and cautery for small BCCs (if service available)
  • Confirmation of diagnosis via dermoscopy
  • Imiquimod for superficial BCC (some areas)

Plastic Surgery/Referral Centers

  • Surgical excision of BCC/SCC with adequate margins
  • Complex reconstructions for large lesions
  • Mohs surgery for high-risk BCC (recurrent, facial)
  • Sentinel lymph node biopsy for high-risk melanoma

Malignant Lesion Management Specialist Only

Basal Cell Carcinoma

  • Standard: Surgical excision with 4mm margin
  • Superficial: 5-FU, imiquimod, cryotherapy, PDT
  • High-risk: Mohs surgery (recurrent, facial, large)

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

  • Standard: Surgical excision with 6mm margin (minimum)
  • High-risk: Wider excision + adjuvant radiotherapy
  • Follow-up: Regular skin checks (risk of second primaries)

Melanoma

  • Localized: Wide local excision with margins based on Breslow thickness
  • Advanced: Immunotherapy, targeted therapy
  • Follow-up: Regular dermatology + lymph node assessment

Treatments to Avoid/Use with Caution

  • Avoid: Topical steroids on AK during 5-FU treatment
  • Caution: Cryotherapy on lower legs in elderly (ulcer risk)
  • Avoid: Over-treatment of benign lesions (SK, milia) on NHS
  • Caution: Pyogenic granuloma - always histology to rule out amelanotic melanoma

Follow-up

Monitoring, safety netting, and review protocols

Safety Netting Essential for All Patients

Clear Instructions to Patients

  • When to return: Any change in size, shape, color, bleeding, pain
  • Timeline: If no improvement with treatment within agreed timeframe
  • Red flags: Rapid growth, bleeding that doesn't stop, new symptoms
  • Document: Record safety netting advice given

Example script: "If this lesion changes in any way, becomes painful, or starts bleeding, please book an urgent appointment or contact 111."

Specific Safety Netting Scenarios

  • Reassured benign: "If it changes, comes back for review"
  • Treatment initiated: "If not improved after [timeframe], return for reassessment"
  • Referred: "If you haven't heard from hospital within 2 weeks, contact them"
  • Watchful waiting: "Any change triggers immediate review"

Review Timeframes

Post-Treatment Reviews

  • 5-FU treatment (AK/Bowen's): Review 3 months post-treatment
    • Warn about post-inflammatory pigment changes
    • Give time for inflammation to settle
  • Cryotherapy: Review 6-8 weeks
  • Salicylic acid (warts): Review 12 weeks if no improvement
  • Pyogenic granuloma removal: Review 2 weeks for wound check, 6 weeks for histology

Watchful Waiting Reviews

  • Molluscum: Review if not resolved in 12-18 months
  • Seborrheic keratosis: Review if becomes symptomatic
  • Skin tags: Review if increase in number/size rapidly
  • Viral warts: Review if spreading or painful

Re-referral Criteria

When to Re-refer to Secondary Care

  • Post-treatment persistence: AK/Bowen's not resolved after 2 courses
  • Recurrence: BCC/SCC at same site after treatment
  • New lesions: Multiple new lesions in high-risk patients
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: Lesion not responding as expected
  • High-risk patients: Immunosuppressed with any new concerning lesion

High-Risk Patient Follow-up

  • Organ transplant recipients: Annual skin checks minimum
  • Previous NMSC: 6-monthly checks for 2 years, then annual
  • Previous melanoma: Dermatology-led follow-up as per protocol
  • Gorlin syndrome: 3-6 monthly review

Documentation Requirements

Essential Documentation Elements

  • History: Duration, symptoms, risk factors
  • Examination: Site, size (mm), appearance, dermoscopy findings if used
  • Diagnosis: Working diagnosis or differential
  • Management plan: Treatment, referral, safety netting
  • Patient understanding: Advice given and patient response
  • Follow-up: Plan and timeframe

Tip: Draw diagram or take photo if system allows

Medicolegal Considerations

Risk Management

  • Always send histology: Even for "obvious" benign lesions
  • Don't miss melanoma: 80% are new lesions → always assess new pigmented lesions
  • Body check: Offer full skin check if one lesion suspicious (multiple lesions common)
  • Referral threshold: Lower threshold for high-risk patients
  • Advice and guidance: Use e-RS A&G for uncertain cases

Key point: Document rationale if choosing not to refer

Patient Education

Prevention and Self-Monitoring

  • Sun protection: SPF 30+, protective clothing, shade
  • Avoid sunbeds: Strongly discourage use
  • Self-examination: Teach ABCDE rule for pigmented lesions
  • Vigilance: Report new/changing lesions promptly
  • Support: Provide written information/leaflets
