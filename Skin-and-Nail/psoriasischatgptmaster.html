Initial assessment & classification

History Full-skin exam Severity & impact

Key history

  • Onset, duration, flares/remissions; sites and extent of involvement.
  • Symptoms: itch, burning, pain, bleeding, scaling; systemic features (fever, malaise, weight loss).
  • Triggers: recent streptococcal infection (esp. guttate), trauma/Koebner, drugs (lithium, Œ≤-blockers, antimalarials, NSAIDs, ACEi, steroid withdrawal), stress, smoking, alcohol, UV, pregnancy, obesity.
  • Articular symptoms: inflammatory joint pain/stiffness, swelling, dactylitis, enthesitis; nail changes.
  • Comorbidities: metabolic syndrome, CVD, IBD, anxiety/depression, VTE, coeliac disease, ocular disease.
  • Previous treatments (topical, phototherapy, systemic, biologics) and response.
  • Impact: use DLQI / patient global assessment if feasible; explore work, relationships, mood.

Consider PEST tool for psoriatic arthritis; score ‚â•3 ‚Üí refer to rheumatology.

Examination & classification

  • Full-skin exam including scalp, nails, flexures, genital area, sacral cleft.
  • Describe type(s): chronic plaque, scalp, facial, flexural, guttate, pustular (local/generalised), erythrodermic, nail disease.
  • Distribution: extensor surfaces, trunk, scalp, flexures; symmetry; halo/Woronoff‚Äôs ring; Koebner sites.
  • Surface: smooth vs scaly vs pustular; colour (consider under-recognised erythema in pigmented skin).
  • Severity: estimate BSA (rule of nines; 1 palm ‚âà 1% BSA); use static PGA if available.
  • Joints: swelling, tenderness, range of motion; enthesitis; dactylitis.
  • Vitals & systemic signs if ill: fever, hypothermia, tachycardia, weight loss, dehydration, oedema.

‚úÖ First-line for mild‚Äìmoderate chronic plaque

Topicals (choose formulations patient will use)
  • Regular emollients (creams/ointments for scale, lotions/gels for widespread disease).
  • Potent topical corticosteroid + vitamin D preparation, once daily at different times of day.
  • Consider salicylic acid if thick scale is problematic.

Avoid very potent steroids in primary care unless under specialist advice. Stop steroids once skin is clear or nearly clear; continue vitamin D alone if needed.

Associated conditions to screen for

  • Psoriatic arthritis ‚Äì up to 30% of people; screen annually and at treatment changes.
  • Metabolic syndrome ‚Äì obesity, hyperlipidaemia, T2DM, NAFLD.
  • CVD & VTE ‚Äì increased risk with more severe disease.

Ongoing care

Follow-up, monitoring & safety netting

Review response Topical steroid safety Lifestyle & information

Follow-up intervals

  • Review at around 4 weeks after starting or changing topical therapy.
  • Then review according to clinical need; at least annually if using multiple intermittent or short courses of potent topicals.
  • Assess severity, impact, adherence, adverse effects; re-screen for psoriatic arthritis and comorbidities.
üõ°Ô∏è
Topical steroid safety
  • Use the lowest potency that controls symptoms, for the shortest duration.
  • Avoid prolonged use on face, flexures, genital skin; watch for atrophy, striae, telangiectasia.
  • Review repeat prescriptions and ensure plan includes ‚Äútreatment breaks‚Äù.

Safety net & self-management

  • Advise to seek urgent help if:
    • Sudden, widespread redness/pustules;
    • Feeling systemically unwell (fever, chills, marked malaise);
    • Rapid worsening after steroid changes; or
    • New joint swelling, severe stiffness or dactylitis.
  • Provide written information and trusted websites.
  • Encourage smoking cessation, alcohol moderation, weight management and physical activity.
üì£
Key patient messages
  • Psoriasis is not infectious; flares can often be controlled with the right plan.
  • Early help for joint symptoms and severe flares makes a difference.
  • Mood and quality of life matter ‚Äì encourage them to mention these.

Background & risk snapshot
  • Systemic, immune-mediated inflammatory skin disease with often chronic relapsing-remitting course.
  • Common: ‚âà1‚Äì3% worldwide; ‚âà1.3‚Äì2.8% of UK population.
  • Onset can be at any age, peaks around 20‚Äì30 and 50‚Äì60 years; family history frequent.
  • Streptococcal infection (especially guttate psoriasis).
  • Drugs: lithium, antimalarials, Œ≤-blockers, NSAIDs, ACEi, some antibiotics and steroid withdrawal.
  • Trauma (Koebner phenomenon), UV, hormonal changes, stress.
  • Smoking (strong link to localized pustular disease) and alcohol; obesity.
  • Plaque psoriasis usually chronic, but spontaneous remissions may occur (sometimes lasting months).
  • Guttate episodes often self-limited (3‚Äì4 months) but ‚âà‚Öì progress to classic plaque disease.
  • Earlier onset may predict more extensive, severe and unstable disease.
  • Generalized pustular psoriasis with systemic upset.
  • Erythrodermic psoriasis (>90% BSA) ‚Äì risks of heart failure, hypothermia, dehydration, malabsorption, anaemia.
  • Severe or atypical psoriasis ‚Üí consider HIV; escalate if rapidly worsening or unwell.
