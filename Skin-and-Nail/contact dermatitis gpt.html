Contact Dermatitis Clinical Reference

Fast decision-making algorithm for busy healthcare professionals

Definition & Classification

Types

  • Allergic Contact Dermatitis: Type IV hypersensitivity reaction after sensitization and re-exposure to specific allergen(s)
  • Irritant Contact Dermatitis: Non-immunological inflammatory reaction from direct toxic effects of irritants (no sensitization required)

Presentation

  • Acute: Erythema and vesiculation; may include dryness, scaling, and bullae
  • Chronic: Dryness, lichenification (thickening of the skin), and fissuring

Clinical Features

Irritant Contact Dermatitis

  • Symptoms: Stinging, burning, dryness, tightness, chapping
  • Signs: Usually restricted to area of contact; vesicles less common
  • Distribution: Often hands/webs of fingers; protected areas remain clear
  • Resolution: Symptoms improve within days of avoidance

Allergic Contact Dermatitis

  • Symptoms: Itching (dominant)
  • Signs: Blistering, weeping, edema in severe cases
  • Timing: Reaction 24-72 hours after re-exposure
  • Distribution: May affect areas not directly in contact with allergen

Assessment Approach

History

  • Onset, location, chronicity of rash
  • Possible precipitants: cosmetics, personal care products, jewelry, detergents, water, occupational exposures
  • Occupation (especially wet work, hairdressing, healthcare, catering)
  • Past medical/family history of atopy

Examination

  • Document distribution and morphology of rash
  • Common sites: Hands (most common), face, eyelids, ears, scalp, neck, axillae, ano-genital area, lower legs, feet
  • Signs of secondary infection

Management Algorithm

1
Suspected Contact Dermatitis
2
Identify and avoid causative agent (if possible)
3
Liberal emollients + soap substitutes (NOT aqueous cream)
4
Consider topical corticosteroids (based on severity, site, age)
5
If secondary infection suspected → Treat appropriately
6
If complete avoidance not possible:
  • Rinse with water/soap substitute after contact
  • Substitute products without identified allergens/irritants
  • Use appropriate protective gloves
  • Avoid chemical accumulation under jewelry
7
If any of following → REFER to dermatology:
  • Severe, chronic, recurrent, or persistent dermatitis (especially hands/face)
  • Previously stable dermatitis now uncontrolled
  • Suspected allergy to topical treatments
  • No response to primary care treatment
  • Atypical features or unclear diagnosis
  • Occupational association

Common Irritants

Occupational Considerations

High-Risk Occupations

Beauticians Cooks Florists Hairdressers Metal workers Dental practitioners

Employer Obligations

  • Risk assessment
  • Exposure control
  • Report to HSE (Health and Safety Executive)

Prognosis

  • Variable: Depends on causative agent and feasibility of avoidance
  • Good: May resolve within weeks if causative agent easily identified and avoided
  • Poor: May be recurrent/persistent if allergens ubiquitous in environment
  • Poor Prognosis Factors: Continued exposure, certain allergens (chromium, epoxy resin), severe presentation, delayed diagnosis
