Assessment

History & Examination Core
  • Pain, swelling, drainage (acute): Common complaint in acute paronychia
  • Swollen, purulent nail fold (acute): Characteristic of acute paronychia on examination
  • Nail plate irregularities (chronic): Common complaint in chronic paronychia
  • Swelling/redness of nail folds (chronic): Common complaint in chronic paronychia
  • Missing cuticle (chronic): Characteristic of chronic paronychia on examination
  • Underlying nail plate abnormalities (chronic): Characteristic of chronic paronychia on examination
Risk Factors Important
Strong Risk Factors:
  • Microscopic or macroscopic injury to nail folds
  • Occupational risks (water/chemical exposure)
  • Barrier damage to nail folds/cuticle
  • Ingrown nail
  • Medications (chemotherapy, EGF inhibitors, retinoids)
Weak Risk Factors:
  • Toddler and adult age groups
  • Female sex
Investigations Critical

First-line Tests

  • Swab for Gram stain, culture, sensitivity (acute): Perform before antibiotics to establish if MRSA is present
  • Swab for Tzanck smear (herpetic): If herpes simplex infection suspected

Consider if Refractory

  • X-ray: Evaluate for osteomyelitis or underlying bone disorder
  • MRI: For resistant or atypical cases
  • Biopsy: Rule out malignancy (squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma)
  • Potassium hydroxide/fungal culture (chronic): May show Candida colonization

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Presence of risk factors (common)
  • Acute presentation: painful, swollen, purulent nail fold
  • Chronic presentation: missing cuticle, nail plate irregularities, swollen nail folds

Diagnosis

‚ñº
Clinical Diagnosis Primary
Acute Paronychia

Definition: Acute purulent infection of nail folds and periungual tissues, usually caused by Staphylococcus aureus

Features: Deep red, erythematous, swollen lateral nail fold with pocket of pus

Confirmation: Gram stain, culture, and sensitivity after drainage

Chronic Paronychia

Definition: Chronic irritant dermatitis of periungual tissues from barrier damage

Features: Missing cuticle, swollen nail folds, irregular superficial plate dystrophy

Diagnosis: Clinical based on typical findings and risk factors

Differential Diagnosis Consider
Herpetic Whitlow: Lancinating pain, vesicles, refractory to antibacterial treatment
Differentiating test: Tzanck smear, HSV culture/PCR
Traumatic Injury: History of injury differentiates from acute paronychia
Squamous Cell Carcinoma: Lack of response to therapy
Differentiating test: Biopsy
Myxoid Cyst: Swelling/translucency over nail folds

Severity Criteria

  • Mild acute paronychia: Less painful, minimal tissue involvement, no systemic signs
  • Severe acute paronychia: Significant pain, extended involvement, fluctuant pus, possible systemic signs

Acute Paronychia - Mild Bacterial Outpatient

First-line Options

  • Bacitracin topical: 500 units/g, apply 2-3√ó daily
  • Mupirocin topical: 2%, apply 2-3√ó daily
  • Fusidic acid topical: 2%, apply 2-3√ó daily
  • Bacitracin/neomycin/polymyxin B: Apply 2-3√ó daily

PLUS: Warm saline, aluminium acetate, chlorhexidine, or povidone-iodine soaks twice daily

PLUS: Incision and drainage of pus collection if present

Acute Paronychia - Severe Bacterial Requires Oral Antibiotics

First-line Oral Antibiotics

  • Dicloxacillin: 500 mg orally every 6 hours for 7-14 days
  • Cefalexin: 500 mg orally every 6 hours for 7-14 days

Penicillin-Allergic Options

  • Clindamycin: 300 mg orally every 6 hours for 7-14 days
  • Erythromycin: 500 mg orally every 6 hours for 7-14 days

Plus: Topical antibacterials and soaks as above. Always send fluid for culture and sensitivity to establish MRSA status.

Acute Paronychia - Herpetic Antiviral Required

Oral Antiviral Therapy

  • Aciclovir: 400 mg orally 3√ó daily for 7-10 days (or 200 mg 5√ó daily)
  • Valaciclovir: 1000 mg orally twice daily for 7-10 days
  • Famciclovir: 250 mg orally 3√ó daily for 7-10 days

Note: Diagnosis requires high clinical suspicion and confirmation by Tzanck smear, direct fluorescent antibody, culture, or PCR.

Chronic Paronychia Lifestyle Modification Critical

First-line Treatment

  • Patient Education: Explain normal nail barrier function
  • Moisture/Irritant Avoidance:
    • Cotton gloves under vinyl gloves for wet work
    • Avoid nail cosmetics (polish, remover, hardeners)
    • Alcohol-based hand cleanser or minimal mild soap
    • Complete hand drying after washing
    • Avoid chemical solvents (paint thinners, polishes)
  • Topical Corticosteroids: Class I or II for 2-3 weeks
  • Clobetasol topical: 0.05%, apply sparingly twice daily for 2-3 weeks
  • Fluocinonide topical: 0.05%, apply sparingly twice daily for 2-3 weeks

Secondary Options

  • Yeast/Bacterial Colonization:
    • Clotrimazole topical: 1%, apply 2-3√ó daily
    • Ciclopirox topical: 0.77%, apply twice daily
    • Fluconazole: 400 mg orally once weekly (rarely necessary)
  • Pseudomonas (green nail): Dilute white vinegar soaks for 5 minutes twice daily
Refractory Cases Specialist Referral

Second-line Options

  • Intra-lesional Corticosteroids:
    • Triamcinolone acetonide: 2.5-5 mg/mL (diluted from 10 mg/mL with lidocaine), intra-lesionally once monthly until improvement, then taper

Third-line Options

  • Surgery: Excision of proximal nail fold (square flap technique) for disease resistant to first- and second-line treatments
  • Retronychia: Nail plate avulsion ¬± NSAID (ibuprofen 200-400 mg every 4-6 hours as needed)

Follow-up

‚ñº
Complications Low Risk
Osteomyelitis (acute)

Deep-seated infection can spread to distal phalanx

Timeframe: Variable | Likelihood: Low

Septic Arthritis (acute)

Joint involvement from severe infection

Timeframe: Variable | Likelihood: Low

Onychomadesis

Separation of nail plate from matrix

Timeframe: Variable | Likelihood: Low

Prognosis Generally Excellent

Acute Paronychia

Excellent prognosis with patient education (to avoid barrier dysfunction) and specific antibiotic therapy.

Chronic Paronychia

Prognosis depends on patient education and adherence to protective measures. High risk of recurrence from occupational risks, nail cosmetic use, or manipulation.

Key Discussion Points:

  • Normal nail barrier function
  • Activities predisposing to chronic paronychia
  • Importance of moisture/contactant avoidance
Primary Prevention Patient Education

Prevention Strategies

  • Irritant avoidance and use of waterproof gloves during high-risk activities
  • Respect nail structures (cuticle not meant to be pushed back)
  • Understand high-risk activities that predispose to paronychia
  • Maintain normal nail barrier to prevent breakdown
When to Investigate Further Red Flags
  • Refractory or atypical cases unresponsive to standard therapy
  • Suspected underlying malignancy (biopsy indicated)
  • Severe or recurrent infections (evaluate for MRSA)
  • Systemic signs of infection
  • Underlying bone pain or swelling (X-ray/MRI for osteomyelitis)
