2. Diagnosis ‚Äì is this pityriasis rosea?

Pattern + distribution + context

Use this when triage suggests a non-emergency rash.

Classic pityriasis rosea
Most cases
  • ‚Ä¢ Herald patch (up to ~90%): 2‚Äì5 cm oval erythematous plaque with collarette of scale and central clearing on trunk, thigh, upper arm or neck.
  • ‚Ä¢ Few days‚Äì3 weeks later: multiple 0.5‚Äì1 cm oval, slightly raised pink-red or hypopigmented lesions with fine peripheral scale.
  • ‚Ä¢ Distribution: symmetrical, trunk & proximal limbs; lesions align with cleavage lines ‚Üí ‚ÄúChristmas tree‚Äù pattern.
  • ‚Ä¢ Palms/soles typically spared; lesions are not vesicular.
  • ‚Ä¢ Itch variable (often mild‚Äìmoderate); possible prodromal headache, malaise, low-grade fever or URTI symptoms.
üéØ
Atypical patterns & skin tone nuances
Look carefully
  • ‚Ä¢ Hypopigmented lesions more common in dark skin ‚Äì collarette scale and distribution remain key clues.
  • ‚Ä¢ Face & scalp involvement more common in children; oral lesions in ~25% (erythematous patches, erosions, petechiae or bullae).
  • ‚Ä¢ Variants (~20%): inverse (face/axillae/groin), few large flexural patches (Vidal), large plaques, papular, purpuric, pustular or vesicular forms.
  • ‚Ä¢ Recurrent cases (3‚Äì4%) may lack herald patch or show fewer/smaller lesions.
Atypical appearance or distribution ‚Üí low threshold for dermatology input or review.

3. Differential diagnosis ‚Äì side-by-side comparison

Columns make it easier to compare with pityriasis rosea at a glance.

Guttate psoriasis
Very common confusion
  • Numerous 2 mm‚Äì1 cm pink/red scaly papules on trunk & limbs.
  • Often 1‚Äì2 weeks after streptococcal throat infection.
  • Thicker scale; look for psoriasis elsewhere (scalp, nails).
  • No herald patch; distribution not along cleavage lines.
Tinea corporis / Pityriasis versicolor
Fungal causes
  • Tinea corporis ‚Äì annular enlarging plaques with active scaly edge and central clearing; usually fewer but larger lesions.
  • Pityriasis versicolor ‚Äì fine-scaly macules/patches on trunk/upper arms that may be fawn, pink, red, brown or very pale.
  • Consider mycology or an empiric antifungal trial if suspicion is high.
Drug-induced PR-like rash
Check medication history
  • Implicated drugs: ACE-inhibitors, beta-blockers, clonidine, gold, interferon, isotretinoin, lamotrigine, metronidazole, NSAIDs, omeprazole, terbinafine, some vaccines.
  • No herald patch; lesions more violaceous; itch often more severe.
  • Eosinophilia may be present.
  • Discuss stopping culprit drug with specialist team if disease-modifying.
HIV seroconversion & secondary syphilis
STI-related causes
  • Acute HIV ‚Äì symmetrical erythematous maculopapular rash, including face, trunk, limbs, palms & soles plus fever, sore throat, lymphadenopathy.
  • Secondary syphilis ‚Äì non-itchy rash, commonly involving palms/soles; may see condylomata lata and mucosal lesions.
  • Have a low threshold for HIV and syphilis serology where risk or doubt exists.
Lichen planus
Purple, polygonal papules
  • Flat-topped purple polygonal papules (1‚Äì10 mm) on wrists, ankles.
  • Wickham‚Äôs striae; oral and genital involvement common.
  • More persistent than pityriasis rosea; may need biopsy/dermatology review.
Pityriasis lichenoides & others
When morphology is unusual
  • Pityriasis lichenoides ‚Äì red-brown papules evolving into vesicles, pustules, haemorrhagic crusts/ulcers with pigmentary change.
  • Also consider erythema annulare centrifugum, erythema migrans, granuloma annulare, subacute cutaneous lupus, leprosy, pregnancy-related eruptions, seborrhoeic dermatitis.
  • Unclear diagnosis after review ‚Üí refer or discuss with dermatology.

4. Management & special situations

Reassure, relieve itch, refer when needed

Core explanation & safety netting
Key action
  • ‚Ä¢ Explain that pityriasis rosea is an acute, self-limiting rash, most often in young adults and children.
  • ‚Ä¢ New lesions may continue to appear for up to 6 weeks; most rashes fade within 2‚Äì6 weeks, some up to 3‚Äì5 months.
  • ‚Ä¢ Not contagious.
  • ‚Ä¢ After resolution, temporary darker or lighter patches can persist for months but there is no scarring; recurrence is uncommon (~3‚Äì4%).
  • ‚Ä¢ Provide written information (local or national dermatology resources) and safety-netting: seek urgent review if fever, new systemic symptoms, blistering, mucosal lesions, or if rash persists >3 months.
üíä
Symptomatic treatment for itch
Relief & adherence
  • ‚Ä¢ Emollients ‚Äì liberal, frequent use. Prefer appropriate base (cream/ointment) per eczema guidance. i MHRA: paraffin and paraffin-free emollients on clothing/bedding can still pose a fire risk. Advise avoiding smoking or naked flames near treated skin or soaked fabrics.
  • ‚Ä¢ Topical corticosteroid once‚Äìtwice daily for up to 4 weeks if itch is moderate‚Äìsevere: mildly potent (e.g. hydrocortisone 1%) or moderately potent (e.g. clobetasone 0.05%, betamethasone valerate 0.025%) on trunk/limbs depending on severity and site.
  • ‚Ä¢ Oral sedating antihistamine at night (e.g. chlorphenamine) if itch disturbs sleep; stop if no benefit after ~2 weeks.
  • ‚Ä¢ Tailor to age, comorbidities, pregnancy/breast-feeding and other drugs; always cross-check BNF/eMC for dosing and cautions.

Pityriasis rosea in first trimester pregnancy
Enhanced caution
  • ‚Ä¢ Reassure that pityriasis rosea is usually self-limiting and in most cases no harm to the pregnancy is seen.
  • ‚Ä¢ Data on miscarriage, preterm delivery and neonatal outcomes are limited and not conclusive; risk appears low in uncomplicated cases.
  • ‚Ä¢ If diagnosis is uncertain, rash extensive or systemic symptoms present, discuss with dermatology and obstetrics. Specialist teams may consider oral antiviral therapy in selected cases.
  • ‚Ä¢ Treat itch as above using agents with the best-known safety profile in pregnancy and minimal effective potency/dose.
üì§
When to refer to dermatology
Referral triggers
  • ‚Ä¢ Uncertain diagnosis after careful review of differentials.
  • ‚Ä¢ Atypical morphology or distribution (e.g. purpuric, vesicular, primarily acral, or features suggestive of other dermatoses).
  • ‚Ä¢ Extensive disease or severe itch not controlled in primary care.
  • ‚Ä¢ Rash persists >3 months despite typical features and conservative management.
  • ‚Ä¢ Pregnancy with extensive rash, systemic symptoms or diagnostic uncertainty (liaise with obstetrics as well).
