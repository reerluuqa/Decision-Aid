Red Flags & Warnings
  • Capsaicin cream is currently unavailable until at least June 2026
  • Avoid NSAIDs - no evidence for use in PHN
  • Pregabalin and gabapentin are Class C controlled substances (Schedule 3)
  • Monitor for signs of abuse/dependence with gabapentinoids
  • Do not use lidocaine plasters on inflamed/injured skin or active herpes zoster lesions

Referral criteria: Severe pain, significant functional impairment, or failure of primary care treatments.

Recommended Actions
  • First-line: Offer self-management advice and consider paracetamol ± codeine for mild-moderate pain
  • Neuropathic pain agents: Amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin based on local policy
  • For allodynia: Consider lidocaine plasters (max 3 plasters for ≤12 hours/day)
  • Vaccination: Shingles vaccine reduces PHN risk by 66-75% in those aged 70-79
  • Re-evaluate treatment after 2-4 weeks and at regular intervals

Titrate slowly: Neuropathic pain drugs take weeks to reach effective dose.

Diagnosis

When to Suspect PHN

Suspect post-herpetic neuralgia in a person with aching, burning, itching, lancinating, or sharp pain, unilaterally and in a dermatomal distribution. Pain may be intermittent or constant.

Examination Findings

  • Deficits in thermal, tactile, pinprick, or vibration sensation within or beyond affected dermatomes
  • Areas of anaesthesia
  • HyperalgesiaExcessive pain in response to noxious stimuli
  • AllodyniaPain precipitated by non-painful stimulus such as touch or pressure

Clinical Context

  • Usually associated with recent history of herpes zoster
  • Pain at site of previous rash
  • Patient may not remember or associate rash with current pain
  • Rarely occurs some time after acute herpes zoster resolution

Management Pathway

Initial Management

Self-Management Advice

  • Wear cotton or silk fabrics (least irritating)
  • Protect sensitive areas with firm bandage, compression clothing, or cling film
  • Consider frequent application of cold packs (unless causes allodynia)
  • Provide patient information from Shingles Support Society

Pain Assessment

  • Degree and nature of pain (constant vs intermittent)
  • Presence/absence of allodynia
  • Impact on daily activities, mobility, sleep
  • Screen for depression/anxiety

Pharmacological Management

Treatment Algorithm

  1. Mild-moderate pain: Paracetamol ± codeine (aware unlikely to be entirely effective alone)
  2. Uncontrolled pain: Add neuropathic pain agent (amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin)
  3. Prominent allodynia or concerns about CNS side effects: Consider lidocaine plasters
  4. Acute rescue therapy: Tramadol (short-term only, not long-term without specialist supervision)

Follow-up & Referral

Follow-up Schedule

  • Early follow-up to assess titration progress and tolerability
  • Use pain diary to monitor effectiveness
  • Re-evaluate lidocaine plaster use after 2-4 weeks
  • If well controlled, consider gradual dose reduction over time

Referral Criteria

  • Severe pain
  • Significant limitation in daily activities
  • Exhaustion of primary care treatment options
  • Consider referral for cognitive behavioral therapy

Background Information

Definition

Pain in a dermatomal distribution which persists for three months or more after either onset or healing of the shingles rash.

Caused by herpes zoster-induced peripheral and central neuronal damage.

Prevalence

  • Lifetime risk of herpes zoster in Europe: 23-30% (up to 50% in ≥80 years)
  • PHN reported in 5-30% following herpes zoster
  • Shingles vaccine reduces PHN incidence by 66-75% in vaccinated cohort (70-79 years)

Risk Factors

  • Increasing age
  • Prodromal pain prior to acute herpes zoster
  • Severe pain, extensive rash (>1 dermatome), ophthalmic involvement
  • Comorbidities: diabetes, respiratory disease, autoimmune disease, severe immunosuppression

Prognosis

  • Highly variable duration; up to 50% experience pain >1 year
  • Satisfactory pain relief achieved in <50% of cases
  • Adversely affects quality of life, physical functioning, sleep, and can cause anxiety/depression
