Always consider biopsy if any of these features are present. Common warts can mimic squamous cell carcinoma, especially in immunocompromised patients where HPV carries higher transformation risk.

Absolute Indications for Biopsy

  • Rapid growth - Sudden enlargement over weeks
  • Non-healing - Lesion fails to heal despite treatment
  • Lack of treatment response - No improvement after adequate therapy
  • Bleeding - Unexplained or persistent bleeding
  • Crateriform/hyperkeratotic appearance - Atypical morphology
  • Immunosuppression history - HIV, transplant, chemotherapy
  • Chronic sun/radiation exposure - Increased SCC risk

High-Risk Patient Groups

  • Immunocompromised patients - More susceptible, more lesions, larger size, different morphology
  • HIV infection - Higher prevalence and recalcitrant disease
  • Transplant recipients - On immunosuppressive medications
  • Chemotherapy patients - Immunosuppressed state
  • Prior actinic keratoses/skin malignancy - Higher transformation risk
Important Note

In immunocompromised patients, some HPV types carry a high risk of transformation into squamous cell carcinomas. Treat aggressively and maintain high suspicion.

Definition & Overview

Clinical Definition

Common warts (verrucae vulgaris) are elevated, round, hyperkeratotic skin papules with a rough greyish-white or light brown surface.

  • Caused by Human Papillomavirus (HPV) infection of keratinocytes
  • Affect most people during their lifetimes
  • Most common in children and young adults
  • Typically occur at sites prone to trauma (knees, elbows, fingers)
  • Filiform warts have stalk-like appearance with multiple spikes

HPV Types Associated

HPV-1 Plantar warts
HPV-2 Common warts
HPV-4 Common warts
HPV-27 Common warts
HPV-57 Common warts
HPV-7 Butcher warts

HPV infection may be clinical, sub-clinical, or latent. Plantar warts are usually associated with HPV-1. Butcher warts (from repetitive trauma) are classically associated with HPV-7.

Risk Factors

Water immersion Nail biting Immunocompromised Meat/fish handling Age <35 years

Strong Risk Factors

  • Water immersion - Swimming, regular dishwashing
  • Meat/fish handling - 50% prevalence in these occupations
  • Nail biting - Increased risk of periungual warts
  • Age under 35 - Only 15% occur in patients >35 years
  • Immunocompromised state - HIV, transplant, chemotherapy

Contributing Factors

  • Skin breaks - Any condition causing breaks increases risk
  • Direct contact - Physical contact with wart
  • Autoinoculation - Spreading from infected skin or shaving
  • Nail biters - Warts may spread to lips and tongue

Clinical Diagnosis

Key Diagnostic Features

  • Rough papule on hand, finger, periungual area, or other skin
  • Filiform papule on body or face with stalk-like appearance
  • Flat, pink, slightly scaly papule on face or digits
  • Tiny black dots after paring - thrombosed capillaries (pathognomonic)
  • Size: Pinpoint to 1 cm, averaging 5 mm
  • Colour: Greyish-white or light brown
  • Satellite lesions - Multiple smaller lesions after initial wart

Typical Presentation

  • History: Lesion enlarging over weeks to months
  • Symptoms: May fissure, bleed, become painful when scratched
  • Location: Hands (fingers, palms), periungual areas, face
  • Configuration: Individual or mosaic pattern (plantar)
  • Filiform warts: Stalk with sharp spikes, common on face
  • Periungual warts: Common in nail biters, around nail fold
Diagnostic Clue

Gentle paring with a scalpel revealing tiny black dots on the surface is a helpful diagnostic clue representing thrombosed capillaries.

Diagnostic Approach Summary

Clinical Diagnosis
History and physical examination alone are sufficient in healthy individuals
Skin Biopsy
If persistent, atypical, or non-responsive lesions. Shows hyperkeratosis, koilocytes, thrombosed capillaries
Immunohistochemistry
May reveal HPV antigen but has low sensitivity

Treatment Algorithm

Quick Decision Guide
1
FIRST ASSESSMENT

Assess Immunocompetence

Determine if patient is immunocompetent or immunocompromised (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy, immunosuppressive medications)

Immunocompetent Patients

üü¢ FIRST-LINE: Watch & Wait or Salicylic Acid
Watch & Wait
Many warts regress spontaneously. Consider especially in children. Clearance: 23% at 2 months, 30% at 3 months, 65-78% at 2 years
Salicylic Acid (17%)
Apply 1-2 drops, cover with occlusive bandage for 12-24 hours. Cure rate up to 75%. Soak and debride first.
Adjunct Option

Duct tape occlusion - 6 days on, 1 day off cycle. Response rates up to 85% by week 8.

üü† SECOND-LINE: Cryotherapy
Cryotherapy with Liquid Nitrogen
Spray gun: Treat to produce blister in 24-48 hours.
Cotton swab: 5 seconds dip, 10 seconds application (for children, facial skin).
Optimal: Quick freeze 10 seconds, slow thaw 40 seconds.
Repeat: Every 2-3 weeks.
Caution

Avoid in patients with Fanconi anemia, cryoglobulinemia, poor peripheral circulation, Raynaud's phenomenon - risk of severe blisters.

Silver Nitrate Alternative

10% solution: Apply every other day for 3-6 weeks. May cause temporary brown skin discoloration.

üî¥ THIRD-LINE: Immunotherapy / Surgery
Candida Antigen
0.3 mL intralesionally every 3 weeks, max 5 treatments. Stimulates immune response.
Imiquimod 5%
Apply once daily at bedtime for 4 weeks. Use every other day on non-facial lesions.
Fluorouracil 2.5%
Apply sparingly at bedtime for 4 weeks after soaking and paring wart.
Surgery
Cold-scalpel or CO2 laser for resistant lesions. Best combined with topical adjuvant.

Immunocompromised Patients

Critical Note

Treat aggressively - some HPV types carry high risk of transformation into squamous cell carcinomas.

üü¢ FIRST-LINE: Immunotherapy + Debridement
Debridement + Salicylic Acid
Soak 5 min, debride with clean instrument, apply 17% salicylic acid under occlusion.
Adjunct Immunotherapy
Candida antigen 0.3 mL intralesionally every 3 weeks (max 5) OR Imiquimod 5% topical.
Important

Consult transplant immunologist, oncologist, or infectious disease physician before prescribing imiquimod or Candida antigen.

üü† SECOND-LINE: Cryotherapy + Immunotherapy
Cryotherapy + Local Immunotherapy
Cryotherapy every 2-3 weeks combined with Candida antigen OR imiquimod OR fluorouracil (2.5%). Apply immunotherapy immediately after lesion thaws.
üî¥ THIRD-LINE: Oral Retinoids
Isotretinoin
0.5 to 1 mg/kg/day orally in 2 divided doses. For patients with multiple resistant warts.
Acitretin
10-25 mg orally once daily. Indicated for immunocompromised patients with multiple resistant warts.
Prescribing Warning

Must be prescribed by physician familiar with adverse effects and management of serious adverse events.

Filiform Warts - Special Treatment

Filiform warts have a stalk-like appearance with multiple spikes and commonly affect the face.

üü¢ FIRST-LINE: Cryotherapy
Best approach: Metal forceps dipped in liquid nitrogen for 5 seconds, applied to base of stalk until entire lesion frozen.
Single freeze-thaw cycle is equally effective as two cycles.
Repeat: Every 2-3 weeks after scab sloughs off.
üü† SECOND-LINE: Surgery
Cold-scalpel: Under local anesthesia, sterile conditions.
CO2 laser: Complete ablation, requires smoke evacuator.
Pulsed-dye laser: No anesthesia needed, induces thrombosis.
üîµ ADJUNCT: Imiquimod
Topical 5% applied once daily at bedtime for 4 weeks to reduce recurrence risk.

Prognosis & Spontaneous Clearance

Natural History in Immunocompetent Patients

Most common warts in healthy people resolve spontaneously. Watch-and-wait approach is reasonable, especially in children.

Treatment Goals
  • Destroy the lesion
  • Induce cytotoxicity against infected cells
  • Promote antiviral immune responsiveness to prevent recurrence

Best outcomes are achieved with a combination of destructive and immune sensitisation approaches.

Patient Education Points

Key Messages for Patients

Warts are common and usually harmless - Many resolve on their own, especially in children
Avoid prolonged water immersion - Swimming and regular dishwashing increase risk
Don't bite nails - This increases risk of periungual warts and spread to lips/tongue
Use separate towels - When cleaning affected skin to avoid auto-inoculation
Avoid trauma to affected areas - May cause warts to spread by auto-inoculation
Skin-to-skin contact - Avoid direct contact with warts on other people
Treatment takes time - 2-3 month trial needed before assessing effectiveness

Emerging & Adjunctive Treatments

Topical Cidofovir

For especially recalcitrant lesions. Not FDA-approved for warts - may need compounding pharmacy.

Recalcitrant Cases

HPV Vaccine

Case reports of decreased warts with vaccination. Further investigation required.

Investigational

Photodynamic Therapy

20% aminolevulinic acid + broad-band light. Low recurrence, minor adverse reactions.

Promising

Hydrogen Peroxide 45%

Phase 3 trials show significantly higher clearance rates. Side effects: pain, erythema, pruritus.

FDA-Approved

Intralesional MMR Vaccine

Immunotherapeutic option encouraging regression of untreated distant lesions.

Immunotherapy

Zinc Supplementation

Oral zinc sulfate reported effective for recalcitrant warts, especially in zinc-deficient populations.
