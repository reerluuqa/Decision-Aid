Fungal Nail Infection Clinical Algorithm

Quick reference guide for diagnosis and management of onychomycosis

Assessment & Diagnosis

1 Clinical suspicion if:
  • Discolored, thickened, brittle nails
  • Toenails affected 7× more than fingernails
  • Common presentations: distal/lateral, superficial white, proximal, subungual hyperkeratosis
2 Assessment:
  • Document symptoms and impact on quality of life
  • Check comorbidities (diabetes, immunosuppression, psoriasis)
  • Examine for associated fungal skin infections
3 Confirmation:
Sampling technique:
  • Clean with 70% alcohol first
  • Include full thickness of affected nail
  • Collect debris from under nail
  • Store at room temperature (do not refrigerate)
Interpret results:
  • Dermatophytes: Microscopy OR culture positive
  • Candida: BOTH microscopy AND culture positive
  • Non-dermatophytes: BOTH positive on ≥2 samples

Management Approach

No Treatment Needed If:

  • Asymptomatic
  • No cosmetic concerns
  • No risk factors for complications
1 Self-Care Advice (All Patients):
  • Keep nails trimmed short
  • Wear well-fitting, non-occlusive footwear
  • Use absorbent cotton socks
  • Maintain good foot hygiene
  • Treat associated tinea pedis
  • Wear protective footwear in communal areas
2 Topical Treatment Consider If:
  • Very early, distal, or superficial involvement
  • Superficial white onychomycosis
  • Contraindication to oral antifungals

Option: Amorolfine 5% nail lacquer

1-2× weekly for 6 months (fingernails) or 9-12 months (toenails)

Low cure rates - manage expectations

3 Oral Antifungal Treatment Consider If:
  • Moderate to severe involvement
  • Symptomatic (pain, discomfort)
  • Significant psychological distress
  • High risk of complications (diabetes, immunosuppression)
  • Multiple nails affected

First-line: Terbinafine

Fingernails: 6 weeks | Toenails: 12 weeks

Monitor LFTs before and during treatment

Alternative: Itraconazole

Pulse therapy: 2 pulses (fingernails) or 3 pulses (toenails)

Each pulse = 200mg twice daily for 1 week, then 3 weeks off

Treatment Failure & Referral

Consider Treatment Failure If:

  • No clinical improvement after 3-6 months
  • Mycological cure not achieved
  • Recurrence after initial response
1 Management Options:
  • Confirm diagnosis with repeat sampling
  • Extend treatment duration
  • Consider alternative antifungal
  • Combination therapy (topical + oral)
2 Refer to Specialist If:
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Treatment failure despite appropriate therapy
  • Severe matrix involvement
  • Immunocompromised patient
  • Child under 12 years
  • Suspected bacterial superinfection or cellulitis

Prognostic Factors

Poor Prognosis Indicators:

  • Lateral edge involvement
  • >50% nail involvement
  • Matrix involvement
  • Nail thickness >2mm
  • Associated paronychia
  • Slow-growing nails
  • Comorbidities (diabetes, PAD, immunosuppression)

Treatment Outcomes:

  • Complete cure rates: 30-60% even with systemic treatment
  • Recurrence rates: 20-50%

Onychomycosis is difficult to eradicate and often recurs. Discuss realistic expectations with patients before starting treatment.
