Initial Assessment

If a person presents with a suspected neck lump, assess the clinical features to determine the underlying cause.

Key Clinical Questions:

  • Onset & Duration: When did it appear? Is it growing rapidly?
  • Location: Anterior triangle, posterior triangle, or midline?
  • Size & Growth: Persistent (>6 weeks) or rapidly growing lumps may indicate malignancy
  • Associated Symptoms: Pain, fever, weight loss, night sweats, dysphagia, hoarseness
Red Flag: Age ≥40 with persistent lump
Caution: Unexplained lump for 2-4 weeks
Stable: Likely reactive lymphadenopathy

Examination Steps:

1. General Inspection: Assess for stridor, superior vena cava compression
2. Palpation: Stand behind patient, palpate neck areas
  • Anterior triangle: Thyroid, submandibular, lymph nodes
  • Posterior triangle: Lymph nodes, carotid, lipoma
  • Midline: Thyroid, thyroglossal cyst, dermoid cysts
3. Lump Characteristics: Position, size, tenderness, mobility, consistency
4. Special Maneuvers: Ask to swallow (thyroid lumps move upwards), protrude tongue (thyroglossal cysts move)

Malignancy Risk Factors:

  • Age ≥40 years
  • Smoking, excess alcohol intake
  • Previous head/neck cancer or irradiation
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis (increased lymphoma risk)
  • Family history of head/neck cancer or endocrine tumours

Differential Diagnosis

Neck lumps can be categorized by their most likely causes based on patient age and clinical presentation.

Children (Mostly Benign)
Reactive Cervical Lymphadenopathy: Most common cause Stable
Congenital Causes: Thyroglossal cyst, branchial cyst, cystic hygroma Stable
Infectious: Viral URTI, EBV, bacterial infection Caution if persistent
Young Adults
Inflammatory/Infectious: Reactive lymphadenopathy, glandular fever Stable
Developmental: Branchial cysts, thyroglossal cysts Stable
Less Common: Lymphoma, thyroid cancer Caution
Adults ≥40 (Increased Malignancy Risk)
Malignancy: Lymphoma, metastatic SCC, thyroid cancer Urgent referral
Thyroid Nodules: 7-15% risk of malignancy Caution
Salivary Gland Tumors: 80% benign, higher malignancy in submandibular Caution

Normal Structures That Can Be Mistaken for Lumps:

  • Transverse process of C1/C2 vertebra
  • Hyoid bone, thyroid/cricoid cartilage
  • Submandibular glands (normal anatomy)

Management Scenarios

Management depends on the suspected cause of the neck lump.

Skin Lump
Concerning features (dark/irregular pigmentation, ulceration, bleeding): Urgent cancer pathway referral
Benign diagnosis (lipoma, epidermoid cyst): Reassure, no action needed
Suspected skin infection/abscess: Antibiotics, consider incision & drainage
Lymphadenopathy
Suspected upper airway obstruction: Emergency hospital admission
Suspected viral URTI: Review if unwell or persistent >7 days
Unexplained lymphadenopathy in adult ≥25: Urgent FBC, consider cancer referral
Persistent cervical lymphadenopathy in adult ≥40: Urgent CXR to exclude lung cancer
Thyroid Lump
Upper airway obstruction (stridor): Emergency admission
Unexplained thyroid lump with red flags: Urgent cancer pathway referral
Non-suspicious nodule with normal TFTs: Routine endocrinology referral
Longstanding unchanging nodule, no red flags: Monitor in primary care
Salivary Gland Lump
Persistent/unexplained parotid/submandibular swelling: Urgent ENT referral (within 2 weeks)

Follow-up & Referrals

Key referral pathways and follow-up recommendations based on NICE guidelines.

NICE Quality Standards for Suspected Cancer:

  • GPs have direct access to diagnostic endoscopy, ultrasound, MRI, X-ray and CT for people with suspected cancer
  • People with suspected cancer who are referred to a cancer service are given written information encouraging them to attend
    • NICE, 2021
Referral Timeframes
Emergency admission: Stridor, superior vena cava obstruction, dysphagia with aspiration Immediate
Urgent suspected cancer pathway: Unexplained neck lump in person ≥45, or persistent at any age Within 2 weeks
Very urgent referral (children/young adults ≤24): Ultrasound findings uncertain or suggestive of sarcoma Within 48 hours
Investigations in Primary Care
Do NOT delay specialist referral by undertaking investigations if serious cause suspected
Consider: FBC for generalized lymphadenopathy, TFTs for thyroid lumps, CXR for ≥40 with supraclavicular nodes
Avoid: Routine neck ultrasound in primary care (may cause diagnostic delay)
Key Decision Points
Persistent lymphadenopathy (>2-4 weeks): Urgent ENT referral
Rapidly growing neck mass: Higher suspicion for malignancy
Lymph node >3cm: More indicative of malignancy
Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy: Consider primary below clavicle (lung, GI, breast)
