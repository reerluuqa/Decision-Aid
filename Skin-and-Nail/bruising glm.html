Bruising Assessment & Management Algorithm

Quick reference guide for clinical decision making

Definition

  • Bruise: Hematoma from bleeding under intact skin due to vascular damage/injury
  • Normal bruising: Follows trauma, resolves in 2-3 weeks, color changes (red→purple→brownish-yellow)
  • Easy bruising: Occurs with minimal/no recognized trauma

Assessment Approach

History

  • Mucocutaneous bleeding (nosebleeds, gum bleeding)
  • Excessive/prolonged bleeding
  • Timing: Immediate (platelet disorder) vs Delayed 24+ hrs (coagulation disorder)
  • Medications: Anticoagulants, antiplatelets, corticosteroids
  • Family history: Bleeding disorders, easy bruising, menorrhagia
  • General health: Fatigue, weight loss, fever, night sweats (malignancy)

Examination

  • Document: Location, pattern, size, shape of bruises (photograph if appropriate)
  • Skin: Petechiae, palpable purpura, scarring, healing
  • Joints: Swelling, tenderness, hypermobility
  • Abdomen: Hepatosplenomegaly, ascites
  • Head/neck: Oropharyngeal bleeding, retinal hemorrhages
  • Lymphadenopathy

Red Flags for Non-Accidental Injury

  • Bruises in non-mobile infants
  • Bruises disproportionate to explanation
  • Patterned bruises (handprints, implements)
  • Bruises on face, ears, neck, abdomen, buttocks, genitals
  • Bruises on both sides of face/head
  • Delayed presentation
  • Inconsistent/inadequate explanations

Investigation

All Patients

  • Urine dipstick (hematuria)

Adults with Unexplained Bruising

  • Urgent (within 48 hrs): FBC with platelets, blood film
  • Clotting screen (PT, aPTT, INR if on warfarin)
  • Consider LFTs, renal/thyroid function

Children with Unexplained Bruising

  • Urgent (within 48 hrs): FBC with platelets, blood film
  • Liaise with pediatrician/pediatric hematologist for further investigations

When to Refer/Admit

Immediate Admission

  • Significant active bleeding not responding to simple measures

Immediate Referral (Suspected Non-Accidental Injury)

  • Children, elderly, vulnerable adults: Refer to social services/police/NSPCC

Urgent Specialist Assessment

  • Children: Unexplained petechiae or hepatosplenomegaly → Leukemia
  • Children: Periorbital bruising with abdominal mass/enlarged organ → Neuroblastoma
  • Adults: FBC suggestive of leukemia → Hematology

Other Referrals

  • Adults with low platelets, abnormal clotting screen, or suspected bleeding disorder → Hematology

Management

  • Simple bruising: Analgesia (paracetamol)
  • Warfarin with abnormal INR: Adjust dose per local protocol
  • Drug-induced thrombocytopenia: Withdraw offending drug if possible
Note: Color of bruise is NOT a reliable indicator of age
