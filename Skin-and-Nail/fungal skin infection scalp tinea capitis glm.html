Fungal Scalp Infection (Tinea Capitis)

Clinical Reference Guide for Diagnosis and Management

Definition & Epidemiology
  • Fungal infection of scalp hair follicles and surrounding skin
  • Main causes: Trichophyton tonsurans (urban areas), Microsporum canis (rural areas)
  • Primarily affects prepubertal children (peak 3-7 years)
  • More common in Afro-Caribbean children
  • Transmission: direct contact or fomites (hairbrushes, hats)
Clinical Features

Non-inflammatory:

  • Scaling and itch
  • Circular patches of hair loss
  • "Black dot" appearance (broken hair stubs)

Inflammatory:

  • Erythema, pustules, crusting
  • Kerion (painful boggy mass)
  • Alopecia, scarring
  • Lymphadenopathy
Diagnostic Approach
1

Suspect based on clinical features

2

Skin and hair sampling for microscopy & culture

3

Results timeline:

  • Microscopy results: 1-2 days
  • Culture results: 2-3 weeks
Management of Complications
  • Secondary bacterial infection: Treat appropriately
  • Id reaction: Continue antifungal, consider topical steroid
  • Kerion: Urgent dermatology referral
  • Severe inflammation: Consider short course of oral corticosteroids
Follow-up & Referral Criteria

Follow-up:

4-8 weeks after treatment completion

Refer to dermatology if:

  • Kerion (urgent)
  • Insufficient experience for children
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Treatment failure
  • Severe/extensive/recurrent infection
  • Severe Id reaction not responding
  • Immunocompromised
Management of Contacts
1

Contact of confirmed case

2

Skin and hair sampling for culture

3

Management based on results:

  • Symptomatic + confirmed infection: Self-care + oral antifungal
  • Asymptomatic carrier: Seek specialist advice:
    • High spore count → May need oral antifungal
    • Low spore load → May use ketoconazole shampoo

Management Algorithm

1

Suspected fungal scalp infection

Is kerion present?
YES → Urgent dermatology referral
NO → Continue to next step
2

Self-care advice:

  • Soften crusts, gently remove
  • Disinfect objects (hats, brushes)
  • Don't share towels
  • Inspect household contacts
3

Treatment decision:

ADULTS

Positive microscopy/culture OR strong clinical suspicion → Oral antifungal

CHILDREN

  • Diagnosis certain + expertise → Oral antifungal
  • OR
  • Seek specialist advice
4

Oral antifungal choice:

Based on location:
Urban area (T. tonsurans likely) → Terbinafine (4 weeks)
Rural area (Microsporum likely) → Griseofulvin (4-8 weeks)
5

Adjust based on culture results:

  • T. tonsurans → Continue/switch to terbinafine
  • Microsporum → Continue/switch to griseofulvin
  • If griseofulvin not tolerated → Consider itraconazole
6

Add topical treatment:

  • Ketoconazole shampoo (2x/week, 2-4 weeks)
  • Imidazole cream (children <5 years, daily x1 week)
7

Review 4-8 weeks after treatment

Self-Care Advice for Patients
  • Attend school/nursery normally once treatment started
  • Soften and gently remove crusts
  • Disinfect or discard items that touch scalp
  • Don't share towels, wash frequently
  • Inspect household contacts regularly
  • If pet suspected, seek veterinary care
