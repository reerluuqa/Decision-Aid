Psoriasis: Diagnosis & Management

Last Revised: December 2024 | Target: Adults (18+) in Primary Care

‚ö†Ô∏è Medical Emergencies

Immediate Action

Arrange immediate same-day specialist dermatology assessment if suspected:

  • Erythrodermic Psoriasis: >90% body surface area affected. Lesions feel warm. Risk of heart failure, hypothermia, dehydration.
  • Generalized Pustular Psoriasis: Rapid widespread erythema followed by sterile pustules. Systemic signs: fever, malaise, tachycardia.

üìù Assessment & History

Clinical Workup

Key Questions

  • Symptoms: Itch, pain, bleeding, scaling.
  • Course: Flares, remissions, triggers (stress, trauma/Koebner, infection, drugs).
  • Systemic: Fever/malaise (rule out emergencies).
  • Joints: Stiffness, pain, swelling (Psoriatic Arthritis).

Screening Tools

Joint Screening (PEST)

Use the Psoriasis Epidemiology Screening Tool (PEST).

If score ‚â• 3 out of 5, consider referral to rheumatologist.

Quality of Life (DLQI)

Assess physical, psychological, and social impact using the Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI).

Severity (PGA)

Physician's Global Assessment (6-point scale):

0 (Clear) to 5 (Very Severe).

üîç Diagnostic Features

Type Features Sites
Chronic Plaque Well-defined plaques, silvery scale, Auspitz sign (bleeding on removal). Extensor surfaces (elbows/knees), scalp, sacrum.
Guttate "Raindrop" lesions (2mm-1cm). Often post-strep infection. Trunk, proximal limbs.
Scalp Thick scale, may adhere to hair shaft. Occipital region, hairline.
Nail Pitting, onycholysis, "oil drop" sign, hyperkeratosis. Fingernails > Toenails.

üíä Management: Trunk & Limbs

Stepwise Approach

Advise: Not infectious, controls symptoms but not cure, lifestyle advice (smoking/alcohol/weight).

Core Treatment: Prescribe generous emollients (500g/week) to reduce scale and itch.

Step 1: Initial Treatment (4 Weeks)

  • Potent Corticosteroid (AM) + Vitamin D (PM) applied separately.
  • OR Combined product (e.g., calcipotriol/betamethasone) applied OD.
  • Review at 4 weeks. If good response, continue until clear.
  • Safety: Max 8 weeks continuous potent steroid at one site. Require 4-week break before restarting steroid.
Step 2: Poor Response (After 4 Weeks)

If poor response to Step 1:

  • Stop the potent corticosteroid.
  • Apply Vitamin D preparation twice daily for up to 12 weeks.
Step 3: Resistant Disease

If poor response to Vitamin D alone (after 8-12 weeks total treatment):

  • Potent Corticosteroid applied twice daily for up to 4 weeks.
  • OR Coal Tar preparation once or twice daily.
Short-Contact Dithranol

Can be offered if educational support for self-use is available. Useful for treatment-resistant plaques.

üíÜ Management: Scalp

Use lotions, solutions, or gels for hair-bearing areas.

Step 1: Initial Therapy

  • Potent Corticosteroid (OD) for up to 4 weeks.
  • OR Vitamin D preparation (OD) if steroids not tolerated/mild disease.
  • OR Coal tar shampoo (adjunct, not monotherapy for severe disease).
Thick Scale? Use a salicylic acid, olive oil, or coconut oil preparation to remove scale before applying potent steroid.
Step 2: Poor Response (After 4 Weeks)
  • Switch to Combined Potent Steroid + Vitamin D (OD) for up to 4 weeks.
  • OR Vitamin D alone (OD) if not already tried.

üõ°Ô∏è Face, Flexures & Genitals

Caution Required
Warning: Do NOT prescribe potent/very potent corticosteroids for face, flexures, or genitals due to risk of atrophy.

Treatment Protocol

  • Mild to Moderate Corticosteroid (OD or BD) for short term (up to 2 weeks).
  • Review after 4 weeks.
  • Maintenance: Only use steroids for 1-2 weeks per month. Requires "treatment break".
  • Off-label option: Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus/pimecrolimus) for difficult sites.

üíß Guttate Psoriasis

Usually self-limiting (resolves 3-4 months).

  • Antibiotics: Do NOT use for guttate psoriasis even if triggered by strep (unless active infection persists).
  • Topical: Emollients, or weak steroid/coal tar if needed.
  • Phototherapy: Referral for UVB if widespread/extensive.

üìã Quick Prescribing Reference

Emollients

Dose: 250-500g per week. Apply liberally and frequently.

Cautions: Fire risk (paraffin). Avoid aqueous cream (skin reactions).

Vitamin D Preparations

Max Dose: Calcipotriol max 100g/week.

Contraindications: Calcium metabolism disorders, severe liver/kidney disease.

Pregnancy: Avoid unless essential.

Adverse Effects: Local irritation, hypercalcemia (rare).

Coal Tar

Contraindications: Sore/acute/pustular psoriasis, infected skin. First trimester pregnancy.

Adverse Effects: Staining, photosensitivity, folliculitis.

üè• Referral Criteria

Dermatology & Rheumatology

Urgent Rheumatology Referral

  • Suspected Psoriatic Arthritis (e.g., PEST score ‚â•3, joint swelling, dactylitis).

Dermatology Referral

  • Diagnostic Uncertainty.
  • Extensive Disease: >10% BSA.
  • Severity: Moderate to severe (PGA) or significant impact on wellbeing (DLQI).
  • Treatment Failure: Resistant to topical therapy or intolerance.
  • Complex Sites: Severe nail disease with functional impact.
  • Phototherapy consideration: e.g., for widespread guttate.
