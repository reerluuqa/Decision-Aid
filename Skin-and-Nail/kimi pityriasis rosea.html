Pityriasis Rosea

First trimester cases: discuss with secondary care. Possible association with adverse outcomes.

Herald patch → Christmas tree distribution on trunk. Common in ages 10-35 years.

Diagnosis & Clinical Features

Classic Presentation in 80% of Cases

When to Suspect Pityriasis Rosea

1
Herald Patch: Single, larger lesion (2-5cm) appears first. 'Collarette' scaling at margin, central depression. Present in up to 90% of cases.
2
Generalized Rash: Appears after 1-3 weeks. Multiple 0.5-1cm oval lesions with peripheral scaling.
3
Distribution: Symmetrical, trunk & proximal limbs. "Christmas tree" pattern along cleavage lines. Face/scalp involvement common in children.

Key Clinical Clues:

  • Color: Pink-red "salmon-colored" (hypopigmented in dark skin)
  • Scale: Peripheral collarette, central clearing
  • Itch: Variable, often mild to moderate
  • Prodrome: Malaise, headache, sore throat (reported but not common)
Ages 10-35
Slightly more common in females
Winter predominance (temperate climates)
Atypical Forms (20% of cases)
  • Inverse: Predominantly face, axillae, groin
  • Pityriasis circinata et marginata: Few large patches in axillae/inguinal region
  • Pityriasis gigantea: Larger lesions
  • Other variants: Pustular, purpuric, vesicular

Urgent Considerations

High Priority Situations

Pregnancy in First Trimester

Action: Discuss with secondary care/dermatology. Consider oral antiviral therapy (acyclovir) if obstetric concerns.

Rationale: Inconclusive but concerning evidence of possible associations with miscarriage, premature delivery, neonatal hypotonia/hyperactivity.

Red Flag Differential Diagnoses

Investigate for before confirming diagnosis:

  • Secondary Syphilis: Non-itchy maculopapular rash, palms/soles
  • HIV Seroconversion: Symmetrical rash including palms/soles

Action: Consider STI screen ± HIV test based on risk assessment.

When to Refer

  • Diagnosis uncertain
  • Atypical presentation suspected
  • Extensive disease or severe itch uncontrolled in primary care
  • Rash persists >3 months
  • First trimester pregnancy with concerns

Management

Self-Limiting Condition

Key Counseling Points

  • Rash may worsen before improving
  • Lesions fade without treatment in 2-6 weeks (up to 3-5 months)
  • Only symptomatic treatment needed for itch
  • Not contagious
  • Temporary hyper/hypopigmentation may occur (no scarring)
  • Recurrence rare (3-4%)

Symptomatic Treatment for Itch:

1
Emollients: Liberal application, especially after bathing. See eczema prescribing guidance.
2
Oral Antihistamines (off-label): Consider sedating option (e.g., chlorphenamine) at night if sleep disturbed. Stop if no relief after 2 weeks.
3
Topical Corticosteroids: Mildly potent (hydrocortisone 1%) or moderately potent (betamethasone valerate 0.025%, clobetasone 0.05%) for up to 4 weeks.

Prescribing Information

Emollients

Liberal application to itchy areas. Applied frequently and generously.

Safety: Advise patients not to smoke or go near naked flames due to fire risk with paraffin-based emollients.

Topical Corticosteroids

  • Mild potency: Hydrocortisone 1%
  • Moderate potency: Betamethasone valerate 0.025%, Clobetasone 0.05%
  • Application: Once or twice daily for up to 4 weeks
  • Choice: Based on itch severity and body site

Oral Antihistamines

  • Off-label use for itch in pityriasis rosea
  • Consider sedating option (chlorphenamine) at night if sleep disturbed
  • Discontinue if no benefit after 2 weeks
  • See CKS Itch - widespread for full prescribing details

Differential Diagnosis

Most Common Mimics
Guttate Psoriasis

Small pink/red scaly papules. Often follows streptococcal throat infection.

Discoid Eczema

Intensely itchy, coin-shaped plaques. May be vesicular or crusted.

Secondary Syphilis

Non-itchy, maculopapular rash. Involves palms/soles.

Drug Reaction

No herald patch, violet-red lesions, severe pruritus, eosinophilia.

Pityriasis Versicolor

Fine scale at edges, variable color (fawn, pink, brown). Affects chest/back/arms.

Tinea Corporis

Annular patches with active scaly edge and clear center. Gradually enlarge.

To differentiate: Look for herald patch, collarette scaling, Christmas tree distribution, and lack of palmar/plantar involvement.
