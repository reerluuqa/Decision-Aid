Pressure Ulcer Clinical Dashboard

Quick reference derived from NICE CKS (last revised Jan 2024). Use the sidebar to navigate between sections.

Red Flags / Do NOT Do
  • Do not massage or rub skin to prevent pressure ulcers
  • Do not give fluids specifically to prevent/treat ulcers if hydration is adequate
  • Do not prescribe nutritional supplements if intake is adequate
  • Do not use topical antiseptics or antimicrobials routinely
  • Do not offer electrotherapy or hyperbaric oxygen
  • Do not use gauze dressings
  • Do not routinely use NPWT (unless to reduce dressing changes)
Risk Assessment
Who to assess

Adults

Use Braden, Waterlow, Norton, or PURPOSE‑T.

Children

Use Braden Q Scale.
Commence on admission/transfer and reassess after surgery or mobility change.

At-risk if limited mobility, sensory loss, cognitive impairment, poor nutrition, prior ulcer, or device use.

Diagnosis & Assessment
  • Non‑blanchable erythema (or colour change in darker skin) over bony prominence
  • Localised skin/warmth change, pain at pressure site
  • Document area and depth; classify with NPUAP‑EPUAP I–IV, unstageable, or suspected deep tissue injury
  • Consider abuse/neglect if history or care is inconsistent
Differentials
Diabetic foot ulcer, moisture lesion, osteomyelitis, pyoderma gangrenosum, bullous pemphigoid, malignancy, peripheral or venous ulcer.
Management of Adult Pressure Ulcers
  • High‑spec foam mattress (or dynamic surface if inadequate)
  • Offload heels completely using heel‑suspension device or cushion; weight along calf
  • Use barrier cream if moisture risk; emollient for dry/inflamed skin
  • Autolytic debridement preferred; consider sharp if rapid clearance needed (by trained staff)
  • Systemic antibiotics only for systemic sepsis, spreading cellulitis, or osteomyelitis
  • Use warm‑moist wound‑healing dressings for cat II‑IV; NO gauze
Devices
High‑spec foam, dynamic support surface, wheelchair cushions, heel suspension, low‑friction clothing (Parafricta) if needed.
Prevention in Adults
  • Reposition ≥6 h if at risk; ≥4 h if high‑risk
  • Skin assessment by trained professional on admission and regularly
  • Provide education on causes, risk factors, early signs
  • High‑spec foam mattress/cushion for high‑risk individuals; dynamic surface if needed
  • Pressure‑relieving seating for prolonged sitters
  • Offload heels; use barrier creams if incontinence/oedema
Prevention in Children & Young People
  • Reposition ≥4 h if at risk; more frequently if high‑risk
  • Assess occipital skin (high‑risk area in children)
  • High‑spec foam cot mattress / overlay; wheelchair assessments for long‑term users
  • Offload heels and scalp where needed; use play experts to encourage repositioning
  • Same skin and barrier guidance as adults
Key Risk Factors
  • Significantly limited mobility, sensory loss, cognitive impairment
  • Poor posture, inability to reposition, nutritional deficiency
  • Prior/current ulcer, general skin moisture/integrity issues
  • Peripheral arterial disease, heart failure, diabetes, hypotension, sepsis
  • Older age > 70 yr (≈60 % of ulcers); neonates also at risk
  • Non‑pressure‑relieving support surface or device use
Heel Pressure‑Ulcer Prevention

Heels are a common site—offload completely using:

  • Heel‑suspension device (weight along calf; avoid Achilles/pop‑vein pressure)
  • Pillow/foam cushion or booties with CLP (constant low‑pressure material)
  • Low‑friction dressings or garments to reduce shear
  • Prophylactic dressing as adjunct if cat I‑II; for cat III‑IV use offloading device

Assess vascular/perfusion status of lower limbs on each review.
