Red Flags & Emergency Actions
Ocular Rosacea Complications

Refer immediately if:

  • Reduced vision
  • Pain on eye movement
  • Pain that keeps patient awake at night
  • Persistent eye discomfort >12 months despite treatment

Pathophysiology
Key Mechanisms:
  • Immune dysregulation - Cathelicidin LL-37, TLR2
  • Micro-organisms - Demodex folliculorum, H. pylori
  • Vascular dysfunction - Hyper-reactivity, increased blood flow
Contributing Factors:
  • UV radiation - Inflammation, neoangiogenesis
  • Genetics - HLA alleles, VEGF polymorphisms
  • Barrier dysfunction - Impaired skin barrier
Diagnostic Criteria
Diagnostic Phenotype (Presence of 1):
Fixed centrofacial erythema

Persistent redness of facial skin

Phymatous changes

Skin thickening, glandular hyperplasia (rhinophyma)

Major Phenotypes (Presence of 2+):
Papules and pustules

Dome-shaped red papules, often in crops

Flushing

Frequent blushing in response to triggers

Telangiectasia

Prominent superficial capillaries

Ocular manifestations

Lid margin telangiectases, conjunctival injection

Clinical Stages
Stage 1: Flushing

Transient erythema (pre-rosacea), may last hours to days

Stage 2: Persistent Erythema

Unremitting redness of the skin

Stage 3: Papules and Pustules

Dome-shaped red papules with or without pustules

Stage 4: Telangiectases

Prominent superficial capillaries

Key Diagnostic Factors
Factor Frequency Clinical Significance
Flushing Common Early and sometimes diagnostic feature
Erythema Common Seen in virtually all patients early in course
Papules and Pustules Common Present in progressive disease
Telangiectases Common Not necessary for diagnosis but hallmark feature
Ocular Manifestations Common Foreign body sensation, tearing, blurry vision
Phymatous Changes Uncommon 20× more common in men, often end-stage
Investigations
First Test to Order:
Clinical Diagnosis - Usually no tests necessary
Other Tests to Consider:
Skin Biopsy

To exclude sarcoidosis, connective tissue disease, mastocytosis

Antinuclear Antibody Titer

If SLE or other connective tissue disease suspected

Management Approach
For All Patients:
Lifestyle Changes

Identify and avoid triggers, maintain trigger diary

Skincare

Wash with emollients, moisturize regularly

Sun Protection

Zinc oxide or titanium dioxide sunscreen, wide-brimmed hat

Phenotype-Specific Treatment:
Fixed Centrofacial Erythema

Topical alpha agonists (brimonidine, oxymetazoline)

Papules and Pustules

Topical azelaic acid/ivermectin/metronidazole ± oral doxycycline

Phymatous Changes

Early: oral isotretinoin; Advanced: surgical options

Ocular Manifestations

Eyelid hygiene, omega-3 fatty acids, topical antibiotics

Special Considerations
Pregnancy:
Isotretinoin is teratogenic - Contraindicated in pregnancy

Tetracyclines may cause tooth staining in the baby

Long-term Use:
Low-dose doxycycline - Safe for long-term use with few adverse effects

No associated development of bacterial resistance

Monitoring & Follow-up
Initial Follow-up

4-6 weeks after starting treatment to assess response and adjust therapy

Maintenance Phase

Every 3-6 months for patients on long-term therapy

Ocular Rosacea

Refer to ophthalmologist if symptoms persist >12 months despite adequate treatment

Prognosis
Expected Course:
  • Lifelong condition with periods of remission
  • Symptoms can be controlled with appropriate treatment
  • Men more likely to progress to advanced disease
Quality of Life Impact:
Improved with Treatment

Appropriate treatment and trigger avoidance significantly improve quality of life

Patient Education
Key Education Points:
  • Nature of the disease and chronicity
  • Importance of trigger identification and avoidance
  • Proper skincare and sun protection
  • Medication adherence and proper application
