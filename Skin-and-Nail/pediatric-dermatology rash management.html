Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)

Routine Management
Key Diagnostic Features
  • Almost always itchy (if not itchy, reconsider diagnosis)
  • Family history of atopy (eczema, asthma, hay fever)
  • Distribution: cheeks → flexures → generalized
  • Appearance: dry, scaly, weepy, rough, or bumpy
  • Excoriation marks from scratching
  • Lichenification in chronic cases
  • Redness difficult to see in pigmented skin (Types V-VI)
Steroid Phobia Management
  • Robust evidence for long-term safety
  • Reassure patients and parents
  • Use appropriate potency for body area
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) as steroid-sparing alternative
  • Apply to damp skin after bathing
  • Use fingertip units for accurate dosing
RED FLAG: Eczema Herpeticum (Kaposi Varicelliform Eruption)
  • HSV infection on top of eczema background
  • Monomorphic punched-out erosions/vesicles
  • Child systemically unwell (fever, malaise)
  • URGENT REFERRAL - requires systemic antivirals
  • Avoid topical steroids until HSV treated
Management Plan
  1. Daily bathing with soap substitute (10-15 min, lukewarm water)
  2. Apply emollient liberally (1000g/month for children)
  3. Emollient as soap substitute - one in shower, one in room
  4. Topical steroids for flare: daily for 5-7 days until controlled
  5. Taper: alternate days, then twice weekly maintenance
  6. Consider topical calcineurin inhibitors for maintenance
  7. Bleach baths (Milton) for recurrent infected eczema
  8. Refer to eczema specialist nurse if struggling
Key Triggers
Heat/overheating Dry winter air Dust mites Stress Viral infections Harsh soaps Wool clothing

Creams Lotions Gels Ointments

Nappy Rash (Napkin Dermatitis)

Differential Diagnosis

General Management
  1. Keep area dry - change nappies frequently
  2. Use loose-fitting disposable nappies
  3. Avoid bubble baths and scented baby wipes
  4. Use fragrance-free barrier cream
  5. If candidal: clotrimazole or miconazole cream
  6. If not improving, consider swab for confirmation

Scabies

Treat Household Contacts
Clinical Presentation
  • Intensely itchy - worse at night
  • Excoriated papules, nodules, burrows
  • Common sites: hands, wrists, ankles, feet, waist, genitals
  • Genital involvement = diagnostic
  • In infants: can affect face and scalp
  • Household contacts usually symptomatic
Treatment Protocol
  1. Permethrin 5% cream - neck down, leave 8-12 hours
  2. Repeat application in 7 days
  3. Wash all bedding, clothing at 60°C
  4. Vacuum furniture and carpets
  5. Household contacts treated simultaneously
  6. Itch persists for 2-6 weeks post-treatment
Special Situations
  • Nodular scabies: Persistent itchy nodules - treat with topical steroids or URX HC
  • Infants & face involvement: Apply permethrin to face (avoid eyes/mouth)
  • Crusted (Norwegian) scabies: Highly contagious - refer urgently for intensive treatment

Impetigo

Mild vs Severe
Clinical Features
  • Honey-colored/crusty lesions - diagnostic hallmark
  • Caused by Staph aureus (most common) or Strep pyogenes
  • Risk factors: crowded living, poor hygiene, warm climate
  • Highly contagious
  • Bullous impetigo: flaccid bullae with yellow crust (Staph)

Eczema Coxsackium

HFMD in Atopic Children
What Is It?
  • Severe form of hand-foot-mouth disease in children with background atopy/eczema
  • Caused by Coxsackie A6 virus
  • More widespread and severe eruption than typical HFMD
  • Can have blisters, erosions, and crusting beyond typical HFMD distribution
  • Child may be systemically unwell with fever
Management
  • Self-limiting condition - conservative management
  • Maintain skin hydration with emollients
  • Supportive care: pain relief, hydration
  • Topical steroids if severe eczematous component
  • AVOID topical antibiotics unless clear secondary infection
  • Swab may give false positives due to normal skin flora
Key Distinction
Atopic background Severe HFMD presentation Conservative management Self-limiting

Molluscum Contagiosum

Self-Limiting - Reassure
Clinical Features
  • Umbilicated (dimpled) pearly papules
  • Multiple lesions, can be clustered
  • Common in children 1-10 years
  • May appear at sites of skin trauma (Koebnerization)
  • Not infected - despite appearing "angry"
  • Self-resolving over months to years
Key Message

"Herald's Patch" appearance: When lesions become red and inflamed, this signals the body's immune system fighting the virus. This is a positive sign - lesions will resolve within weeks!

Management Options
  • First line: Reassurance and observation
  • Parents often worried - explain natural history
  • If treating: molluscum curettage, cryotherapy
  • Topical treatments: potassium hydroxide, cantharidin
  • Avoid treatment in pigmented skin - risk of PIH
When to Treat
Cosmetic concern Secondary infection Parent insistence
When NOT to Treat
Asymptomatic Resolving Pigmented skin (PIH risk)

Infantile Hemangioma

URGENT REFERRAL Criteria
Key Features
  • Appears after birth (unlike port-wine stain)
  • Rapid growth phase: 0-6 months
  • Involution phase: 6-12 months onwards
  • 80% resolve by age 4
  • Bright red/"strawberry" appearance
URGENT REFERRAL - High Risk Locations
  • Periocular: Risk of amblyopia (vision obstruction)
  • Lips: Risk of feeding difficulties
  • Chin/beard area: Risk of airway involvement (PHACE syndrome)
  • Scalp: Risk of underlying structural abnormalities
  • Lumbosacral area: Risk of spinal dysraphism
  • Ulceration: Painful, may require intervention
Management
  • Asymptomatic small hemangiomas: Reassurance
  • High-risk lesions: Urgent referral to pediatrics/dermatology
  • First-line treatment: Oral propranolol (very effective)
  • Topical beta-blockers for thin, superficial lesions
  • Ulcerated lesions: wound care, analgesia
Natural History
Birth: Not visible 2-4 weeks: Appears 0-6 months: Growth 6-12 months: Stable 1-4 years: Involution Age 4: 80% resolved

Capillary Malformation

Differentiate from Hemangioma
Key Distinctions from Hemangioma
  • Present at birth (vs. hemangioma appears after)
  • Flat, red/pink patch (port-wine stain)
  • Does NOT grow rapidly then involute
  • May thicken and darken with age
  • Can be associated with syndromes (Sturge-Weber, Klippel-Trenaunay)
Management
  • Small, asymptomatic: Reassurance
  • Large lesions: Refer for assessment
  • Periorbital/forehead: Rule out Sturge-Weber (seizures, developmental delay)
  • Pulsed dye laser can lighten appearance
  • Multidisciplinary assessment for extensive lesions
Syndrome Association
V1 distribution: Sturge-Weber Limb involvement: K-T syndrome Midline: Spinal dysraphism

Milia

No Treatment Needed
Key Message

Completely normal finding in newborns. No treatment required. Self-resolves within weeks.

Clinical Features
  • 1-2mm pearly white/yellow papules
  • Most commonly on face (nose, cheeks, forehead)
  • Epidermal inclusion cysts
  • Very common in newborns (40-50%)
  • Often called "milk spots"
  • Parents may be concerned - reassure!

Pityriasis Rosea

Self-Limiting
Clinical Presentation
  • Herald patch: Large annular scaly patch (2-10cm) - appears first
  • Followed 1-2 weeks later by widespread eruption
  • "Christmas tree" distribution along skin cleavage lines
  • Usually mild viral prodrome (fever, malaise) before rash
  • Mostly affects trunk and proximal limbs
  • Usually not very itchy
Management
  • Self-limiting: Resolves in 6-8 weeks
  • Emollients for comfort
  • Topical steroids for pruritus
  • Antihistamines if itchy
  • Reassure about benign nature
Differential: Guttate Psoriasis
Post-strep infection Droplet-shaped papules More scales Often on trunk/limbs

Pityriasis Alba

Common in Skin of Colour
Clinical Features
  • Hypopigmented (lighter) patches on face
  • Common in children with atopic background
  • Slightly scaly surface
  • More noticeable in darker skin types
  • Usually asymptomatic or mild itch
  • Affects children more than parents worry!
Management
  • Reassurance: Pigmentation returns over months
  • Emollients for skin barrier support
  • Low-potency topical steroids if inflamed
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) for face
  • Sun protection to prevent tanning contrast
Differentiate from Vitiligo
Hypopigmented vs depigmented Has some pigment (vitiligo = white) Often scaly Blends gradually
Woods Lamp
Vitiligo: Bright white Pityriasis: No fluorescence

Cradle Cap

Infantile Seborrheic Dermatitis
Clinical Features
  • Greasy yellow scales on scalp
  • Usually not itchy
  • Most common in infants (10% of babies)
  • Affects parents more than child!
  • Self-limiting condition
  • May extend to face, neck, nappy area
Treatment Options
  1. Conservative: Gentle brushing, baby shampoo
  2. If persistent: Emollient applied to scale, leave overnight
  3. Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (off-label under 12)
  4. Topical steroid lotion for inflammation
  5. Selenium sulfide shampoo for older children
  6. Most resolve by 6-12 months
Red Flags - Consider Referral
Not responding to treatment Secondary infection Extensive body involvement Severe inflammation
Cocois Ointment
Messy but effective Leave overnight Shampoo next morning

Alopecia Areata

Refer for Treatment
Clinical Features
  • Non-scarring alopecia - hair regrows!
  • Sudden onset patchy hair loss
  • Exclamation mark hairs at margins
  • Yellow dots (emptied follicles) on dermoscopy
  • Can affect any hair-bearing area
  • Autoimmune condition
Important: Rule Out Tinea Capitis!
  • Tinea capitis: Scaly, broken hairs, may be inflamed
  • Alopecia areata: Smooth, complete hair loss
  • Consider fungal scrape/wood lamp if any doubt
  • Refer to dermatology if unclear
Management
  1. Start topical potent steroid (dermovate) on scalp
  2. Continue for 4 weeks, then twice weekly maintenance
  3. Refer to dermatology for further treatment
  4. Intralesional steroid injections for adults
  5. JAK inhibitors (new treatment for >12 years)
  6. Psychological support if extensive
Patterns
Patchy AA (most common) Alopecia totalis (all scalp) Alopecia universalis (all body) Ophiasis (occipital pattern)
Ophiasis Pattern
Harder to treat Occipital involvement Poorer prognosis

Tinea Capitis

Oral Treatment REQUIRED
CRITICAL: Topical Treatments Do NOT Work!

Oral systemic therapy is essential. Topical antifungals alone will not cure scalp ringworm.

Clinical Features
  • Patchy hair loss with scaling
  • Black dots (broken hairs at scalp)
  • May be inflamed (erythematous, pustular)
  • Usually not itchy
  • Most common in young children
  • Contagious - family/household contacts need treatment
Treatment Dose Duration Notes
Griseofulvin 20-25mg/kg/day 6-12 weeks First-line in children, licensed
Terbinafine 62.5mg (<20kg)
125mg (20-40kg)		 4-6 weeks Off-license, but more effective, fewer side effects
Adjuncts Ketoconazole shampoo 2x/week Reduces transmission to contacts
Important Points
Treat WHOLE FAMILY Ketoconazole shampoo for contacts Can cause scarring if untreated 6-12 weeks treatment needed Off-license terbinafine commonly used

Kerion

Complication of Tinea Capitis
URGENT MANAGEMENT
  • Boggy, fluctuant, inflamed mass on scalp
  • Often mistaken for bacterial abscess
  • DO NOT INCISE AND DRAIN - this worsens scarring
  • DO NOT REFER FOR SURGERY
  • Requires prolonged oral antifungal therapy (8-12 weeks)
  • May leave permanent scarring alopecia
Clinical Features
  • Complication of untreated tinea capitis
  • Severe inflammatory response to fungal infection
  • Pustules, crusting, purulent discharge
  • Hair loss in affected area
  • Regional lymphadenopathy may be present
  • Fever and malaise in severe cases
Management Plan
  1. Confirm diagnosis with fungal culture
  2. Start oral terbinafine (preferred) or griseofulvin
  3. Treatment duration: 8-12 weeks minimum
  4. Consider short course of oral steroids if severe inflammation
  5. Refer to dermatology for ongoing management
  6. Warn about permanent scarring risk
