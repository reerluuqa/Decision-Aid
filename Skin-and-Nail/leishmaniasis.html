Immediate Action for Suspected VL:

  • Assess for massive splenomegaly & prolonged fever.
  • Order FBC immediately (look for pancytopenia).
  • Check HIV status (co-infection risk).

Clinical Classification

More common. Skin lesions at bite site (face, arms, legs).

  • Localized CL: Single/multiple ulcers (volcano-like).
  • Diffuse CL: Multiple non-ulcerative nodules.
  • Mucosal (ML): Nasal/oropharyngeal destruction (espundia).
  • Leishmaniasis Recidivans: Lesions surrounding healed scar.
Old World New World

Serious/Disseminated. "Kala-azar" (Black fever).

  • Systemic: Infiltration of spleen, liver, bone marrow.
  • Post-Kala-azar: Macular/nodular rash months/years after VL tx.

Risk: Cell-mediated immunosuppression (HIV, malnutrition).

Clinical Presentation & History

Cutaneous Signs

  • Painless open sores/ulcers.
  • Indurated rims (crusty in Old World).
  • 'Chiclero's ulcer' (ear pinna).
  • Sporotrichoid nodules.

Visceral Signs (Red Flags)

  • Prolonged fever (weeks/months).
  • Weight loss & wasting.
  • Massive splenomegaly.
  • Skin darkening (South Asia).
Travel History Check: Endemic areas include Americas, East Africa, North Africa, West/South-East Asia, Southern Europe.

Key Risk Factors

Immunosuppression

HIV (CD4 < 200), organ transplant, TNF-alpha antagonists.

Environmental Exposure

Rural endemic areas, poverty, poor housing, evening/night exposure.

Vector/Animal Contact

Sand fly bites. Proximity to dogs (reservoirs) or infected humans.

Aetiology & Management Overview

Pathogen: Obligate intracellular protozoa (genus Leishmania). Over 20 species (e.g., L. tropica, L. donovani, L. braziliensis).

Vector: Infected female phlebotomine sand fly.

Treatment Strategy:

  • Depends on clinical presentation (CL vs VL).
  • Depends on specific parasite species & strain.
  • Depends on geographic acquisition (Old World vs New World).
  • Local therapy vs Systemic therapy.
