Candida – Skin · One‑Page Algorithm

CKS · Apr 2025

1) Initial Assessment

Age ≥ 1 month Setting Primary care

Typical features

  • Localized soreness & itch; thin‑walled pustules on red base.
  • Curd‑like white‑yellow scale; moist red flexures with satellite pustules.
  • Toe/finger webs: maceration, thick crust.

Differentiate from

  • Dermatophyte tinea (drier, scaly, sharp border, no satellites)
  • Bacterial: impetigo / cellulitis
  • Erythrasma
  • Flexural psoriasis (glazed, sharply demarcated)
  • Atopic eczema

Risk factors

  • Immunocompromise (HIV, chemo, steroids); extremes of age
  • Antibiotics or inhaled/oral corticosteroids
  • Obesity / macerated skin folds; poor hygiene
  • Diabetes, Cushing’s, iron deficiency; high‑oestrogen states
  • Barrier disorders: psoriasis, seborrhoeic eczema

Tests?

  • Usually clinical diagnosis.
  • Swab M&C if secondary bacterial infection, immunocompromised, or diagnosis uncertain.
  • Consider screening for underlying cause in recurrent/widespread cases (e.g., diabetes, anaemia).
Safety first

Red Flags → Admit / Urgent referral

  • Suspected systemic candidiasis (e.g., sepsis, peritonitis, meningitis).
  • Severe immunocompromise + systemic features.

Refer (routine)

  • Widespread or recurrent infection with no identified cause in primary care.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Failure to respond to appropriately escalated therapy.

2) First‑Line Management (Primary Care)

Use topical therapy if infection is not widespread and the person is not significantly immunocompromised.

Adults

  • Topical antifungal: imidazole (clotrimazole, econazole, miconazole, ketoconazole) or terbinafine.
  • Application: typically 2–3×/day; continue ≥2 weeks (miconazole: continue 10 days after healing).

Children

  • Topicals: clotrimazole, econazole, or miconazole.

Inflammation / Itch

  • Add hydrocortisone 1% cream once/twice daily × 7 days.
  • Review day 7:
    • Resolved → stop steroid.
    • Improved → continue steroid another 7 days.
    • No response → stop steroid; reassess diagnosis ± seek advice.

3) Escalation

If topical therapy fails, infection is widespread, or person is significantly immunocompromised:

≥ 16 years

  • Fluconazole 50 mg PO OD × 2 weeks, then review.
  • If resolved → stop. If persistent → extend by 2 weeks and consider swab, specialist advice, or dermatology referral (low threshold if severe).

< 16 years / contraindicated

  • Seek specialist advice.

4) Supportive & Preventive Advice

  • Avoid occlusion: tight clothing, non‑breathable fabrics.
  • Change dressings, incontinence pads, or nappies before saturation.
  • Wash with a soap substitute; dry skin folds thoroughly.
  • Address obesity (weight management support).

5) Prescribing Notes & Interactions

Topical antifungals
  • Clotrimazole 1%: apply 2–3×/day; continue ≥2 weeks. Can irritate. Damages latex contraceptives.
  • Miconazole 2%: apply 2×/day; continue 10 days after healing. Damages latex and enhances warfarin—monitor INR.
  • Econazole 1%: 2×/day until healed. Damages latex; rare anticoagulant interaction—monitor if on oral anticoagulants.
  • Ketoconazole 2% (adults): 1–2×/day; a few days beyond healing. Local irritation possible.
  • Terbinafine 1% (adults): thin layer 1–2×/day for 14 days; distinguish irritation vs hypersensitivity.
Oral fluconazole – key safety
  • Contraindicated: pregnancy (advise ≈1‑week washout before conceiving after dosing), acute porphyria, and with certain drugs (e.g., ergotamine, erythromycin, pimozide, quetiapine, reboxetine).
  • Caution: QT‑risk, hepatic or renal impairment (halve maintenance doses if eGFR < 50), concurrent hepatotoxins.
  • Common AEs: GI upset, headache, rash.
  • Interactions: CYP2C9/2C19/3A4 inhibition—long tail 4–5 days after stop. Warfarin (↑INR), statins (↑myopathy risk), benzodiazepines/opioids (↑sedation), phenytoin, ciclosporin, tacrolimus/sirolimus, sulfonylureas (warn hypoglycaemia), rifabutin (uveitis), zidovudine, tretinoin, etc.

Snapshot Summary

  • Localized → topical antifungal ± short hydrocortisone.
  • Widespread / refractory / immunocompromised → fluconazole (≥16y) or specialist advice.
  • Admit if systemic features.
  • Give preventive advice every time.
