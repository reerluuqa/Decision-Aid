Perioral (Periocular) Dermatitis

Clinical Reference Guide • Syn: Periorificial Dermatitis
Updated: Dec 17, 2023

STOP Topical Steroids Immediately

Action: Discontinue all facial topical steroids, moisturizers, and cosmetics.

Warning: Rebound Flare
Symptoms will likely worsen for 3–7 days after stopping steroid creams ("rebound flare") before improving. Do not restart steroids. Wean potent steroids over 1–2 weeks to minimize flare if necessary.

Clinical Snapshot

Common inflammatory rash. Not eczema despite the name. Predominantly affects women aged 15–45 years.

  • Distribution: Perioral (around mouth), Periocular (eyes), Perinasal.
  • Key Sign: Sparing of the vermilion border (lip edge) immediate skin.
  • Morphology: Monomorphic small red papules and pustules (vesicopustules).
  • Skin Tone: Pink-red erythematous base. Dry/flaky surface common.
  • History: Recent use of topical steroids (accidental or direct).

Common Triggers & Causes

Category Examples
Steroids Topical creams, nasal sprays, inhalers (accidental transfer).
Cosmetics Heavy moisturizers, petrolatum-based products, sunscreens.
Physical UV light, heat, wind.
Other Fluoridated toothpaste, hormonal changes.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Rosacea: flushing, telangiectasia, rhinophyma. Older patients.
  • Acne Vulgaris: comedones, nodules, cysts present.
  • Seborrheic Dermatitis: affects scalp/hairline, presternal area.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Pruritus (itch) is usually more intense.
  • Impetigo: Golden crusts, honey-colored.

Management Plan

Zero Therapy (First Line) Stop all facial products (creams, makeup, sunscreen). Wash face with warm water only. Use bland, non-occlusive emollient if dry.
Discontinue Steroids If stopping abruptly causes severe flare, taper potency or substitute with topical pimecrolimus 1% (non-steroid).
Hygiene Measures Wash hands thoroughly after applying steroid creams to other body parts to prevent accidental transfer to face.

Pharmacological Treatment

If symptoms persist > 4 weeks:

Mild Cases Topical Antibiotics

Metronidazole 0.75–1% or Erythromycin 1%. (Rotate if ineffective).

Moderate/Severe Systemic Antibiotics (6-8 weeks)

1st Line: Tetracycline (e.g., Lymecycline 408mg OD or Doxycycline 100mg OD).
Pregnancy/Children <8: Erythromycin or Azithromycin.

Refractory

Low-dose Isotretinoin.

When to Refer

Refer to Dermatologist or GPwER if:

  • Diagnosis is unclear.
  • Suspected Lupoid variant (severe red-brown papules, scarring).
  • Failure to respond to standard therapy.
