Lichen Simplex Chronicus

Last Updated: Mar 27, 2024 • BMJ Best Practice Source
Neurodermatitis Itch-Scratch Cycle
Red Flags & Critical Actions
Biopsy Required If:
  • History/Physical is atypical.
  • Lesions do not respond to appropriate therapy.
  • Suspect Squamous Cell Carcinoma (malignancy risk).
  • Rule out Hypertrophic Lichen Planus or Mycosis Fungoides.
Rule Out Systemic Causes (Secondary LSC):
  • Renal Failure (Check Creatinine/Urea).
  • Obstructive Biliary Disease (Check LFTs, Gamma-GT).
  • Hodgkin's Lymphoma (Check FBC, lymph nodes).
  • Thyroid Disorders (Check TSH, Free T4).
Clinical Snapshot
  • Severe, Intractable Pruritus: Worse at night, exacerbated by heat/sweat.
  • Paroxysms: Sudden itch attacks → intense scratching → satisfaction → refractory period.
  • Psychiatric Link: Onset often during stress, anxiety, or depression.
  • Atopic Diathesis: Personal/Family history of eczema, allergic rhinitis, or asthma.
  • Lichenification: Exaggerated skin markings (criss-cross mosaic pattern).
  • Plaques: Erythematous (acute) to Violaceous (chronic). Well-circumscribed.
  • Common Locations: Neck, Ankle, Scalp, Vulva/Scrotum, Extensor Forearms.
  • Signs of Damage: Excoriations, linear fissures (folds), post-inflammatory hyper/hypopigmentation.
Pathophysiology

A chronic itch-scratch cycle caused by mechanical disruption of the epidermal barrier. Type C non-myelinated sensory nerves are stimulated, conveying itch/pain to the CNS.

Classifications:
Primary De novo on normal skin. Triggers are psychological/environmental.
Secondary On underlying dermatosis (e.g., Atopic Dermatitis) or systemic pruritus.
Management Algorithm

First Line (Core Therapy)

1
Potent Topical Corticosteroids (e.g., Fluocinonide, Clobetasol).
Regimen: Max 2 weeks continuous on lesion. Avoid face/intertriginous areas.
2
Emollients
Frequent application of bland, unperfumed creams/ointments (best after showering).
3
Barrier Protection
Clothing modification (Silk for genital), Unna boot (lower leg), or Occlusion.

Symptom Control & Adjuncts

A
Nocturnal Pruritus
Sedating antihistamines (Hydroxyzine, Doxepin) 2hrs before bed. Increases deep sleep ratio.
B
Psychological Intervention
SSRIs (Fluoxetine) or Clomipramine if anxiety/depression present. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT).
C
Refractory Cases
Intra-lesional Triamcinolone (weekly for 6-8 wks). Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (steroid-sparing).
Key Differentials
Condition Key Differentiators from LSC Diagnostic Test
Hypertrophic Lichen Planus Localized to shins, often surrounded by smaller polygonal lesions. Wickham striae present. Skin Biopsy (Band-like lymphocytic infiltrate).
Psoriasis Symmetrical, silvery scales, no lichenification. Less intense itch. Skin Biopsy (Regular psoriasiform hyperplasia, confluent parakeratosis).
Scabies Diffuse eruption, burrows present in genitalia. Skin Scrapings (Mites, eggs).
Squamous Cell Carcinoma Sun-exposed, hyperkeratotic, painful, not pruritic. Skin Biopsy (Keratinocyte atypia, dermal invasion).
Mycosis Fungoides Bathing-suit distribution, atrophic appearance. Biopsy + PCR (T-cell gene rearrangement).
Lab Workup (If Indicated)
  • Patch Test: If allergic contact dermatitis suspected.
  • KOH Prep / Fungal Culture: Rule out Tinea/Candidiasis.
  • Serum TSH / Free T4: Thyroid dysfunction.
  • Creatinine / Urea: Renal failure.
  • LFTs / Gamma-GT: Obstructive biliary disease.
  • FBC: Polycythaemia rubra vera.
Prevention & Follow-up

LSC is difficult to manage and often recurring.

  • Identify & remove environmental irritants (heat, sweat, harsh soaps).
  • Treat underlying dermatoses immediately (e.g., Atopic Dermatitis flares).
  • Monitor for steroid atrophy (if long-term use).
  • Monitor for secondary infections.
Special Populations
Children < 12yrs Use Low-to-Mid potency steroids (e.g., Hydrocortisone, Triamcinolone). Avoid high potency.
Genital LSC Silk underwear less irritating. Avoid occlusion if infection risk high.
Elderly Lower doses of sedating antihistamines to avoid confusion/CNS depression.
