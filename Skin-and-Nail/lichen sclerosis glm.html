Lichen Sclerosus

Clinical Reference Guide
Last updated: June 2024

What is Lichen Sclerosus?

A chronic inflammatory dermatosis affecting anogenital region in women and glans/foreskin in men.

  • No systemic disease outside skin
  • Higher incidence in females (3%) vs males (0.07%)
  • Two peaks in women: prepubertal & postmenopausal
  • In men: young boys and adult men

Aetiology & Risk Factors

  • Autoimmune in genetically predisposed
  • Associated with thyroid disease (women)
  • 10-12% positive family history
  • Men: increased BMI, CAD, diabetes
  • Preceding infections may provoke
  • Trauma & moist environment precipitate

Extra-genital Cases

Female Presentation

Key Symptom: Severe itching (worse at night)
  • Itch - can disturb sleep
  • Pain with fissures/erosions → dyspareunia
  • Perianal lesions → constipation
  • May be asymptomatic

Signs:

  • White patches (figure-8 pattern)
  • Scarring → labial shrinking
  • Vagina & cervix always spared
  • In girls: may mimic sexual abuse

Male Presentation

  • Usually on prepuce, glans, coronal sulcus
  • Soreness, hemorrhagic blisters
  • Itching NOT common
  • Dyspareunia, painful erections
  • Poor urinary stream if meatal scarring

Signs:

  • White patches on glans/prepuce
  • Hemorrhagic vesicles or purpura
  • Phimosis, prepuce wasting
  • Perianal involvement rare

Diagnostic Investigations

Biopsy

  • • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • • Suspected malignancy
  • • Atypical presentation
  • • Treatment failure

Swabs

  • • Not routine
  • • Erosive disease
  • • Exclude infection
  • • Candidiasis/HSV

Blood Tests

  • • Autoimmune screen
  • • Thyroid function
  • • Only if indicated
  • • Clinical features present

Female Anogenital LS Treatment

First-Line: Clobetasol Propionate 0.05%

Proven efficacy - more effective than pimecrolimus
  • Month 1: Once daily (at night)
  • Month 2: Alternate nights
  • Month 3: Twice weekly
  • Review: At 3 months

Use half fingertip unit. 30g tube should last 12 weeks.

Maintenance Treatment

  • As-needed use of very potent steroids
  • Ointment bases less allergenic
  • Patient preference determines base choice

Treatment Resistance

  • Intralesional triamcinolone (specialist setting)
  • Biopsy required first
  • Consider compliance issues

Male Anogenital LS Treatment

Medical Treatment

  • Clobetasol propionate 0.05% daily
  • Until remission, then gradually reduce
  • Repeat courses if relapse
  • Intermittent use to maintain remission

Surgical Options

Circumcision indicated if:
  • Phimosis present
  • No response to steroids after 1-3 months

Contraindicated Treatments

DO NOT USE:
  • Estrogen or testosterone creams (no better than petroleum jelly)
  • Testosterone in children (adverse effects)
  • Tacrolimus/pimecrolimus as first-line (malignancy concerns)

Scarring Complications

  • Common - causes urinary symptoms
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • May require surgery
  • Men: 20% urethral involvement
  • Women: Labial fusing
  • Constipation from perianal fissures
Surgical Options: Circumcision, meatal dilatation, vulval surgery, oral mucosa grafts for urethral strictures

Other Complications

Dysaesthesia

  • • Vulvodynia/penile dysaesthesia
  • • Neuropathic pain
  • • No response to steroids
  • • Treat with topical lidocaine 5%

Clitoral Pseudocyst

  • • Debris under adhesions
  • • Pain or recurrent infection
  • • Refer to gynecologist

Sexual Dysfunction

  • • Significant QoL impact
  • • Offer referral
  • • Address psychological aspects

Clinical Decision Algorithm

Initial Assessment

Red Flags - Immediate Action

Refer urgently if:
  • Suspicious lesions (possible SCC)
  • Severe symptoms unresponsive to treatment
  • Children with atypical presentation

Standard Management

Can manage in primary care:
  • Typical presentation
  • Mild to moderate symptoms
  • Responding to treatment

Step 1: Diagnosis

  • Clinical diagnosis usually sufficient
  • Document with photographs
  • Biopsy if atypical or suspected malignancy

Step 2: First-Line Treatment

  • Clobetasol propionate 0.05%
  • Women: 1 month daily → 1 month alternate → 1 month twice weekly
  • Men: Daily until remission, then taper

Step 3: Review at 3 Months

  • If responding → continue maintenance
  • If not responding → check compliance
  • Consider biopsy if poor response

Step 4: Specialist Referral

  • Treatment failure despite compliance
  • Atypical or pigmented lesions
  • Men with persistent phimosis
  • Suspicious of malignancy
Follow-up Schedule:
  • Women: Annual review (primary care if stable)
  • Men: Urology referral if urinary symptoms
  • All: Immediate review for new lumps, skin changes, or non-healing ulcers

Quick Reference Tips

For Clinicians

  • 30g clobetasol = 12 weeks supply
  • Use half fingertip unit per application
  • Ointment base less allergenic
  • Document with photos

Patient Education

  • Wash with bland emollients
  • Avoid irritants & tight clothing
  • Use lubricants if needed
  • Report any changes immediately
