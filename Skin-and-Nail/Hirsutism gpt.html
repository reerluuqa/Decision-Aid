Hirsutism – Primary Care Algorithm
Jump to Algorithm

At‑a‑Glance

Based on NICE CKS (October 2024): primary care management of hirsutism in women ≥18 years.

Scope
Diagnosis, initial investigations, and first‑line management in primary care.
Audience
Busy clinicians seeking a rapid decision aid at the point of care.
Key principle
Triage red flags early; treat by severity & quality‑of‑life impact; consider referral thresholds.

Initial Assessment

1
History: onset & progression; menstrual/ovulatory history; fertility; drugs (danazol, valproate, anabolic steroids, etc.); systemic clues (PCOS, Cushing’s, thyroid, prolactin, acromegaly); family history.
2
Examination: distribution of terminal hair (vs vellus); Ferriman‑Gallwey scoring (≥8 typical threshold; adjust for ethnicity); signs of virilization (voice deepening, clitoromegaly, ↑muscle bulk); BMI/central adiposity; QoL impact.
Tip: Treatment decisions depend on patient‑reported distress as much as the numerical score.

Red Flags – Urgent Endocrine Referral (<2 weeks)

  • Sudden or rapid progression of hair growth.
  • Virilization: deep voice, ↑muscle bulk, clitoromegaly.
  • Pelvic/abdominal mass.
  • Total testosterone > 6–7 nmol/L (or strong suspicion of androgen‑secreting tumour).

Investigations (when indicated)

  • Total testosterone (reliable specialty assay) in abnormal score or local sexual hair with clinical endocrine signs.
  • If moderate/severe or discordant features → add total & free testosterone (AM sample).
  • 17‑hydroxyprogesterone (AM, follicular phase) to screen for non‑classical CAH in hyperandrogenaemia/high‑risk groups (e.g., Ashkenazi Jewish, Hispanic, Eastern European) or positive FHx.
  • Consider TSH, prolactin, and tests for Cushing’s/acromegaly based on clinical suspicion.
  • No tests if mild, localized, stable growth with normal score and no endocrine features.

Differential Diagnosis

Hirsutism: androgen‑dependent distribution (face, chest, abdomen, lower back, upper arms, thighs).
Hypertrichosis: generalized, non‑sexual pattern; congenital, systemic (hypothyroid, porphyria, etc.) or drug‑induced (minoxidil, ciclosporin, glucocorticoids, phenytoin).

Management – Premenopausal

1
Foundational: lifestyle/weight loss if overweight or obese; discuss ongoing cosmetic hair reduction (shaving, waxing, depilatories, bleaching; or electrolysis/laser for longer‑term reduction).
2
Mild facial: topical eflornithine 11.5% cream — results in 6–8 weeks; stop if no benefit by 4 months; benefits reverse ≈8 weeks after stopping.
3
Wider/severe: offer a COC containing ethinylestradiol (prefer lowest effective EE dose (≈20 µg) + low‑VTE‑risk progestogen in higher VTE risk patients). Discuss VTE risks and standard COC contraindications.
Note: Use of COCs for hirsutism is off‑label except Dianette® (cyproterone/EE) which is licensed for moderate–severe hirsutism. Avoid any cyproterone‑containing medication if there is a history of meningioma.
4
Refer to specialist if COC is contraindicated or ineffective after ≥6 months.

Management – Postmenopausal

  • Cosmetic methods remain central; encourage weight optimisation where appropriate.
  • Eflornithine for facial hair (as above).
  • Otherwise refer for specialist evaluation/treatment.

When to Refer (Non‑Urgent)

Biochemical/Investigational

  • Total testosterone 4–6 nmol/L (or higher if persistent on repeat).
  • Elevated 17‑hydroxyprogesterone or abnormal endocrine testing.

Clinical

  • Suspected endocrine disorder needing secondary care diagnosis/management.
  • Failure of primary care therapy (e.g., COC after ≥6 months, or intolerance/contraindication).

Prescribing Notes – Eflornithine 11.5% (Vaniqa®)

How to Use

  • Apply thin layer BID (≥8 h apart) to face/under chin only; rub in thoroughly.
  • Cosmetics after 5 min; avoid washing the area for 4 h post‑application.
  • May combine with shaving/waxing (apply ≥5 min after hair removal).

Safety

  • Do not use in pregnancy, breastfeeding, or <19 years.
  • Common AEs: mild acne, irritation, erythema; reduce to daily if irritated; stop if persistent.
  • No known drug interactions.
