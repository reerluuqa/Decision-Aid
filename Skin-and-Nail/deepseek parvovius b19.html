Parvovirus B19 Infection

Clinical Decision Support for Primary Care

Last revised: February 2022

Quick Clinical Actions

Pregnant Patient

Urgent referral to fetal medicine specialist if confirmed at any stage

Red Flags

Severe anaemia, immunocompromised, fetal complications

Diagnostic Pathway

Clinical diagnosis vs. lab testing indications

Management Scenarios

Children, adults, pregnant women, contacts

Red Flags & Immediate Actions

Urgent referral to fetal medicine specialist if parvovirus B19 infection is confirmed at ANY stage of pregnancy

High-Risk Groups Needing Urgent Assessment:

Pregnant Women
Immunocompromised
Haematological Disorders
  • Pregnancy complications: Non-immune hydrops fetalis (3-11% if infection at 9-20 weeks), fetal death (5-10% overall)
  • Severe anaemia symptoms: Breathlessness, lightheadedness, lethargy, confusion in at-risk individuals
  • Transient aplastic crisis: In people with sickle cell disease, thalassaemia, or other haemolytic disorders
  • Neurological complications: Encephalitis, meningitis, stroke, peripheral neuropathy

Diagnosis & Investigations

Clinical Diagnosis (Healthy Individuals)

Children: Characteristic "slapped cheek" facial rash + biphasic illness

Adults: Symmetrical polyarthropathy + contact history

No lab testing needed
Key Clinical Features

Incubation: 14-21 days

Prodrome (1 week): Low-grade fever, headache, coryza, pharyngitis

Rash: Facial erythema ("slapped cheek") → lacy reticular rash on trunk/limbs (1-3 weeks)

Infectious period: Up to 10 days BEFORE rash appears

When to Test

  • Pregnant women (suspect or exposure)
  • Immunocompromised individuals
  • Haematological disorders
  • Suspected transient aplastic crisis
Contact local virology/microbiology immediately for pregnant women with suspected infection or exposure

Management Scenarios

Children/Adults
Pregnant Women
Exposure Management
Self-limiting illness in healthy children/adults. Symptomatic treatment only.

Key Management Points:

  • School/Nursery:
    No need to stay off once rash appears (no longer infectious). Inform school for contact tracing.
  • Symptom Relief:
    Paracetamol/NSAIDs for fever/arthralgia as needed.
  • Work:
    No need to stay off if symptoms controlled.
  • Referral:
    If severe/persistent arthropathy (consider rheumatoid arthritis).
URGENT: Contact local virology immediately for all pregnant women with suspected infection or exposure

Management Pathway:

  1. Immediate specialist contact for lab testing advice
  2. Blood tests: Parvovirus B19 IgM/IgG + rubella serology
  3. If IgM positive → urgent referral to fetal medicine
  4. Serial fetal ultrasound from 4 weeks post-infection
  5. Fetal blood sampling/transfusion if hydrops develops
Fetus most vulnerable at 17-24 weeks gestation
Overall fetal loss rate: 5-10%

Significant Contact Definition:

Same room for ≥15 minutes OR face-to-face contact

General Population

  • Avoid contact with at-risk individuals until no longer infectious
  • Household exposure higher risk than occupational

Pregnant Women

  • Immediate serological testing
  • Avoid other pregnant women until status known
  • Repeat test at 1 month if initially negative

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Rubella Pink macules starting head/neck, petechiae on palate
Measles Koplik's spots, severe prodrome (cough, coryza, conjunctivitis)
Scarlet Fever Sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue, pharyngitis
Roseola Infantum High fever then rash, children <3 years
Enterovirus/Adenovirus Common cause of rubella-like rash
Other Considerations
  • Tropical viruses (Dengue, etc.) if recent travel
  • Drug reactions (especially anti-epileptics)
  • CMV, HIV, glandular fever, syphilis with lymphadenopathy

Epidemiology & Background

Prevalence

  • Children (15 yrs):
    ~50% seropositive
  • Adults:
    50-60% seropositive
  • Childbearing age:
    60-70% seropositive
  • Pregnancy infection:
    1-3% susceptible women (↑ to >10% in epidemics)

Key Facts

  • Transmission:
    Respiratory droplets, transplacental, blood products
  • Seasonality:
    Late winter/early spring outbreaks every 3-4 years
  • Peak age:
    6-10 years
  • Asymptomatic rate:
    25-50% of infections
