Overview

Definition ‚ñº
  • Chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting sebaceous gland-rich areas
  • Well-defined erythematous plaques with greasy yellowish scales, usually symmetrical
  • Dandruff = mild non-inflammatory form limited to scalp
  • Infantile form (cradle cap) peaks at first 3 months, resolves by 8-12 months
Key Associations ‚ñº
  • HIV infection (30-83% prevalence in immunocompromised)
  • Parkinson's disease and other neurological disorders
  • Down's syndrome
  • Triggers: stress, weather, certain medications

Clinical Features

Adults & Adolescents ‚ñº
  • Face (87.7%): nasolabial folds, glabellar, eyebrows, behind ears
  • Scalp (70.3%): dandruff to honey-coloured crusts, often itchy
  • Chest/back (26.8%): petaloid or pityriasiform lesions
  • Flexures: axillae, groin, breast folds, umbilicus
  • Skin of colour: may present with hypopigmented scaly patches
  • Pruritus common, can be severe
Infants ‚ñº
  • Scalp: thick white/yellow greasy scales (cradle cap)
  • Face: erythematous, flaky, salmon-coloured plaques
  • Body folds: moist, shiny, non-scaly lesions
  • Trunk: sharply limited erythematous plaques
  • Usually minimal itch, infant rarely scratches
  • In children of colour: erythema, flaking, hypopigmentation

Assessment

Key Questions ‚ñº
  • Onset, evolution, distribution of features
  • Aggravating/relieving factors
  • Associated features: failure to thrive, immunosuppression
  • Past medical history: HIV, Parkinson's disease
  • Medication review: chlorpromazine, cimetidine, gold, interferon-alpha, methyldopa
Examination ‚ñº
  • Look for typical erythematous scaly patches in sebaceous areas
  • Assess severity: mild flaking to widespread crusting
  • Consider investigations if underlying cause or alternative diagnosis suspected

Differential Diagnosis

Key Differentials ‚ñº
  • Psoriasis: thicker plaques with silvery scales, nail pitting
  • Atopic dermatitis: family atopy history, different distribution
  • Tinea capitis: scalp scaling, may have alopecia
  • Pityriasis versicolor: trunk/upper arms, varied pigmentation
  • Rosacea: papules/pustules on central face
  • Contact dermatitis: sharply demarcated at contact site
  • Secondary syphilis: palms/soles, systemic symptoms

Management

üí° Adult SD is chronic - long-term maintenance often needed. Infant SD is self-limiting.

Scenario: Infants (0-12 months)

Parent Advice ‚ñº
  • Harmless, non-contagious, self-limited (resolves in few months)
  • Massage emollient into scalp to loosen scales, then comb before washing
  • Daily bathing with emollient as soap substitute
  • Frequent nappy changes + barrier emollients (zinc/castor oil or white soft paraffin)
Treatment Options ‚ñº
  • Topical imidazole: clotrimazole 1% 2-3√ó/day OR miconazole 2% twice daily for up to 4 weeks
  • Topical steroid: 1% hydrocortisone once/twice daily for up to 2 weeks if significant inflammation
  • Consider steroid only if no response to imidazole or severe inflammation

Scenario: Scalp & Beard (‚â•12 months)

First Line Treatment ‚ñº
  • Ketoconazole 2% shampoo: twice weekly for 4 weeks, then every 1-2 weeks for maintenance
  • Leave on for 5 minutes before rinsing
  • Alternative shampoos: zinc pyrithione, coal tar, salicylic acid (OTC)
  • Remove scales with keratolytic (salicylic acid) before shampooing if needed
For Severe Inflammation ‚ñº
  • Scalp: Betamethasone valerate 0.1% or mometasone furoate 0.1% scalp application once/twice daily
  • Beard: Use mild steroid (hydrocortisone 1%) due to skin thinning risk
  • Short course only - not for continuous long-term use

Scenario: Face & Body (‚â•12 months)

First Line Treatment ‚ñº
  • Ketoconazole 2% cream: once or twice daily for up to 4 weeks
  • Alternative imidazoles: clotrimazole 1%, miconazole 2%
  • Maintenance: use ketoconazole cream twice weekly to once every other week
  • Can use ketoconazole shampoo as body wash
For Flares ‚ñº
  • Mild topical corticosteroid: hydrocortisone 1% for up to 2 weeks
  • Consider potency based on site (see Quick Actions)
  • Short-term use only to settle inflammation
General Measures ‚ñº
  • Gentle soap-free wash, light moisturiser after washing
  • Non-comedogenic makeup products
  • Fruit consumption may reduce flares
  • Stress management (stress exacerbates condition)
  • Eyelid hygiene if involved (see Blepharitis CKS)

Prescribing Information

Topical Imidazoles ‚ñº
  • Ketoconazole: Skin reactions, folliculitis. Use with caution in pregnancy
  • Clotrimazole: Low systemic absorption, not known harmful in pregnancy
  • Miconazole: Caution with warfarin (theoretical interaction). Caution in pregnancy
