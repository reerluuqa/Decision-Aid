Comprehensive History

  • Duration & Evolution: When did lesion appear? How has it changed?
  • Associated Symptoms: Itch, pain, bleeding, rapid growth
  • Previous Similar Lesions: History of seborrheic keratosis, skin cancers
  • Previous Treatments: What has been attempted? Response?
  • Family History: Skin cancers, familial syndromes
  • Personal History: Previous skin cancers (very important)
  • Sun Exposure: Lifetime exposure, blistering sunburns, residence in sunny countries
  • Occupational Exposure: UV radiation, chemicals
  • Immunosuppression: Increased malignancy risk, requires closer monitoring

High-Risk Factors

Age >60 years: Higher malignancy risk

Immunosuppression: Increased surveillance needed

Previous skin cancer: Vigilant monitoring essential

Rapid change: Any evolving lesion requires urgent assessment

Site-Specific Concerns

  • Palms/Soles/Mucous Membranes: Atypical sites for benign lesions Refer
  • Private Areas: Dermatofibromas uncommon - consider referral Caution
  • Chest Wall: Deep-seated or tethered lesions need ultrasound Investigate
  • Scalp: Difficult to monitor - consider referral if atypical

Diagnosis

Key Principle

Differentiation from malignant conditions is critical. When in doubt, refer for dermatology assessment. Clinical judgment and dermoscopy (if available) aid diagnosis.

Seborrheic Keratosis

Appearance: "Stuck-on", waxy, "barnacles of aging"

Color: Black, brown, flesh-colored (variable pigmentation)

Size: Variable, can be large/unsightly

Site: Face, chest, upper back, shoulders, scalp

Features: Crumbly, fragile, keratin plugs/horns

Age: >60 years (90% prevalence)

Management: Observe, cryotherapy, shave excision Stable

Melanoma can arise within - refer if concerning features

Actinic Keratosis

Appearance: Sandpaper-like texture, pink-red, may be pigmented

Size: Usually <1cm

Site: Sun-exposed areas (face, scalp, ears, forearms, dorsal hands)

Symptoms: Often asymptomatic, may be itchy

Risk: 0.025-16% annual progression to SCC Treat

Key Point: Must treat - cannot just observe

Prevalence: 60% of SCC arises from AK

Cherry Angiomas

Appearance: Bright to dark red, round, dome-shaped papules

Size: 1-5mm

Site: Trunk, upper/lower extremities (scattered)

Age: >40 years (85% prevalence)

Behavior: Stable, slow enlargement, bleed if traumatized

Prognosis: No malignant transformation

Management: Observation, laser/electrocautery if troublesome Benign

Lipomas

Prevalence: 2% of population

Age: 40-50 years peak

Appearance: Soft, doughy consistency, mobile

Sign: "Slip sign" - pathognomonic

Size: Variable

Site: Trunk, proximal extremities

Red Flags: Tethered, firm, rapid growth → consider sarcoma Investigate

Note: Multiple lipomas (>5) → consider familial lipomatosis, Gardner syndrome

Dermatofibromas

Origin: Benign fibrous histiocytomas

Size: 3-10mm typically

Site: Lower legs predominantly

Appearance: Variegated pigmentation, dermal nodule

Sign: "Dimple sign" - pathognomonic Pinch test

Variant: DFSP (dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans) - rare malignancy

Management: Observe, excise if enlarging or diagnostic uncertainty Monitor

Skin Tags (Acrochordons)

Associations: Obesity, diabetes mellitus, pregnancy, friction

Appearance: Soft, pedunculated, skin-colored to hyperpigmented

Size: 1-5mm

Site: Neck, axilla, groin, eyelids

Note: >5 skin tags may suggest metabolic syndrome

HPV Association: Genital tags - consider referral to GUM

Management: Cryotherapy, electrocautery Elective

Milia

Origin: Epidermal inclusion cysts with keratin

Types: Primary (spontaneous, neonates) or secondary (post-injury)

Appearance: Small white dome-shaped papules

Site: Face predominantly, also genitalia, areola

Behavior: Asymptomatic, spontaneous resolution

Neonatal: "Epstein pearls" - common, harmless

Management: Observation Self-resolving

Solar Lentigines

Alternative Names: Age spots, sun spots (NOT liver spots)

Cause: Increased UV exposure

Gender: Equal distribution

Skin Types: More common in types 1-3 (>70%)

Color: Light to dark brown, pink

Size: 5mm-2cm

Site: Sun-exposed sites

Management: Sun protection mainstay Benign

Caution: Differentiate from melanoma/melanoma in situ

Keratoacanthoma - SPECIAL CONSIDERATION

Appearance: Rapidly enlarging, volcano-like, crater with keratinous material

Size: 1-2cm

Site: Sun-exposed areas

Growth: Very rapid proliferation

Controversy: Considered well-differentiated SCC variant by some pathologists

Clinical Reality: Cannot distinguish from SCC clinically

Management: Excision - do NOT observe Excise

Prognosis: Once excised and confirmed as KA, no further treatment needed

Diagnostic Uncertainty

When faced with atypical features:

  • Atypical sites (palms, soles, mucosa, private areas) Refer
  • Rapid evolution or change Urgent Referral
  • Loss of mobility/tethering Investigate
  • Multiple atypical lesions Refer
  • Patient anxiety despite reassurance Consider Referral

Treatment

Treatment Principles

Indications for active treatment: Symptomatic relief, cosmetic concerns, diagnostic uncertainty, malignant potential

Observation is appropriate for: Classic benign features, no symptoms, no concerning characteristics

Seborrheic Keratosis

Observation: Acceptable if typical features, no concerns

Cryotherapy: Most common intervention

Shave Excision: For histology or concerning features

Laser: Available in some centers (limited NHS)

Topical Therapies: Variable effectiveness

Actinic Keratosis

ALWAYS TREAT - Cannot observe due to SCC risk

Gold Standard: Cryotherapy

Surgical: Shave excision or curettage with cautery

Topical:
• 5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)
• Imiquimod cream (Aldara)
• Ingenol mebutate (Picato)

Photodynamic Therapy (PDT): Very effective but expensive, requires specialized unit

Cherry Angiomas

Observation: First-line (benign nature)

Laser Therapy: For cosmetic concerns

Electrocautery: For frequent bleeding or irritation

Shave Excision: For troublesome lesions

Sebaceous Cysts

Observation: If <2cm, asymptomatic, no cosmetic concern

Infected: Incision & drainage + antibiotics

Definitive: Complete surgical excision (remove entire sac)

Important: Do NOT excise during active infection (risk of recurrence)

Lipomas

Observation: Most common approach - small, asymptomatic, typical features

Surgical Excision: Indicated for:
• Large size causing symptoms
• Cosmetic concerns
• Diagnostic uncertainty
• Rapid growth or change
• Loss of mobility/tethering

Investigation: Ultrasound if atypical features

Referral: To relevant specialty based on location

Dermatofibromas

Observation: Acceptable for stable, typical lesions

Excision: Indicated for:
• Diagnostic dilemma
• Enlarging lesion
• Atypical features (consider DFSP)
• Patient preference

Techniques: Shave excision, full-thickness excision

Cryotherapy: Limited effectiveness

Skin Tags

Observation: Appropriate if asymptomatic

Cryotherapy: Simple, effective

Electrocautery: For multiple or large tags

Important: Eyelid tags → refer to ophthalmology

Patient Education: Benign, age-related, not contagious, may recur

Solar Lentigines

Conservative Management: Mainstay of treatment

Sun Protection: Critical - prevent further lesions

Patient Education: Benign but monitor for changes

Cosmetic Options: Laser, topical bleaching agents (private)

Keratoacanthoma

DO NOT OBSERVE - Clinically indistinguishable from SCC

Definitive Treatment: Excision

Approach: Treat as SCC until proven otherwise

After Excision: If pathology confirms KA, no further treatment

Rationale: Growth phase → maturation → resolution, but cannot risk waiting

Teledermatology & Documentation

  • Photographs: Essential for monitoring and referrals
  • Dermoscopy: If available - enhances diagnostic accuracy
  • Teledermatology: Increasingly common - high-quality images required
  • Cross-polarized & Dermoscopic Images: Preferred for specialist review

Follow-up

Patient Education

  • Benign Nature: Emphasize non-cancerous diagnosis (when certain)
  • Age-Related Changes: Many lesions are normal part of aging
  • Not Contagious: Reassure about transmission concerns
  • Recurrence: Possible after treatment - set expectations
  • Cosmetic Nature: NHS may not fund purely cosmetic treatments
  • Self-Examination: Teach ABCDE criteria for home monitoring
  • Sun Protection: Critical for prevention and risk reduction

Red Flag Symptoms - Urgent Review

ABCDE Changes: Any evolution warrants medical review

New Symptoms: Itch, pain, bleeding in existing/longstanding lesion

Rapid Growth: Especially over weeks to months

Change in Shape/Color: Any variation from baseline

Non-Healing: Lesion that fails to resolve

Elevation: New raised area within flat lesion

Follow-up Scheduling

  • Observation Cases: Review in 3-6 months or sooner if changes
  • Post-Treatment: 4-6 weeks to assess healing
  • High-Risk Patients: Annual skin checks (immunosuppressed, previous skin cancer)
  • Multiple Actinic Keratoses: 3-6 month intervals (cumulative risk 10-15% over 5 years)
  • Post-Excision: Suture removal (if applicable) at 7-14 days
  • Histology Review: Ensure patient receives and understands results

Sun Protection Counseling

  • Sunscreen: SPF 30+ broad-spectrum, daily application
  • Reapplication: Every 2 hours when outdoors
  • Protective Clothing: Wide-brimmed hats, long sleeves, UV-protective fabric
  • Avoid Peak Hours: 10am-4pm when UV index highest
  • Seek Shade: Especially during midday sun
  • No Blistering Sunburns: Significant skin damage indicator
  • Regular Skin Checks: Monthly self-examination

Patient Resources

  • Skin Self-Check Videos: Available on YouTube/charity websites
  • Patient.info: Reliable information leaflets
  • Charities: British Skin Foundation, Melanoma UK
  • Teledermatology: Local services for remote review
  • Photography: Teach patients to take serial photos for monitoring

When to Seek Further Help

Clinical Uncertainty: Always err on side of caution

Diagnostic Dilemma: Refer for dermoscopy or biopsy

Atypical Features: Any deviation from classic presentation

Patient Concern: Anxiety despite reassurance

Multiple Lesions: Difficult to monitor - dermatology input

Suspicion of SCC: Two-week wait referral

Documentation Checklist

  • Clinical Description: Size, color, shape, surface, texture, mobility
  • Site: Precise location and number of lesions
  • History: Duration, evolution, symptoms, risk factors
  • Differential Diagnosis: Record your thoughts
  • Management Plan: Treatment, follow-up, safety netting
  • Patient Education: Topics discussed and understanding
  • Photographs: Attach if taken

Key Takeaways

1. When in Doubt, Refer: Better to over-refer than miss malignancy

2. Actinic Keratosis Always Needs Treatment: 60% of SCC originates from AK

3. Keratoacanthoma Cannot Be Distinguished from SCC: Always excise

4. Palms/Soles/Mucosa Are Red Flags: Not typical for benign lesions

5. Rapid Change Is Concerning: Urgent referral warranted

6. Patient Anxiety Matters: Refer if persistent concern

7. Use Teledermatology: Excellent resource when available

8. Document Everything: Photos, measurements, plans

This algorithm is based on dermatology specialist presentation and NICE/BMJ Best Practice guidelines. Always use clinical judgment and refer when uncertain.
