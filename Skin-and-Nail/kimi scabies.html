Quick Diagnosis Guide

üîë Key Symptoms

  • Intense generalized pruritus, worse at night
  • Symptoms start 3-6 weeks after primary infestation
  • 1-3 days for reinfestation (prior sensitization)
  • Absence of itch does NOT exclude scabies (infants, neurological conditions)

üìç Classic Distribution

  • Fingers, wrists, elbows, axillae
  • Periumbilical area, waist, buttocks
  • Breasts, genitalia (penile/scrotal papules = sexual transmission)
  • Spares back and head (except in infants/elderly)

üî¨ Diagnostic Tests

  • Ink burrow test: Simple bedside test
  • Microscopy: 100% specific but <50% sensitive
  • Scrape 4+ lesions with #15 blade dipped in liquid paraffin
  • Look for mites, eggs, or fecal material

‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Judgement

  • Diagnosis is often clinical based on history + examination
  • Consider differential diagnoses (dermatitis, insect bites, lice)
  • Animal scabies: shorter incubation, no burrows, self-limiting
  • When uncertain ‚Üí refer to dermatology

Treatment Pathway

üë•

Simultaneous Contact Treatment is MANDATORY

Without this, treatment will fail
  • All household members
  • All sexual partners within past month
  • All close personal contacts (even if asymptomatic)
  • Consider GUM referral for partner notification

üß¥ Permethrin 5% Cream

  • Licensed for ages 2 months+
  • Two applications, 7 days apart
  • Apply to whole body (including scalp/face in infants & elderly)
  • Wash off after 8-12 hours
  • Reapply if hands washed within 8 hours

üíä Oral Ivermectin

  • 200 mcg/kg per dose (two doses, 7 days apart)
  • Taken on empty stomach with water
  • No food 2 hours before/after
  • Round up to nearest 3mg tablet
  • Preferred in eczema or skin disease

Itching may persist 2-4 weeks after successful treatment due to immune response. Retreat if new burrows appear or symptoms persist beyond 4 weeks.

Prescribing Details

Permethrin 5% Cream Dosing

Age-based Dosing (per application)

  • Adults & >12 years: 30g (max 60g per application)
  • 6-12 years: 15g
  • 1-5 years: 7.5g
  • 2 months-1 year: 3.75g

Application Method

  • Cool, dry skin (not after hot bath)
  • Include: between fingers/toes, under nails, wrists, axillae
  • Infants: include scalp, face, ears (avoid eyes/mouth)
  • Use mittens to prevent oral ingestion
  • Parents/carers should wear gloves

Ivermectin Contraindications & Cautions

üö´

DO NOT PRESCRIBE IF:

  • Weight <15kg (children under 6 years)
  • Pregnancy (manufacturer advises avoid)
  • Severe hepatic impairment
  • Breastfeeding (unless benefit outweighs risk)
  • Known hypersensitivity

‚ö†Ô∏è Adverse Effects

  • Transient pruritus exacerbation
  • Hypereosinophilia, liver dysfunction
  • Very rare: Stevens-Johnson syndrome
  • Multiple other rare effects (see BNF)

‚ö†Ô∏è Drug Interactions

  • Warfarin/Acenocoumarol: Potentiates anticoagulant effect
  • Levamisole: Increases ivermectin exposure
  • Corticosteroids: May reduce efficacy if used before permethrin

Red Flags & Special Cases

üè•

CRUSTED SCABIES = EMERGENCY

Highly contagious, requires isolation
  • Thousands/millions of mites - highly contagious via direct contact AND environment
  • Who's at risk: Immunocompromised (HIV, lymphoma, steroids), elderly, neurological disorders, physical incapacity
  • Presentation: Thick crusts, erythematous plaques, fissures, minimal/no pruritus, malodorous
  • Action: URGENT referral, isolation, barrier nursing, combination therapy (topical + oral ivermectin)
  • Investigate: Underlying immunodeficiency

üë∂ Infants <2 months

  • Scabies rare in this age group
  • Permethrin not licensed (<2 months)
  • Action: Seek specialist advice (paediatric dermatologist)
  • May require alternative treatment approach

üîÑ Treatment Failure

  • First check: Did all contacts get treated?
  • Persistence <2-4 weeks: May be normal post-scabetic itch
  • Persistence >4 weeks OR new burrows: Retreatment needed
  • Resistant scabies: Consider oral ivermectin + topical combination
  • Refer to dermatologist for persistent nodular scabies

Differential Diagnosis

ü¶ü Other Infestations

  • Pubic lice: Itchy papules in coarse body hair
  • Body lice: Excoriations, secondary infection
  • Animal scabies: Shorter incubation, no burrows, self-limiting
  • Insect bites: Papules/papulovesicles, no linear burrows

ü©∫ Dermatological Mimics

  • Atopic eczema: Dry, itchy rash, often childhood onset
  • Contact dermatitis: Allergen/irritant exposure history
  • Bullous pemphigoid: Elderly, tense blisters
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis: Extensor surfaces, extremely itchy
