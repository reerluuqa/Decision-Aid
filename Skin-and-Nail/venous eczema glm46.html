Assessment

Clinical History

  • Symptoms of venous insufficiency: pain, heavy legs, leg fatigue, itching, swelling, aching, and/or discomfort
  • Symptoms typically worse at end of day and relieved by leg elevation
  • Previous history of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), cellulitis, or venous leg ulcers
  • Duration and progression of symptoms
  • Impact on quality of life and daily functioning

Risk Factors

  • Advancing age
  • Trauma to lower limbs
  • Being overweight or obese
  • Family history of venous insufficiency
  • Immobility
  • Chronic swelling of lower leg, aggravated by hot weather and prolonged standing
  • Varicose veins

Physical Examination

  • Assess and document nature and severity of skin changes
  • Hyperpigmentation (brown-red discolouration)
  • Venous eczema: red, itchy, scaly, or flaky skin with possible blisters and crusts
  • Lipodermatosclerosis: painful, hardened, tight, red or brown skin
  • Atrophie blanche: ivory white depressed atrophic plaques surrounded by red spots
  • Examine for varicose veins (standing and lying positions)
  • Assess for pitting oedema

Complications Assessment

  • Chronic pain
  • Impetiginization or cellulitis
  • Secondary eczema
  • Contact dermatitis
  • Skin ulceration
Urgent Referral Required: If ABPI < 0.5 (severe arterial insufficiency), compression stockings are contraindicated and urgent vascular assessment is needed.
Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

Venous eczema and lipodermatosclerosis are diagnosed clinically based on characteristic skin changes in the context of chronic venous insufficiency.

Venous Eczema: Inflammatory condition characterized by red, itchy, scaly, or flaky skin, which may have blisters and crusts on the surface.
Lipodermatosclerosis: Results from chronic inflammation and fibrosis of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue of the lower legs. May be acute (painful inflammation above ankles) or chronic (hardened, tight skin).

Differential Diagnosis

  • Contact dermatitis
  • Atopic eczema
  • Cellulitis
  • Erysipelas
  • Panniculitis
  • Necrobiosis lipoidica

Excluding Arterial Insufficiency

Before prescribing compression therapy, it is essential to exclude arterial insufficiency by measuring the Ankle Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI).

ABPI Value Interpretation Action
< 0.5 Severe arterial insufficiency Urgent vascular referral. Compression contraindicated.
0.5 - 0.8 Arterial disease or mixed arterial/venous disease Vascular referral. Reduced compression only under specialist advice.
0.8 - 1.3 Normal arterial circulation Compression stockings generally safe to use.
> 1.3 Possible arterial calcification Vascular referral. ABPI result unreliable.
Treatment

Skin Care

Emollients - Apply regularly (at least twice daily) to maintain skin hydration and barrier function. Use liberally and continue even when skin appears improved.
Topical Corticosteroids - Treat flares with appropriate potency topical steroids. Flares of lipodermatosclerosis may require very potent topical corticosteroids.

Compression Therapy

Important: Only initiate compression therapy after excluding arterial insufficiency through ABPI measurement.
  • Class 2 (medium) stockings are suitable for most people
  • Try Class 1 (light) stockings if Class 2 is not tolerated
  • Class 3 (strong) stockings may be necessary for severe disease if response to Class 2 is inadequate
  • Ensure proper fitting and provide education on correct use

Self-Care Advice

  • Elevate legs above hips when resting to reduce swelling
  • Avoid standing for prolonged periods
  • Keep physically active with regular exercise
  • Perform leg exercises to assist circulation
  • Weight management if overweight or obese
  • Avoid skin injury to prevent ulceration
  • Monitor skin condition for changes or damage

Managing Complications

Pain - Simple analgesics such as paracetamol with or without codeine.
Infection - Treat infected eczema with appropriate topical or systemic antibiotics.
Contact Dermatitis - Refer to dermatologist for patch testing if suspected.
Referral Considerations: Consider referral to vascular service for people with skin changes, especially if varicose veins or venous leg ulcers are present.
Follow-up

Monitoring

  • Regular review to assess response to treatment
  • Monitor for improvement in skin condition and symptoms
  • Check compliance with compression therapy and skin care regimen
  • Reassess ABPI if symptoms change or if peripheral arterial disease develops

Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral Required:
  • ABPI < 0.5 (severe arterial insufficiency)
  • Sudden deterioration in skin condition
  • Suspicion of malignancy
Specialist Referral Considered:
  • Inadequate control of symptoms with primary care management
  • Suspected contact dermatitis (for patch testing)
  • ABPI 0.5-0.8 (arterial disease or mixed arterial/venous disease)
  • ABPI > 1.3 (possible arterial calcification)
  • Recurrent cellulitis
  • Presence of varicose veins amenable to intervention

Patient Education

  • Provide information about the chronic nature of the condition
  • Explain the importance of ongoing skin care and compression therapy
  • Discuss signs of complications that should prompt medical review
  • Offer resources for further information and support
