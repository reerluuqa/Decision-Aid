Vitiligo CKS Guide

Revised April 2025 | Rapid Primary Care Dashboard

Referral Criteria

Urgent/Routine Diagnosis Uncertain

Features atypical (inflammation, scaling, scarring).

Urgent Rapid Progression

Spreading quickly or active disease.

Routine Segmental Vitiligo

Unilateral/dermatomal distribution (stabilizes quickly, harder to treat).

Routine High Impact

>10% Body Surface Area OR Significant psychosocial distress.

Routine Treatment Failure

No response to topical treatments or contraindications exist.

Diagnosis & Assessment

Presentation: Well-demarcated, chalky white macules. Non-scaly. Often symmetrical (non-segmental).

  • Onset: Age? Speed? Trigger (stress, trauma)?
  • Symptoms: Usually asymptomatic, itch may precede lesions.
  • History: Family hx of vitiligo or Autoimmune (Thyroid, Addison's, B12, Diabetes).
  • Skin Type: Fitzpatrick classification (affects treatment options).
  • Koebner Phenomenon: Depigmentation at trauma sites.
  • Trichrome: Three shades of color.
  • Inflamed borders: Redness at edges.
  • Confetti-like: Small scattered macules.
  • Pityriasis Versicolor: Scale Fine scale, upper trunk.
  • Pityriasis Alba: Children Ill-defined, subtle scale, face/arms.
  • Post-inflammatory: History of eczema/psoriasis.
  • Halo Naevus: Mole with white ring.
  • Chemical Depigmentation: Occ hx (phenols, dyes).
Investigations:
Check Thyroid Function & Antibodies (TPO) for all patients. Consider B12/Folate or Glucose if indicated.

Psychosocial Support

Assess impact on QoL (DLQI, PHQ-9). Vitiligo carries high risk of anxiety/depression.

  • Mild distress: Self-help resources (see below).
  • Mod/Severe distress: Refer for psychological services (CBT).
Resources:
  • The Vitiligo Society
  • Changing Faces (Camouflage advice)

Management Steps

1. General Advice (For All)

  • Sun Protection: SPF 50+, 5-star UVA. (Prescribe: Uvistat® ACBS).
  • Avoid Trauma: Reduce friction (clothing) to prevent Koebnerization.
  • Camouflage: Offer referral to skin camouflage service. (Prescribe: Dermacolor®, Covermark® ACBS).

2. Topical Corticosteroids (TCS)

First Line Off-label

For adults with non-segmental vitiligo (Body/Limbs).

Rx: Potent (e.g., Betamethasone 0.1%) or Very Potent (e.g., Clobetasol)
Dose: Apply OD (Once Daily).
Review: Every 3-6 months. Monitor for atrophy/striae.

Note: Avoid potent TCS on face/neck long-term.

3. Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (TCI)

Consider Face/Flexures

Alternative to TCS for Face/Neck or children. *

Rx: Tacrolimus 0.03%/0.1% or Pimecrolimus
Dose: Apply BD (Twice Daily).
Safety: Avoid in pregnancy. Sun protection crucial.

* Caveat: CKS states "if expertise available" or seek specialist advice. Check local formulary.

Prognosis & Skin Types

TypeFeatures
I-IIPale/Fair. Burns easily. No/Poor tan.
III-IVWhite to Light Brown. Tans.
V-VIBrown to Black. Rarely burns.

Note: Vitiligo is more visually prominent in Types IV-VI, potentially causing higher psychosocial impact.

  • Non-segmental: Unpredictable. Flares and stability. Spontaneous repigmentation uncommon.
  • Segmental: Rapid onset, stabilizes quickly. Persistent.
  • Best response: Face, neck, trunk.
  • Poor response: Hands, feet, lips.
