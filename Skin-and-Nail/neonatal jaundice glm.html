Neonatal Jaundice Clinical Algorithm

A Quick Reference Guide for Healthcare Professionals

Definition

  • Yellow coloration of skin and sclerae due to bilirubin accumulation
  • Prolonged jaundice: Persists beyond 14 days in term infants (≥37 weeks) or 21 days in preterm infants (<37 weeks)

Risk Factors

  • Preterm delivery/Low birth weight
  • Jaundice within first 24 hours
  • Family history of jaundice requiring treatment
  • Breastfeeding with suboptimal intake
  • Bruising/cephalhaematoma
  • East Asian or Mediterranean ethnicity
  • Dehydration
  • Down syndrome
  • Maternal diabetes
  • Family history of G6PD deficiency

Assessment

Visual examination:

  • Examine in bright, natural light
  • Blanch skin (e.g., press on nose)
  • Check sclerae and gums
  • Note: Visual assessment alone is NOT reliable for determining severity

History:

  • Age at onset and duration
  • Feeding history (wet/dirty nappies)
  • Urine/stool color (dark urine/pale stools = red flag)
  • Signs of illness
  • Family history

Referral Urgency

EMERGENCY (999): Jaundice in unwell baby or with features of bilirubin encephalopathy (lethargy, poor feeding, irritability, vomiting, abnormal muscle tone)
URGENT (within 2 hours): Jaundice appearing <24 hours of age
URGENT (within 6 hours): All other babies with suspected or obvious jaundice
SAME-DAY referral: Prolonged jaundice in otherwise well baby (may be booked within few days)

Management

  • All neonates with visible jaundice need bilirubin measurement
  • Reassure parents that jaundice is usually transient and harmless
  • Encourage breastfeeding mothers to breastfeed frequently
  • Advise parents to seek urgent medical attention if jaundice suspected within first 24 hours
  • Provide written information on neonatal jaundice

Red Flags

Jaundice within first 24 hours
Dark urine or pale chalky stools (suggests liver disease)
Signs of bilirubin encephalopathy: lethargy, poor feeding, irritability, abnormal muscle tone, high-pitched cry, apnoea, seizures, coma
Prolonged jaundice beyond expected timeframe

Prognosis

  • Most cases are physiological and harmless
  • Resolves by 2 weeks in term infants
  • Phototherapy prevents bilirubin toxicity in most cases requiring treatment
  • Prognosis for pathological jaundice depends on underlying cause
