Acute Cellulitis Clinical Algorithm

Quick Reference Guide for Primary Care Management

Definition & Assessment

  • Cellulitis: Acute bacterial infection of dermis/subcutaneous tissue
  • Classic presentation: Unilateral red, hot, swollen, tender skin with rapid spread
  • Common pathogens: Streptococcus pyogenes, Staphylococcus aureus
  • Commonly affects lower limbs but can occur anywhere

Risk Factors

  • Skin breaks (trauma, ulcers, fungal infections)
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Immunocompromise
  • Obesity
  • Oedema/lymphoedema
  • Venous insufficiency
  • Peripheral vascular disease

Diagnostic Approach

  • History: Symptoms, duration, recent trauma, risk factors
  • Examination: Document extent (consider marking borders), check for portal of entry, assess vital signs
  • Eron Classification:
    • Class I: No systemic toxicity, no uncontrolled comorbidities
    • Class II: Systemically unwell OR well but with comorbidity
    • Class III: Significant systemic upset or limb-threatening infection
    • Class IV: Sepsis or life-threatening infection

Clinical Decision Algorithm

Step-by-step management approach for acute cellulitis

1

Assess Severity & Determine Need for Admission

Admit if:

  • Signs of serious conditions (necrotizing fasciitis, orbital cellulitis, osteomyelitis, sepsis, septic arthritis)
  • Systemic illness (confusion, tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension)
  • Unstable comorbidities
  • Limb-threatening infection
  • Severe immunocompromise

Consider referral/specialist advice if:

  • Facial cellulitis (unless very mild)
  • Infection near eyes/nose
  • Spreading infection not responding to oral antibiotics
  • Lymphangitis
  • Cannot take oral antibiotics
  • Possible unusual pathogens (water exposure, penetrating injury, infection acquired outside UK)
  • Recurrent cellulitis (≥2 episodes in 12 months)
2

Primary Care Management

  • Oral antibiotics: First-line flucloxacillin or clarithromycin if penicillin allergic
  • Mark infection extent with surgical marker pen
  • Supportive care:
    • Paracetamol/ibuprofen for pain/fever
    • Adequate hydration
    • Elevate affected limb
  • Follow-up: Review in 2-3 days if no improvement
  • Safety netting: Seek help if symptoms worsen rapidly or don't improve
3

Prevention of Recurrence

  • Treat underlying causes (e.g., fungal foot infections)
  • Skin care with emollients to prevent dry/cracked skin
  • Consider prophylactic antibiotics for recurrent cases (discuss with specialists)
