Diagnosis & Clinical Presentation

Age: 1 month onwards

Definition

Acute paronychia is a localized, superficial infection or abscess of the lateral and proximal skin fold around a nail (perionychium). It develops suddenly and lasts a few days (vs. chronic paronychia which lasts >6 weeks).

Typical Features

Location

Base of fingernail (toenail possible)

Symptoms

Pain, swelling, tenderness of nail folds

Onset

History of trauma 2-5 days earlier

Affected Area

Usually one finger

Examination Findings

Digital Pressure Test

Ask patient to oppose thumb and affected finger. Increased pressure causes blanching and demarcation of any pus collection.

Visual Findings
  • Red, tender, swollen lateral/proximal nail folds
  • Visible collection of pus may be present
  • In severe cases: extension to proximal nail edge with fluctuance
  • Subungual abscess (floating nail) if under nail plate

Management Pathway

Stepwise Approach
1

Initial Assessment

Determine if fluctuant pus collection or abscess is present

2

Non-fluctuant / No Abscess

Advise warm soaks 10-15 mins, 3-4 times daily. Consider topical fusidic acid cream for minor, localized infection.

3

Fluctuant Pus / Abscess Present

Incision & Drainage Required. Perform in primary care if expertise available, otherwise refer to surgical unit/ED.

4

Antibiotic Indications

Prescribe oral antibiotics if: I&D not performed, significant pain, cellulitis, fever, comorbidities (diabetes, immunosuppression).

First-line Antibiotics

Flucloxacillin: 250-500mg QDS for 5-7 days

Clarithromycin: 250-500mg BD for 5-7 days

Erythromycin: Preferred in pregnancy/breastfeeding

Topical fusidic acid: 3-4 times daily for 7 days

Treatment Options

Evidence-based

Flucloxacillin

First-line

Dose: Adults: 250-500mg QDS for 5-7 days. Children: age-based dosing.

Indications: First-line for non-penicillin allergic patients.

Contraindications: Penicillin allergy, flucloxacillin-associated jaundice.

Clarithromycin

First-line (Penicillin Allergy)

Dose: Adults: 250-500mg BD for 5-7 days. Children: weight-based dosing.

Indications: Penicillin allergy, pregnancy (erythromycin preferred).

Key Interactions: Statins, anticoagulants, drugs that prolong QT interval.

Erythromycin

Alternative

Dose: Adults: 250-500mg QDS for 5-7 days. Children: age-based dosing.

Indications: Preferred in pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Contraindications: Acute porphyrias, QT prolongation, electrolyte disturbances.

Fusidic Acid Cream

Topical

Dose: Apply 3-4 times daily for 7 days (max 10 days to avoid resistance).

Indications: Minor, localized infection without fluctuant pus collection.

Caution: Avoid contact with naked flames after application (fire accelerant).

Warm Soaks

Advise all patients: Apply moist heat (warm soaks) for 10-15 minutes, 3-4 times daily to alleviate pain, localize infection, and hasten drainage.

Incision & Drainage Required

Indication: Fluctuant pus collection or abscess present.

Procedure: May be performed in primary care if expertise available. Otherwise refer to surgical unit or Emergency Department per local protocol.

Post-procedure: Consider oral antibiotics if significant pain, cellulitis, fever, or comorbidities.

Red Flags & Emergency Referrals

Immediate Referral Required

  • Systemically unwell (fever, tachycardia)
  • Signs of deep infection: septic tenosynovitis, osteomyelitis, septic arthritis
  • Runaround infection (extends around nail to both folds)
  • Rapidly spreading cellulitis
  • Immunocompromised patients with severe infection

When to Swab

Enlarging paronychia or inflammation of surrounding tissue

Recurrent infection or no response to treatment in 2-3 days

Immunosuppressed patients or those with diabetes

History of MRSA contact or doubt about diagnosis

Complications

Potential Serious Complications
  • Septic tenosynovitis: Pain on passive extension, symmetrical swelling, flexed posture
  • Osteomyelitis: Deep, boring pain; may require X-ray for diagnosis
  • Nail loss or permanent nail deformity
  • Deep space infections: Thenar, midpalmar, hypothenar spaces
  • Septic arthritis: Joint swelling, pain with movement

More common in immunocompromised patients or neglected/mistreated cases.

Prescribing Information

Dosing & Safety

Key Drug Interactions

Clarithromycin

Contraindicated: Statins (simvastatin, lovastatin), ergot alkaloids, colchicine, domperidone, ivabradine

Caution: Anticoagulants, calcium channel blockers, QT-prolonging drugs

Flucloxacillin

Interactions: Methotrexate, anticoagulants, probenecid, live vaccines

Hepatotoxicity risk: Monitor in >50 years, treatment >2 weeks, hepatic impairment

Adverse Effects

Common Adverse Effects

Flucloxacillin: GI upset (common), rash, hepatitis/cholestatic jaundice (rare)

Clarithromycin: GI upset, taste disturbance, headache, QT prolongation

Erythromycin: GI upset (very common), QT prolongation, hearing impairment

Fusidic acid: Skin reactions, application site irritation

Vaccine Interactions

Live cholera vaccine: Avoid antibiotics from 14 days before to 10 days after vaccination.

Live typhoid vaccine: Avoid antibiotics from 3 days before to 3 days after vaccination.

Follow-up & Patient Advice

Self-care & Prevention

Follow-up Schedule

1

Initial Visit

Assess, diagnose, initiate treatment. Provide self-care advice.

2

Routine Follow-up

Not usually needed. Most paronychia drain spontaneously.

3

Return if...

Paronychia becomes fluctuant, persists, or doesn't improve.

4

Urgent Return

If systemically unwell (fever) or signs of complications.

Patient Self-care Advice

Hygiene

Keep affected areas clean and dry

Trauma Prevention

Avoid further trauma or nail manipulation

Nail Care

Trim hang nails smoothly, avoid nail biting

Moist Work

Wear cotton-lined gloves if hands in water

Special Populations

Immunocompromised patients: Remain vigilant against minor trauma to fingertips/nails.

Occupational exposure: Treatment unlikely to succeed if wet/moist environment continues.
