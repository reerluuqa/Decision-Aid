Dupuytren's Disease

One-Page Clinical Reference Algorithm

Reference: Based on NICE CKS guidance, last revised April 2025. Designed for busy physicians as a quick reference tool during patient consultations.

Definition & Background

  • Benign, progressive fibroproliferative disorder of hand/finger fascia
  • Features: skin thickening, nodules (palm), cords extending to fingers
  • Can lead to disabling contractures
  • Often bilateral (one hand usually more affected)
  • Part of fibromatosis group (Ledderhose, Peyronie's, Garrod's)

When to Suspect

  • Skin thickening, tethering, pitting, or dimpling on palm
  • Reduced hand/finger range of motion or function
  • Firm nodules fixed to skin/deep fascia (common at distal palmar crease)
  • Longitudinal cords extending toward fingers
  • Flexion deformity at MCP/PIP joints (4th/5th fingers most common)
  • Consider ectopic lesions (knuckles, feet, penis)

Assessment

History:

  • Risk factors: age >40, male, family history, Northern European descent
  • Associated conditions: diabetes, smoking, high alcohol intake, epilepsy
  • Functional impact: work/leisure limitations
  • Pain (uncommon, but may occur in early stages)

Examination:

  • "Tabletop test": inability to lay palm flat indicates contracture
  • Document location and severity of nodules, cords, contractures
  • Assess both hands (compare severity)

Differential Diagnosis

  • Callus, ganglion, giant cell tumor, inclusion cyst
  • Stenosing tenosynovitis (trigger finger)
  • Ulnar nerve palsy, post-trauma/infection
  • Joint diseases (RA, OA), diabetic cheiroarthropathy
  • Camptodactyly, Volkmann's contracture

Primary Care Management

No contracture or functional loss:

  • Reassurance (not cancerous, but progressive)
  • Explanation of condition and potential need for future surgery
  • Pain management if needed (nodules usually resolve spontaneously)
  • Advise return if contracture develops
  • Consider referral for painful nodules (clinical judgment)

With contracture and/or functional loss:

  • REFER to hand surgeon or specialist

Referral Criteria

  • Contracture with functional impairment
  • Significant loss of hand function affecting daily activities
  • Painful nodules (based on clinical judgment)
  • Diagnostic uncertainty

Secondary Care Treatments

Treatment Description Recurrence Rate
Needle fasciotomy Minimally invasive, local anesthesia, rapid recovery ~50% at 3-5 years
Segmental aponeurotomy Small incisions to remove cord segments Moderate
Fasciectomy Surgical removal of involved fascia (partial/limited) ~20% at 10 years
Dermofasciectomy Removal of involved skin and fascia with skin graft ~8% (lowest)

Prognosis

  • Progressive condition (75% develop more advanced disease)
  • 35% progress to contracture over 18 years
  • More rapid progression: men, age <50, strong family history
  • Surgery improves function but doesn't stop disease process
  • Recurrence possible (varies by procedure)
