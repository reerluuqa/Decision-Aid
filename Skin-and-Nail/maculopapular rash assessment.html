Maculopapular Rash Assessment

EMERGENCY CONDITIONS - IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

Anaphylaxis | SJS/TEN | Sepsis | Meningococcaemia | Toxic Shock Syndrome

Definition & Key Facts

What is Maculopapular Rash?

Macule: Flat, circumscribed lesion ≤1 cm

Papule: Elevated, circumscribed lesion ≤1 cm

Patch: Macule >1 cm

Plaque: Papule >1 cm

Quick Fact

Term "maculopapular" is non-specific - many eruptions may be mislabeled. Always consider the clinical context.

Primary Differential Categories
  • Anaphylaxis - Acute onset, respiratory/cardiovascular compromise
  • Drug-induced eruptions - Most common cause in adults
  • Viral exanthems - Multiple viruses can cause this pattern
  • Bacterial infections - Scarlet fever, meningococcaemia, etc.
  • Rickettsial diseases - Tick/flea/louse/mite transmitted
  • Systemic/rheumatological - Kawasaki, Still's disease, GVHD

Key Historical Clues

History to Obtain
  • Onset and progression of rash
  • Recent medication changes (especially antibiotics, anticonvulsants)
  • Travel history and seasonal exposure
  • Illness contacts and vaccination status
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, arthralgia, respiratory)
  • Prior allergic reactions
  • Occupational exposures
  • Animal/insect exposures
Physical Exam Focus
  • Vital signs: Fever, hypotension, tachycardia
  • Skin: Distribution, morphology, mucosal involvement
  • Lymph nodes: Cervical, generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Oropharynx: Koplik spots, strawberry tongue, erosions
  • Eyes: Conjunctival injection, photophobia
  • Joints: Swelling, tenderness, ROM
  • Neurological: Altered mental status, meningismus

Life-Threatening Emergencies

Anaphylaxis IMMEDIATE

Onset: Minutes to hours after exposure

Triggers: Drugs, foods, insect stings

Critical Signs

  • Respiratory distress (bronchospasm, edema)
  • Hypotension, shock
  • Skin changes present in >80%
Treatment Actions:
1
Call for help immediately
2
Remove trigger if possible
3
IM Adrenaline 0.3-0.5mg (0.3-0.5mL of 1:1000)
4
Lie flat, establish airway, give O2
5
Repeat IM adrenaline after 5 minutes
6
IV fluid bolus for hypotension
SJS / TEN LIFE THREATENING

Onset: 4-28 days after drug exposure

Common culprits: Anticonvulsants, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, allopurinol

SJS < 10% BSA
SJS/TEN overlap 10-30% BSA
TEN > 30% BSA

Key Features

  • Dusky purple macules, target lesions
  • Mucosal involvement (ocular, oral, genital)
  • Positive Nikolsky's sign
  • Mortality up to 30%
Immediate Actions:
1
Stop ALL non-essential drugs
2
Admit to burns unit or ICU
3
Airway protection, wound care

Meningococcaemia EMERGENCY

Early sign: Maculopapular rash (transient, lasts <48 hours)

Later: Classic petechial/purpuric eruption

Critical Points

  • Maculopapular rash may precede classic signs
  • Consider in any rash + fever in high-risk settings
  • High-risk: dormitories, prisons, immunocompromised
  • Nuchal rigidity usually present

Treatment

Empirical antibiotics immediately - do NOT delay for investigations

Toxic Shock Syndrome EMERGENCY

Caused by: Staph aureus exotoxin (TSS-toxin-1)

Associations: Post-surgical, menstruation, wound infections

Diagnostic Criteria

Fever >39°C + hypotension + desquamating rash + ≥3 organ systems involved

  • Scarlatiniform rash spreading centripetally
  • Edema of extremities
  • Strawberry tongue
  • Desquamation 1-2 weeks later (palms/soles)
Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome URGENT

Affected: Children ≤6 years (rare in adults with renal/immune issues)

Clinical Features

  • Fever, malaise, tender skin prodrome
  • Erythema begins on head, generalizes rapidly (hours)
  • Superficial bullae, desquamation in 2-5 days
  • Nikolsky's sign positive
  • Perioral crusting, oral mucosa spared

Mortality: 3% children, >50% adults

Viral Exanthems

Measles (Rubeola) HIGHLY CONTAGIOUS
11-12d
Incubation
5d
Rash duration

Prodrome: Fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis (3Cs)

Koplik spots: Pathognomonic grey-white buccal papules

Rash Pattern

Starts on forehead/ears → spreads cephalocaudally → becomes confluent, especially face/trunk

Complications

Pneumonia, otitis, myocarditis, encephalitis, SSPE

Rubella (German Measles) MILD IN CHILDREN

Incubation: 16-18 days

Prodrome: Fever, headache, URI symptoms (1-5 days)

Rash Pattern

Rose-pink macules begin on face → spreads cephalocaudally → fades in same order (2-3 days)

  • Forscheimer spots: Petechial macules on soft palate (characteristic)
  • Tender cervical lymphadenopathy: Common
  • Arthralgia/arthritis: Common in adults

⚠️ Critical: Congenital Rubella

In utero infection causes severe complications - major reason for vaccination

Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) COMMON

Transmission: Saliva (kissing disease)

Peak age: 14-25 years

Incubation: 4-8 weeks

Classic Triad

Fever + lymphadenopathy + exudative pharyngitis

  • Rash: Day 4-6, trunk → extremities, petechiae common
  • IMPORTANT: Ampicillin/amoxicillin induces rash in ~50% with EBV
  • This is NOT true allergy - do not label patient
Erythema Infectiosum (Fifth Disease) USUALLY BENIGN

Cause: Parvovirus B19

Peak age: 4-10 years

Season: Winter/spring

Stage 1: "Slapped Cheeks"

Bright facial erythema sparing nose and perioral area

Stage 2: Lacy Reticular Rash

Erythematous macules/papules on arms and legs (1-3 weeks)

  • Joint symptoms more common in adults
  • Mild fever may accompany rash

High-Risk Groups

Pregnant women (fetal anemia/hydrops), immunocompromised, sickle cell

Roseola (Sixth Disease) INFANCY

Cause: HHV-6 or HHV-7

Age: Most common 6mo-2 years

Seropositivity: 90% by 12 months

Classic Presentation

High fever 3-5 days → abrupt rash with defervescence

  • Rash: Rose-pink, trunk and proximal extremities
  • Nagayama spots: Red papules on soft palate (characteristic)
  • Febrile seizures may occur
Enteroviruses (Coxsackie, Echovirus) SUMMER/AUTUMN

Transmission: Faecal-oral or respiratory

Incubation: 3-6 days

Season: Summer and autumn

  • Generalized maculopapular rash
  • May have petechiae, oral erosions, conjunctival hemorrhage
  • Fever and pharyngitis common

Hand-Foot-Mouth Disease

Coxsackie A16/A7 - vesicular eruption on palms/soles + vesicular stomatitis

Cytomegalovirus (CMV) COMMON

Seroprevalence: ~60% (developed), ~90% (developing)

Primary infection: Often asymptomatic

  • May present with fever + non-specific maculopapular eruption
  • Can cause infectious mononucleosis-like syndrome
  • Important in transplant/immunocompromised patients
Dengue Fever ENDEMIC AREAS

Transmission: Aedes mosquito

Geography: SE Asia, Pacific, Caribbean, Latin America

Travel history essential

Warning Signs

  • Facial flushing → generalized maculopapular rash
  • Myalgia, arthralgia ("breakbone fever")
  • Dengue hemorrhagic fever: Petechiae, bleeding, shock
Mpox (Monkeypox) PHEIC 2024

Incubation: 6-13 days (range 1-21 days)

2024 outbreak: Public Health Emergency of International Concern

Rash Progression

Macules → papules → vesicles → pustules (sequential stages)

  • Typically moves from face distally to extremities
  • 2022 outbreak: genital lesions, variable presentation
  • Human-to-human: skin contact, droplets, fomites
Acute HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome) CONSIDER TESTING

Timing: 3-6 weeks after exposure

Frequency: 10-12% of primary infections

  • Morbilliform rash + flu-like illness
  • Symptoms: Fatigue, sore throat, lymphadenopathy, myalgia
  • Screen with: HIV viral RNA or p24 antigen
Zika Virus MOSQUITO-BORNE

Transmission: Aedes mosquito, sexual

Asymptomatic: ~80% of infections

  • Maculopapular rash (often itchy)
  • Fever, arthralgia, conjunctivitis
  • Lower limb petechiae in ~10%

Major Concern

Congenital microcephaly - important in pregnancy

Chikungunya SELF-LIMITED

Transmission: Aedes mosquito

Hallmark: Severe arthralgia (large joints)

  • Maculopapular rash in ~50%
  • Usually with fever onset, sometimes after defervescence
  • Extremities most commonly affected
  • Rash may be pruritic (occasionally)
Ebola Virus HIGH MORTALITY

Incubation: 1-21 days (typically 5-12)

Infectious: Only when symptomatic

Rash Features

  • Maculopapular in 25-52% (outbreak dependent)
  • Non-pruritic, erythematous
  • May become purpuric/petechial with coagulopathy
  • May be difficult to see in dark skin

Bacterial Infections

Scarlet Fever HIGHLY INFECTIOUS

Cause: Group A Strep toxin

Age: 90% in children <10 years

Transmission: Respiratory droplets

Classic Presentation

Sandpaper-like erythematous rash + sore throat

  • Pastia lines: Linear petechiae in skinfolds (axilla, groin, antecubital)
  • Strawberry tongue: Red, swollen with prominent papillae
  • Pharyngeal exudates, palatal petechiae

Treatment

Penicillin/antibiotics to prevent complications and limit transmission

Note: 2022-2023 saw increased iGAS cases in UK, Europe, North America

Rickettsial Diseases (Spotted Fevers) TICK-BORNE

Transmission: Ticks, fleas, lice, mites

Season: Summer/early autumn

Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever

  • Mortality >20% if untreated
  • ~50% don't recall tick bite
  • Rash: Petechial, starts wrists/ankles → spreads centrally
  • Palms/soles involved (characteristic)
  • Fever, headache, GI symptoms, malaise

Treatment

Doxycycline is treatment of choice (even in children)

Secondary Syphilis STI

Stage: 4-10 weeks after primary infection

Classic Rash Distribution

Trunk, extremities, including palms and soles

  • Maculopapular, often copper-colored
  • May be subtle, easily missed
  • Other findings: lymphadenopathy, mucous patches
Kawasaki Disease CHILDREN <5y

Age: Primarily children <5 years

Season: Winter/spring in US

Diagnostic Criteria

Fever ≥5 days + ≥4 of 5 principal signs:

  1. Bilateral conjunctival injection
  2. Cervical lymphadenopathy (usually unilateral)
  3. Oropharyngeal changes (cracked lips, strawberry tongue)
  4. Peripheral extremity changes (erythema, edema, desquamation)
  5. Polymorphous rash

Critical: Cardiac Complications

Coronary artery aneurysms (leading cause of death)

Myocarditis, congestive heart failure

Treatment: IVIG + aspirin within 10 days of fever onset

Drug-Induced Eruptions

Key Facts
  • Most common cause of maculopapular rash in adults
  • 1-3% of patients on any medication
  • Up to 6.7% of hospital inpatients
  • 91% of drug eruptions present as maculopapular rash
  • Onset: 6-10 days (first exposure) or within 3 days (re-exposure)
  • Can occur after first dose or up to 3 weeks later
Common Culprits
Anticonvulsants Penicillins Cephalosporins Macrolides TMP-SMX NSAIDs Allopurinol Antifungals

Also: Chemotherapeutics (cytarabine, hydroxyurea, paclitaxel), Immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab), EGFR inhibitors

Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reactions (SCARs)
Condition Features
SJS/TEN Mucosal erosions, skin detachment, high mortality
DRESS Fever, facial edema, hepatitis (80%), 5-10% mortality
AGEP Acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis
DRESS Syndrome

Latency: 2-6 weeks (longer than typical drug eruptions)

Clinical Features

  • Maculopapular rash + fever + malaise
  • Facial swelling (prominent)
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Hepatic involvement (80%)
  • Respiratory, cardiac, renal possible

Treatment: Immediate drug withdrawal + supportive care

Other Causes

Acute Graft-versus-Host Disease POST-TRANSPLANT

Context: After allogeneic HSCT

Incidence: 30-50%+ (decreasing over time)

Onset: 1-3 weeks post-transplant

  • Erythematous, pruritic maculopapular rash
  • Starts: Hands/feet (acral), upper back, ears, cheeks, neck
  • May progress to diffuse erythroderma and desquamation
  • GI and liver involvement follow skin findings

Prognosis

Moderate to severe aGVHD: 50% mortality

Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis / Adult-Onset Still's Disease AUTOINFLAMMATORY

Pattern: Periodic high fever (evening spikes) + arthralgias

Rash timing: Transient, recurs with fever

Rash Characteristics

  • Non-pruritic, erythematous
  • Trunk and pressure sites favored
  • Adult form: Salmon-pink, accompanies fever

Diagnosis clue: Periodic fevers in absence of infection + arthritis

Erythema Multiforme TARGET LESIONS

Associations: HSV, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, drugs

Characteristic Lesion

Target lesions: annular erythema with central blistering, outer erythematous zone, zone of normal skin in between

  • Typically < 10% BSA
  • May have mucosal involvement (painful erosions)
  • Can cause difficulty eating/urinating
  • Self-limiting but often relapsing
Acute Viral Hepatitis (B, C) HEPATITIS

Timing: During viraemic phase

  • Rash may be maculopapular
  • Other findings: vasculitis, urticaria, lichen planus
  • Abdominal pain common

Treatment Guidelines

Anaphylaxis Protocol
1
Call for help
2
Remove trigger if possible (stop infusion)
3
Lie patient flat
4
IM Adrenaline 0.3-0.5mg (anterolateral thigh)
5
Establish airway, give high-flow O2
6
Monitoring: SpO2, ECG, BP
7
Repeat IM adrenaline after 5 min if no response
8
IV fluid bolus for hypotension
Sepsis Bundle (Hour-1)
1
Measure lactate, remeasure if >2 mmol/L
2
Obtain blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics
3
Broad-spectrum antibiotics early
4
Rapid crystalloid bolus (hypotension or lactate >4)
5
Vasopressors if needed (noradrenaline, MAP ≥65)
SJS/TEN Management

Immediate Actions

  • Stop ALL non-essential drugs immediately
  • Admit to burns unit or ICU
  • Airway protection
+
Fluid/electrolyte resuscitation
+
IV antibiotics for secondary infection
+
Pain management
+
Nutritional support (may need parenteral)
+
Thermoregulation
+
Wound care and surgical debridement

Consider: IVIG, ciclosporin, or corticosteroids

Scarlet Fever

Treatment

First-line: Penicillin V (10 days)

Alternative: Amoxicillin, cephalexin (if penicillin allergic)

Why Treat?

  • Reduce risk of complications (including iGAS)
  • Limit onward transmission
Rickettsial Infections

Treatment of Choice

Doxycycline - even in children and pregnancy

Evidence supports doxycycline as safe and more effective than alternatives

General Management Principles

Supportive Care
  • Symptomatic treatment (antihistamines, antipyretics)
  • Skin moisturization
  • Comfort measures, pain control
  • Temperature regulation
When to Admit
  • Unstable vital signs
  • Unable to maintain hydration
  • Severe mucosal involvement
  • Signs of secondary infection
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • High-risk patient factors
Infection Control
  • Isolate if contagious (measles, varicella, etc.)
  • Contact precautions when indicated
  • Notify public health for reportable diseases
  • Consider exposure prophylaxis for contacts

Quick Reference

Emergency Actions

  • Anaphylaxis → IM Adrenaline
  • Sepsis → Antibiotics + fluids
  • SJS/TEN → Stop drugs + burn unit
  • Meningococcaemia → Abx immediately

Key Diagnoses

  • Drug reaction (most common adult)
  • Viral exanthem (children)
  • Scarlet fever (sandpaper rash)
  • Kawasaki (fever + mucocutaneous)

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Respiratory distress
  • Hypotension/shock
  • Mucosal erosions
  • Positive Nikolsky sign
  • Altered mental status
