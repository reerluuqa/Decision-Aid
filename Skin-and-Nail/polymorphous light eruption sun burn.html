At a Glance

Definition

Benign, recurrent skin disease in genetically predisposed people after UVR exposure.

Demographics

Women > Men (2:1). Onsets in adolescence/young adulthood.

Classic Presentation

Itchy papules, plaques, or papulovesicles on sun-exposed sites (V-neck, arms).

Trigger

Intense UVR (mainly UVA 315-400nm). Spring/Summer onset.

Diagnosis & Critical Actions

RED FLAG: Rule Out Lupus (SLE)

Skin biopsy is rarely needed for PLE, but is mandatory if you suspect SLE. Look for persistent lesions, malar rash, or systemic symptoms.

History Clues
  • Recurrent lesions after first intensive sun of the year.
  • Delay of hours to days after exposure.
  • Remits spontaneously within days.
  • Natural Hardening: Symptoms reduce over summer.
Physical Exam
  • Site: V-neck, extensor arms, hands, legs. Face less common (except kids).
  • Pattern: Monomorphous in same patient (but polymorphous between patients).
  • Type: Papular (most common), Plaque, Papulovesicular.

Treatment Algorithm

Step 1: Assess Severity

Mild Disease Protocol

1st Line

Sufficient for typical PLE (small papules, few plaques).

  • Topical Corticosteroid (Low Potency)
    Hydrocortisone 1-2.5% or Prednicarbate 0.1%
    Apply sparingly twice daily.
  • Emollients
    Apply 2-3 times daily (water-based/lotion preferred). Cool in fridge.

Severe Disease Protocol

Intervention

Use for thick plaques, vesiculobullous variants, or widespread erythema.

  • Potent Topical Corticosteroid
    Triamcinolone 0.025-0.1% or Clobetasol 0.05%
    Apply sparingly twice daily for 7 days max.
  • Adjunct Oral NSAIDs
    Ibuprofen 300-400mg q6-8h (Max 2400mg)
    Hastens resolution of symptoms.
  • Oral Corticosteroids (Consider)
    Prednisolone 0.5-1mg/kg
    Taper 50% every other day, cease after 8 days.

Prophylaxis & Prevention

Photoprotection

Sun Avoidance: Strict advice (even in shade, UVA penetrates).

Clothing: Tightly woven, UPF-rated fabrics.

Sunscreen: Broad spectrum (UVA/UVB), SPF 25+ with antioxidants.

Hardening

Phototherapy: Consider UVB or PUVA at start of summer.

Natural: Gradual increase exposure (cautiously).

Key Differentials

Condition Differentiating Features Action
Photo-allergic Reaction History of photosensitiser (e.g. sunscreens, drugs). Dissemination common. Photo-patch test
Chronic Actinic Dermatitis Persists for weeks. Thick plaques, eczematous. UVB usually responsible. Biopsy
Solar Urticaria Wheals (hives) appearing within minutes, disappear quickly (1 hr). Phototest
Systemic Lupus (SLE) Persistent lesions, stinging/burning. Malar rash. Systemic signs. Rule Out / Biopsy
