Quick Reference
RED FLAGS - Refer Immediately
  • Severe, uncontrolled pain despite maximal tolerated therapy
  • Significant functional impairment in daily activities, mobility, or sleep
  • Signs of depression/anxiety requiring psychological intervention
  • Exhausted primary care options - consider specialist medications or interventions
FIRST-LINE MANAGEMENT
  • Paracetamol ± Codeine for mild-moderate pain
  • Neuropathic agents: Amitriptyline, Duloxetine, Gabapentin, or Pregabalin
  • Lidocaine plasters for localized/allodynic pain
  • Self-care advice + pain diary for monitoring
KEY TIMING

Definition: Pain persisting ≥3 months after shingles rash onset/healing. Early intervention improves outcomes. Follow up within 2 weeks of starting neuropathic medication.

Diagnosis

When to Suspect Post-Herpetic Neuralgia

Suspect PHN in patients with:

  • Aching, burning, itching, lancinating, or sharp pain in a unilateral dermatomal distribution
  • Pain persists ≥3 months after shingles rash onset or healing

Key Examination Findings

Sensory Deficits

Thermal, tactile, pinprick, or vibration sensation deficits within/ beyond affected dermatomes

Hyperalgesia

Excessive pain response to noxious stimuli

Allodynia

Pain from non-painful stimuli (touch/pressure)

Clinical Tip

Patients may not recall shingles rash or associate current pain with prior episode. Rarely, PHN can occur long after resolution of acute herpes zoster.

Management
Self-Management Advice
  • Wear cotton/silk fabrics to minimize irritation
  • Apply protective layer (firm bandage, compression clothing, cling film)
  • Cold packs (unless allodynia present)
Treatment Plan Considerations
  • Pain characteristics: constant vs intermittent, allodynia present?
  • Impact assessment: sleep, mobility, daily activities
  • Mood screening: evaluate for depression/anxiety

Treatment Algorithm

1. PARACETAMOL ± CODEINE
For mild-moderate pain. Often insufficient alone but may contribute to relief.
2. NEUROPATHIC PAIN AGENT
Amitriptyline, Duloxetine, Gabapentin, or Pregabalin. Titrate according to response and tolerability.
3. LIDOCAINE PLASTERS
For mild pain, prominent allodynia, elderly patients, or as adjunct to oral therapy.
4. TRAMADOL (Short-term)
For acute rescue therapy only. Do NOT use long-term without specialist supervision.

Prescribing Information

Neuropathic Pain Treatments

Amitriptyline

Start 10-25mg nocte, titrate to 75mg. Monitor for anticholinergic side effects.

Duloxetine

Start 30mg daily, increase to 60mg. Caution in hepatic impairment.

Gabapentin

Titrate from 300mg daily to 3600mg daily in divided doses. Schedule 3 controlled drug.

Pregabalin

Start 75mg BD, titrate to 300mg BD. Schedule 3 controlled drug. Risk of dependence.

Tramadol

50-100mg QDS PRN. Use short-term only. Risk of dependence and seizures.

Capsaicin Cream

UNAVAILABLE until June 2026. Consider alternative topical agents.

Lidocaine Plasters (Versatis/Ralvo)

Dosing
  • Cover painful area once daily for up to 12 hours
  • Maximum 3 plasters simultaneously
  • 12-hour plaster-free interval required
  • Treatment-free period every 2-4 weeks to reassess
Cautions
  • Do NOT apply to inflamed/injured skin
  • Caution in severe cardiac/hepatic/renal impairment
  • Hypersensitivity to amide-type local anesthetics
Risk Factors & Prognosis
Risk Factors
  • Increasing age (especially >60 years)
  • Severe acute pain and rash severity
  • Ophthalmic involvement (V1 distribution)
  • Comorbidities: Diabetes, respiratory disease, immunosuppression
Prognosis
  • Duration: Up to 50% experience pain >1 year
  • Pain relief: Satisfactory control achieved in <50% of cases
  • Prevention: Shingles vaccine 66-75% effective in preventing PHN
Follow-up Strategy
Early Follow-up (2 weeks)
  • Assess dose titration progress
  • Evaluate tolerability and adverse effects
  • Confirm patient understanding of titration process
  • Review pain diary if provided
If Treatment Fails
  • If poorly tolerated: Titrate slower or switch
  • If ineffective at max dose: Switch or add agent
  • If pain controlled: Consider gradual reduction over time
  • If inadequate response: Refer to specialist
