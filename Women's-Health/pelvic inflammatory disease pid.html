Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

A one-page clinical algorithm for rapid assessment and management in primary care.

1. Assessment & Diagnosis

Maintain a low threshold for empirical treatment. Diagnosis is clinical; do not delay for test results.

Key Symptoms (Recent Onset)

  • Bilateral lower abdominal or pelvic pain
  • Deep dyspareunia (painful intercourse)
  • Abnormal vaginal bleeding (intermenstrual or postcoital)
  • Abnormal vaginal/cervical discharge

Key Examination Findings

  • Pelvic tenderness on bimanual exam (cervical motion, uterine, or adnexal)
  • Mucopurulent discharge or cervical friability on speculum exam

2. Immediate Actions & Red Flags

Action Details & Triggers
Pregnancy Test Mandatory for all to exclude ectopic pregnancy.
Urgent Hospital Admission Admit immediately if any of the following are present:
  • Pregnancy confirmed or ectopic cannot be excluded.
  • Severe systemic illness (high fever, vomiting, signs of peritonitis).
  • Palpable adnexal mass (suspected tubo-ovarian abscess).
  • Uncertain diagnosis where surgical emergencies cannot be ruled out.
  • Unable to tolerate oral treatment regimen.

3. Outpatient Management & Treatment

If no red flags for admission, proceed with outpatient care. Start empirical antibiotics immediately after taking swabs.

Antibiotic Regimens (14-Day Course)

Tier / Condition Recommended Regimen
First-Line (Low/High Gonorrhoea Risk) Ceftriaxone 1g IM (single dose)
PLUS
Doxycycline 100mg twice daily
PLUS
Metronidazole 400mg twice daily
Second-Line Ofloxacin 400mg twice daily + Metronidazole 400mg twice daily
OR
Moxifloxacin 400mg once daily
If M. genitalium Confirmed Moxifloxacin 400mg once daily
MHRA Warning: Fluoroquinolones (Ofloxacin, Moxifloxacin) carry a risk of disabling and irreversible side effects. Prescribe only when other antibiotics are inappropriate and provide patient safety information.

4. Patient Advice

  • Pain Relief: Recommend paracetamol and/or ibuprofen.
  • Sexual Contact: Abstain from all intercourse until both patient and partner(s) complete treatment and are symptom-free.
  • Contraception: Advise barrier methods to reduce future STI risk.
  • IUD/IUS in situ:
    • Mild/Moderate PID: Can remain. Reassess in 72 hours; if no improvement, remove.
    • Severe PID: Remove immediately. Consider emergency contraception if removed.

5. Partner Management

  • All sexual partners from the last 6 months require screening and treatment.
  • Advise partners to attend a specialist sexual health service.
  • If managed in primary care, offer empirical treatment: Doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7 days while awaiting test results.

6. Follow-Up

Review within 72 hours

  • Assess for clinical improvement. If not improving, check compliance, reassess diagnosis, and consider admission.
  • Review swab results and adjust antibiotics based on sensitivities if needed.

Arrange "Test of Cure" (TOC)

  • Gonorrhoea: TOC 2-4 weeks post-treatment.
  • M. genitalium: TOC 4 weeks post-treatment.
  • Chlamydia: TOC 3-5 weeks post-treatment only if symptoms persist, compliance was poor, or re-exposure is suspected.

This is a clinical aide for quick reference and does not replace full clinical guidelines or professional judgment. Based on NICE CKS on Pelvic Inflammatory Disease.
