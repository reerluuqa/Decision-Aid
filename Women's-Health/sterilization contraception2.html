This guidance covers the general principles of male and female sterilization. It does not cover other contraceptive methods, emergency contraception, or assessment for sexually transmitted infections.

Patient Assessment

Essential Assessment Criteria

Comprehensive assessment required before proceeding with sterilization

Apply WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria for Contraceptive Use to assess suitability

Key Assessment Points

  • Evaluate the patient's understanding of the permanent nature of sterilization
  • Assess mental capacity to make an informed decision
  • Determine risk of future regret (particularly important in younger patients)
  • Discuss partner's suitability for sterilization when applicable
  • Consider alternative long-acting reversible contraception (LARC) methods

Information to Provide

Verbal and written information about the procedure
Failure rates compared to other contraceptive methods
Advantages, disadvantages, and potential risks
Information on alternative contraceptive methods
Sterilization reversal success rates (31-97%)
NHS does not routinely offer reversal procedures
Male Sterilization (Vasectomy)

Procedure Details

  • Minimally invasive procedure under local anesthesia
  • Interrupts vas deferens to prevent sperm in ejaculate
  • Methods: coagulation, ligation, or clips

Effectiveness

Failure rate: ~1 in 2000 (0.05%) after confirmed azoospermia

Post-Procedure Guidance

  • Continue effective contraception until azoospermia confirmed
  • Schedule post-vasectomy semen analysis (PVSA) at 12 weeks
  • Special clearance: <100,000 non-motile sperm/mL at ≥7 months if motile sperm persist

Aftercare Advice

  • Take NSAIDs for pain (if no contraindication)
  • Wear supportive underwear, especially first 48 hours
  • Avoid sexual activity for 2-7 days
  • Rest and avoid strenuous activity until pain permits
Seek medical help if: persistent bleeding, pain, infection, or rapidly enlarging scrotal hematoma
Female Sterilization (Tubal Occlusion)

Procedure Methods

  • Laparoscopic: Most common, under general anesthesia
  • Hysteroscopic: Essure® micro-inserts (discontinued)
  • Mini-laparotomy: Rare, used when laparoscopy contraindicated

Effectiveness

Failure rate: ~1 in 200 (0.5%)

With Filshie clips: 1 in 333-500 (lower failure rate)

Post-Procedure Guidance

Laparoscopic: Continue current contraception for at least 7 days
Hysteroscopic: Use additional contraception for at least 3 months until occlusion confirmed by imaging

Aftercare Advice

  • Analgesia for pain/discomfort
  • Keep incision(s) clean and dry for 1-2 days
  • Avoid strenuous activity and sex for 1 week
Seek medical help if: fever, severe abdominal pain, missed period with pain (possible ectopic pregnancy), leg pain/swelling (DVT), dysuria (UTI), or red/painful scars (infection)
Risks and Safety Information

Male Sterilization Risks

Small risk of hematoma and/or infection
Chronic post-vasectomy pain (1-14%)
No increased risk of impotence or testicular cancer
Possible association with prostate cancer (not causal)

Female Sterilization Risks

Risk of ectopic pregnancy if failure occurs
General anesthesia risks
Bowel, bladder, or vessel injury (risk of laparotomy: ~2/1000)
Risk of death: ~1/12,000 procedures
May have protective effect against ovarian cancer

Important Notes

  • Both procedures: No protection against sexually transmitted infections (STIs)
  • Both procedures: Considered permanent; NHS does not routinely offer reversal
  • Female sterilization: No significant changes to hormone levels
  • Female sterilization: Not associated with increased risk of breast, cervical, or endometrial cancer
Key Recommendations Summary
Provide comprehensive verbal and written information before procedure
Assess understanding of permanence and risk of regret
Discuss alternative LARC methods
Ensure PVSA completion for vasectomy patients
Document informed discussion and consent process
Reinforce that sterilization does not protect against STIs
Clinical Tip: The pre-sterilization assessment process is designed to ensure that people at risk for regret are identified and fully informed about alternative long-acting reversible contraceptive methods.
