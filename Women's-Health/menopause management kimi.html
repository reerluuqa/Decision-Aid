Assessment
Clinical Evaluation
  • Age & History: Average menopause age 51 (range 45-55). Early menopause 40-44, POI <40
  • Menstrual Pattern: Irregular cycles (2-3 weeks or many months), ~10% have abrupt cessation
  • Symptom Onset: Perimenopause typically begins mid-to-late 40s
Symptom Assessment

~80% experience symptoms, ~25% severe. Symptoms last on average >7 years.

Vasomotor Symptoms (80%) Sleep Disturbance Mood Changes Loss of Libido Urinary/Vaginal Symptoms
  • Hot Flushes/Sweats: Affect face, head, neck, chest; few minutes duration
  • Sleep: Secondary to VMS, psychosocial factors; contributes to depression/irritability
  • Mood: Anxiety, irritability, memory loss; increased risk of depression
  • Urogenital: Dyspareunia, dryness, recurrent UTIs, urinary frequency
  • Other: Brittle nails, skin thinning, hair loss, generalized aches
Risk Factor Screening
Cardiovascular Risk: Blood pressure, lipids, HbA1c, renal & liver function (NHS Health Check for >40s)
Screening Status: Ensure up-to-date cervical screening & mammograms

Risk Factors for Premature Menopause: Smoking, low socio-economic status, age at menarche, parity, OCP history, BMI, ethnicity, family history

Red Flags / High Risk
Age <40 with amenorrhoea (POI) Surgical menopause Chemotherapy/radiotherapy induced Severe mood disturbance
Diagnosis
Diagnostic Criteria (Clinical)
Diagnosis is CLINICAL in women >45 years: No laboratory tests required if healthy
  • Perimenopause: VMS + irregular periods
  • Menopause: No period for ≥12 months (not using hormonal contraception)
  • POI/Early: Age <40-44 with amenorrhoea + elevated gonadotrophins
Investigations (When Required)

FSH Level:

  • Women on hormonal contraception with amenorrhoea (single FSH at age 50, stop contraception 1 year later if menopausal range)
  • Women <45 with suspected menopause
Important: Single raised FSH is NOT diagnostic - indicates lack of ovarian response at a point in time. Normal FSH does not rule out perimenopause.
Tests NOT Recommended (Age >45)
  • Anti-Müllerian hormone
  • Inhibin A or B
  • Estradiol (routine use)
  • Antral follicle count / ovarian ultrasound
  • LH (often automatically done but unhelpful)
  • Testosterone
Differential Diagnosis

Consider other causes of secondary amenorrhoea:

Pregnancy Hypogonadotrophic hypogonadism

Younger Women: Diagnosis may be harder in early stages - consider all possibilities

Treatment
Individualised Approach
Provide adequate information about symptoms and treatment options, including benefits and risks. Use NICE discussion aid (2024).

Women facing treatment-induced menopause need pre-treatment counselling about menopause and fertility.

Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)

Most effective treatment for menopause symptoms

Vasomotor Symptoms Mood/Libido Vaginal/Bladder Symptoms
  • Onset: VMS improve within 4 weeks, maximal benefit by 3 months
  • Duration: May need continued treatment (average symptom duration >7 years)
  • Reassessment: Annually review benefits vs risks
Topical HRT: Vaginal symptoms respond slower and recur if stopped. Excellent evidence for efficacy in atrophic vaginitis. Non-hormonal lubricants also effective for dryness.
Non-Hormonal Treatments
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT):
  • Beneficial for VMS, low mood, anxiety
  • Can be used with HRT or alone if HRT contraindicated
  • Evidence supports menopause-specific CBT
Prescribable Alternatives:
Clonidine (licensed for VMS) SSRIs/SNRIs (unlicensed) Gabapentinoids (unlicensed) Oxybutynin (unlicensed) Fezolinetant (NK3 antagonist) - Private only
Over-the-Counter Alternatives:
Phyto-oestrogens: Black cohosh, soy, red clover. Limited evidence, significant placebo effect. Safety concerns in breast cancer patients.
  • Black Cohosh: Some evidence for VMS, but reduces tamoxifen effectiveness
  • Isoflavones/Red Clover: Estrogenic activity - avoid in breast cancer
  • Safety: Limited data on VTE risk; inconsistent active ingredient concentrations
  • THR Mark: Advise Traditional Herbal Registration Certification for safest OTC option
Management of POI & Early Menopause (Age <45)
High Risk: Osteoporosis, CVD, Dementia HRT Recommended until age 51
  • Early Menopause (40-44): Up to 20% of women
  • POI (<40): ~1% of women
  • Benefits: HRT provides hormones that would naturally be present; no increased breast cancer risk vs normally menstruating women
  • Dosing: May need higher doses for symptom control, especially after surgical menopause
  • Contraindications: Caution in oestrogen-sensitive cancers - discuss with oncologist
  • Referral: Consider specialist menopause clinic (NHS availability varies)
Healthy Lifestyle Measures
  • Smoking: Stopping smoking beneficial
  • Weight: Weight loss if overweight
  • Alcohol: Limit alcohol intake
  • Exercise: Regular aerobic exercise recommended
  • Calcium: Ensure adequate intake (~700mg/day)
  • Caffeine: Avoidance or reduction may help
Follow-up
Monitoring Response
  • VMS: Assess improvement at 4 weeks, maximal benefit by 3 months
  • Vaginal Symptoms: Slower response, may take longer to assess efficacy
  • Mood/Libido: Evaluate psychological and relationship factors
  • Urogenital: Assess pH, atrophy, recurrent infections
Persistence: VMS persist in >50% of women for >7 years - expect longer treatment durations
Reassessment Schedule
Annual Review Minimum Earlier if symptoms persist
  • Annually: Assess benefits vs risks of ongoing treatment
  • Symptom Review: Evaluate efficacy and side effects
  • Health Check: Cardiovascular risk, cervical screening, mammography
  • Medication Review: Check adherence, dosing requirements
Special Populations - Enhanced Follow-up

POI/Early Menopause:

  • Continue HRT until at least age 51
  • Monitor bone density (DEXA scan)
  • Cardiovascular risk assessment
  • Fertility discussions if relevant

Breast Cancer Survivors:

  • Coordinate care with oncology team
  • Non-hormonal alternatives preferred
  • THR-marked products if using herbal remedies
  • Avoid: Black cohosh (reduces tamoxifen effect), isoflavones (estrogenic)
When to Stop / Switch Treatment
Stopping HRT: Vaginal symptoms often recur after stopping. Consider gradual tapering.
  • Side Effects: Switch route/dose if intolerance
  • Inadequate Response: Consider dose adjustment or alternative therapy
  • Contraindications: Stop immediately if new contraindications develop
  • Patient Preference: Respect informed decisions to stop
  • Age 51+: Reassess need for continuation in POI/early menopause
Current Controversies & Considerations

Bioidentical HRT:

  • Regulated (rBHRT): Prescribed, MHRA-approved (micronised progesterone, oestradiol)
  • Compounded (cBHRT): Not MHRA-evaluated, BMS advises against use
  • Evidence: Micronised progesterone may have lower breast cancer risk (more data needed)

NICE vs BMS Guidance:

  • NICE updated November 2024 with discussion aid
  • BMS expressed concerns about some evidence and figures
  • Use shared decision-making with patients
