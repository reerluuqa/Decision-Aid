Always consider early referral to specialist fertility unit

Assessment

Identify risk factors and clinical presentation

Risk Factors

High Risk
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
  • Age under 30 years
  • Rapidly rising oestrogen levels & large number of follicles
  • Use of hCG for luteal phase support
  • Low body weight
  • Previous history of OHSS
  • High levels of anti-Mullerian hormone (AMH)

Clinical Presentation

Caution

Timing: Symptoms start within 24 hours of hCG administration, become severe after 7-10 days

  • Bloating (often first symptom)
  • Abdominal pain (mild to severe)
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Shortness of breath (pleural effusions)
  • Reduced urine output
  • Signs of haemoconcentration

Physical Examination Findings

  • Abdominal distension and tenderness
  • Enlarged ovaries on palpation
  • Evidence of ascites (fluid thrill, shifting dullness)
  • Decreased breath sounds (pleural effusion)
  • Signs of dehydration

Diagnosis

Classify severity and confirm with investigations

Classification System

Mild OHSS
Stable
  • • Abdominal bloating
  • • Mild abdominal pain
  • • Ovarian size usually <8 cm
Moderate OHSS
Caution
  • • Moderate abdominal pain
  • • Nausea ± vomiting
  • • Ultrasound evidence of ascites
  • • Ovarian size usually 8-12 cm
Severe OHSS
Urgent
  • • Clinical ascites (occasionally hydrothorax)
  • • Oliguria
  • • Haemoconcentration (haematocrit >45%)
  • • Hypoproteinaemia
  • • Ovarian size usually >12 cm
Critical OHSS
Critical
  • • Tense ascites or large hydrothorax
  • • Haematocrit >55%
  • • White cell count >25 × 10⁹/L
  • • Oligo-anuria
  • • Thromboembolism
  • • Adult Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

Diagnostic Investigations

Routine
Imaging
  • Pelvic ultrasound (ovarian size, ascites, torsion risk)
  • Chest X-ray (pleural effusion)
  • Abdominal girth measurement
Laboratory
  • FBC (haematocrit, WBC)
  • U&E and creatinine (renal function)
  • Liver function tests

Key thresholds: Haematocrit >45% indicates severe, >55% critical. WBC >15 × 10⁹/L concerning, >25 × 10⁹/L critical.

Treatment

Evidence-based management strategies

Prevention Strategies

Prophylaxis
  • Individualised stimulation regimes based on risk stratification
  • Use GnRH agonist trigger instead of hCG (reduces OHSS but may reduce live birth rates unless embryos frozen)
  • Freeze-all embryos strategy with transfer in subsequent cycle
  • Avoid exogenous and endogenous hCG for luteal phase support
  • Consider metformin in women with PCOS
  • Cabergoline (dopamine antagonist) in high-risk women after oocyte retrieval
  • Use GnRH antagonist protocols in high-risk women

Mild to Moderate Management

Caution
Outpatient Care
  • Paracetamol and/or opiates for pain
  • Avoid NSAIDs
  • Safe anti-emetics (consider pregnancy)
  • Increase oral fluids, drink to thirst
Monitoring
  • Review every 1-2 days
  • Moderate cases: admit for thromboprophylaxis
  • Educate on red flag symptoms

Severe/Critical Management

Urgent

Hospital/ICU admission required - multidisciplinary approach essential

Supportive Care
  • IV fluids if oral intake inadequate
  • Colloid (albumin) if remains fluid-depleted
  • Electrolyte monitoring (hyponatraemia common)
  • Avoid diuretics
  • Aspiration of ascites/pleural effusion for symptom relief
Monitoring
  • Intense monitoring for complications
  • ARDS surveillance
  • AKI monitoring
  • Thromboembolism vigilance
  • Pericardial effusion assessment

Follow-up

Monitoring and long-term care planning

Monitoring Parameters

Daily
  • • Weight
  • • Abdominal girth
  • • Fluid balance
  • • Urine output
Every 2-3 days
  • • Ultrasound (ovaries, ascites)
  • • Haematocrit
  • • Electrolytes
  • • Renal function
As indicated
  • • Liver function tests
  • • Coagulation profile
  • • Chest X-ray
  • • Doppler (torsion risk)

Complications to Monitor

Serious
  • Thromboembolism (major cause of morbidity)
  • Adult Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)
  • Ovarian torsion (1-2% with very enlarged ovaries)

Rare but fatal: Death can occur in critical cases

Discharge Criteria

Stable
  • Symptoms resolving (bloating, pain, nausea)
  • Able to maintain oral hydration
  • Urine output >1000ml/day
  • Haematocrit <45% and stable
  • No evidence of ascites progression
  • Patient educated on warning signs

Patient Education

  • Provide 24/7 contact number for concerns
  • Seek urgent medical attention for: severe pain, breathing difficulty, reduced urine output, dizziness, leg swelling/pain
  • Avoid strenuous activity due to ovarian torsion risk
  • Continue thromboprophylaxis if prescribed
