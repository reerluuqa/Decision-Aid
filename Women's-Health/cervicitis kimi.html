Cervicitis Management Algorithm

Evidence-based clinical decision support tool for the assessment, diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up of cervicitis based on BMJ Best Practice guidelines (April 2025)

Assessment
History Taking
Vaginal discharge: Purulent, foul-smelling, mucopurulent consistency Common
Postcoital/intermenstrual bleeding: Suggests cervical involvement High Risk
Dysuria/urinary frequency: When associated with discharge, suggests cervicitis Common
Dyspareunia: Painful intercourse, may indicate STI Uncommon
Pelvic pain: Acyclic pelvic pain may suggest PID High Risk
Risk Factor Assessment

Strong Risk Factors

  • Age 15-24 years (highest prevalence)
  • Multiple or new sexual partners
  • Inconsistent condom use
  • Previous STI diagnosis
  • Bacterial vaginosis co-infection
  • Sex work exposure
  • Absence of hydrogen peroxide-producing lactobacilli

Additional Factors

  • Infertility patients (tubal factor risk)
  • Education less than 12 years
  • Substance use disorders
  • Recent unprotected intercourse
  • Partner with concurrent partners
  • Partner with known STI
Physical Examination Findings
Cervical Examination
Friable cervix: Easily induced bleeding with cotton swab Diagnostic
Mucopurulent discharge: From cervical os, positive predictive value for gonorrhea Diagnostic
Cervical tenderness: On digital examination or swab use Common
Strawberry cervix: Erythematous, punctate lesions (Trichomonas) Specific
Vaginal/Vulvar Findings
Erythema: Inflammatory discharge drainage Possible
Normal appearance: Does NOT exclude cervicitis Important
Clinical Tip: Clinical suspicion is generally sufficient to justify therapy, especially in high-risk patients. Don't delay treatment while awaiting test results in symptomatic high-risk individuals.
Diagnosis
Initial Investigations
1
Pregnancy Test

Perform urinary or blood pregnancy test in all sexually active women

Essential
2
Wet Mount Examination

Evaluate cervical discharge for leukorrhea (>10 WBCs/hpf), trichomonads, clue cells

Sensitivity 50% for Trichomonas
3
Nucleic Acid Amplification Testing (NAAT)

Gold standard for detecting C. trachomatis, N. gonorrhoeae, M. genitalium, T. vaginalis

Sensitivity >99% for endocervical specimens
4
Culture & Additional Tests

Thayer-Martin culture for gonorrhea, Gram stain for bacterial vaginosis, rapid tests for Trichomonas (30-45 min)

Specific
Diagnostic Criteria
Accepted Diagnostic Criteria: Easily induced cervical bleeding with cotton swab AND/OR mucopurulent cervical discharge
Leukorrhea: >10 WBCs per high-power field on vaginal fluid microscopy PPV 92% in non-pregnant women
Easily induced bleeding: Friable cervix on examination High PPV for chlamydial infection
Screening Recommendations
Adolescents & Reproductive Age (<25 years)
Annual screening: For C. trachomatis and N. gonorrhoeae Evidence-based
High-risk screening: Consider opt-out testing Increases uptake
Pregnant Patients
Initial visit: Screen for chlamydia and gonorrhea Standard of care
Third trimester: Rescreen high-risk patients For those at risk
Differential Diagnosis
Conditions to Consider
Cervical ectropion: Commonly in adolescents, pregnant women, OCP users - no purulent discharge Benign
Cervical dysplasia/cancer: History of abnormal Pap smear, heavy/irregular bleeding Requires colposcopy
Pelvic inflammatory disease: Abdominal pain, cervical motion tenderness, fever Immediate treatment
Testing Tip: If any STI is diagnosed, offer full STI panel including HIV, hepatitis B/C, and syphilis testing.
Treatment
Primary Goal: Prevent sequelae (PID, tubal infertility, ectopic pregnancy, chronic pelvic pain). Treat partners to prevent reinfection.
Initial Management - High-Risk Non-Pregnant Women
Empiric Therapy (While awaiting results)
High-risk definition: Age <25 years OR new sex partner OR partner with concurrent partners OR partner with STI
Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 7 days First-line
Ceftriaxone (add if gonorrhea risk) 500 mg IM single dose (<150 kg) OR 1000 mg IM single dose (≥150 kg) Adjunct if gonorrhea risk
Metronidazole (if sexual assault history) 2 g orally single dose For trichomonas coverage
Treatment by Confirmed Pathogen - Non-Pregnant
Chlamydia trachomatis
Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 7 days First-line
Alternative: Azithromycin 1 g orally single dose If doxycycline contraindicated
Alternative: Levofloxacin 500 mg orally once daily for 7 days Fluoroquinolone warnings apply
If concurrent gonorrhea not excluded, add ceftriaxone as above
Neisseria gonorrhoeae
Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (<150 kg) OR 1000 mg IM single dose (≥150 kg) First-line
If cephalosporin allergy: Gentamicin + Azithromycin Gentamicin 240 mg IM single dose PLUS Azithromycin 2 g orally single dose Consult ID specialist
Alternative: Cefixime (if ceftriaxone unavailable) 800 mg orally single dose Less effective
PLUS (if chlamydia not excluded): Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 7 days Always add
Trichomonas vaginalis
Metronidazole 500 mg orally twice daily for 7 days First-line (84-98% success)
Alternative: Tinidazole 2 g orally single dose 92-100% success
Bacterial Vaginosis
Metronidazole 500 mg orally twice daily for 7 days OR 0.75% vaginal gel 5g nightly for 5 days First-line
Clindamycin (intravaginal cream) 2% cream 5g nightly for 7 days Alternative
Clindamycin cream weakens latex condoms/diaphragms for 5 days after use
Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Type 2
Aciclovir 400 mg orally three times daily for 7-10 days First-line
OR Famciclovir 250 mg orally three times daily for 7-10 days Alternative
OR Valaciclovir 1 g orally twice daily for 7-10 days Alternative
Treatment in Pregnancy
Main goal: Prevent maternal, postnatal, and neonatal infection. Same principles as non-pregnant.
Chlamydia in Pregnancy
Azithromycin 1 g orally single dose First-line
Alternative: Amoxicillin 500 mg orally three times daily for 7 days Safe in pregnancy
Test of cure at 3 weeks and repeat at 3 months for high-risk patients
Gonorrhea in Pregnancy
Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (<150 kg) OR 1000 mg IM single dose (≥150 kg) First-line
PLUS (if chlamydia not excluded): Azithromycin 1 g orally single dose Dual coverage
Trichomonas in Pregnancy
Metronidazole 500 mg orally twice daily for 7 days Safe in pregnancy
Defer breastfeeding for 12-24 hours after treatment. Tinidazole not recommended in pregnancy.
HSV in Pregnancy
Acicloval OR Valaciclovir Aciclovir 400 mg TDS OR Valaciclovir 500 mg BD Safe and effective
Prophylaxis from 36 weeks Aciclovir or Valaciclovir until delivery Reduces cesarean sections
Pediatric Considerations
Presumptive treatment not indicated due to low risk. Follow local protocols for STI care in adolescents.
Abuse concerns: Detection of gonorrhea, chlamydia, syphilis, or HSV in pre-adolescent children carries high likelihood of sexual assault. Report to appropriate authorities.
Partner Management
Notify all partners from preceding 60 days: Evaluate and treat with recommended regimen Essential
Avoid intercourse: For 7 days after treatment completion Prevents reinfection
Expedited Partner Therapy (EPT): Deliver medication/prescription to partner through patient Effective for chlamydia/gonorrhea
EPT laws vary by location. Check local health department for legality.
Follow-up
Monitoring Schedule
1
Test of Cure

Only required for persistent symptoms, alternative regimens, or pregnancy

Timing: 1 week after treatment
2
Repeat Screening

Re-screen at 3-12 months following possible exposure

Standard protocol
3
Post-Treatment Screening

3-4 months after treatment for gonorrhea/chlamydia (due to reinfection risk)

Low retesting rates - use reminders
Adolescents: Re-screening within 4 months can be increased from 16% to 50% with patient counseling and reminders.
Complications to Monitor
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
Timeframe: Short-term
Likelihood: Medium (40% risk if untreated)
Action: Pelvic exam, ultrasound, expanded antibiotics Urgent
Tubo-Ovarian Abscess (TOA)
Timeframe: Long-term
Likelihood: Medium
Action: Hospital admission, IV antibiotics, possible drainage Urgent
Ectopic Pregnancy
Timeframe: Long-term
Likelihood: Medium
Action: Medical or surgical management as indicated Urgent
Infertility
Timeframe: Long-term
Likelihood: Medium (20% risk with untreated chlamydia)
Action: Complete workup including tubal patency testing Serious
Chronic Pelvic Pain
Timeframe: Long-term
Likelihood: Low
Action: Multidisciplinary pain management Manageable
Chronic Persistent Cervicitis
Timeframe: Long-term
Likelihood: Low
Action: Evaluate for non-infectious causes (Mycoplasma, irritants) Refer to gynecology
Prognosis
Excellent with appropriate treatment: Full recovery expected if treated promptly Good prognosis
Adolescents: High recurrence risk (up to 9.3%), especially with gonococcal infection Requires close follow-up
Subclinical PID: May lead to tubal-factor infertility even with adequate treatment Important counseling point
Prevention Strategies
Primary Prevention
Barrier protection: Consistent condom use Highly effective
Screening programs: May reduce PID by >50% Population health impact
Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP): Doxycycline within 72h for high-risk MSM/TGW CDC 2024 recommendation
Secondary Prevention
Partner treatment: Treat all recent partners Essential
Avoid irritants: No vaginal douches, spermicides Reduces recurrence

This algorithm is based on BMJ Best Practice guidelines (April 2025) and CDC STI Treatment Guidelines (2021)

Disclaimer: Clinical judgment should always prevail. Adapt treatment based on local resistance patterns, allergies, and individual patient factors.
