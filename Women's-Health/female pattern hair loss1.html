Female Pattern Hair Loss

Clinical Algorithm for Diagnosis and Management

Assessment

  • History Pattern (gradual thinning over crown/frontal scalp), timing, risk factors (age, family history, menopause), psychological impact
  • Examination Scalp inspection, pattern assessment, hair pull test, check for androgen excess features

Ludwig Scale

Stage 1

Minimal thinning

Stage 2

Moderate thinning

Stage 3

Severe thinning

Investigations

  • Routine Usually not needed for typical presentation
  • Consider if atypical Thyroid function (TSH, FT4), FBC, ferritin, vitamin D
  • If androgen excess Free-androgen index, prolactin (stop hormonal contraception 2 months prior)

Differential Diagnosis

  • Non-scarring alopecia Telogen effluvium, alopecia areata, traction alopecia, trichotillomania
  • Scarring alopecia Frontal fibrosing alopecia, tinea capitis, discoid lupus erythematosus, lichen planopilaris
  • Systemic causes Endocrine disorders (hypothyroidism, PCOS), nutritional deficiencies, medications

Management

  • First-line Minoxidil 5% (1mL twice daily to affected areas). Takes 3-6 months for effect, must continue indefinitely
  • Second-line Consider referral to dermatology for anti-androgens, low-level laser therapy, hair transplantation
  • Supportive care Psychological support, cosmetic options, lifestyle modifications (smoking cessation, stress management)

Red Flags

  • Sudden onset hair loss May indicate telogen effluvium or other underlying conditions
  • Scarring, inflammation, or pain Suggests scarring alopecia requiring urgent referral
  • Signs of androgen excess Hirsutism, virilization - requires endocrine evaluation
  • Severe psychological distress May require mental health support

Prognosis

  • Progression Progressive but variable rate; women rarely develop complete baldness
  • Treatment response Better in younger patients with shorter duration of hair loss
  • Discontinuation Leads to reversal of benefits within 3-6 months; rebound shedding may occur
