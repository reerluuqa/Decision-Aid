📋 Approaching Dyspareunia

GP Quick Reference – Reviewed June 2024 | Source: BMJ 2018;361:k2341

Prevalence: ~7.5% of sexually active women
Definition Causes Presentation Assessment Examination Investigation Management Psychosexual Summary

⚡ Key Points at a Glance

  • Dyspareunia may result from a combination of psychological, physical and social issues.
  • Physical dyspareunia may be superficial or deep; psychosexual issues may be harder to categorise.
  • In assessment: define the sexual problem → identify physical cause → gather psychosexual info.
  • Examination is important but NOT absolutely necessary if psychosexual cause is most likely – don't cause pain!
  • Tailor management to likely aetiology and issues identified.
  • Psychosexual counselling aims to aid patient understanding through receptive listening & reflective questioning.

Basics Definition & Implications

Dyspareunia = pain caused by any kind of vaginal penetration – not just intercourse. The term 'painful sex' is heteronormative and narrow.

Wider Implications for the Patient

💔 Limits ability to have sexual intimacy with anyone.
🩸 Prevents use of tampons / moon cups – reduces period management options.
🔬 May stop her tolerating or attending smear tests.
🏥 May be unable to undergo gynaecological examinations or procedures.
👶 May worry about ability to have a baby, may choose not to or avoid having children.
😞 May feel she is not 'normal'.

Differential Causes of Dyspareunia

⚠️ Physical and psychological issues often co-exist. Psychosexual causes are less easily categorised.
🔶 Superficial Dyspareunia 🔴 Deep Dyspareunia
  • Recurrent candida
  • Herpes
  • Bartholin's abscess / cyst
  • Vulval dermatoses (dermatitis / lichen sclerosus)
  • Postnatal 'atrophy' (low oestrogen – especially breastfeeding)
  • Vaginismus (primary or secondary)
  • Postmenopausal vulvovaginal atrophy
  • Neuropathic pain / vulvodynia (e.g. after perineal/obstetric trauma)
  • Neurological disease
  • Vaginal structural abnormalities (septum, scarring)
  • Female genital mutilation
  • Malignancy or post-radiotherapy
  • Endometriosis Cardinal sign
  • Pelvic pathology (fibroids, cysts, malignancy)
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction (mechanical, anatomical, prolapse)
  • Pelvic congestion syndrome (postcoital ache – controversial)
  • Non-gynaecological:
    • Irritable bowel syndrome
    • Inflammatory bowel disease
    • Recurrent UTI
    • Bladder pain syndrome
  • Iatrogenic: post-surgical scarring, post-radiotherapy

Clues How Might a Patient Present?

  • Comes specifically with a history of painful intercourse.
  • Describes relationship problems resulting from (and/or contributing to) sexual difficulties.
  • Not attending for smear tests or intimate examinations.
  • Uses other 'calling cards':
🔍 Look out for 'calling card' presentations: depression, anxiety, bleeding issues, contraception problems — may reflect embarrassment or lack of confidence to disclose sexual issues.

History Assessment – Holistic Approach

Three goals: ① Define the sexual problem → ② Identify any medical/gynaecological problem → ③ Gather psychosexual information

① Define the Sexual Problem

  • Be sensitive, non-judgemental.
  • Clarify what the patient means by 'painful sex' – let her tell the story.
  • Onset & duration:
    • Sudden onset (especially after a trigger) → more likely psychosexual.
    • Gradual development → more characteristic of physical problem.
  • Where is the pain?
    • Around / inside the vaginal entrance at start of coitus → consider dermatosis, vaginismus, vulvodynia.
    • Pelvic pain during deeper penetration → consider endometriosis.
  • Relationship? Explore any issues voiced (grief, fear, loss, abuse).

② Identify Medical / Gynae Problem

  • Other vulval symptoms? (itching, burning, dysuria)
  • Any risk of STI?
  • Gynaecological symptoms? (dysmenorrhoea, abnormal bleeding)
  • Any traumatic obstetric experiences?

③ Gather Psychosexual Info – the LOFTI Approach

L Listen

Open-ended questions & brief silences. Listen to language used, tone of voice. What is said and not said?

O Observe

Body language & behaviour. Defensive? Childlike? Anxious? Inappropriate? Dissociative / disconnected?

F Feelings

Be aware of feelings in the room. Your feelings may reflect the patient's subconscious feelings.

T Thinking

What role are you playing? (parent, teacher?) What is her motivation? Why presenting now?

I Interpreting

Assess overall picture. How does the patient come across? How does this fit her story?

🚨 Dissociation (disconnection with self, feelings or body, or switching off from reality) can suggest a history of sexual abuse.

Exam What to Look for During Examination

⚠️ The aim is to obtain helpful information and NOT to cause further pain. Examination may not be necessary if a clear psychosexual cause is likely and examination may be traumatic.
  • 🖐️ Abdominal Palpation
    • May reveal a pelvic mass.
  • 👁️ Vulval Examination – Look for:
    • Dermatosis: erythema, scarring, fissuring, skin changes.
    • Vulvovaginal atrophy: shiny, delicate, thin/pale mucosa.
    • Female genital mutilation.
  • 🔍 Vaginal & Speculum Examination
    • Palpate for areas of tenderness (vaginal septum, endometriotic nodules).
    • Use appropriate-size speculum and lots of lubricant.
    • Consider swabs if indicated and smear if due.

Ix Further Investigation

If examination is normal, simple reassurance may be helpful – but may not be. This can be an opportunity to explore the patient's view of what is causing her pain.
  • Consider further investigation of positive examination findings (e.g. ultrasound, gynaecological or dermatological referral).
  • 🔬 Check serum ferritin in patients with pruritus vulvae (BMJ recommendation).

Rx Management of Dyspareunia

Individualise the management plan according to likely cause.

🟢 General Advice

  • Perineal massage (inert oil e.g. coconut) twice daily – helps reassociate with genitalia, reduces vulvodynia/vaginismus.
  • Vulval health measures: avoid irritants (soaps, synthetic wipes/sanitary products). Use simple emollients for washing.
  • Consider lubricants and topical analgesics (e.g. 5% lidocaine ointment) for intercourse.

🔵 Treatment by Specific Condition

  • Vulvovaginal atrophy → Offer topical oestrogens.
  • Vulval dermatitis / dermatosis → Prescribe suitable-potency topical steroid + recommend emollient use.
  • Vulvodynia → May require multidisciplinary input (psychosexual counselling, physiotherapy, pain management).
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction (muscle overactivity/tightness, pelvic organ prolapse, chronic pelvic pain) → Refer to women's health physiotherapy (NG210, 2021).

Approach Psychosexual Approach – Tips

This is different from the traditional healthcare model. The professional's role is to facilitate patient understanding through receptive listening and reflective questioning, not to diagnose and prescribe.
  • Explore background and timing of the problem to find clues regarding aetiology.
  • Reflect back thoughts or emotions the patient voices, e.g.:
    "You seem very angry when you talk about your labour."
    "I wonder what made you feel so tense when I offered you an examination."
  • Avoid being overly reassuring or trying to 'correct' beliefs – explore them instead.
