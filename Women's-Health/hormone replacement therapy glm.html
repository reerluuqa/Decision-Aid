HRT Clinical Companion

Immediate Action: Women with a uterus must NOT take oestrogen-only HRT (High Endometrial Cancer Risk). Add cyclical progestogen or use continuous combined HRT.

Primary Indication

Relief of Vasomotor Symptoms (VMS) (hot flushes/night sweats) affecting 80% of women. 25% report severe impact on QoL.

< 3 months Time to maximum benefit

Critical VTE Risk

Oral HRT: Doubles risk of Venous Thromboembolism.

Action: Offer Transdermal HRT (standard therapeutic dose) for women with BMI ≥ 30 or other risk factors.

Core Indications

  • Vasomotor Symptoms (VMS): Hot flushes and night sweats (lasts >7 yrs for >50% women).
  • Genitourinary Syndrome (GSM): Atrophic vaginitis, urogenital atrophy.
  • Under 45 Menopause: High risk of osteoporosis.
  • Under 40 (POI): Continue until ~50 years (natural menopause age).

Treatment Types

Oestrogen (+ Progestogen):
For women with a uterus to prevent endometrial hyperplasia.

Tibolone:
Post-menopausal use. Combines oestrogenic, progestogenic, and weak androgenic activity.

Raloxifene:
For osteoporosis only (does NOT reduce VMS).

When NOT to use (Contra-indications)

  • Pregnancy / Breastfeeding
  • Undiagnosed vaginal bleeding
  • Current / history of breast cancer (refer to specialist)
  • History of VTE (unless anticoagulated)
  • Active arterial disease
  • Severe active liver disease

VMS Reduction

Most effective treatment available.

80% Report symptoms alleviated

Bone Health

Reduces risk of fragility fractures.

Benefit maintained during treatment but decreases once stopped.

Quality of Life

Improves sleep, muscle aches/pains, and mood.

Consider for low mood arising from menopause (CBT may also help).

GSM Improvement

Topical oestrogen improves urinary symptoms.

Vaginal atrophy reduced, pH normalized.

Cardiovascular

Starting HRT <60 or within 10 years of menopause may reduce risk of CHD and all-cause mortality (BMS view).

*Note: NICE discussion aid suggests 1 extra case of CHD per 1000 women over 5 years.

Breast Cancer Risk (NICE 2024 Update): Extra cases over 20 years (from age 50):
1 in 50 (Continuous Combined) | 1 in 70 (Sequential) | 1 in 200 (Oestrogen Only).

Venous Thromboembolism (VTE)

Oral: Higher Risk Transdermal: Safe

Recommendation: Use Transdermal HRT for women with BMI ≥ 30.

Stroke & CHD

Baseline Risk: Depends on cardiovascular factors.

NICE 2024: CHD risk may not increase with combined HRT, but risk of stroke is slightly increased.

Recommendation: Transdermal preferred for arterial risk factors.

Ovarian Cancer

Meta-analysis (2015) showed increased risk even with <5 years use.

1 in 1,000 Extra ovarian cancer users
1 in 1,700 Extra ovarian cancer deaths

Dementia

Risk slightly increased if HRT started at age 65+.

Context: Limited relevance to UK practice as starting >65 is uncommon.

Continuous Combined 1 per 50 users Highest relative risk among HRT types.
Sequential Combined 1 per 70 users Common for perimenopausal women.
Oestrogen Only 1 per 200 users Lowest risk. BMS states 'little or no change' risk.
Vaginal Oestrogen None observed Safe for women with history of breast cancer (usually).

Contraception & HRT

HRT is NOT a contraceptive.
  • Aged <50: Fertile for 2 years after last period.
  • Aged ≥50: Fertile for 1 year after last period.
  • Women 50+: Do NOT prescribe Combined Oral Contraceptive Pill.
  • Combo Option: Oestrogen HRT + Levonorgestrel IUS (Mirena) is optimal.

Follow-Up Schedule

  • Initial Review: 3 months after starting or changing HRT. Check effectiveness & side-effects.
  • Routine Review: At least annually thereafter.
  • Critical Check: Ensure women with uterus are not taking unopposed oestrogen.
  • Duration: Topical oestrogen for GSM usually continued long-term.

Quick Management Actions

Safe Action

Switch to Transdermal: If BMI rises or VTE risk factors develop.

Safe Action

Add Progestogen: Switching from sequential to continuous combined after 1-5 years removes endometrial cancer risk.

Monitor

Blood Pressure: HRT may have beneficial effect compared to combined contraceptive pill.
