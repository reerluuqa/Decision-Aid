Trichomoniasis – One‑Page Clinical Algorithm

At‑a‑glance flow
1 Suspect from symptoms / risks

3 Treat + screen for other STIs
4 Treat partners (current + 4 w)

6 Review; test‑of‑cure if symptomatic

If specialist referral declined/unavailable, manage in primary care and seek advice for complex scenarios (pregnancy, lactation, allergy, HIV with complications).

1 · Suspect & Assess

Clinical picture & risk assessment

  • Women: vaginal discharge (frothy yellow‑green in 10–30%), vulval itching, dysuria, odour; ± vulvitis/vaginitis, strawberry cervix (cervicitis).
  • Men: often asymptomatic; urethral discharge (20–60%), dysuria, frequency, irritation; rarely balanitis/balanoposthitis.
  • Risk factors: multiple partners in last year, inconsistent condom use, concurrent GC/CT (women), bacterial vaginosis.

Up to 50% of women and 15–50% of men can be asymptomatic.

2 · Examination & Testing

What to do now

  • Women: Abdomen PRN; inspect vulva; speculum: look for frothy yellow‑green discharge ± fishy odour; strawberry cervix may be seen; test vaginal pH — >4.5 suggests TV.
  • Sampling: High‑vaginal swab (posterior fornix) for NAAT (preferred) ± microscopy/culture if available. Use Amies with charcoal; refrigerate if delay (≤48 h at 4°C).
  • Self‑swab: If microscopy not done, a self‑administered vaginal swab for NAAT is recommended.
  • Men: Urethral or self‑taken penile‑meatal swab for NAAT; signs may be absent despite symptoms of urethritis.
  • Also test for: CT, GC, HIV, syphilis ± hepatitis if high risk.
3 · Management (Primary care if GUM unavailable)

Treatment choices

Adults (not pregnant/breastfeeding)

  • Metronidazole 400–500 mg PO BD × 7 days
  • Alternative: Metronidazole 2 g PO single dose
  • Confirmed metronidazole allergy → seek GUM advice

Pregnancy / Breastfeeding

  • Symptomatic: Metronidazole 400 mg PO BD × 7 days
  • Avoid 2 g single dose during pregnancy/breastfeeding
  • Asymptomatic pregnancy: seek specialist advice
  • Inform midwife/obstetrician where relevant

People with HIV

  • Metronidazole 500 mg PO BD × 7 days
  • Allergy → seek GUM advice

General steps

  • Provide written info; discuss complications & recurrence risk
  • Offer full STI screen (if not already done)
  • Arrange follow‑up to review symptoms and contact tracing
4 · Partner Management

Treat & screen partners

  • Treat all current partners and any partners in the past 4 weeks.
  • Treat simultaneously to prevent reinfection.
  • Offer CT/GC/HIV/syphilis testing to partners.
5 · Advice to Patients

Counselling pearls

  • Abstain from sex for ≥1 week after starting treatment and until both patient and partners have completed therapy and follow‑up.
  • Possible metronidazole side‑effects (GI upset, metallic taste); avoid alcohol (disulfiram‑like reaction possible) during therapy and for ≥48 h after last dose.
  • Provide links/leaflets (NHS, BASHH).
6 · Follow‑Up

When & what to review

  • Assess persistent or recurrent symptoms → consider treatment failure or reinfection.
  • Confirm contact tracing completed; review STI results.
  • Test of cure only if symptoms persist or recur.
7 · If Treatment Failure

Before escalating

  • Check adherence and vomiting of doses; review drug interactions.
  • Exclude reinfection; ensure partners were treated concurrently.
  • Reconsider diagnosis; rule out alternative causes of discharge/urethritis.
  • Offer repeat testing (leave ≥48 h after finishing initial treatment).
  • Repeat 7‑day metronidazole (400–500 mg BD) if not pregnant/breastfeeding.
  • Refer/seek GUM advice if repeat regimen fails, if pregnant/breastfeeding, or in confirmed allergy.
Safety · Prescribing Notes (Metronidazole)

Contraindications / cautions & interactions

Contraindications & cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to metronidazole or other nitroimidazoles.
  • Cockayne syndrome: risk of severe hepatotoxicity → use only if essential; monitor LFTs before, during, and after.
  • Severe CNS disease (risk of neurological aggravation).
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: consider ~one‑third daily dose given once daily.

Key interactions

  • Alcohol: possible disulfiram‑like reaction → avoid during and for ≥48 h post‑course.
  • Warfarin (↑ anticoagulation) → monitor INR, adjust dose.
  • Lithium (↑ levels) → consider dose reduction; monitor levels, creatinine, electrolytes.
  • Phenytoin/phenobarbital (↑ MTZ metabolism) → effect may be reduced.
  • Ciclosporin (↑ levels) → monitor drug level & creatinine.
  • Fluorouracil (↑ toxicity), busulfan (↑ toxicity) → monitor closely/avoid high doses.
  • Ergot derivatives (risk of ergotism) → avoid/monitor; stop if symptoms.
  • Live cholera & typhoid vaccines → avoid within recommended windows.

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

  • Widely used in pregnancy; data do not indicate ↑ congenital malformation; manufacturer advises use only if essential.
  • Avoid short high‑dose (2 g single) regimens in pregnancy/breastfeeding.
  • Significant drug passes into milk and may impart bitter taste.

For pregnant or breastfeeding patients needing further therapy after first‑line, seek GUM/obstetric advice.

Reminders

Also consider / rule out

  • BV, vulvovaginal candidiasis, CT/GC, PID
  • Non‑infective causes: foreign body, irritant/allergic vaginitis, GSM, dermatological and malignant causes as per history/exam
Documentation

Record

  • Symptoms, duration, sexual history, partner details (last 4 weeks)
  • Examination findings, pH, tests sent and results
  • Regimen, counselling given, partner notification plan
Practical

Logistics

  • Mark lab request “suspected trichomoniasis”; ensure transport & storage conditions are met
  • If microscopy unavailable → self vaginal swab NAAT (women) / penile‑meatal NAAT (men)
