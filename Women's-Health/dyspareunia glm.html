Dyspareunia Clinical Guide

Last Updated: Jan 2025 • Peer Reviewed • Est. 7 min read
CLINICAL RED FLAGS
Suspected PID
Post-menopausal Bleed
Sexual Assault
Postpartum Trauma
Quick Tip

Always consider a biopsychosocial approach. Psychological factors often coexist with physical pathology.

Definition & Basics
GPPPD Note
Superficial Pain at vulva/introitus or lower vagina. Begins on penetration.
Deep Pain in upper vagina/deep pelvis. During/after thrusting.

DSM-5 Note: Dyspareunia and Vaginismus are now classified as Genito-Pelvic Pain/Penetration Disorder (GPPPD).

Differential Diagnosis

Vaginismus

Marked tightening/tensing of pelvic muscles.

Vulvodynia

Chronic vulvar pain without clear cause.

Atrophy

Menopause-related thinning/dryness.

Infection

Thrush, STIs, Herpes (cervical).

Dermatology

Lichen sclerosus, eczema, psoriasis.

Trauma

Episiotomy scars, FGM, birth injury.

Endometriosis

Common cause of deep dyspareunia.

PID

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease.

Fibroids / Masses

Ovarian cysts or neoplasm.

Adhesions

Post-surgical scar tissue.

GI/Bladder

IBS, Interstitial Cystitis, UTI.

Retroverted Uterus

Structural position causing impact pain.

Psychosocial

History of abuse, anxiety, fear.

Dryness

Lack of arousal, breastfeeding, Sjögren's.

Pelvic Floor Dysfunction

Hypertonic muscles affecting sensation.

Clinical Assessment
Key Step

History Taking

Onset: Recent vs. Lifelong?
Location: Superficial vs. Deep?
Timing: Before, during, or after sex?
History of abuse/assault?
Obstetric history (Episiotomy)?
Menopause status / Breastfeeding?

Examination Tips

Explain steps & ensure consent.
Use patient-held mirror.
Single-finger vaginal exam initially.
Cotton-bud test for vulvodynia.
Check for cervical excitation (PID).

Sensitive Exam Approach

Patients with vaginismus may not tolerate vaginal exam. Reassurance and patient control are paramount to minimize anxiety.

Investigations
  • Swabs: STI and non-STI infections.
  • Urine: Dipstick/MC&S (UTI).
  • Laparoscopy: If endometriosis/adhesions suspected.
  • Imaging: Ultrasound for masses/fibroids.
Management Plan
General Measures
Multidisciplinary approach (Psychosexual, Physio, CBT).
Technique
Modify positions, increase foreplay/lubrication.
Atrophy
Vaginal estrogens (safe) or SERMs (Ospemifene).
Infection
Treat underlying pathogen (Antibiotics/Antifungals).
Skin Disorders
Emollients, cotton underwear, avoid irritants.
Surgery
Last resort (e.g. for fibroids, adhesions, or FGM repair).
