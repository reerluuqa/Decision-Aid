Postnatal Depression

Updated: Jan 12, 2024

Urgent Action Required

  • Psychiatric Referral/Emergency Assessment if risk of self-harm or harm to child.
  • Exclude Postnatal Psychosis (mania, mixed episode, hallucinations/delusions about baby).
  • Exclude Bipolar Disorder before starting antidepressants (risk of precipitating mania).
  • Child Protection: Consider involvement if significant risk of neglect or harm.

Initial Screening (Who to Ask)

Routine

NICE (UK) Recommends asking these 2 questions at first contact and postnatally:

1. During the past month, have you often been bothered by feeling down, depressed, or hopeless?

2. During the past month, have you often been bothered by having little interest or pleasure in doing things?

If YES to either, proceed to full assessment using EPDS or PHQ-9.

Validated Tools

Tools
EPDS Score >12 suggests major depression (PPV 57%).
Score >10 to minimize false negatives.
PHQ-9 Operating characteristics nearly identical to EPDS.
MDQ / Highs Screen for Bipolar before meds. MDQ recommended by ACOG.

Risk Assessment: Self-Harm & Baby

Critical

Ask Compassionately:

  • Feel life is not worth living?
  • Thoughts of harming the baby?
  • Frequency of thoughts?
  • Do they have a plan? (Lethality/Means)

Intrusive Thoughts:

Common (anxiety). Unwanted thoughts about harming baby are usually not acted upon.

Red Flag: Thoughts are experienced as comforting or accompanied by psychosis.

Immediate Actions:

  • Do not leave alone if psychosis imminent.
  • Assess supports (partner, family).
  • Emergency eval if intent/plan present.

Key Diagnostic Factors

Symptoms
Depressed Mood Abnormal for person, present most of day, uninfluenced by circumstances.
Anhedonia Loss of interest/pleasure in normally pleasurable activities.
Biological Sx Sleep disturbance (esp. early waking), appetite changes, low energy, psychomotor agitation/retardation.
Cognitive Poor concentration, excessive guilt, loss of confidence/self-esteem.
Anxiety/Bonding High anxiety common. Difficulty bonding/feeling emotional numbness.

Risk Factors

Etiology
Factor Association
Past History Strongest predictor. Past mood/anxiety disorder, especially antenatal depression (5x risk).
Psychosocial Recent stressful life events, poor social support, partner violence, low income.
Medication Discontinuation of psychopharmacological treatments.
Sleep Sleep deprivation and maternal fatigue linked to new-onset symptoms.
Obstetric Complications of pregnancy/birth (small but significant effect).

Classification & Timeline

Definitions

Postnatal Blues

Transient (30-80%). Mood swings, tearfulness, anxiety.
Onset: 2-3 days postpartum.
Resolve: By 2 weeks.

Postnatal Depression

Clinical depression causing impairment.
Duration: 3-6 months avg (can last years).
Defined here as up to 12 months.

Postnatal Psychosis

Psychiatric Emergency.
Acute onset of mania/depression with psychosis.
Incidence: 1 in 1000.

Treatment Algorithm Overview

Action

Mild to Moderate

First-line treatment.

  • Psychological Therapy (CBT, IPT).
  • Social support interventions.
  • Watchful waiting if very mild (rare in clinical practice).

Severe / Declines Therapy / Ineffective

Escalation required.

  • Antidepressants recommended (SSRIs/SNRIs typically).
  • Consider breastfeeding safety (most compatible).
  • Monitor closely for side effects and adherence.

Non-Response / High Risk

Specialist care.

  • Psychiatric Referral.
  • Review diagnosis (exclude Bipolar).
  • Consider combination therapy (Meds + Psychotherapy).
  • Higher level of care (hospital) if suicide risk.

Prevention

Primary

Identify high-risk women (e.g., history of depression). Provide enhanced psychosocial support.

Secondary

Early detection via screening questions to minimize duration of untreated episode.

Complications

Risks
  • Mother: Reduced quality of life, chronic depression, suicide (leading cause of maternal death).
  • Child: Impaired behavioral, emotional, cognitive outcomes. Bonding issues.
  • Family: Relationship strain.

Patient Discussion & Follow-up

  • Reassurance: Explain this is common and treatable. Reduce fear of baby being taken away (unless significant risk).
  • Monitoring: Regular review of symptoms and medication side effects.
  • Prognosis: Most recover, but risk of recurrence exists in subsequent pregnancies.

Epidemiology

Data
  • Prevalence: ~10-20% of new mothers.
  • High Income: Lower (6.9% - 12.9%).
  • Low/Middle Income: Higher (~20%).
  • Comorbidity: Often concurrent with anxiety disorders.
  • Onset: Often starts during pregnancy (perinatal depression).

Pathophysiology & Theory

Theory

Exact cause unknown. Likely interaction of:

  • Hormonal: Fluctuations (estrogen, progesterone) post-delivery. Subgroup may have abnormal sensitivity.
  • Genetic: Heritability ~38-54%. Some unique genetic components distinct from general depression.
  • Immune: Exaggerated inflammatory response post-labour.
  • Psychosocial: Stress of major life transition and sleep deprivation.
