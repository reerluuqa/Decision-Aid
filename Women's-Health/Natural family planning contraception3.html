NICE CKS: Natural Family Planning

A One-Page Clinical Reference for Primary Care

Last Revised: June 2021 | Target: NHS UK Primary Care

Overview

This guide summarizes the two main natural family planning methods:

  • Fertility Awareness Methods (FAM): Tracking fertility signs to avoid pregnancy.
  • Lactational Amenorrhoea Method (LAM): Postpartum contraception via breastfeeding.
Note: These methods do not protect against STIs. Refer to trained practitioners (via FertilityUK.org) for FAM instruction.

Fertility Awareness Methods (FAM)

Key Fertility Indicators (Use in Combination)

  • Cervical Secretions: Wet, clear, slippery = fertile. Avoid intercourse or use barrier method until 3 dry days.
  • Basal Body Temperature (BBT): Sustained rise for ≥3 days post-ovulation = end of fertile window.
  • Calendar Method: Estimate ovulation (12–16 days before menses); fertile window ~8–9 days.
  • Cervical Changes: High, soft, open cervix = fertile. Not reliable alone.

Efficacy

Perfect Use

1–9% failure per year

Typical Use

24% failure per year

Advantages

  • No hormones or devices
  • No physical side effects
  • Culturally/religiously acceptable
  • Immediate reversibility
  • Promotes fertility awareness

Disadvantages

  • High user dependency
  • Requires daily tracking and training
  • Not suitable for irregular cycles
  • No STI protection
  • Some tools may be costly
Clinical Advice: Refer to trained FAM instructor (FertilityUK.org). Avoid use in postpartum (non-breastfeeding), perimenopause, or if poor compliance is anticipated.

Lactational Amenorrhoea Method (LAM)

All 3 Criteria Must Be Met:

Criterion Details
Amenorrhoea No vaginal bleeding after 56 days postpartum
Full/Near-Full Breastfeeding ≥85% breast milk; ≤4 hrs between feeds (day), ≤6 hrs (night)
Time Since Birth Less than 6 months postpartum

Efficacy

Perfect Use

0.5% failure in 6 months

Typical Use

2% failure in 6 months

Advantages

  • Highly effective if criteria met
  • No cost or devices
  • Encourages exclusive breastfeeding
  • No action needed during intercourse
  • No hormonal side effects

Disadvantages

  • Only effective for first 6 months
  • Fails with mixed feeding or reduced frequency
  • Convenience issues with frequent feeding
  • No STI protection
Clinical Advice: Counsel on transitioning to another method before 6 months. If any criterion fails, start alternative contraception immediately.

Withdrawal Method (Coitus Interruptus)

Not recommended as a primary method due to high failure rate.

Perfect Use

4% failure/year

Typical Use

22% failure/year

Pre-ejaculate may contain sperm. Avoid reliance without backup.

Clinical Management Summary

  • Assessment: Apply WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC).
  • Information to Provide:
    • Efficacy vs. LARC and other methods
    • Need for training (FAM) or strict adherence (LAM)
    • No STI protection → advise condoms if at risk
  • Referrals:
    • FAM: Refer to trained instructor via FertilityUK.org
    • LAM: Support breastfeeding continuation and plan next method

When to Switch Contraceptive Method

Method Trigger to Switch
FAM Desire for more effective method, difficulty tracking, irregular cycles, poor compliance
LAM ≥6 months postpartum, return of menses, reduced breastfeeding, introduction of solids/formula
