Postnatal Depression

BMJ Best Practice Quick Reference • Last updated Jan 2024

1. Screening Entrance

All postnatal women

FIRST STEP

Two-Question Screen:

1

During past month, often bothered by feeling down/depressed/hopeless?

2

Little interest/pleasure in doing things?

If YES to either → Proceed to EPDS

2. Able to look forward with enjoyment

2. Differential Dx

Rule out first

Baby Blues

Day 2-3 → resolves Day 14

Self-limiting
  • • Mood swings, tearfulness
  • • No impairment of function
  • • No suicidal ideation

Postnatal Depression

Weeks 2-12 (up to 12 months)

Treat
  • • Persistent low mood, anhedonia
  • • Functional impairment
  • • Anxiety prominent feature

Postnatal Psychosis

First week (often Day 3)

EMERGENCY
  • • Delusions, hallucinations, confusion
  • • Rapid deterioration
  • • High risk harm to baby/self

Non-directive counselling

Adjunct: Consider antidepressant if previous severe depression or no response

Moderate

EPDS 13-19

CBT or IPT (if preference/non-response)

OR

Antidepressant (SSRI)

EPDS ≥20

⚠️ Screen for bipolar before antidepressants

Usually first-line

+ Psychological Therapy

CBT/IPT if limited response

ECT: Consider for severe suicidality, psychosis, or treatment resistance

Pharmacological Safety in Lactation

Click rows for details
Medication Safety Profile Notes Priority
Sertraline EXCELLENT Extensive safety data; low infant exposure 1st CHOICE
Paroxetine VERY GOOD Low transfer to breast milk 2nd CHOICE
Citalopram/Escitalopram GOOD Monitor infant for sedation Alternative
Venlafaxine/Duloxetine (SNRIs) CAUTION Limited data; seek specialist advice If SSRIs fail
Monitoring: All breastfed infants exposed to psychotropics require monitoring for sedation, irritability, sleep/feeding changes. Avoid St John's Wort in breastfeeding.

Before Starting Antidepressants

  • Screen for bipolar disorder (MDQ)
  • Risk assessment (self-harm/baby)
  • Discuss risks/benefits of treatment vs. no treatment
