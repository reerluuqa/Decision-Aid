DSM-5-TR Classification

üìñ Diagnostic Requirements

All sexual dysfunctions require 3 criteria:

Minimum 6 months duration
75-100% of sexual encounters
Clinically significant distress

Female Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder (SIAD)

1

Lack of/reduced sexual interest/arousal manifested by ‚â•3 of:

  • Absent/reduced interest in sexual activity
  • Absent/reduced sexual/erotic thoughts or fantasies
  • No/reduced initiation of sexual activity, unreceptive to partner's attempts
  • Absent/reduced sexual excitement/pleasure during sexual activity
  • Absent/reduced interest/arousal in response to sexual cues
  • Absent/reduced genital/non-genital sensations during sexual activity

Female Orgasmic Disorder (FOD)

2

‚â•1 of the following symptoms in almost all (75-100%) sexual encounters:

  • Marked delay in, marked infrequency of, or absence of orgasm
  • Markedly reduced intensity of orgasmic sensations

Genito-Pelvic Penetration Pain Disorder (GPPPD)

3

Not covered in detail in this algorithm - see separate guidance for:

  • Vaginismus
  • Dyspareunia
  • Provoked vestibulodynia

Persistent Genital Arousal Disorder/Genito-Pelvic Dysesthesia (PGAD/GPD)

4

Not in DSM-5-TR but recognized by ISSWSH:

  • Persistent/recurrent, unwanted/intrusive, distressing genital arousal sensations
  • Duration ‚â•3 months
  • May include other dysesthesia (burning, itching, pain)
  • Not associated with concomitant sexual interest/thoughts/fantasies

Psychological Treatment Modalities

üß† Psycho-education

  • Didactic information about sexual function
  • Challenging myths and misconceptions
  • Socratic questioning to evoke emotion
  • Bibliotherapy (self-help materials)
  • Effective for arousal and desire disorders
First-line

üîÑ Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

  • Identify and challenge maladaptive thoughts
  • Reduce problematic behaviors
  • Improve affect and sexual satisfaction
  • Large effect size for sexual desire
  • Moderate effect on sexual satisfaction
Evidence-based

ü§≤ Sex Therapy

  • Sensate focus exercises
  • Reduce performance anxiety
  • Improve communication and feedback
  • Original efficacy rates: 65-95%
  • 5% relapse rate at 5 years
Highly Effective

üßò Mindfulness-Based Approaches

  • Stay present, avoid distractions
  • Reduce self-criticism and evaluation
  • Improve genital-subjective arousal concordance
  • Beneficial for abuse history
  • Enhances relationship happiness
Emerging Evidence

Specific Treatments by Disorder

üî• Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder (SIAD)

üíä Adjunctive Pharmacology

  • Vaginal oestrogen: For vulvovaginal atrophy
  • Vaginal DHEA: Approved in US for dyspareunia
  • Systemic oestrogen: If other menopausal symptoms

‚ö†Ô∏è Investigational (Use with Caution)

  • Testosterone: Only if marked androgen deficiency
  • Flibanserin: US-approved but limited efficacy
  • Bremelanotide: Questionable benefit, adverse effects

üåü Female Orgasmic Disorder (FOD)

‚úã Behavioral Treatments

  • Directed self-stimulation: 80-90% success for lifelong FOD
  • Coital alignment technique (CAT): Direct clitoral pressure
  • Vibrostimulation: Enhanced stimulation options
  • Mindfulness training: Stay focused on sensations

üíä Substance/Medication-Induced Sexual Dysfunction

Prognosis & Treatment Response

üìà Psychological Treatments

  • Meta-analysis evidence: Clear benefit for orgasmic dysfunction and low desire
  • CBT and sex therapy: Highly effective for lifelong FOD
  • Mindfulness-based approaches: Growing evidence base
  • Couple-based interventions: Most effective post-cancer treatment
üíä
Placebo Response: Marked placebo response in pharmacological studies (up to 30-40%) indicates that positive attitude and anticipation are key ingredients for improvement
