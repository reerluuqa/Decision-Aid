UKMEC Contraceptive Eligibility Criteria

The UK Medical Eligibility Criteria (UKMEC) provide evidence-based guidance for prescribing safe and suitable contraceptive options for women with medical conditions or undergoing treatments. These guidelines assist clinicians in making informed decisions while considering individual patient factors.

Step 1: Patient Assessment

Key Assessment Questions

Comprehensive medical history is essential before prescribing any contraceptive method.
  • Age and parity: Current age, number of pregnancies and deliveries
  • Menstrual history: Cycle regularity, flow, dysmenorrhea
  • Medical conditions: Current and past illnesses, surgeries
  • Cardiovascular risk: Smoking status, blood pressure, migraine history
  • Metabolic conditions: Diabetes, BMI, history of bariatric surgery
  • Reproductive health: Breastfeeding status, postpartum period, STI risk
  • Malignancy history: Current or past breast cancer, other hormonally-sensitive cancers
  • Medications: Current prescriptions, over-the-counter drugs, supplements

High-Priority Screening

These factors require immediate attention as they may be UKMEC Category 4 (absolute contraindications):
Current breast cancer with CHC or IUS
Migraine with aura using CHC
SLE with antiphospholipid antibodies + CHC
Smoker >15/day & ≥35y using CHC
Major surgery with immobilisation + CHC
BP ≥160/100 mmHg with CHC

BMI Assessment

BMI significantly influences contraceptive choice, particularly for combined hormonal contraception:

BMI Range UKMEC Category for CHC Clinical Implications
BMI < 30 1 No restrictions
BMI ≥ 30 2 Benefits outweigh risks
BMI ≥ 35 3 Exercise caution; consider alternatives
Step 2: UKMEC Category Classification

Understanding UKMEC Categories

The UKMEC system categorizes contraceptive methods based on safety profiles for specific medical conditions:

1
No Restriction

A condition for which there is no restriction for the use of the contraceptive method.

Always usable
2
Advantages Outweigh Risks

A condition where the advantages outweigh the theoretical or proven risks of using the method.

Broadly usable
3
Risks Outweigh Advantages

A condition where risks generally outweigh advantages. Use is not generally recommended unless more appropriate methods are unavailable.

Counsel/caution
4
Unacceptable Health Risk

A condition that represents an unacceptable health risk if the contraceptive method is used.

Do not use

Category Interpretation Rules

  • Categories are not additive: UKMEC categories for different conditions do not sum up
  • Multiple Category 2: If multiple UKMEC 2 categories coexist, use clinical judgement
  • Multiple Category 3: If multiple UKMEC 3 conditions exist, method generally becomes UKMEC 4
  • Guidance, not replacement: UKMEC guides but does not replace clinical judgement

UKMEC Evolution

2000: WHO developed the WHOMEC (WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria)

2006: First UKMEC published by FSRH Clinical Effectiveness Unit

2016: Latest UKMEC introduced new condition categories and reorganized method presentation

New conditions added: Bariatric surgery, organ transplant, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, rheumatoid arthritis

Postpartum

0-21 days: CHC contraindicated (VTE risk)
Progestogen-only: Safe from day 1
IUD/IUS: Can be fitted from 4 weeks

Bariatric Surgery

UKMEC based on current BMI, not procedure
Oral methods may have reduced efficacy with malabsorptive procedures
LARC methods preferred

Genetic Risk

BRCA carriers: CHC UKMEC 3
Family history alone: UKMEC 1
Consider non-hormonal methods

Step 4: Follow-up & Implementation

Clinical Decision-Making Process

  1. 1 Assess patient characteristics: Age, BMI, smoking, medical history, medications
  2. 2 Identify contraindications: Check for UKMEC Category 4 conditions for each method
  3. 3 Evaluate cautions: Review UKMEC Category 3 conditions requiring careful consideration
  4. 4 Consider patient preferences: Efficacy, side effects, convenience, personal choice
  5. 5 Document decision: Record UKMEC categories considered and rationale for choice

Monitoring & Review

Injection Users
  • Review every 2 years for BMD effects
  • Consider stopping at age 50 years
  • Assess weight changes and metabolic parameters
CHC Users
  • Check blood pressure every 6-12 months
  • Annual weight and BMI assessment
  • Monitor for migraine development
IUD/IUS Users
  • Check threads after first period
  • Annual examination if indicated
  • Assess for infection risk with new partners
Postpartum
  • Assess breastfeeding status at 6 weeks
  • Evaluate VTE risk factors before CHC
  • Consider early IUD insertion benefits

Key Practice Points

LARC First

Consider Long-Acting Reversible Contraceptives (LARC) as first-line, especially for women where pregnancy would be high-risk.

Migraine Clarification

Distinguish migraine with aura (UKMEC 4 for CHC) from migraine without aura (UKMEC 2). Aura lasts 5-60 minutes with visual or neurological symptoms.

VTE Risk

BMI ≥30 increases VTE risk with CHC. At BMI ≥35, consider alternatives. No upper BMI limit for CHC if no other risk factors.

Timing Matters

Postpartum VTE risk highest in first 21 days. CHC contraindicated until 6 weeks if breastfeeding, 3 weeks if not.

Documentation Requirements

  • Medical history and risk factors assessed
  • UKMEC categories identified for each method discussed
  • Patient preferences and concerns documented
  • Risks, benefits, and alternatives discussed
  • Informed consent obtained
  • Follow-up plan established
