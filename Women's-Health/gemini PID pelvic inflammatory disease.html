1. Patient Presentation & Suspicion

Suspect PID in sexually active females with recent onset of:
  • Bilateral lower abdominal or pelvic pain
  • Deep dyspareunia
  • Abnormal vaginal bleeding (intermenstrual or postcoital)
  • Abnormal purulent vaginal or cervical discharge
Key Examination Findings: Cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness, uterine tenderness.

2. Immediate Assessment & Triage

Perform a pregnancy test in ALL suspected cases to exclude ectopic pregnancy.

ADMIT TO HOSPITAL IF ANY ARE PRESENT:
  • Clinically pregnant or confirmed ectopic pregnancy.
  • Severe systemic illness (fever, nausea, vomiting).
  • Palpable adnexal mass (suspected tubo-ovarian abscess).
  • Significant diagnostic uncertainty (e.g., appendicitis cannot be excluded).
  • Unable to tolerate or follow an outpatient oral antibiotic regimen.

3. Primary Care Management Pathway (No Red Flags)

Step 1: Investigations

Take vaginal swabs for Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, and M. genitalium (if available). DO NOT DELAY TREATMENT while awaiting results.

Step 2: Start Empirical Antibiotics IMMEDIATELY

Step 3: Provide Key Patient Advice

  • Sexual Abstinence: No intercourse until both patient and partner(s) complete treatment and are symptom-free.
  • IUD Management: Can remain in mild/moderate cases if improving within 72 hrs. Remove in severe cases or if not improving. Assess need for emergency contraception if removed.

4. Follow-Up & Partner Management

Timeline Action
Within 72 Hours Review patient for clinical improvement. If not improving, check compliance, reconsider diagnosis, consider admission. Review swab results and adjust antibiotics if needed.
Partner Management Advise that all sexual partners from the last 6 months must be contacted, tested, and treated. Refer partners to a sexual health service.
Test of Cure (TOC) Arrange 3-5 weeks post-treatment if Gonorrhoea or M. genitalium was diagnosed, symptoms persist, or poor compliance/partner treatment is suspected.
