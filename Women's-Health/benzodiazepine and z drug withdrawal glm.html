Benzodiazepine & Z-Drug Withdrawal

NICE CKS Clinical Reference | Last Revised: May 2024

Ages 16+

Exclusion Criteria (Do Not Use This Tool For):

Management of people taking benzodiazepines with illicit drugs, dependence on other drugs (including alcohol), overdose management, or dependence in neonates/children/pregnancy.

High Risk Factors
  • High dose & length of treatment (>4 weeks)
  • Use of high potency / short half-life (e.g., Lorazepam, Alprazolam)
  • History of alcohol/sedative dependence
  • Comorbid psychiatric/personality disorders
Complications of Long-term Use
  • Over-sedation, falls, accidents
  • Tolerance & Dependence
  • Cognitive effects (memory, reduced coping)
  • Depression (risk of suicidal tendencies)
  • Increased mortality risk
Common Withdrawal Symptoms
Benzodiazepines: Sweating, insomnia, tremor, palpitations, anxiety, panic, rarely seizures.

Z-Drugs: Sleep disturbance, anxiety, palpitations, perceptual disturbances (hypersensitivity).
Assessment Questions
  • Is the person willing to withdraw?
  • Is this a suitable time (stable life circumstances)?
  • Are symptoms of depression present? (Manage first)
  • Is there severe insomnia? (Consider non-drug Tx first)
  • Are medical problems well-controlled?
Suitable for Primary Care?
Yes, if:
  • Willing, committed, and compliant
  • Has adequate social support
  • No history of complicated drug withdrawal
  • Condition for prescription resolved or harms > benefits

CRITICAL WARNING

Do NOT stop abruptly. Unless serious adverse effects occur. Abrupt cessation can cause severe withdrawal, psychosis, or seizures. Slow tapering is mandatory.

Refer to Specialist
Seek specialist advice or referral if the patient has:
  • History of alcohol or other drug use/dependence
  • Concurrent severe medical or psychiatric disorder
  • Personality disorder
  • History of drug withdrawal seizures
  • Hepatic dysfunction (before switching to Diazepam)
Consider Switching to Diazepam

Switch to an approx. equivalent dose of diazepam, then taper down.


Consider for:
  • Short-acting potent benzodiazepines (Alprazolam, Lorazepam)
  • Preparations hard to dose in small increments
  • Difficulty withdrawing directly due to high dependency
*Avoid diazepam accumulation in hepatic dysfunction (use Oxazepam instead).

The Withdrawal Process

Step 1
Preparation
  • Shared decision making on schedule.
  • Agree on rate (can be 3 months to 1 year).
  • Reassure they are in control.
  • Address underlying anxiety/insomnia first.
Step 2
Tapering
  • Titrate according to symptom severity.
  • Persevere or delay next step if difficult.
  • If intolerable: Return to last tolerated dose.
  • Review frequently.
Step 3
Support
  • Offer CBT if needed.
  • Advise against alcohol/illicit drug substitution.
  • Manage anxiety: Relaxation techniques.
  • Consider Beta-blockers ONLY for severe physical symptoms if other measures fail.
If Depression Emerges
Consider suspending drug withdrawal until the depression resolves. Antidepressants may be considered if depression or panic disorder coexist.

Prognosis
  • Success: Withdrawal is possible in most people once problems are explained.
  • Timeline: Acute symptoms usually peak 3-14 days but can continue up to 6 weeks.
  • Protracted Syndrome (10-15%): Can last >1 year (anxiety, insomnia, cognitive impairment, motor symptoms).
  • Poorer Outcomes: Associated with higher dosage, rapid tapering, lower education, severe baseline anxiety.
Prevalence (England 2017-2018)
  • 1.4 million adults prescribed benzodiazepines.
  • 1 million adults prescribed z-drugs.
  • 120k on continuous benzos (5% of users).
  • Prescribing higher in women and older adults.
Prescribing Guidance
  • Max Duration: 4 weeks (including tapering phase).
  • Dose: Lowest effective dose.
  • Review: Regularly.
