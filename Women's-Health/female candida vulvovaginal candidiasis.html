Action Key Considerations

  • Frequency: Is it an isolated acute episode or recurrent (‚â•4 symptomatic episodes/year)?
  • Risk Factors: Recent antibiotics, uncontrolled diabetes, immunosuppression, pregnancy, local irritants.
  • Red Flags: Foul-smelling/purulent discharge (bacterial infection?), abnormal bleeding (gynaecological cancer?), urinary frequency/urgency (UTI?).
Examination
  • When to Examine: Routinely offer for recurrent infection, treatment failure, or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Signs of Severe Infection: Extensive erythema, oedema, vaginal fissuring, excoriation.
Investigations
  • Acute Infection: Not routinely needed if diagnosis is clear.
  • Recurrent/Treatment Failure/Uncertainty: High Vaginal Swab (HVS) for microscopy and culture.
  • Consider: HbA1c (if recurrent/severe), STI screen (if at risk).

‚ö†Ô∏è 4. Managing Treatment Failure

Definition: Symptoms have not resolved within 7-14 days of initial treatment.

Step Actions to Take
Step 1: Review & Reassess
  • Check patient compliance with initial treatment.
  • Consider if symptoms are due to irritation from topical treatment.
  • Re-examine and send HVS for culture with full speciation and sensitivity testing.
Step 2: Re-treat
  • Treat ongoing infection based on HVS results.
  • If poor compliance was the issue, prescribe an alternative preparation (e.g., oral instead of intravaginal).
Step 3: Refer Seek Specialist Advice or Refer if:
  • Diagnosis is uncertain or symptoms persist after a second course of treatment.
  • A non-albicans or azole-resistant Candida species is identified.
  • The woman has uncontrolled diabetes.

üí° 5. Key Patient Advice

  • Hygiene: Use simple emollients as a soap substitute. Avoid irritants like soaps, shower gels, vaginal douching, and tight-fitting synthetic clothing.
  • Contraception: Advise that topical imidazole preparations (creams/pessaries) may damage latex condoms and diaphragms.
  • Partners: Routine treatment of asymptomatic male sexual partners is not necessary. Manage suspected balanitis if present.
  • Over-the-Counter: Inform patients that many recommended treatments are available over-the-counter.
