Assessment & Initial Evaluation

Comprehensive history, examination & risk stratification

‚ñº

üéØ Key Principle

First, determine the patient's age group and pregnancy status. This determines the differential diagnosis and urgency of evaluation.

üìä Age-Based Classification

üë∂ Premenarchal (Before Menarche)

  • Neonatal uterine bleeding (days 3-5 of life) - normal physiological
  • After neonatal period: ALWAYS abnormal
  • Consider trauma, foreign body, infection, sexual abuse, malignancy
Sexual abuse must be considered

üå∏ Reproductive Age (Non-Pregnant)

  • Postcoital bleeding: cervical ectropion, polyps, cervicitis, cancer
  • Intermenstrual bleeding: hormonal, structural, malignant causes
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding: evaluate for underlying pathology

ü§∞ Reproductive Age (Pregnant)

  • First trimester: Ectopic pregnancy, miscarriage
  • Second/third trimester: Placental abruption, placenta praevia, preterm labor
Ectopic pregnancy is life-threatening

üëµ Postmenopausal

  • Any bleeding >12 months after last period is abnormal
  • >90% of endometrial cancers present with postmenopausal bleeding
  • 9% of women with postmenopausal bleeding have endometrial cancer
Urgent cancer investigation required

üìù History Taking

All Patients

  • Characterize bleeding: Frequency, volume, duration, pattern
  • Precipitating factors: Intercourse, trauma, medication
  • Associated symptoms: Pain, discharge, dizziness, shoulder pain

Pregnancy-Related Red Flags üö®

  • Unstable vital signs (hypotension, tachycardia)
  • Severe abdominal pain or acute abdomen
  • Shoulder tip pain (diaphragmatic irritation)
  • Cervical motion tenderness
  • Dizziness or syncope

Reproductive Age Women

  • Last menstrual period date
  • Contraception use and adherence
  • Sexual activity and STI risk
  • Previous cervical screening results
  • HPV vaccination/infection history

Postmenopausal Women

  • Exact date of last menstrual period
  • Hormone replacement therapy use
  • Risk factors: obesity, tamoxifen use, PCOS history
  • Family history of gynecological cancers

Prepubertal Girls üö®

  • Signs of puberty development (breast, pubic hair)
  • History of trauma (straddle injuries common)
  • Possible foreign body insertion
  • Behavioral changes suggesting abuse
  • Always consider possibility of sexual abuse

üî¨ Physical Examination

DO NOT perform digital vaginal examination if placenta praevia is suspected until ultrasound excludes it
Vital Signs

Hemodynamic stability assessment. Check BP, pulse, temperature in all patients with significant bleeding.

General Inspection

Vulva, perineum, anal area for lesions, fissures, or external sources of bleeding.

Speculum Examination

Identify cervical/vaginal lesions: polyps, ectropion, cancer, cervicitis, foreign bodies.

Bimanual Examination

Cervical motion tenderness, adnexal mass/tenderness, uterine size/mobility.

Abdominal Examination

Uterine size (pregnancy), tenderness, guarding, signs of intra-abdominal bleeding.

Special Considerations

Prepubertal girls rarely need internal exam; consider sedation/anesthesia. Follow abuse protocols if indicated.

üß™ Initial Investigations

Pregnancy Status (All Women of Reproductive Age)

  • Urinary hCG: Immediate bedside test
  • Serum hCG: Quantitative if pregnancy confirmed or diagnostic uncertainty
  • Serial hCG: 48-hour intervals to monitor trend (<50% drop suggests failing pregnancy, >50% rise suggests ongoing pregnancy)

Hematological & Baseline Tests

  • Full Blood Count: Assess anemia from blood loss
  • Blood Group & Rh Status: Essential for pregnant women (determines anti-D need)
  • Kleihauer-Betke Test: Quantifies feto-maternal hemorrhage

Infection Screening (When Indicated)

  • NAAT for Chlamydia & Gonorrhea: High sensitivity/specificity
  • Wet mount microscopy: Trichomonas, bacterial vaginosis, candidiasis
  • Vaginal pH testing: >4.5 suggests bacterial infection
  • HIV, Hepatitis B/C, Syphilis: Offer with any STI diagnosis

Endocrine Tests (Precocious Puberty)

  • Serum LH, FSH, Estrogen: Differentiate gonadotropin-dependent vs independent
  • Androgen panel: DHEA, DHEA-S
  • Bone age assessment: Left hand/wrist radiograph

üîç Imaging & Special Investigations

Transvaginal/Transabdominal Ultrasound (First-Line)

  • Pregnancy: Confirm location (intrauterine vs ectopic), viability, gestational age
  • Postmenopausal bleeding: Endometrial thickness ‚â§4mm has >99% negative predictive value for cancer
  • Structural lesions: Fibroids, polyps, ovarian masses, placenta location
  • Placental abruption: 96% specific but only 24% sensitive
Colposcopy

For persistent postcoital bleeding or suspicious cervical lesions. Allows directed biopsy of abnormal areas.

MRI

Assess myometrial invasion in endometrial cancer, parametrial involvement in cervical cancer.

CT Scan

Staging for suspected advanced gynecological cancers (pelvis, abdomen, nodes, liver metastases).

Endometrial Biopsy

Indicated in postmenopausal bleeding with thickened endometrium, risk factors, or recurrent bleeding.

Cervical Biopsy

Any suspicious cervical lesion visible on speculum or colposcopy examination.

Differential Diagnosis

Age-specific differentials with key diagnostic features

‚ñº

üö® Emergency Conditions (Immediate Action Required)

Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy

LIFE-THREATENING Surgical Emergency
  • History: Amenorrhea, abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, shoulder tip pain
  • Exam: Hypotension, tachycardia, abdominal guarding, cervical motion tenderness
  • Investigations: Positive hCG, no intrauterine pregnancy on US, free fluid in pouch of Douglas
  • Action: Emergency resuscitation, blood transfusion, immediate surgery

Placental Abruption

Feto-Maternal Emergency 2nd/3rd Trimester
  • History: Abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, uterine contractions, risk factors (HTN, smoking, cocaine)
  • Exam: Tender, hard uterus; hypotension/tachycardia if severe
  • Investigations: FBC (anemia, thrombocytopenia), coagulation profile (DIC), ultrasound (limited sensitivity)
  • Action: Stabilization, monitoring, prepare for delivery

Placenta Praevia (Major)

Painless Bleeding 2nd/3rd Trimester
  • History: Painless vaginal bleeding, previous cesarean, multiple pregnancies
  • Exam: Avoid digital examination until US confirms! Soft, non-tender uterus
  • Investigations: Transabdominal US confirms placenta overlying cervical os
  • Action: Expectant management, prepare for cesarean delivery

ü©∏ Common Diagnoses by Age Group

üë∂ Premenarchal Girls

Neonatal Uterine Bleeding (Normal)

Self-limiting Days 3-5 of life

Due to maternal progesterone withdrawal. Resolves spontaneously, no treatment needed.

Vulvovaginitis

Most Common Cause Usually benign

Irritation from poor hygiene, foreign bodies (toilet paper, toys), infection. Rarely causes significant bleeding.

Sexual Abuse

Must Exclude Reportable

Non-specific findings often normal. Look for behavioral changes, somatic complaints, anogenital injuries. Follow local protocols.

Rhabdomyosarcoma (Rare)

Malignant Peak <2 years

"Grape-like" cluster at vaginal opening. Requires urgent oncology referral. Passage of tissue fragments is concerning.

Genital Trauma

Straddle Injuries Common

Falls onto bicycle crossbar, chair arm, playground equipment. Examine under anesthesia if significant injury or urethral involvement suspected.

üå∏ Reproductive Age (Non-Pregnant)

Cervical Ectropion

Common & Benign Hormonal

Endocervical glandular epithelium visible on ectocervix. Common with OCP use, pregnancy, adolescence. Usually asymptomatic, may cause postcoital bleeding.

Cervical Polyp

Benign Neoplasm Usually >40 years

Smooth, lobular, friable mass seen on speculum. May cause contact bleeding. Usually benign but should be removed and histologically confirmed.

Cervicitis

Infectious STI Risk

Mucopurulent discharge, friable cervix, contact bleeding. Caused by Chlamydia, Gonorrhea, Trichomonas, Mycoplasma. "Strawberry cervix" with Trichomonas.

Cervical Cancer

Malignant HPV-Related

Contact bleeding, malodorous discharge, dyspareunia, pelvic/back pain. Risk increases with age: 1/44,000 (age 20-24) to 1/2,400 (age 45-54). Colposcopy and biopsy required.

Endometrial Cancer

Usually >50 years Obesity = Risk

Intermenstrual bleeding, postmenopausal bleeding. Risk factors: obesity, nulliparity, PCOS, tamoxifen, unopposed estrogen. Transvaginal US and endometrial biopsy needed.

Ovarian Cancer

Non-specific Symptoms Adnexal Mass

Bloating, nausea, dyspepsia, urinary urgency may predominate. Pelvic mass on examination. Transvaginal US shows complex mass. CA-125 may be elevated. CT for staging.

Vaginal Cancer

Rare <1% of GYN Cancers HPV-Related

Primary vaginal cancer rare; usually metastatic. Postcoital bleeding, visible lesion on speculum. Requires biopsy for diagnosis.

Vaginitis

Common Infection/Atrophic

Candida: thick white discharge, pruritus, normal pH. Bacterial vaginosis: fishy odor, pH >4.5, clue cells. Atrophic: thin, friable mucosa in hypoestrogen state.

ü§∞ Reproductive Age (Pregnant)

Miscarriage (Spontaneous Abortion)

25% of pregnancies 1st Trimester

Vaginal bleeding ¬± clots, pelvic pain. US shows no viable intrauterine pregnancy. Serial hCG may be needed. Management: expectant, medical (misoprostol), or surgical depending on type.

Subchorionic Hematoma

Usually resolves

Bleeding from chorionic membrane detachment. Seen on ultrasound. Most resolve spontaneously; may increase miscarriage risk if large.

Cervical Insufficiency

2nd Trimester

Painless cervical dilation with bleeding. Risk of preterm delivery. May require cervical cerclage.

Vasa Praevia

Fetal Vessels Over OS

Fetal vessels cross cervical os. Painless vaginal bleeding, often with membrane rupture. Catastrophic fetal hemorrhage risk. Emergency cesarean required.

üëµ Postmenopausal Women

Endometrial Cancer

Most Common (9%) >90% Present with PMB

Unprovoked bleeding >12 months after menopause. Risk factors: obesity, unopposed estrogen, tamoxifen, HNPCC. Endometrial thickness >4mm on US requires biopsy.

Atrophic Vaginitis

Very Common Hormone Deficiency

Thin, friable mucosa due to hypoestrogenism. May bleed with intercourse, pessary use, or spontaneously. Treat with topical estrogen.

Hormone Replacement Therapy Bleeding

Common (First 4-6 months)

Irregular bleeding common initially. If persistent beyond 6 months or heavy, investigate for endometrial pathology.

Cervical/Vaginal Cancer

Risk Increases with Age

Postcoital or unexplained bleeding. Speculum examination may show lesion. Requires biopsy and staging.

Endometrial Polyps

Benign

Focal endometrial thickening on US. May cause intermenstrual or postmenopausal bleeding. Hysteroscopy and removal recommended.

Treatment & Management

Evidence-based management pathways by condition

‚ñº

üö® Emergency Management

Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy: Stabilize and Transfer to Operating Theatre Immediately

1. Hemodynamic Resuscitation

  • IV Access: Two large-bore cannulas (14-16G)
  • Fluid Resuscitation: Crystalloids, colloids as needed
  • Blood Products: O-negative or type-specific blood if unstable
  • Monitoring: Continuous BP, pulse, urine output, oxygen saturation

2. Surgical Management

  • Stable patient: Laparoscopic salpingectomy preferred
  • Unstable patient: Laparotomy may be required
  • Conservative: Salpingostomy if future fertility desired and contralateral tube healthy

3. Post-Operative Care

  • Rh-negative: Give anti-D immunoglobulin
  • Follow-up: Serial hCG until negative
  • Fertility: Counseling about future pregnancy risk (10-27% recurrence)
Placental Abruption: Stabilize Mother & Fetus, Prepare for Delivery

1. Initial Stabilization

  • Maternal stabilization: IV access, monitoring, oxygen
  • Fetal monitoring: Continuous CTG
  • Blood work: FBC, coagulation profile, crossmatch
  • Anti-D: If Rh-negative

2. Management Strategy

  • Mild abruption (Grade 1): Expectant if mother & fetus stable, <34 weeks
  • Moderate-Severe (Grade 2-3): Deliver immediately (usually vaginal if possible)
  • DIC: Replace blood products (FFP, cryoprecipitate, platelets)

üë∂ Premenarchal Girls

Neonatal Uterine Bleeding

  • Management: Reassurance only, self-limiting
  • Duration: Resolves within days without intervention
  • Parent education: Explain physiological cause

Vulvovaginitis

  • Hygiene measures: Avoid harsh soaps, bubble baths, tight clothing
  • Foreign body removal: Under anesthesia if needed
  • Infection: Treat specific pathogen (bacterial, candidal)
  • Prevention: Cotton underwear, front-to-back wiping

Suspected Sexual Abuse

  • Immediate: Follow local child protection protocols
  • Reporting: Mandatory reporting to authorities
  • Examination: Specialist pediatric gynecology, possible forensic exam
  • Support: Child advocacy center involvement, counseling services
  • Documentation: Photographic evidence, detailed notes

üå∏ Reproductive Age (Non-Pregnant)

Cervical Ectropion

  • Asymptomatic: No treatment needed
  • Bothersome bleeding: Cauterization (silver nitrate, diathermy)
  • Observation: Often resolves after stopping OCPs or postpartum
  • Cervical screening: Ensure up-to-date cytology

Cervical Polyp

  • Removal: Office polypectomy (twist, ligate, or excise)
  • Histology: Always send for pathological examination
  • Follow-up: Recheck at next smear test
  • Recurrence: Possible, repeat removal if needed

Cervicitis

  • Testing: NAAT for Chlamydia, Gonorrhea, Trichomonas
  • Antibiotics: Empirical treatment while awaiting results
    • Chlamydia: Azithromycin 1g single dose OR Doxycycline 100mg BD 7 days
    • Gonorrhea: Ceftriaxone 500mg IM single dose
    • Trichomonas: Metronidazole 2g single dose
  • Partner treatment: Essential for STIs
  • Test of cure: Repeat NAAT in 3 months

Cervical Cancer

  • Referral: Urgent gynecology oncology (2-week wait pathway)
  • Staging: MRI pelvis, CT chest/abdomen, examination under anesthesia
  • Early stage: Surgery (cone biopsy, trachelectomy, hysterectomy)
  • Advanced stage: Chemoradiation (cisplatin-based)
  • Follow-up: Regular surveillance for recurrence

Endometrial Cancer

  • Referral: Urgent gynecology oncology
  • Staging: Total hysterectomy, bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy, lymphadenectomy
  • Adjuvant therapy: Radiotherapy, chemotherapy based on stage/grade
  • Hormonal: Progestins for fertility-sparing in selected cases
  • Surveillance: Regular follow-up for 5+ years

Vaginitis

  • Candida: Fluconazole 150mg single dose OR topical azole (clotrimazole, miconazole)
  • Bacterial vaginosis: Metronidazole 500mg BD 7 days OR 2g single dose
  • Trichomonas: Metronidazole 2g single dose (partner treatment essential)
  • Atrophic: Topical estrogen cream, vaginal tablet, or ring

ü§∞ Pregnancy-Related Management

Threatened Miscarriage

  • Expectant management: If viable pregnancy on US, mild bleeding
  • Bed rest: Not proven beneficial but commonly advised
  • Avoid intercourse: Until bleeding resolves
  • Rh prophylaxis: Anti-D 50¬µg if Rh-negative and <12 weeks, 250¬µg if ‚â•12 weeks
  • Follow-up: Repeat scan in 1-2 weeks or if bleeding worsens

Inevitable/Incomplete Miscarriage

  • Expectant: Allow spontaneous completion if stable, <13 weeks
  • Medical: Misoprostol 800¬µg vaginal or sublingual (repeat after 3 hours if needed)
  • Surgical: Manual vacuum aspiration or D&C if heavy bleeding, infection, or patient preference
  • Anti-D: As above for Rh-negative women
  • Follow-up: Confirm complete evacuation (US or hCG)

Missed Miscarriage

  • All options available: Expectant, medical, or surgical
  • Medical: Mifepristone 200mg oral followed by misoprostol
  • Surgical: D&C or vacuum aspiration, especially if >13 weeks
  • Timing: Patient can choose based on preference and stability

Ectopic Pregnancy (Unruptured)

  • Expectant: If hCG <1000 IU/L and falling, small size, asymptomatic
  • Medical: Methotrexate 50mg/m¬≤ IM single dose if criteria met
    • Hemodynamically stable
    • No fetal cardiac activity
    • Heterogeneous mass <3.5cm
    • Initial hCG <5000 IU/L
  • Monitoring: Serial hCG days 4, 7 until negative
  • Surgery: If rupture risk, high hCG, or failed medical treatment

Subchorionic Hematoma

  • Expectant: Most resolve spontaneously
  • Pelvic rest: Avoid intercourse, heavy lifting
  • Monitoring: Weekly ultrasound if symptomatic
  • Prognosis: Generally good unless very large or recurrent bleeding

Placenta Praevia (Minor, Stable)

  • Expectant: If no active bleeding, fetus <36 weeks
  • Pelvic rest: No intercourse, speculum, or digital exams
  • Corticosteroids: If <34 weeks and bleeding risk
  • Delivery: Planned cesarean at 36-37 weeks for major praevia
  • Emergency: Immediate cesarean if major bleeding

Vasa Praevia

  • Emergency cesarean: Immediate delivery if ruptured
  • Antenatal diagnosis: Hospitalization at 32 weeks, steroids, planned cesarean at 34-35 weeks
  • Fetal mortality: >60% if undiagnosed and ruptures

üëµ Postmenopausal Women

Endometrial Cancer

  • Referral: Urgent gynecology oncology (2-week pathway)
  • Surgery: Total hysterectomy, BSO, lymphadenectomy (staging)
  • Adjuvant: Radiotherapy ¬± chemotherapy based on stage/grade
  • Surveillance: Every 3-6 months for 2 years, then annually
  • Prognosis: 5-year survival 80% for stage I, 15% for stage IV

Atrophic Vaginitis

  • First-line: Topical vaginal estrogen (cream, tablet, or ring)
  • Systemic: Consider if vasomotor symptoms also present
  • Non-hormonal: Vaginal moisturizers, lubricants
  • Dosing: Daily for 2 weeks, then twice weekly maintenance
  • Alternative: Ospemifene (SERM) if estrogen contraindicated

HRT-Related Bleeding

  • First 4-6 months: Reassurance, may be irregular
  • Persistent >6 months: Investigation required (US, biopsy)
  • Dose adjustment: Consider changing progestogen type/dose
  • Alternative: Switch to continuous combined regimen

Endometrial Polyps

  • Hysteroscopic removal: Gold standard treatment
  • Histology: Always send for pathological examination
  • Recurrence: Possible; consider levonorgestrel IUD for prevention
  • Postmenopausal: Removal recommended even if asymptomatic

ü¶† Sexually Transmitted Infections

Chlamydia trachomatis

  • First-line: Doxycycline 100mg PO twice daily for 7 days
  • Alternative: Azithromycin 1g PO single dose
  • Pregnancy: Azithromycin 1g PO single dose (doxycycline contraindicated)
  • Partner treatment: Essential, test for other STIs
  • Test of cure: NAAT at 3 months

Neisseria gonorrhoeae

  • First-line: Ceftriaxone 500mg IM single dose (1g if >150kg)
  • Chlamydia co-treatment: Add doxycycline 100mg BD 7 days if not excluded
  • Partner treatment: Essential
  • Test of cure: NAAT at 2 weeks

Trichomonas vaginalis

  • Metronidazole: 2g PO single dose OR 500mg BD 7 days
  • Partners: Treat all sexual partners
  • Test of cure: NAOT at 3 months

Bacterial Vaginosis

  • Symptomatic: Metronidazole 500mg BD 7 days OR 2g single dose
  • Topical: Metronidazole 0.75% gel daily 5 days OR clindamycin 2% cream nightly 7 days
  • Asymptomatic: Treatment not usually needed unless procedures planned

Follow-up & Referral

Monitoring, prevention & long-term care pathways

‚ñº

üè• Referral Guidelines

Immediate/Urgent Referral (Same Day/24-48 Hours)

Emergency Department Gynecology On-Call
  • üö® Ruptured ectopic pregnancy
  • üö® Severe placental abruption with maternal/fetal compromise
  • üö® Major placenta praevia with active bleeding
  • üö® Vasa praevia with ruptured membranes/bleeding
  • üö® Suspected cervical or endometrial cancer
  • üö® Suspected child sexual abuse
  • üö® Severe genital trauma

Urgent Referral (Within 2 Weeks)

2-Week Wait Pathway
  • Postmenopausal bleeding (any)
  • Persistent intermenstrual bleeding >3 months
  • Heavy postcoital bleeding >3 episodes
  • Suspicious cervical lesion on examination
  • Thickened endometrium >4mm in postmenopausal women
  • Atypical glandular cells on cervical cytology
  • Sus ovarian mass on US with concerning features

Routine Referral

Outpatient Clinic
  • Cervical polyps for removal
  • Recurrent cervicitis/vaginitis
  • Atrophic vaginitis not responding to first-line Rx
  • HRT counseling for bleeding management
  • Endometrial polyp removal (hysteroscopy)

Specialist Referrals

  • Gynecologic Oncology: Confirmed or suspected cancers
  • Reproductive Endocrinology: Complex hormonal issues, PCOS, precocious puberty
  • Urogynecology: Associated urinary tract trauma or fistula
  • Child Protection: Suspected abuse (always involve social services)
  • Psychology/Counseling: Support after miscarriage, abuse, cancer diagnosis

üìä Follow-up Schedules

After Miscarriage

Physical Follow-up

  • 1 week: Review bleeding, pain, infection signs
  • 2-3 weeks: Confirm complete evacuation (US or hCG <5 IU/L)
  • 6 weeks: Cervical screening if due, assess menstrual return

Emotional Support

  • Early: Acknowledge grief, provide counseling resources
  • 6 weeks: Screen for depression/anxiety
  • Next pregnancy: Preconception counseling, early US

After Ectopic Pregnancy

Monitoring

  • Serial hCG: Days 4, 7 post-treatment until negative
  • Contraception: Advise 3 months after methotrexate (folate supplementation)
  • Fertility: Discuss 10-27% recurrence risk, early US in next pregnancy
  • Emotional: Support for pregnancy loss, future fertility concerns

After Cervical Cancer Treatment

Surveillance

  • Years 1-2: Every 3-6 months (history, examination, smear if cervix preserved)
  • Years 3-5: Every 6 months
  • >5 years: Annually
  • Imaging: Annual CXR, CT/MRI if symptoms
  • Late effects: Lymphedema, vaginal stenosis, bowel/bladder issues

After Endometrial Cancer Treatment

Surveillance

  • Every 3-6 months: For 2 years
  • Every 6 months: For 3 more years
  • Annually: After 5 years
  • Examinations: History, physical exam, vault smear if indicated
  • Imaging: CT/MRI for symptoms or exam findings
  • Recurrence risk: Highest in first 2-3 years

Postmenopausal Bleeding Follow-up

Normal Investigations

  • Reassure: If endometrial thickness ‚â§4mm and no risk factors
  • Recurrent bleeding: Re-investigate (repeat US, hysteroscopy)
  • HRT: Review at 3, 6 months then annually
  • Cervical screening: Ensure up to date

Endometrial Hyperplasia (Benign)

  • Follow-up biopsy: Every 6-12 months
  • Progestin therapy: LNG-IUD or oral progestins
  • Risk assessment: Progression to cancer risk increases with atypia

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention & Health Promotion

Cervical Cancer Prevention

  • HPV Vaccination: Offer to eligible age groups (9-45 years)
  • Cervical Screening: 25-64 years (3-yearly 25-49, 5-yearly 50-64)
  • Smoking Cessation: Reduce cervical cancer risk
  • Safe Sex: Condoms reduce HPV transmission

Endometrial Cancer Prevention

  • Weight Management: Obesity ‚Üë estrogen exposure
  • Diabetes Control: Insulin resistance is risk factor
  • PCOS Management: Cyclical progestins to oppose estrogen
  • Tamoxifen: Counsel about risks, consider surveillance

STI Prevention

  • Barrier Protection: Condoms reduce transmission
  • Partner Notification: Essential for treatment success
  • Regular Screening: High-risk individuals
  • Vaccination: Hepatitis B (if not immune)

Ectopic Pregnancy Prevention

  • STI Screening: Early treatment of chlamydia/gonorrhea
  • Smoking Cessation: Reduces risk
  • Early US: Confirm intrauterine pregnancy in next pregnancy
  • LNG-IUD: Reduces but doesn't eliminate risk

Menstrual Health Education

  • Normal vs abnormal: Teach women to recognize concerning bleeding
  • Record keeping: Menstrual apps/calendars
  • When to seek care: Heavy bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, postcoital bleeding
  • Iron supplementation: If heavy menstrual bleeding

üîç Quality Improvement

Audit Indicators

  • Time from postmenopausal bleeding presentation to investigation
  • Proportion of pregnant women with bleeding who have documented Rh status
  • Anti-D administration rate in Rh-negative pregnant women
  • Follow-up rate after abnormal cervical screening
  • STI testing rates in women with cervicitis

Key Performance Targets

  • Postmenopausal bleeding: Investigation within 2 weeks
  • 2WW referrals: Seen within 2 weeks
  • Suspected cancer: Diagnosis within 28 days (UK target)
  • STI results: Communicated within 2 weeks
  • Miscarriage follow-up: Contact within 2 weeks

Reducing Variation

  • Standardized referral pathways by age group
  • Protocolized management of bleeding in pregnancy
  • Integrated care pathways for suspected cancer
  • Electronic decision support tools
  • Regular multidisciplinary team meetings
