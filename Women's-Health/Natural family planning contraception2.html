Natural Family Planning

Clinical Reference Guide for Healthcare Professionals

Overview

Natural family planning (NFP) methods are based on identifying fertile days to prevent pregnancy. Two primary approaches are used:

  • Fertility Awareness Methods (FAM) - Monitoring fertility indicators
  • Lactational Amenorrhoea Method (LAM) - Postpartum breastfeeding method

Fertility Awareness Methods

Monitoring fertility indicators

Definition

Contraception based on monitoring fertility indicators to identify fertile/infertile days

Key Indicators

  • Basal Body Temperature (BBT) - Rise after ovulation confirms fertile period ended
  • Cervical Secretions - Clear, wet, slippery secretions indicate approaching ovulation
  • Calendar Method - Track cycle length (minimum 12 cycles)
  • Cervical Changes - Not recommended as sole indicator

Assessment

  • Apply WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria
  • Assess menstrual regularity
  • Evaluate partner commitment and compliance

Efficacy

Perfect Use
1-9%
pregnancy rate/year
Typical Use
24%
pregnancy rate/year

Counseling Points

Advantages

  • No chemicals/hormones
  • No side effects
  • Acceptable to all faiths
  • Immediately reversible

Disadvantages

  • Less effective than other methods
  • Requires training & daily monitoring
  • Not suitable for irregular cycles
  • No STI protection

Lactational Amenorrhoea Method

Postpartum breastfeeding method

Definition

Temporary postpartum contraception using exclusive breastfeeding to suppress ovulation

Criteria for Use

ALL must be met:

  • Complete amenorrhoea (no periods)
  • Fully/nearly fully breastfeeding (≥85% feeds as breast milk)
  • Feeding intervals ≤4 hours daytime, ≤6 hours nighttime
  • <6 months postpartum

Assessment

  • Confirm all LAM criteria are met
  • Assess breastfeeding pattern and frequency
  • Discuss transition to another method when criteria no longer met

Efficacy

Perfect Use
0.5%
pregnancy rate/6 months
Typical Use
2%
pregnancy rate/6 months

Counseling Points

Advantages

  • Effective for first 6 months
  • Encourages exclusive breastfeeding
  • No cost
  • No hormonal side effects

Disadvantages

  • No STI protection
  • Unreliable after 6 months
  • Frequent breastfeeding may be inconvenient
  • Effectiveness reduced with supplemental feeding

Not recommended as primary contraceptive method

  • Typical use: 22% pregnancy rate in first year
