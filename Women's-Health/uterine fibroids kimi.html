Uterine Fibroids Management

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Tool | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment & History

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Heavy menstrual bleeding (80% of symptomatic cases) Common
  • Pelvic pressure and bloating (34-57%) Moderate
  • Infertility or recurrent pregnancy loss Uncommon
  • Urinary frequency/constipation (32-37%) Variable
  • Palpable irregular pelvic mass on examination

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Rapid uterine growth, especially post-menopause Urgent Referral
  • Severe acute pelvic pain (possible torsion) Emergency
  • Heavy bleeding with hemodynamic instability Immediate
  • Postmenopausal bleeding Rule out cancer

Risk Factors

Strong: Age 40s, Black ethnicity, Obesity, Vitamin D deficiency
Moderate: Hypertension, Early menarche, Nulliparity
Weak: Red meat intake, Alcohol, Smoking (protective)

Physical Examination

  • Firm, enlarged, irregularly shaped uterus on bimanual exam
  • May be asymptomatic (most common presentation)
  • Assess for anemia signs (pallor, fatigue)
  • Rule out adnexal masses
Diagnostic Investigations

First-Line Investigation

  • Pelvic Ultrasound: Transvaginal ± transabdominal Gold Standard
  • Visualizes location (submucosal/intramural/subserosal), size, number
  • 100% sensitivity, 94% specificity for submucous fibroids
  • Cannot reliably distinguish fibroids from sarcomas

Additional Imaging

Sonohysterography

High sensitivity for submucous fibroids. Differentiates polyps from fibroids. Consider

Hysteroscopy

Definitive for intracavitary lesions. Allows biopsy. Visual confirm

MRI

Best for mapping fibroids, distinguishing adenomyosis, preoperative planning. Special cases

Endometrial Sampling

  • Indicated for abnormal bleeding >40 years or risk factors Rule out cancer
  • 90% accuracy for detecting endometrial carcinoma
  • Can be done concurrently with hysteroscopy

Key Differentials

  • Adenomyosis: Diffusely enlarged uterus, MRI distinguishes Common
  • Endometrial polyp: Sonohysterography helps differentiate Common
  • Leiomyosarcoma: Rapid growth, indistinct borders on MRI Rare but serious
  • Pregnancy: Always rule out in reproductive age
Treatment Options

Treatment Algorithm Overview

  • Fertility desired: Medical therapy → Myomectomy Preserve uterus
  • Fertility not desired: Medical therapy → UAE/Hysterectomy Definitive
  • Uterine preservation: Myomectomy or UAE Patient choice

Medical Therapy

GnRH Agonists (Leuprorelin) Short-term

35-65% fibroid shrinkage, amenorrhea. Use 3-6 months preoperatively. Bone loss risk

Levonorgestrel IUD First-line

Significantly reduces bleeding. For fibroids <3cm, no cavity distortion. Well tolerated

GnRH Antagonists (Elagolix/Relugolix) Approved

Reduces heavy bleeding. With add-back therapy up to 24 months. New option

Important: Effects reverse after stopping. Not for long-term use.

Surgical Options

Myomectomy Fertility preserving

Abdominal, laparoscopic, or hysteroscopic. Recurrence 15-28%. Risk of recurrence

UAE No fertility

Minimally invasive. 29% need further therapy at 3-5 years. Pregnancy risks

Hysterectomy Definitive cure

Abdominal, vaginal, or laparoscopic. No recurrence. Permanent

FDA warns against power morcellation due to sarcoma spread risk.

Emerging/Alternative Therapies

  • MRgFUS: Non-invasive, precise fibroid ablation. Limited availability. Promising
  • Ulipristal: Restricted due to liver injury risk. Only for selected non-surgical cases. Caution
  • Vitamin D: May reduce fibroid size. Safe adjunct. Safe
Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring Protocol

  • Asymptomatic: Annual pelvic exam sufficient Stable
  • Rapid growth: Especially post-menopause, requires MRI/surgical exploration Urgent
  • Post-treatment: Document normal adnexal exam to avoid missing ovarian pathology
  • Symptom recurrence: Prompt evaluation for re-intervention

Complications to Monitor

Recurrence Medium risk

10% need hysterectomy within 5-10 years post-myomectomy. Higher with multiple fibroids.

Obstetric Complications High risk

Miscarriage, malpresentation, preterm birth, abruption, postpartum hemorrhage.

Degeneration Medium risk

Red degeneration in pregnancy causes acute pain. NSAIDs with caution.

Prognosis

  • Medical therapy: Symptoms return within 12 weeks of stopping GnRH analogues. Bridge to menopause. Temporary
  • Myomectomy: 81% reduction in heavy bleeding. 40% conception rate post-surgery. Good
  • UAE: 28% need further invasive therapy at 3-5 years. Pregnancy complications higher. Moderate
  • Hysterectomy: Definitive cure. No recurrence. High satisfaction. Excellent

Patient Counseling Points

Advise patients to report abnormal bleeding, discharge, or symptom recurrence. Postmenopausal bleeding always requires evaluation.
