Pelvic Inflammatory Disease — Quick Reference Algorithm

Aligned to NICE CKS (last revised June 2024). Designed for fast decision‑making in primary care.

Red flags — admit / urgent same‑day review

  • Pregnant or ectopic pregnancy suspected.
  • Severe systemic illness or adnexal mass suggesting tubo‑ovarian abscess or pelvic peritonitis.
  • Systemically unwell with diagnostic uncertainty, or unable to follow/tolerate outpatient management.

Care pathway overview

1 Suspect PID (history + exam)
➜
2 Pregnancy test + swabs (don’t delay antibiotics)
➜
3 Start empiric antibiotics now
➜
4 Review at 72 hours; adjust if needed
Abstain from sex until both patient and partners complete treatment & are symptom‑free. Offer analgesia (paracetamol and/or ibuprofen if no contraindications).

Assessment — make a working diagnosis

Typical symptoms
  • New pelvic/lower abdominal pain (often bilateral), deep dyspareunia.
  • Abnormal bleeding: intermenstrual, post‑coital, or heavy menses.
  • Mucopurulent vaginal/cervical discharge; secondary dysmenorrhoea.
  • RUQ/shoulder pain (consider perihepatitis).
  • ± fever, nausea/vomiting or malaise (may be absent).
Examination
  • Cervical motion, uterine and/or adnexal tenderness; ± palpable mass.
  • Abnormal discharge and/or cervical friability.
Key questions
  • Pregnancy risk; recent IUD insertion; sexual history (new/multiple partners).

Investigations (do not delay treatment)

  • Urine/serum pregnancy test for all sexually active patients.
  • Endocervical/vaginal swabs for C. trachomatis and N. gonorrhoeae; consider M. genitalium if available.
  • Wet‑mount microscopy for pus cells if available; absence makes PID unlikely.
  • Consider CRP/ESR, WCC.
  • Offer HIV, hepatitis, and syphilis serology as part of STI screen.

A negative swab does not exclude PID — treat on clinical grounds.

Empiric antibiotic treatment

High risk of gonorrhoea
• Ceftriaxone 1 g IM once, then
• Doxycycline 100 mg PO bd × 14 days +
• Metronidazole 400 mg PO bd × 14 days
Low risk of gonorrhoea — first line
Same as above (Ceftriaxone 1 g IM once → Doxycycline + Metronidazole for 14 days).
Second‑line options
• Ofloxacin 400 mg PO bd × 14 days + Metronidazole 400 mg PO bd × 14 days
• Levofloxacin 500 mg PO od × 14 days + Metronidazole 400 mg PO bd × 14 days
• Moxifloxacin 400 mg PO od × 14 days (monotherapy)
Special situations
• M. genitalium positive: Moxifloxacin 400 mg PO od × 14 days.
• All first/second‑line unsuitable: Ceftriaxone 1 g IM once → Azithromycin 1 g PO weekly × 2 weeks.
• Very early pregnancy risk: start empiric therapy — benefits outweigh risks.

Fluoroquinolones: prescribe only when other recommended antibiotics are inappropriate; counsel on rare but potentially serious adverse effects (per MHRA).

IUD in situ

  • Mild–moderate PID: IUD can remain if improving within 48–72 hours of antibiotics; remove if no improvement.
  • Severe PID at presentation: remove IUD.

If removed, consider emergency contraception, follow‑up pregnancy testing, and ongoing contraception.

Follow‑up

  • Review within 72 hours. If not improving, check adherence, reconsider diagnosis, and consider urgent admission.
  • Continue antibiotics even if swabs are negative.
  • Metronidazole intolerance with mild–moderate PID: may stop metronidazole but continue other agents.
  • Further review 2–4 weeks after completion: symptom resolution, adherence, partner treatment, contraception advice.
  • Test‑of‑cure / re‑testing (as indicated):
    • Gonorrhoea: repeat test 2–4 weeks post‑treatment.
    • Chlamydia: repeat at 3–5 weeks if symptoms persist or concerns about adherence/contact tracing.
    • M. genitalium: repeat at 4 weeks post‑treatment.

Managing sexual partners

  • Current and recent partners (past 6 months) should attend sexual health services for screening, treatment, and contact tracing.
  • If a male partner cannot attend, offer empirical doxycycline 100 mg bd × 7 days while awaiting results.
  • If the index case is M. genitalium positive, screen (and, if positive, treat) partners accordingly.
  • Abstain from sexual activity until all parties have completed therapy and are symptom‑free (and test‑of‑cure performed where indicated).

Consider differential diagnoses

  • Ectopic pregnancy; threatened miscarriage.
  • Acute appendicitis.
  • Ovarian cyst complications (torsion, rupture, haemorrhage).
  • Endometriosis.
  • UTI/pyelonephritis; renal colic.
  • Acute gastroenteritis; IBS; diverticulitis; bowel obstruction/perforation/necrosis.
  • Mittelschmerz; ruptured corpus luteal cyst; functional pelvic pain.
