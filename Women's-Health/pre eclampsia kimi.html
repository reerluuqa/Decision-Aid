üìä Monitoring Protocol
‚ñº
Inpatient Monitoring
  • BP: At least 4 times daily (ward) or continuously (ICU)
  • Labs: FBC, LFTs, renal function at least twice weekly
  • Fetal: Cardiotocography at diagnosis, then as indicated
  • Doppler: Umbilical artery velocimetry twice weekly
Postnatal Monitoring
  • BP: Continue until recovery apparent
  • Antihypertensives: Review after 2 weeks
  • Resolution: Usually within 2-4 days postpartum
  • Follow-up: If not resolved by 6 weeks, reconsider diagnosis
‚ö†Ô∏è Complications
‚ñº
Fetal Growth Restriction

Found in ~30% of women with pre-eclampsia. Requires regular ultrasound monitoring.

Eclampsia

Rare but serious complication. Incidence reduced with magnesium sulfate prophylaxis.

Pulmonary Oedema

Largely a postnatal event. Prevented by careful fluid management (80 mL/hour restriction).

Placental Abruption

Associated with sudden fetal demise and severe maternal complications.

Pregnancy-Associated Stroke

Untreated systolic hypertension poses the greatest risk. Prevented by BP management.

Renal Failure

Usually acute tubular necrosis associated with sepsis or abruption. Rare in developed world.

üîÆ Long-term Prognosis
‚ñº
Recurrence Risk
  • Overall: ~16% in subsequent pregnancies
  • Early onset (28-34 weeks): ~33% risk
  • Late onset (34-37 weeks): ~25% risk
  • Risk reduced with: Change of partner, aspirin prophylaxis
Long-term Associations
  • Cardiovascular disease: 2-3x increased risk of hypertension, stroke
  • Type 2 Diabetes: Increased risk, especially with early onset disease
  • Renal Disease: History of early preterm pre-eclampsia associated with CKD
  • Prevention: Regular cardiovascular risk assessment recommended
