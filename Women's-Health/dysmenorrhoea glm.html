Dysmenorrhoea Quick-Scan

Last updated: Jul 30, 2025
Clinical Reference
URGENT: Rule Out Emergencies First

Exclude pregnancy and acute abdomen immediately in all patients presenting with lower abdominal/pelvic pain.

Ectopic Pregnancy

Suspicion of pregnancy? Mandatory serum/urine test required.

Action: Urgent sonographic evaluation & Gynae consult.

Ovarian Torsion

Acute onset pain, nausea/vomiting, unilateral adnexal mass.

Action: Urgent surgical evaluation to preserve ovary.

Appendicitis

Anorexia, fever, pain not related to menses.

Action: WBC count, CT Abdomen, Surgical consult.

Acute PID

Cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness, fever.

Action: Immediate antibiotics (broad-spectrum) to prevent infertility.

Step 1: Classification

Differentiating based on history is critical for management.

P

Primary Dysmenorrhoea

No pelvic pathology

  • Onset: 6-12 months after menarche.
  • Pain: Crampy, lower abdominal, radiates to back/thighs.
  • Timing: Onset with or a few hours before menses; lasts 8-72 hours.
  • Assoc Sx: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, fatigue, headache.
  • Response: Usually responds to NSAIDs/COCP.
Clinical Note: Diagnosis is clinical. Investigations usually unremarkable.
S

Secondary Dysmenorrhoea

Presence of pelvic pathology

  • Onset: Years after menarche (often 30s/40s).
  • Pain: "Congested", pelvic heaviness, back pain.
  • Timing: Starts before menses, progresses through luteal phase, worsens with bleeding.
  • Assoc Sx: Heavy bleeding, irregular bleeding, dyspareunia, discharge.
  • Response: Refractory to simple analgesia.
Clinical Note: Investigate for pathology (Endometriosis, Fibroids, Adenomyosis, PID).

Step 2: Assessment

Focused History

  • Menarche & Onset: Primary (6-12mo post-menarche) vs Secondary (Years later).
  • Pain Nature: Spasmodic (Cramps) vs Congested (Heaviness).
  • Cycle Impact: Heavy bleeding suggests Fibroids/Adenomyosis/Polyps.
  • Red Flags: Deep dyspareunia, post-coital bleeding, discharge.
  • Social/Obs: Contraceptive use, fertility plans, sexual history.

Physical Exam Findings

Primary Usually unremarkable. Pelvic exam often unnecessary in young, non-sexually active females.
Endometriosis Tender nodules (uterosacral), fixed retroverted uterus, adnexal masses.
Fibroids Enlarged, mobile, firm/multi-nodular uterus.
Adenomyosis Diffusely tender, globular enlarged uterus.
PID Cervical motion tenderness (Chandelier sign), bilateral adnexal tenderness.

Step 3: Investigations

Only if suspecting Secondary Dysmenorrhoea or treatment failure.

Imaging

  • Pelvic Ultrasound (Transvaginal): Gold standard for Fibroids, Polyps, Endometriomas, IUCD position.
  • Saline Infusion Sonography: Better for polyps/submucous fibroids.
  • MRI: If ultrasound equivocal or for complex adenomyosis/surgical planning.

Labs & Specialized

Pregnancy Test:
Exclude in ALL abdominal pain.
Swabs (NAAT):
Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, M. genitalium.
Inflammatory:
WBC, ESR, CRP (Non-specific).
CA-125:
If ovarian mass (Non-specific).

Laparoscopy

Gold standard for Endometriosis diagnosis. Consider if pain persists despite medical therapy or to assess adhesions.

Differential Diagnosis

Endometriosis High Priority

Sx: Progressive pain, dyspareunia, dyschezia.

Exam: Nodules, fixed uterus, tenderness.

Dx: Laparoscopy (Gold Std) or USS for endometriomas.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease Infection

Sx: Bilateral pain, discharge, fever.

Dx: Cervical motion tenderness + Adnexal tenderness + Swabs.

Action: Treat immediately.

Fibroids (Leiomyoma)

Sx: Heavy/prolonged bleeding, "pressure" symptoms.

Exam: Enlarged, firm, irregular uterus.

Dx: Pelvic USS.

Adenomyosis

Sx: Parous women, heavy bleeding, pain.

Exam: Globular, diffusely tender uterus.

Dx: USS or MRI.

Uterine Polyps

Sx: Intermenstrual bleeding, heavy flow.

Dx: Hysteroscopy (Diagnostic & Therapeutic).

Ovarian Cyst (Haemorrhagic) Acute

Sx: Sudden unilateral mid-cycle pain.

Dx: USS (Complex cyst), Laparoscopy if severe.

Mullerian Duct Anomaly

Sx: Cyclical pain shortly after menarche.

Dx: Pelvic mass (haematometra), MRI/USS.

Asherman's Syndrome

Hx: D&C post-pregnancy, scanty menses.

Dx: Hysteroscopy, Hysterosalpingography.

Cervical Stenosis

Hx: Surgery (LLETZ), cyclic pain.

Dx: USS for fluid collection (haematometra).

IUCD related

Hx: Insertion timing, especially Copper IUCD.

Note: Levonorgestrel IUS can treat dysmenorrhoea.

Quick Management Guide

Condition Primary Action Secondary/Adjunct
Primary Dysmenorrhoea NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, etc.) - Start at onset of pain/menses. Hormonal Contraception (COCP) to inhibit ovulation.
Endometriosis Hormonal suppression (Combined Pill, Progestins, IUS). Referral for Laparoscopy/Diagnosis if refractory.
Fibroids/Polyps Tranexamic acid (for bleeding) / NSAIDs. Hysteroscopic resection or Myomectomy (Surgical).
Adenomyosis Levonorgestrel-releasing IUS (Highly effective). COCP or Hysterectomy (definitive).
