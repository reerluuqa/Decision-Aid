Gonorrhoea – Quick Reference (June 2025)

1. Suspect

  • Men: mucopurulent urethral discharge, dysuria (90 % symptomatic).
  • Women: altered vaginal discharge, dysuria, lower abdominal pain (≈50 % symptomatic).
  • Risk factors: 15–24 yrs | MSM | multiple partners | prior STI | black Caribbean ethnicity | deprivation.

2. 60-second Assessment

StepAction
HistorySymptoms, sexual contacts & practices, condom use, STI/HIV history.
Exam• Men – penis, testes, epididymis.
• Women – speculum & bimanual for PID.
• All – pharynx, rectum, eyes.
Red flagsFever, joint pain/swelling, rash (DGI); severe pelvic pain (PID).

5. Partner Rx & Follow-Up

  • Notify partners: symptomatic urethral men → 2 weeks | all others → 3 months.
  • Empirical Rx contacts within 14 days of exposure.
  • Follow-up ≈ 1 week: TOC not required after ceftriaxone 1 g unless pharyngeal infection, resistance, or persistent symptoms.

6. Key Counselling

  • Abstain from sex until 7 days after both partners treated.
  • Screen for chlamydia, HIV, syphilis.
  • Provide NHS patient leaflet & safer-sex advice.

7. Admission / Urgent Referral

  • Suspected disseminated gonorrhoea (DGI).
  • Severe PID or conjunctival gonorrhoea.
  • Treatment failure or antibiotic allergy.
