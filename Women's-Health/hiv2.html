HIV Infection and AIDS: Clinical Guidance

Condensed reference for healthcare professionals based on NICE CKS

Last revised: March 2025

When to Suspect HIV

  • Persistent generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats, or diarrhea
  • Recurrent infections (e.g., shingles, oral thrush, bacterial pneumonia)
  • Opportunistic infections or AIDS-defining conditions
  • Sexual health concerns, STIs, or high-risk sexual behavior
  • History of injecting drug use, incarceration, or migration from high-prevalence country (>1% adult HIV prevalence)
  • Pregnancy (routine offer of testing)
  • Partner known to be HIV-positive

Diagnosis

  • Test: 4th generation HIV p24 antigen/antibody combination immunoassay (blood)
  • Rapid point-of-care tests acceptable if followed by lab confirmation
  • Reactive test → Confirm with differentiation assay (HIV-1/2) and viral load/CD4
  • Diagnosis confirmed by positive immunoassay + differentiation assay or positive virological test (e.g., RNA PCR)

Immediate Actions After Diagnosis

  • Refer urgently (within 7 days) to specialist HIV service
  • Discuss transmission risks, prevention, partner notification, mental health, and ART adherence
  • Do not initiate ART in primary care — manage by specialists

Screening Recommendations

Offer opt-out HIV testing to:

  • All patients in high-prevalence areas (diagnosis rate >2/1000)
  • Pregnant women (routine antenatal screening)
  • Anyone with HIV risk factor(s)
  • Individuals attending sexual health, drug services, or TB clinics

Repeat testing annually in ongoing risk.

Acute HIV (Seropositive Illness)

  • Suspect in flu-like illness (fever, rash, pharyngitis, myalgia) 2–6 weeks post-exposure
  • If high suspicion but negative/indeterminate test → test HIV RNA (PCR)
  • Urgent referral — early ART may improve outcomes

Referral & Admission Criteria

Refer Urgently (within 7 days)

  • Suspected acute HIV with symptoms
  • Opportunistic infections (e.g., PCP, toxoplasmosis)
  • CD4 < 200 cells/µL
  • AIDS-defining illness (e.g., Kaposi’s sarcoma)
  • ART toxicity or failure

Admit Immediately

  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Severe infection (e.g., PJP pneumonia, sepsis)
  • Neurological compromise

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

  • Consider PEP if: recent (≤72 hrs) high-risk exposure (e.g., condomless sex with HIV+ partner, needle-stick)
  • Start PEP within 72 hours (sooner = better)
  • Typical regimen: tenofovir/emtricitabine + dolutegravir for 28 days
  • Refer to sexual health or A&E for assessment and initiation
Note: PEP not needed after exposure to an HIV-positive partner with sustained undetectable viral load.

Long-Term Management in Primary Care

  • Monitor for cardiovascular, renal, hepatic, and bone disease
  • Vaccinations: annual flu, pneumococcal, HPV, hepatitis A/B
  • Screen for depression, anxiety, and substance use
  • Support sexual & reproductive health (including safe conception)
  • Lifestyle advice: smoking cessation, alcohol, diet, exercise
  • Coordinate with specialist HIV team — shared care encouraged

Key Clinical Messages

  • Early diagnosis saves lives — maintain a low threshold for testing.
  • All diagnosed cases require urgent specialist referral.
  • PEP must start ≤72 hours post-exposure.
  • Undetectable = Untransmittable (U=U) — reinforce with patients.
  • No cure, but ART enables near-normal life expectancy.
