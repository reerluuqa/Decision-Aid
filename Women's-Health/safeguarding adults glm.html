üìò Core Principles

What is Abuse?

A single or repeated act or lack of appropriate action, within a relationship of trust, which causes harm or distress to a vulnerable person.

Who is a Vulnerable Adult?
Aged 18+ who needs community care services by reason of mental/other disability, age, or illness, and is unable to protect themselves against significant harm or exploitation.

üö© 10 Types of Abuse (Care Act 2014)

Common

Physical Abuse

Physical violence, misuse of medication, inappropriate restraint or sanctions.

Domestic Abuse

Psychological, physical, sexual, financial, emotional abuse. Includes 'honour'-based violence.

Sexual Abuse

Non-consensual sexual contact or activity.

High Reported

Psychological Abuse

Emotional abuse, threats, humiliation, blaming, controlling, intimidation, harassment.

Financial or Material

Theft, fraud, exploitation, pressure re: wills/property, misuse of benefits or possessions.

Most Common

Neglect & Acts of Omission

Ignoring medical needs, withholding medication/food/heating, failure to provide access to services.

Discriminatory Abuse

Racist, sexist, or abuse based on a person's disability or other protected characteristics.

Organisational Abuse

Neglect and poor care practice within an institution (e.g., hospital, care home).

Modern Slavery

Slavery, human trafficking, forced labour, and domestic servitude.

Self-Neglect

Neglect of personal hygiene, health, or surroundings. Includes hoarding behaviour.

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors & Red Flags

Risk Factors

  • Lack of mental capacity
  • Increasing age / Physical dependence
  • Low self-esteem
  • Previous history of abuse
  • Social isolation
  • Lack of access to health/social services
  • Negative experience of disclosing abuse

Signs of Potential Abuse

  • Physical: Unexplained injuries, poor skin care, unkempt appearance, weight loss.
  • Behavioural: Withdrawn, aggressive, irritable, emotionally labile.
  • Context: Inability to reach drink/feed self, inability to ask for help.

üó£Ô∏è Managing the Conversation

If you suspect abuse, you must act. Do not assume someone else will.

1
Ensure Privacy

Make sure the alleged abuser is NOT present. Ensure interpreter/trusted person support if needed.

2
Establish Facts

Clarify the nature, circumstances, and cause of injury/neglect. Ask about safety.

3
Explain Next Steps

Be clear what will happen with the information disclosed.

üöÄ Management & Referral

IMMEDIATE

If life is in danger: Ring 999/112/911.

STANDARD

If significant risk of abuse: Report to local safeguarding team/authority. Lead agency is the Local Authority.

DOCUMENT

Record keeping: Gather all appropriate information. Report to safeguarding lead.

üè• Specifics: Care Homes / Settings

If resident is in immediate danger (or risk to others):

  • Follow immediate actions.
  • Report ASAP.

If resident refuses action but you suspect abuse:

  • Safeguarding referral MUST still be made.
  • Follow NICE guidance.
üîí Confidentiality & Capacity Guidelines

Information Sharing: Serious case reviews identify lack of information-sharing as a major issue. Share concerns whilst respecting confidentiality.

Mental Capacity Act 2005: If a person retains capacity, doctors cannot make 'best interest' decisions for them. However, respect for choices must be balanced against welfare. Patients must be informed confidentiality is not absolute if there is risk of harm to others or public interest.

Legal Frameworks:

  • England/Wales: Care Act 2014.
  • Scotland: Adult Support and Protection (Scotland) Act 2007.
