Quick Decision Flow

  1. Identify method type.
    • FAM: Track fertility indicators (daily BBT, cervical mucus, cycle length, cervix changes ± device).
    • LAM: Use only if all criteria are met: <6 months postpartum, amenorrhoeic, exclusive/nearly exclusive breastfeeding (≥85% feeds) with no gaps >4h (day) or >6h (night).
  2. Assess suitability. Apply WHO MEC; check cycle regularity, daily monitoring ability, partner cooperation, postpartum status.
  3. Counsel on effectiveness vs alternatives. Compare to LARC; ensure understanding of typical‑use failure rates above.
  4. Agree behaviour during fertile days (FAM). Abstain or use condoms (higher failure if used around ovulation).
  5. Plan review & support. Refer to trained FAM practitioners; provide written resources (NHS, Sexwise, Fertility UK).
  6. For LAM: Set a switch plan. Move to another method if menses returns, breastfeeding frequency reduces, or after 6 months.
STI protection: NFP methods do not protect against STIs, including HIV. Use condoms if at risk.

What the Methods Involve

Fertility Awareness Methods (FAM)

  • BBT: Ovulation ↗︎ progesterone ↗︎ temperature; 3 consecutive elevated days ≈ end of fertile window.
  • Cervical secretions: ↑ wet/slippery/clear as ovulation approaches; avoid sex or use condoms until 3 dry days.
  • Cervix changes: High/soft/open = fertile; low/firm/closed for 3 days = end of window (sole‑use not recommended).
  • Cycle length: Ovulation ~12–16 days before menses; fertile time ~8–9 days; early‑cycle pregnancy risk low but never zero.
  • Devices: Some combine temperature and cycle data; LH kits are designed to aid conception, not routinely recommended for contraception.

Lactational Amenorrhoea Method (LAM)

  • Breastfeeding suppresses ovulation; efficacy depends on strict feeding pattern (day & night).
  • Effectiveness falls with: return of menstruation, decreased feeding frequency, introduction of other foods, or >6 months postpartum.
  • Effect of expressing on efficacy is uncertain; may reduce protection.

Counselling Essentials

FAM – Pros

  • No hormones; no physical adverse effects.
  • Acceptable across faiths/cultures; involves partner.
  • Improves body‑awareness of normal/abnormal secretions.
  • Immediately reversible.

FAM – Cons

  • Less effective than LARC; requires daily monitoring and training.
  • Not suitable if cycles irregular or while breastfeeding.
  • Indicators affected by illness, stress, travel, hormones.
  • Requires abstinence or condoms during fertile days.

LAM – Pros

  • Effective up to 6 months postpartum when criteria met.
  • Encourages exclusive breastfeeding; no cost; no side effects.
  • Usable immediately after childbirth.

LAM – Cons

  • Unreliable once supplementary feeds begin or after 6 months.
  • Requires frequent day & night feeds; may be impractical.
  • No STI protection.

  • For fertile days under FAM: abstain or use condoms (higher failure near ovulation).
  • Reinforce that estimates of “safe” days are imprecise with irregular cycles or uncertain LMP; pregnancy risk is never zero.
  • Set follow‑up for technique review, questions, and transition planning.

Clinic Summary

Use FAM or LAM with highly motivated patients who understand limitations and failure rates. Always compare with LARC effectiveness, ensure STI risk counselling, and create a clear transition plan when LAM criteria lapse.
