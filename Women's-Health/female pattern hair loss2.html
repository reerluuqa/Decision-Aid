Female Pattern Hair Loss – Quick Reference Algorithm

Fast memory aide for primary care consultations
1-page clinical aid

1) Initial Assessment

Typical pattern
Diffuse crown thinning • Parting widens • Frontal hairline preserved
Hair pull test
Usually negative (may be + in active shed)
Scale
Use Ludwig 1–3

History

  • Onset, tempo, areas; shedding vs thinning; compensatory styling.
  • Triggers: severe illness/infection, childbirth, surgery, major stress, new drugs, restrictive diet/weight loss.
  • Medical: thyroid disease, PCOS/endocrine, autoimmune; nutritional status.
  • Gynae: menarche, menses pattern, contraception/HRT, fertility, pregnancy.
  • Family history of hair loss; psychosocial impact.

Examination

  • Scalp: scaling, inflammation, pustules, scarring, tenderness/pruritus.
  • Distribution: typical female pattern; note atypical Olsen “Christmas tree” or bitemporal recession.
  • Hair pull test: compare frontal vs occipital.
  • Look for androgen excess: hirsutism, severe acne; consider menstrual irregularity, galactorrhoea, insulin resistance.
Typical & mild → reassurance / consider OTC
Atypical / rapid / shedding → investigate
Scarring / red flags → refer

Ludwig Scale (Descriptor)

  • Stage 1: Slight part-width widening; frontal hairline maintained.
  • Stage 2: More evident crown thinning with many short, thin hairs.
  • Stage 3: Marked crown loss; wide part; frontal hairline preserved.

Hair Pull Test Tips

  • Grasp 50–100 hairs; >3 extracted = positive (standardize technique).
  • Diffuse positive → evaluate for telogen effluvium (may coexist).

2) Investigations (only if indicated)

Diagnosis is clinical; test if atypical, red flags, or features suggest alternate causes.

If telogen effluvium suspected / atypical

Screen labs

FBC Ferritin TFTs 25-OH Vitamin D
If androgen excess signs

Endocrine tests

  • Free-androgen index* (AM, cycle day 2–5; stop CHC ≥2 mo before).
  • Prolactin.
  • Abnormal or uncertain → seek specialist advice / refer.

*Per PCOS diagnostic guidance; interpret within clinical context.

3) Differentiate

Non‑scarring
Telogen effluvium • Alopecia areata • Traction • Trichotillomania • Drug‑induced • Hair fragility
Scarring
Frontal fibrosing • Lichen planopilaris • Discoid lupus • Tinea capitis • Dermatomyositis
Red flags for alternative dx: rapid/profound shedding, patchy loss incl. brows/lashes, scalp inflammation or scarring, systemic disease, recent new meds (e.g., antidepressants, anticoagulants), hypothyroid features.

4) Management in Primary Care

Counsel & Educate

  • Course: slow, unpredictable progression; rarely total baldness.
  • Shampooing does not worsen loss.
  • Provide reputable resources (BAD, Alopecia UK, NHS pages).

Options

Cosmetic

Wigs & camouflage

  • Wigs/hairpieces (NHS eligibility varies); styling, colouring, fibres.
  • Avoid heavy extensions → traction risk.
Drug

Topical minoxidil

  • 2% solution (OTC/private; age 18–65).
  • 5% foam (prescribable & OTC).
  • Assess response at 6 months → if working, continue indefinitely.
  • No response by 12 months → discontinue & consider referral.
Surgical (private)

Hair transplantation

Consider only if poor response to less invasive options.

5) When to Refer

  • Atypical pattern, scarring, extensive loss → Dermatology (ideally hair specialist).
  • Clinical/biochemical androgen excess → Endocrinology (exclude secreting tumour).
  • No response to primary‑care treatment → consider specialist options (e.g., 5‑ARI, anti‑androgens* , LLLT, surgery).
  • Marked psychosocial impact → consider psychological support.

*Specialist‑initiated; not recommended to start in primary care.

6) Prescribing Pearls – Topical Minoxidil

Contraindications

  • <18 or >65 years.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding.
  • Inflamed/infected/irritated scalp; shaved scalp.
  • Untreated hypertension; known sensitivity to ingredients.
  • Non‑FPHL hair loss (sudden/patchy/unknown cause).

Adverse Effects

  • Local irritation/erythema (solution contains propylene glycol).
  • Temporary shedding at 2–6 weeks → usually resolves.
  • Hypertrichosis from product transfer; apply thin layer, allow to dry.
  • Rare hypotension; stop if chest pain, tachycardia, syncope, dizziness, oedema.

Counselling & Interactions

  • Use as directed; more does not improve results.
  • Apply to scalp only; wash hands after; product is highly flammable.
  • Theoretical potentiation with other antihypertensives.
  • Other topical agents (e.g., steroids, tretinoin, dithranol) ↑ absorption.
Expectations: improvement may take 3–6 months; benefit maintained only with continued use. Discontinuation → loss of gains within 3–6 months and possible rebound shedding.
