Pruritus Vulvae

Clinical Algorithm for Fast Decision Making

Background

Definition: Itching of the vulva (mons pubis, labia majora/minora, clitoris, perineum, external urethral/vaginal openings)

Prevalence: Most common vulval disorder; 1 in 10 women seek help

Common Causes: Dermatological conditions (50%), infections, hormonal changes

Assessment Approach

History

  • Symptom characteristics (location, duration, onset)
  • Trigger factors (sexual activity, menses, exercise)
  • Associated symptoms (discharge, dysuria)
  • Hygiene practices & products used
  • Medications (topical & systemic)
  • Medical history (skin disorders, diabetes)
  • Menopause status

Examination

  • Visual inspection (color, texture, lesions)
  • Speculum exam (mucosa, discharge)
  • Extra-genital sites (skin, oral mucosa)
  • Look for excoriation, lichenification
  • Assess vulval architecture

Investigations

  • Vaginal swab: If discharge present or infection suspected
  • Blood tests: If generalized pruritus (FBC, ferritin, TFTs, LFTs, renal function, glucose/HbA1c)
  • Consider referral for: Vulvoscopy, biopsy, allergy testing

Differential Diagnosis

Dermatological (50%)

Irritant contact dermatitis, allergic contact dermatitis, lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, psoriasis, atopic dermatitis

Infections

Candidiasis, bacterial vaginosis, trichomoniasis, threadworm, herpes simplex, pubic lice/scabies

Hormonal

Atrophic vaginitis (menopause, breastfeeding), pregnancy changes

Other Causes

Pre-malignant/malignant lesions, systemic conditions, neurological causes, incontinence

Management Principles

Identify & treat underlying cause
Avoid irritants (soaps, perfumes, tight clothing)
Emollients regularly
Gentle hygiene without over-washing
Cotton underwear only
Topical corticosteroids (potency depends on condition)
Sedating antihistamines for nocturnal itch
Avoid scratching (cold compresses may help)

Red Flags for Referral

  • Suspected malignancy (ulceration, fixed lump, bleeding)
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Failure to respond to initial treatment
  • Suspected lichen sclerosus or lichen planus
  • Suspected VIN or vulval neoplasia
  • Significant psychological impact
