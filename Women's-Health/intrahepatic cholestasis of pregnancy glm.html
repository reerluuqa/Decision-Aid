Severe ICP
Bile Acids ≥100 µmol/L

Stillbirth Risk: High (Increases significantly at 35 weeks).

  • Timing: Deliver at 35–36 weeks.
  • UDCA: Recommended.
  • Monitoring: Inpatient/Intensive surveillance.
Moderate ICP
Bile Acids 40–99 µmol/L

Stillbirth Risk: Similar to background population.

  • Timing: Deliver 38–39 weeks (individualized).
  • UDCA: Recommended (Reduces spontaneous preterm birth).
  • Monitoring: LFTs weekly from 32 wks.
Mild ICP
Bile Acids 10–39 µmol/L

Stillbirth Risk: No significant increase vs background.

  • Timing: Expectant management to 39–40 wks (if stable).
  • Tx: Emollients, sedating antihistamines.
  • UDCA: Consider if itching severe.
Pharmacology

1st Line: Ursodeoxycholic Acid (UDCA)

Reduces itch (marginally) and ALT. Reduces risk of spontaneous preterm birth in ≥40 µmol/L.

Dose: Consult specialist (typically 10-15mg/kg/day divided doses).

Vitamin K (Phytomenadione)

Indicated if steatorrhoea present or taking bile acid binders (e.g., colestyramine).

Symptomatic Relief

  • Topical emollients (aqueous cream with menthol).
  • Sedating antihistamines (e.g., Chlorphenamine) for sleep.
Red Flags & Emergency
  • Bile Acids ≥100 µmol/L: Plan immediate delivery at 35-36 weeks.
  • Reduced Fetal Movements: Immediate assessment (CTG/USS).
  • Steatorrhoea/Jaundice: Risk of malabsorption & coagulopathy. Check Vit K.
  • Severe Pruritus: Affects sleep/mental health. Consider early delivery for maternal indications.
Diagnosis & Investigations

Definition: Pruritus + raised bile acids + liver dysfunction, absent other causes.

1st Line Tests

Test Key Finding
Bile Acids (Non-fasting) > Upper Lab Limit (Mild: ≥10/19, Severe: ≥100)
LFTs (ALT/AST) Raised in ~80% of women
Bilirubin Raised in ~10%

*Repeat bile acids every 1-3 weeks if symptomatic, weekly from 32 wks.

Differential Diagnosis
Condition Differentiator
Acute Fatty Liver Hypoglycaemia, coagulopathy, encephalopathy
HELLP Thrombocytopenia, Haemolysis, Hypertension
Viral Hepatitis Prodrome, malaise, positive virology
PEMP (Rash) Itchy rash in striae (ICP has NO rash)
Risk Factors

Strong

  • Previous ICP (Recurrence ~70%)
  • Family History of ICP
  • Hepatitis C
  • Cholelithiasis

Weak

  • Multi-fetal pregnancy
  • Assisted reproduction
  • Chronic Hep B
  • Specific Ethnicities (Chilean, Indian-Asian)
Clinical Tips (Theory)
  • Pruritus: Worse at night. palms/soles classic, but can be generalized. No rash (excoriations only).
  • Pathophysiology: Bile acid gradient reverses, exposing fetus. Taurocholic acid causes fetal arrhythmia (sudden stillbirth theory).
  • Labs: LFTs normalize by 3 months postpartum. If not, investigate other liver pathology.
