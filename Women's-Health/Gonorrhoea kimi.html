Gonorrhea Management Algorithm

BMJ Best Practice Guidelines | Last updated: May 2025

Assessment & History
High-Risk Groups Urgent Referral

Key Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors:
• Age 15-24 years (4-5x higher rates)
• Men who have sex with men (MSM)
• Black ancestry (8-9x higher in US)
• Prior/current STI history
• Multiple recent partners
Behavioral Risks:
• Inconsistent condom use
• Partner's risk factors
• Substance use
• Commercial sex work
• History of abuse

Sexual History (5 P's)

Partners

Sex of partners, number in prior 2 months/1 year

Prevention of Pregnancy

Contraceptive use, trying to conceive

Protection from STIs

STI/HIV prevention methods used

Practices

Oral/vaginal/anal sex, condom use

Past STIs

Prior STI or HIV diagnoses

Physical Examination

Male Genital Tract:
• Inspect penis, glans, meatus
• Palpate testis, epididymis, cord
• Prostate exam if indicated
Female Genital Tract:
• Inspect external genitalia
• Speculum exam (NO lubricant)
• Bimanual exam for PID
• Cervical motion tenderness
Extragenital:
• Pharyngeal exam
• Rectal exam
• Conjunctival inspection
• Skin lesions check
Red Flags - Urgent Referral: Disseminated infection (DGI), meningitis, endocarditis, severe PID with surgical abdomen, tubo-ovarian abscess, neonatal sepsis
Diagnosis & Testing
NAAT Preferred Culture for Resistance

Primary Diagnostic Tests

NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Test)
Preferred method
• Urine, urethral, cervical, vaginal
• Self-collected or clinician-collected
• High sensitivity
• Not FDA-approved for pharynx/rectum in US
Culture
Gold standard
• All sites including pharynx/rectum
• Tests antimicrobial susceptibility
• Lower sensitivity for pharynx (~50%)
• Requires viable transport
Gram Stain
Men only
• Urethral discharge only
• >99% specific, >95% sensitive
• Intracellular gram-negative diplococci
• Not sufficient for women or asymptomatic

Testing by Patient Type

Men with Urethral Discharge

Gram stain initial test → confirm with culture or NAAT

Men without Discharge

First-void urine NAAT or urinalysis (leukocyte esterase)

Women

NAAT of self-collected vaginal swab, urine, or endocervical swab

Non-Genital Sites

Pharynx/Rectum: NAAT if available, otherwise culture

Infants

Conjunctival exudates culture + antibiotic susceptibility

Differential Diagnoses

Chlamydia trachomatis
• Most common co-infection (10x more common)
• Dual NAAT testing recommended
Trichomonas vaginalis
• Wet prep low sensitivity
• Consider if treatment failure
Mycoplasma genitalium
• No commercial test available
• Consider if persistent symptoms
Screening Recommendations: All sexually active women <25 years annually; MSM at all exposure sites annually (q3-6 months if high risk)
Treatment Protocols
Monotherapy Preferred Dual Therapy for PID Weight-Based Dosing

General Principles

  • Monotherapy: Ceftriaxone single dose (azithromycin stewardship)
  • Chlamydia coverage: Add doxycycline 7 days if not excluded
  • Partner treatment: Last 60 days or most recent partner
  • Test-of-cure: 7-14 days for pharynx, 3 months for reinfection check

Uncomplicated Infection (≥45kg)

Standard Regimen
Ceftriaxone

500 mg IM single dose (<150kg)
1000 mg IM single dose (≥150kg)

Alternative if Unavailable
Cefixime

800 mg PO single dose
Lower efficacy for pharynx

Cephalosporin Allergy
Gentamicin + Azithromycin

Gentamicin 240 mg IM single dose
Azithromycin 2 g PO single dose

Complicated Infections

Mild-Moderate PID
Ceftriaxone + Doxycycline + Metronidazole

• Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose
• Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x 14 days
• Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x 14 days
Outpatient treatment with 72h reassessment

Severe PID (Hospitalize)
IV Ceftriaxone + Doxycycline + Metronidazole

• Ceftriaxone 1 g IV q24h
• Doxycycline 100 mg IV/PO q12h
• Metronidazole 500 mg IV/PO q12h
Switch to oral after 24-48h clinical improvement

Conjunctivitis
Ceftriaxone

1 g IM single dose
Higher dose than urogenital

Disseminated Gonococcal Infection (DGI)
IV Ceftriaxone

1 g IV/IM q24h
Hospitalize + 24-48h IV → 7 days oral

Special Populations

Neonates (≤45kg)
Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime

• Ophthalmia: 25-50 mg/kg IV/IM x1 (max 250 mg)
• DGI/Bacteremia: 25-50 mg/kg IV/IM q24h x7 days
• Meningitis: 10-14 days treatment

Children ≤45kg
Ceftriaxone

• Uncomplicated: 25-50 mg/kg IV/IM x1 (max 250 mg)
• Complicated: 50 mg/kg IV/IM q24h
• Consider sexual abuse evaluation

Pregnant Women
Ceftriaxone ± Azithromycin

• Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose
• Azithromycin 1 g PO single dose if chlamydia not excluded
• Complicated: Hospitalize + specialist care

Warning: Fluoroquinolones require susceptibility testing (gyrA wild-type) and have serious adverse effects. Use only when absolutely necessary and no alternatives exist.
Follow-up & Monitoring
Test-of-Cure 3-Month Rescreen Complication Watch

Test-of-Cure Protocol

  • Indicated: Non-recommended regimen used, pharyngeal infection, persistent symptoms
  • Timing: 7-14 days post-treatment (pharynx), 2-3 weeks (NAAT clearance)
  • Method: Culture preferred (avoid false positive NAAT)
  • Not needed: If first-line regimen used and symptoms resolved

Rescreening Schedule

3-Month Rescreen

All patients with gonorrhea due to high reinfection risk (7-12% within 12 months)

Pregnant Women

Retest in 3rd trimester if high risk persists to prevent neonatal complications

High-Risk MSM/TGW

Screen every 3-6 months if on PEP or high-risk behaviors

Complications to Monitor

  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Up to 1/3 of women with gonorrhea. Leads to chronic pelvic pain (40%), tubal infertility (10.8%), ectopic pregnancy (9.1%)
  • Epididymitis: <5% of men, can cause infertility or chronic inflammation
  • Ophthalmia Neonatorum: Can cause blindness if untreated. Prevent with prophylactic eye drops
  • Disseminated Gonococcal Infection: Skin lesions, septic arthritis, meningitis, endocarditis. Hospitalization required.
  • Fitz-Hugh-Curtis Syndrome: Perihepatic inflammation mimicking cholecystitis

Partner Management

  • Evaluate and treat all partners from preceding 60 days
  • If no recent partners, treat most recent partner before 60 days
  • Expedited Partner Therapy (EPT) legal in some US states for heterosexual patients
  • Partners should abstain from sex until treatment completed (7 days) and symptoms resolved
  • Provide patient resources and counseling on risk reduction
Reinfection Risk: 7-12% reinfection within 12 months. Emphasize partner treatment and safe sex practices.
Prognosis: Appropriate treatment resolves infection. Key morbidity is infertility, ectopic pregnancy, and chronic pelvic pain from PID. Death rare but possible from disseminated infection.
