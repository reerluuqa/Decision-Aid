Acute Stress Reaction

Clinical Reference | Based on ICD-11 & UK Guidelines
Est. reading time: 7 min
Last updated: Nov 2022
⚠️
CRITICAL DIAGNOSTIC THRESHOLD

Acute Stress Reaction symptoms are generally limited to the first month after a traumatic event. If symptoms persist > 1 month, consider PTSD or Adjustment Disorder.

ICD-11 Definition

What is it?

Transient emotional, somatic, cognitive, or behavioural symptoms resulting from exposure to an event of an extremely threatening or horrific nature.

Note: Distinct from DSM-5 'Acute Stress Disorder'. In UK, ICD-11 is the standard.

High Risk Groups

  • Road casualties (injured or witnesses)
  • Refugees & asylum seekers
  • First responders (Police, Ambulance, Fire)
  • Military personnel
  • Domestic violence survivors

Symptom Clusters (Pattern of Response)

Intrusion (Re-experiencing)

  • Spontaneous memories of event
  • Recurrent dreams/nightmares
  • Flashbacks (intense distress)

Avoidance

  • Avoiding thoughts/feelings triggering reminders
  • Withdrawing from social interaction

Hyperarousal

  • Reckless or aggressive behavior
  • Hypervigilance / Easily startled
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Autonomic signs (tachycardia, sweating)

Mood & Cognitive

  • Being in a daze / confusion
  • Despair, sadness, anger
  • Amnesia, depersonalization, derealisation

Differential Diagnosis

Consider:
  • PTSD
  • Adjustment Disorder
  • Panic Disorder
Rule Out:
  • Schizophrenia (psychotic features)
  • Organic causes (Dementia, head injury)
  • Alcohol misuse / Withdrawal
Co-morbidity:
  • Depression (Low mood common)
  • Physical injury from event

First-Line Management

Watchful Waiting Standard

Symptoms often abate within hours/days. No treatment may be required.

Psycho-education Advised

Advice on relaxation techniques and sleep hygiene.

Problem-solving counselling Limited Evidence

Specific Interventions (Severe Cases)

Trauma-Focused CBT (TF-CBT) Recommended (WHO)

Involves exposure treatment/challenge of negative thoughts. Helpful if causing significant impairment.

EMDR Insufficient Evidence

WHO felt insufficient evidence for 1st month use. Requires trained therapist.

Pharmacological Management

AVOID / CONTRAINDICATED

Red Flags: Meds to Avoid

  • Benzodiazepines: Should NOT be offered to reduce acute traumatic stress symptoms within the first month. Risk of tolerance/dependence.
  • Hypnotics: Should not be offered for insomnia. Use sleep hygiene advice instead.

BNF Note: Benzos indicated only for short-term relief of severe anxiety (lowest dose, shortest time).

SAFE ACTION

Permitted Options

  • Beta-blockers: Can help relieve physical symptoms of acute stress.
  • Profile: Not addictive, not tranquillisers, no drowsiness/performance impact. Can be taken as required.
