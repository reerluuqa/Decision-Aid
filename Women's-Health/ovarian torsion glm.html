Ovarian Torsion

Clinical Overview

Definition: Twisting of the ovary (and/or fallopian tube) on vascular supports, blocking blood flow.

Presentation: Acute severe pelvic/abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting. Often non-specific. High clinical suspicion is key.

Red Flags & Symptoms

Suspect torsion if:

  • ⚠ Sudden onset severe pain
  • ⚠ Nausea / Vomiting (70%)
  • ⚠ Palpable adnexal mass
  • ⚠ Pain radiating to back/flank
  • ⚠ History of fertility treatment
Neonates: Feeding intolerance, vomiting, abdominal distension.

Immediate Actions

1. Prep for Surgery ⚡

Nil by mouth. Refer urgently. Do not delay for starvation if highly suspicious.

2. Laparoscopy ⚡

Preferred method. Detorsion is priority to preserve function.

3. Intra-op Decision

Conservative: Detorsion (even if necrotic look).
Radical: Salpingo-oophorectomy (Post-menopausal or malignancy suspected).

Key Differentials

Ectopic Pregnancy

Missed menses, vaginal bleed. Positive pregnancy test.

Appendicitis

Periumbilical to RLQ pain. Fever, leukocytosis common.

PID

Cervical motion tenderness, discharge, fever. STI history.

Ruptured Cyst

Sudden pain, mid-cycle. No torsion signs on US.

Renal Colic

Flank to groin pain. Haematuria.

Special Considerations

  • Pregnancy: Risk 5:10,000. Highest 6-14 wks. Laparoscopy safe up to 20 wks.
  • Pediatrics: 46% have normal ovary. Conservative detorsion vital.
  • Post-Menopausal: High malignancy risk (22-25%). Oophorectomy often preferred.

Pathophysiology

Enlargement allows rotation. Venous outflow compromised before arterial inflow, leading to oedema and necrosis. Ischaemia can cause peritonitis.
