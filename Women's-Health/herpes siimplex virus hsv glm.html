Immediate Actions (Red Flags)

CNS Involvement

Suspected or confirmed Encephalitis or Meningitis.

High Risk of Mortality

Disseminated / Visceral

Oesophagitis, Hepatitis, Pneumonitis. Common in immunocompromised.

Requires Infectious Disease Consultant.

3rd Trimester Primary

Primary or non-primary first episode in 3rd trimester.

Risk: High vertical transmission.

Diagnosis & Approach

Gold Standard

HSV PCR from swab of active lesion.

  • Higher sensitivity than culture.
  • Distinguishes HSV-1 vs HSV-2.
‚õî Do NOT use PCR for screening asymptomatic patients (intermittent shedding).

Serology

Type-specific Glycoprotein G-based tests only.

Useful for atypical complaints or undiagnosed ulcers.

‚õî Avoid IgM: Not type-specific, cannot distinguish primary vs recurrent.

Acute Management

Genital: 1st Episode

Critical: Start immediately, do not wait for labs.

Reduces neurological complications & duration.

Start < 72h 7-10 Days
Oral Antivirals

Genital: Recurrent

Episodic: Initiate at first symptom (prodrome).

Effective for shortening outbreak severity.

Oral HSV (Herpes Labialis)

Topical creams have minimal benefit.

Oral therapy preferred.

Docosanol/Penciclovir may reduce healing time slightly.

Suppression & Prevention

Daily Suppression

  • Reduces recurrences by 70-80%.
  • Reduces transmission risk by ~50%.

Offer for: Frequent/severe recurrences or to reduce transmission to partners.

Pregnancy Management

Suppressive Therapy: Start at 36 weeks until delivery.

Reduces recurrence at delivery & need for C-section.

Recurrent episode: Treat within 48-72h of onset.

Key Differentials

Syphilis

Single, non-tender ulcer vs Herpes painful.

Chancroid

Single, deep, painful ulcer. (Herpes = multiple painful).

Mpox

Vesiculopustular rash, sequential stages. May involve palms/soles.
