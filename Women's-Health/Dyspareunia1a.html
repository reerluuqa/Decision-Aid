🩺 Dyspareunia Dashboard

Clinical Quick Reference · Reviewed June 2024 · BMJ 2018;361:k2341
📊 ~7.5% sexually active women 🔄 Biopsychosocial aetiology ⚡ Superficial & Deep types
🔍
💡 Dyspareunia = pain caused by any kind of vaginal penetration, not just intercourse. The term is broader than 'painful sex'.

Impact on Patient's Life

💔Cannot have sexual intimacy
🩸Can't use tampons / cups
🔬Avoids smear tests
🏥Can't tolerate gynae exams
👶Fears about having a baby
😞Feels she's not normal

🚪 How Might She Present?

🗣️

Direct Presentations

Explicitly mentions pain
  • History of painful intercourse
  • Relationship problems arising from sexual difficulties
  • Not attending for smear tests or intimate exams
🎭

'Calling Card' Presentations

Hidden behind other complaints

May reflect embarrassment or lack of confidence to disclose sexual issues:

Depression Anxiety Bleeding issues Contraception problems
⚠️ Physical and psychological issues often co-exist. Psychosexual causes are less easily categorised.

🎯 3-step holistic approach: Define the problem → Identify medical/gynae cause → Gather psychosexual info
1

Define the Sexual Problem

▼
  • Be sensitive, non-judgemental
  • Clarify what patient means by 'painful sex' — let her tell the story
  • Establish onset and duration:
⚡ Sudden onset Especially after a trigger → more likely psychosexual
📈 Gradual development More characteristic of a physical problem

📍 Where is the pain?

🔶 At vaginal entrance, start of coitus
Think → dermatosis, vaginismus, vulvodynia
🔴 Pelvic pain, deeper penetration
Think → endometriosis
  • Relationship? Explore any issues voiced (grief, fear, loss, abuse)
2

Identify Medical / Gynaecological Problem

▼
  • Any other vulval symptoms? (itching, burning, dysuria)
  • Any risk of STI?
  • Any gynaecological symptoms? (dysmenorrhoea, abnormal bleeding)
  • Any traumatic obstetric experiences?
3

Gather Psychosexual Info — LOFTI Approach

▼
L Listen Open-ended Qs & brief silences. Language, tone. What's said and not said.
O Observe Body language. Defensive? Childlike? Anxious? Dissociative?
F Feelings Feelings in the room. Your feelings may mirror the patient's subconscious.
T Thinking Your role? (parent, teacher?) Patient's motivation? Why now?
I Interpreting Overall picture — how does the patient come across? Does it fit her story?
🚨 Dissociation (disconnection with self/feelings/body, switching off from reality) can suggest a history of sexual abuse.
⛔ Aim: obtain helpful information, NOT cause further pain. Examination is NOT absolutely necessary if a psychosexual cause is most likely and examination may be traumatic.
🖐️

Abdominal Palpation

  • May reveal a pelvic mass
👁️

Vulval Examination — Look for:

  • Dermatosis: erythema, scarring, fissuring, skin changes
  • Vulvovaginal atrophy: shiny, delicate, thin/pale mucosa
  • Female genital mutilation
🔍

Vaginal & Speculum Examination

  • May not be necessary if clear psychosexual cause & exam may be traumatic
  • Palpate for areas of tenderness (vaginal septum, endometriotic nodules)
  • Use appropriate-size speculum + lots of lubricant
  • Consider swabs if indicated, smear if due

🔬 Further Investigation

💡 If examination is normal, reassurance may help — but if not, use this as an opportunity to explore the patient's view of what is causing her pain.
  • Consider further Ix of positive findings (USS, gynae or derm referral)
  • ✓ Check serum ferritin in patients with pruritus vulvae (BMJ recommendation)
🎯 Individualise the management plan according to likely cause.
🟢

General AdviceFor all patients

🫒Perineal massage with inert oil (e.g. coconut) twice daily — helps reassociate with genitalia, reduces vulvodynia/vaginismus
🧴Vulval health: avoid irritants (soaps, synthetic wipes/sanitary products). Use simple emollients for washing
💧Consider lubricants and topical analgesics (e.g. 5% lidocaine ointment) for intercourse
💊

Condition-Specific TreatmentMatch treatment to diagnosis

Vulvovaginal atrophyTopical oestrogens
Vulval dermatitis / dermatosisSuitable-potency topical steroid + emollient
VulvodyniaMultidisciplinary: psychosexual counselling, physiotherapy, pain management
Pelvic floor dysfunctionRefer women's health physio (NG210, 2021)
If muscle overactivity/tightness, prolapse, or chronic pelvic pain
🧠 This is different from the traditional model. Your role is to facilitate patient understanding through receptive listening & reflective questioning — NOT to diagnose and prescribe.
🔍 Explore background & timing of the problem to find clues regarding aetiology.
🪞 Reflect back thoughts or emotions the patient voices to help her understand her problem.
💬 "You seem very angry when you talk about your labour."
💬 "I wonder what made you feel so tense when I offered you an examination."
🚫 Avoid being overly reassuring or trying to 'correct' the patient's beliefs — explore them instead.
