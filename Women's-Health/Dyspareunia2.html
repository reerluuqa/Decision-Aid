Dyspareunia Clinical Guide

Quick Reference for Primary Care | Reviewed June 2024

At a Glance

Painful vaginal penetration affecting ~7.5% of sexually active women. Not just "painful intercourse"—impacts tampon use, smear tests, intimacy, and fertility.

Superficial

Dermatoses, vaginismus, vulvodynia, atrophy, infection, trauma

Deep

Endometriosis, PID, fibroids, IBS, pelvic floor dysfunction

Key Approach

Holistic: Define problem → Identify physical cause → Psychosexual assessment (LOFTI)

Safety

Examination aims to inform, NOT cause pain. Skip if psychosexual cause likely.

! Definition & Wider Implications
▼

Dyspareunia = pain caused by any vaginal penetration, not just penile-vaginal intercourse.

Avoid heteronormative assumptions. "Painful sex" assumes heterosexual intercourse is the primary issue.

Beyond Sexual Intercourse:

  • Limits ability to have sexual intimacy with anyone
  • Prevents tampon/moon cup use → period management issues
  • Smear test avoidance (fear/cannot tolerate)
  • Gynaecological examinations/procedures impossible
  • Fertility concerns → may avoid having children
  • Psychological impact: "Doesn't feel normal"
S Superficial Dyspareunia
▼
Entrance/Introital Pain
  • Recurrent candida
  • Herpes
  • Bartholin's abscess/cyst
  • Vulval dermatoses (dermatitis/lichen sclerosis)
  • Postnatal atrophy (low oestrogen, especially breastfeeding)
  • Vaginismus (primary or secondary)
  • Postmenopausal vulvovaginal atrophy
  • Neuropathic pain/vulvodynia (post-obstetric/perineal trauma)
  • Neurological disease
  • Vaginal structural abnormalities (septum, scarring)
  • Female genital mutilation
  • Malignancy or post-radiotherapy
D Deep Dyspareunia
▼
Pelvic/Deep Pain
  • Endometriosis (cardinal sign)
  • Pelvic pathology (fibroids, cysts, malignancy)
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction (mechanical, anatomical, prolapse)
  • Pelvic congestion syndrome (postcoital ache)*

Non-Gynaecological:

  • Irritable bowel syndrome
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Recurrent UTI
  • Bladder pain syndrome
  • Iatrogenic: post-surgical scarring, post-radiotherapy

*Pelvic congestion syndrome remains controversial

P How Patients Present
▼

Direct Presentation

  • Specific history of painful intercourse/penetration
  • Relationship problems (cause and effect)
  • Smear test non-attendance

"Calling Cards" (Indirect)

  • Depression or anxiety
  • Bleeding issues
  • Contraception problems
  • Reflects embarrassment or lack of confidence to discuss sexual issues
A Assessment: Holistic Approach
▼

Three aims: 1) Define sexual problem 2) Identify medical/gynaecological problem 3) Gather psychosexual information

Focused History

Define the Problem

  • Sensitive, non-judgemental approach
  • Let her tell the story
  • Onset: Sudden (psychosexual) vs Gradual (physical)
  • Location: Entrance (dermatosis/vaginismus) vs Deep (endometriosis)
  • Relationship context (grief, fear, loss, abuse)

Medical Screen

  • Other vulval symptoms (itching, burning, dysuria)
  • STI risk?
  • Gynaecological symptoms (dysmenorrhoea, abnormal bleeding)
  • Traumatic obstetric experiences?

The LOFTI Approach (Psychosexual)

Listen

Open questions, brief silences. Note language, tone, anger, sadness. What's said vs unsaid?

Observe

Body language. Defensive? Childlike/regressive? Anxious? Dissociative (disconnected)?

Feelings

Awareness of room's feelings. Doctor's feelings may reflect patient's subconscious.

Thinking

What role are you playing (parent/teacher)? Patient's motivation? Why present now?

Interpreting

Overall picture: How does patient come across? Does it fit the story? Dissociation suggests history of sexual abuse.

! Examination: Safety First
▼
Primary Aim: Obtain helpful information and NOT to cause further pain.

When to Examine

  • Abdominal palpation (pelvic mass?)
  • Vulval inspection (dermatosis, atrophy, FGM)
  • Vaginal/speculum only if necessary

When to SKIP examination:

If clear psychosexual cause likely and examination may be traumatic.

Technique (If Performing)

  • Use appropriate-size speculum
  • Lots of lubricant
  • Palpate for tenderness, septum, endometriotic nodules
  • Swabs if indicated
  • Smear if due

What to Look For

Vulval Signs:

  • Dermatosis: erythema, scarring, fissuring, skin changes
  • Atrophy: shiny, delicate, thin/pale mucosa
  • Female genital mutilation

Internal:

  • Vaginal septum
  • Endometriotic nodules
  • Pelvic mass
Rx Management
▼

Individualise according to likely cause.

General Measures

  • Perineal massage: Twice daily with inert oil (e.g., coconut) to reassociate with genitalia and reduce vulvodynia/vaginismus
  • Vulval health: Avoid irritants (soaps, synthetic wipes), use simple emollients for washing
  • Lubricants and topical analgesics: 5% lidocaine ointment for intercourse

Specific Treatments

  • Atrophy: Topical oestrogens
  • Dermatosis: Suitable-potency topical steroid + emollient
  • Vulvodynia: Multidisciplinary (psychosexual counselling, physiotherapy, pain management)
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction: Refer women's health physiotherapy (NG210, 2021)

Psychosexual Approach

Different from traditional medical model.

  • Role: Facilitate patient understanding through receptive listening and reflective questioning
  • Explore background/timing for aetiology clues
  • Reflect back emotions: "You seem very angry when you talk about your labour"
  • Avoid being overly reassuring or 'correcting' beliefs—explore them
  • Don't try to 'fix' immediately

Training: Institute of Psychosexual Medicine

Further Investigation

  • If examination normal: reassurance may help, OR opportunity to explore patient's view of cause
  • Ultrasound for positive findings
  • Gynaecological/dermatological referral
  • Check serum ferritin in patients with pruritis vulvae (BMJ recommendation)

Summary: Dyspareunia results from psychological, physical and social issues. Differentiate superficial vs deep. Use LOFTI for psychosexual assessment. Examination aims to inform, not cause pain. Tailor management to aetiology—consider coconut oil massage, topical treatments, and psychosexual counselling via IPM.
