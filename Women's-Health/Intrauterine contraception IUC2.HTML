Types of IUC Available

Levonorgestrel IUDs (LNG-IUDs)

Available options: Benilexa®, Levosert®, Mirena® (52mg LNG), Kyleena® (19.5mg LNG), Jaydess® (13.5mg LNG)

  • Duration: 3-8 years depending on device
  • Effective: 7 days after insertion
  • Special considerations:
    • 52mg devices can be used until age 55 if inserted at 45+
    • May reduce menstrual bleeding/pain
    • Can be used for menorrhagia management

Copper IUDs (Cu-IUDs)

Available options: Copper T380 A®, Multiload® Cu375, GyneFix® 330, Novaplus T380® Ag

  • Duration: 5-10 years depending on device
  • Effective: Immediately after insertion
  • Special considerations:
    • Can be used until menopause if inserted at 40+ (with ≥300mm² copper)
    • Effective for emergency contraception
    • No hormonal side effects

Patient Assessment Algorithm

Assessment Flowchart

Patient considering IUC?
Confirm not pregnant (Reasonably certain? See FSRH guidelines)
Discuss contraindications
Absolute: Unexplained vaginal bleeding, pelvic infection, uterine distortion, malignancy, Wilson's disease
Relative: Consider risk factors for STIs, bleeding disorders, severe valvular heart disease
Choose appropriate IUC type
LNG-IUD preferred if: Heavy bleeding, dysmenorrhea, endometriosis, adenomyosis
Cu-IUD preferred if: Non-hormonal desired, immediate efficacy needed, emergency contraception required
LNG-IUD: Any time if reasonably certain not pregnant (additional contraception may be needed)
Cu-IUD: Any time if reasonably certain not pregnant (effective immediately)

Managing Common Problems

Non-visible threads

Check for expulsion/pregnancy/perforation. Consider ultrasound to locate device.

  • If threads visible high in canal: consider thread retrieval
  • If not visible: pregnancy test and ultrasound evaluation

Unscheduled bleeding (LNG-IUD)

Common in first 3-6 months, usually improves with time.

  • Rule out pregnancy, infection, pathology
  • Consider short-course NSAIDs if troublesome
  • Reassure patient that pattern typically improves

Pelvic pain

New-onset pain: assess for infection, perforation, pregnancy.

  • If infection suspected: consider removal + antibiotics
  • If perforation suspected: urgent referral for imaging/surgical removal
  • If mild: analgesia and reassessment

Suspected expulsion

Examine for device, consider ultrasound.

  • If confirmed: discuss alternative contraception
  • If partial expulsion: remove and replace if desired
  • Risk factors: postpartum insertion, nulliparity, previous expulsion

Key Counseling Points

STI Protection

IUC does not protect against sexually transmitted infections. Recommend condoms for STI prevention.

Fertility Return

Fertility returns immediately after removal. No delay in conception after IUD removal.

LNG-IUD Bleeding

Common to have altered bleeding patterns (often lighter/absent periods) especially in first 3-6 months.

Cu-IUD Bleeding

May experience heavier/more painful periods, especially in first few months after insertion.

Follow-up

Return for follow-up if concerns about threads, persistent pain, abnormal bleeding, suspected pregnancy, or expulsion.

Insertion/Removal

Must be inserted and removed by trained healthcare provider. Cannot be self-managed by patient.

When to Refer

Difficult insertion/removal
Suspected uterine perforation
Pregnancy with IUC in situ
Persistent pelvic pain not responding to initial management
Management of complex malpositioned devices
Pelvic infection requiring specialized care
