Definition

Fertility is delayed if a woman of reproductive age has not conceived after 1 year of unprotected vaginal sexual intercourse or therapeutic donor insemination.

Epidemiology

WHO estimates that 1 in 6 people experience infertility in their lifetime (17.5% lifetime prevalence).

In the US, 8.5% of married women aged 15-49 have difficulty getting pregnant after 1 year of trying.

Common Aetiologies
  • Ovulatory dysfunction (14.3%)
  • Diminished ovarian reserve (26.9%)
  • Tubal factor (10.4%)
  • Endometriosis (6.5%)
  • Uterine factors (5.8%)
  • Unexplained infertility
Age Factor
Critical: Fecundity decreases with age and accelerates at 35 years

Probability of pregnancy decreases by about 3% to 5% for every year over 30 years of age.

Diagnostic Approach

The evaluation of infertility focuses on identifying specific pathophysiologies.

First Step: Male evaluation with at least a semen analysis is necessary while evaluating the female partner

Female aetiologies reported in ART procedures include:

  • Tubal factor (10.4%)
  • Ovulatory dysfunction (14.3%)
  • Diminished ovarian reserve (26.9%)
  • Endometriosis (6.5%)
  • Uterine factors (5.8%)
Assessment of Ovulation
History: A history of monthly menstrual cycles with premenstrual symptoms is indicative of ovulation

Women with regular cycles do not routinely require additional tests to confirm ovulation.

If testing is needed:

  • Luteal-phase progesterone: >9.5 nmol/L (>3 ng/mL) indicates ovulation
  • Urinary LH prediction kits: More accurate than basal body temperature
Anatomical Assessment

Transvaginal ultrasound scan (TVUS/S) enables assessment of:

  • Ovaries (follicular development, antral follicle count)
  • Uterine structure (congenital abnormalities, fibroids, polyps)
  • Hydrosalpinges (tubal pathology)

3D ultrasound has emerged as the imaging tool of choice for assessment of the female pelvis.

Strong Risk Factors
Age >35 years

The strongest predictor of pregnancy; fecundity decreases with age due to dwindling oocyte numbers and poorer oocyte quality.

STI History

Chlamydia and gonorrhoea infections affect fertility by causing damage to the genital anatomy.

Body Fat (High/Low)

Very high or very low body fat can lead to ovulatory dysfunction and menstrual irregularities.

Smoking

Chemicals in cigarette smoke accelerate follicular apoptosis and may affect different stages of reproduction.

Auto-immune Disease

Systemic lupus erythematosus, thyroid dysfunction, and inflammatory bowel disease have been associated with infertility.

History of Appendicitis

Inflammation of the appendix can lead to tubal injury because the appendix sits adjacent to the right fallopian tube.

History of C-section

Emergency cesarean birth was associated with a 13% lower probability of subsequent fertility, elective cesarean birth with a 14% lower probability.

Psychiatric Disease

Several dopaminergic antagonist medications can suppress hypothalamic-pituitary function and increase prolactin.

Substance Misuse

Limited evidence suggests marijuana use may be associated with anovulation and menstrual irregularities.

Alcohol/Caffeine

Alcohol consumption may decrease fecundity. Evidence on caffeine's effect is low-quality and conflicting.

Hormonal Testing
Ovulation Confirmation
  • Luteal-phase progesterone: >9.5 nmol/L (>3 ng/mL) indicates ovulation
  • Urinary LH prediction kits: More accurate than basal body temperature
If Anovulation Confirmed
  • FSH and LH: Hyper-gonadotrophic or hypogonadotrophic hypogonadism
  • Oestrogen levels: Assess ovarian function
  • Free testosterone: PCOS or other causes of hyper-androgenism
  • TSH: Thyroid dysfunction
  • Prolactin: Pituitary tumor
Imaging Options
Transvaginal Ultrasound

Enables assessment of pelvic organs, including:

  • Ovaries (follicular development, antral follicle count)
  • Uterine structure (congenital abnormalities, fibroids, polyps)
  • Hydrosalpinges (tubal pathology)
3D Ultrasound

Emerging as the imaging tool of choice for assessment of the female pelvis. Cost effective, non-invasive and highly acceptable to women.

Hysterosalpingo-contrast Sonography (HyCosY)

Uses TVUS with a reflective medium injected transcervically. Avoids radiation exposure with high sensitivity (93%) and specificity (90%).

Management Approach
Key Principle: Management options are diagnosis-dependent, with emphasis on female age

Management options include:

Expectant Treatment

For younger women with less severe infertility factors.

Medical Therapy

For ovulatory dysfunction, endometriosis, etc.

Surgical Therapy

For anatomical issues like tubal blockage, uterine abnormalities.

Assisted Fertility Treatments

For severe infertility factors or when other treatments fail.

Treatment Algorithm Overview
1
Initial Assessment

Complete history, physical examination, and initial investigations for both partners.

Decision Point: Based on female age and duration of infertility
2
Treatment Based on Diagnosis
  • Ovulatory dysfunction: Lifestyle changes, ovulation induction
  • Tubal factor: Surgical repair or IVF
  • Diminished ovarian reserve: IVF with donor eggs if needed
  • Endometriosis: Medical suppression, surgical excision, or IVF
  • Unexplained infertility: Expectant management, IUI, or IVF
Decision Point: Based on treatment response and duration
3
Advanced Reproductive Technologies

If initial treatments fail or are not appropriate:

  • Intrauterine Insemination (IUI)
  • In Vitro Fertilization (IVF)
  • Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI)
  • Donor eggs or sperm
  • Surrogacy
Age-Specific Considerations
< 35 years: Can try less invasive options for longer
35-37 years: Consider more aggressive treatment earlier
> 37 years: Prompt referral to fertility specialist recommended
> 40 years: Consider IVF with donor eggs
Prognosis

Fertility outcomes are dependent on:

  • Female age: Most important factor
  • Infertility diagnosis: Some causes more treatable than others
  • Duration of infertility: Longer duration decreases success rates
  • Access to assisted fertility treatments: Improves outcomes
