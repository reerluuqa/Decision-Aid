HRT & Osteoporosis QuickRef

Clinical Pearl: Most fragility fractures occur in people WITHOUT osteoporotic T-scores. Use FRAX or Q-Fracture for risk assessment, adjusted for additional factors.
Quick Triage: When to Assess Fracture Risk ▼

Select patient age group for immediate guidance:

< 50 years Perimenopause / Early menopause
50-64 years Postmenopausal
≥ 65 years Older postmenopausal

Age <50: Risk Assessment

Do NOT routinely assess unless MAJOR risk factors present:
  • Untreated premature ovarian insufficiency (menopause <40y)
  • Previous fragility fracture
  • Current/frequent oral/systemic steroid use

Action If any above present → Assess risk + consider DEXA

Age 50-64: Risk Assessment

Consider assessment if ANY risk factors present (see Risk Factors tab)

Use FRAX or Q-Fracture. Adjust FRAX for:

  • Type 1 or 2 Diabetes
  • Parkinson's disease
  • Recurrent falls
  • Height loss ≥4cm / Thoracic kyphosis

Age ≥65: Risk Assessment

Routinely assess ALL women (NICE CG146)

Calculate fracture risk regardless of other risk factors.

Next Step DEXA scan if indicated by risk score

Key Definitions ▼
Osteoporosis:

T-score ≤ -2.5 at femoral neck

Prevalence: 2% at 50y → 25% at 80y
Fragility Fracture:

Fracture following low-level trauma

Common sites: spine, hip, wrist. Hip fractures: 20% fatal, 50% cause permanent disability
Lifestyle Advice (SIGN) ▼
  • Stop smoking
  • Alcohol <14 units/week
  • Regular weight-bearing exercise
  • Balance/flexibility training (falls prevention)
  • Healthy weight + balanced diet
  • Adequate calcium & vitamin D

HRT: Bone Protection ▼
Licensed for: Osteoporosis prophylaxis (not treatment)

Mode of Action

Reduces rate of bone turnover → antiresorptive effect

Key Evidence

  • Cochrane: Reduced hip, vertebral & any fracture with oestrogen-only and combined HRT
  • Meta-analysis (28 RCTs): RR 0.74 (CI 0.69–0.80) for total fractures
  • Effect is dose and duration related
  • Benefit declines after discontinuation (may persist longer-term in some studies)
When to Prescribe HRT for Bones ▼
Offer only if benefits outweigh risks for individual woman

First-line for:

  • POI (menopause <40y) – all guidelines agree
  • Early menopause (40-45y) per BMS

Consider for:

  • Postmenopausal ≤60y at low risk of malignancy/thrombosis (NOGG)
  • Younger postmenopausal women not meeting bisphosphonate threshold (SIGN)
  • Women >60y benefiting from HRT for symptoms (BMS)
Stop/reassess at age 60 (NOGG) – switch to alternative osteoporosis treatment if needed

Guideline Comparison: HRT for Osteoporosis ▼
Guideline HRT Indication for Osteoporosis Key Constraints
NICE NG23 (2015) • Management of POI (<40y)
• Treat menopausal symptoms (inform of fracture benefit as secondary) 		Not first-line for established osteoporosis in standard menopause
SIGN 142 (2021) • Consider for prevention of fractures in younger postmenopausal women
• Tibolone alternative 		• Only after individual risk vs benefit assessment
• If threshold for bisphosphonates not met
• Lowest effective dose, shortest time
NOGG (2024) • First-line option in postmenopausal women ≤60y at low risk of malignancy/thromboembolic disease • Consider stopping at age 60y after risk assessment
• Reassess fracture risk when stopped; offer alternative if appropriate
BMS Consensus (2023) • First-line for POI and early menopause (40-45y)
• Consider for prevention in menopausal/postmenopausal including >60y if benefiting from HRT for symptoms 		Individualized assessment for women >60y
Summary: All guidelines support HRT for bone protection in younger women (especially POI). NOGG specifically positions HRT as first-line prevention up to age 60 in appropriate candidates. NICE focuses on symptom management with bone benefit as secondary.
