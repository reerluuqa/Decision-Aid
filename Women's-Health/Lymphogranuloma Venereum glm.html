Lymphogranuloma Venereum

Clinical Decision Support Algorithm - BMJ Best Practice (March 2025)

STI caused by Chlamydia trachomatis serovars L1, L2, L3

üîç

Assessment

Risk evaluation & clinical features

‚ñº

Key Risk Factors

  • Concurrent STIs Strong Risk

    Particularly gonorrhoeal proctitis, genital herpes, syphilis

  • Risky sexual behavior Strong Risk

    Anonymous/casual partners, unprotected intercourse

  • HIV seropositivity Strong Risk

    Meta-analysis: 67-100% of LGV cases in MSM co-infected with HIV

  • Travel to endemic regions Risk

    Southeast Asia, Latin America, Caribbean, Africa

  • Age 20-40 years Risk

    Peak sexual activity period

Clinical Stages

Primary Stage Mild

  • Painless penile or vulvar inflammation
  • Ulceration at inoculation site
  • Spontaneously heals within days
  • Often unnoticed by patient

Secondary Stage Moderate

  • Painful unilateral inguinal/femoral lymphadenopathy
  • Bubo formation (groove sign 20%)
  • Proctocolitis in MSM
  • Fever, malaise, arthralgias
  • Back/abdominal pain

Tertiary Stage Severe

  • Chronic progressive genital oedema
  • Scarring & fibrosis
  • Genital elephantiasis
  • Esthiomene (women)
  • Saxophone penis (men)
  • Strictues & fistulae

Key Diagnostic Factors

Common
  • Presence of risk factors
  • Inguinal lymphadenopathy
  • Fever/malaise/arthralgias
  • Lower abdominal/back pain
Uncommon
  • Genital/anal ulcer
  • Groove sign of Greenblatt
  • Genital elephantiasis/saxophone penis
  • Anogenital sinus tracts/fistulae

Differential Diagnosis

Consider: Syphilis, genital herpes, gonococcal proctitis, chancroid, granuloma inguinale/donovanosis, inflammatory bowel disease

Note: LGV lesions are generally confined to rectum/distal sigmoid colon vs. Crohn's disease

Diagnosis

Laboratory confirmation & criteria

‚ñº

Diagnostic Approach

Definitive Diagnosis: Identification of LGV-specific Chlamydia trachomatis DNA

Step 1: Initial Testing Required

  • NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Test) - detects all C. trachomatis genovars
  • Specimens: Genital ulcers, cervical swabs, rectal swabs (if proctocolitis), bubo aspirates, urethral swabs, urine
  • Note: Cannot distinguish LGV from non-LGV serovars

Step 2: LGV-Specific Testing Confirmatory

  • PCR-based genotyping or real-time PCR for L1, L2, L3
  • Results may take several weeks
  • Presumptive diagnosis if NAAT-positive + clinical syndrome

Step 3: Supportive Tests

  • Gram stain anorectal specimens >10 WBC/hpf suggests proctitis
  • Anoscopy/proctoscopy for proctocolitis evaluation
  • Imaging (CT/MRI) for suspected deep pelvic/rectosigmoid involvement
  • Serology - limited value; high titres supportive but not diagnostic

Co-infection Screening Essential

HIV
Syphilis
Gonorrhea
Herpes
Hepatitis B/C

Key Point: HIV co-infection rate 67-100% in MSM with LGV. Offer PrEP if HIV-negative.

Diagnostic Criteria

CDC Criteria
  • Compatible clinical findings + supportive confirmatory test
  • NAAT-positive for C. trachomatis + clinical syndrome
  • Complement fixation titre >1:64 (in context)
  • Exclusion of other causes
IUSTI Criteria
  • Detection of LGV-specific DNA (two-step: NAAT then LGV-specific NAAT)
  • If molecular testing unavailable: High antibody titre (IgA anti-MOMP) + compatible syndrome

Treatment

Antibiotics & surgical management

‚ñº

First-Line Treatment Preferred

Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID 21 days

Recommended for all stages of LGV

  • High efficacy across all serovars
  • Well-tolerated in most patients
  • May be sufficient for rectal LGV in 7-14 days per limited data, but insufficient evidence for shorter courses

Alternative Regimens

Azithromycin 1 g PO Once weekly √ó 3 weeks

Indications: Pregnancy, lactation, tetracycline allergy

  • Not confirmed in clinical trials but generally accepted
  • Effective in one trial (125 patients): cure rates comparable to doxycycline
  • Use when adherence concerns exist
Erythromycin base 500 mg PO QID 21 days

Indications: Pregnancy, lactation, tetracycline allergy

  • Less preferred due to GI side effects
  • Slower resolution compared to doxycycline

Stage-Specific Management

Primary LGV

21 days doxycycline sufficient

Lesions heal without scarring

Secondary LGV

Aspirate buboes via lateral approach

Avoid incision & drainage to prevent sinus tracts

Tertiary LGV

Surgical reconstruction considered

Several months after antibiotics, when active disease resolved

Surgical Management Select Cases

Indications for Surgery:

  • Advanced anorectal disease (strictures, fistulae)
  • Bowel obstruction
  • Rectovaginal fistula
  • Gross destruction of anal canal/sphincter
  • Persistent genital elephantiasis (esthiomene, saxophone penis)

Timing: Several months after completing antibiotics with evidence of disease resolution

Partner Management Required

Contact anyone exposed within:

  • 60 days to 3 months of symptom onset
  • Direct contact with lesions or discharge

Action: Assessment, counseling, post-exposure prophylaxis (doxycycline 100 mg BID √ó 7 days per US CDC)

European guidelines: Minimum 3 weeks treatment for contacts

Special Populations

HIV Co-infection
  • Same antibiotic regimen
  • Close follow-up required
  • Consider drug interactions
Pregnancy/Lactation
  • Avoid doxycycline
  • Use azithromycin or erythromycin
  • 21-day course required
Asymptomatic Infection
  • 7-21 days doxycycline (expert opinion)
  • European guidelines: minimum 21 days
  • Single-dose azithromycin: 20% failure rate

Follow-up

Monitoring & complications

‚ñº

Monitoring

Test of Cure
  • Recommended: NAAT 3-4 weeks post-treatment
  • European guidelines: Not required if 21-day doxycycline completed
  • If alternatives used: Test-of-cure 4-6 weeks post-treatment
Clinical Resolution
  • Symptom improvement within 7 days
  • Bubo reduction within 2-3 weeks
  • Proctitis symptoms resolve in 1-2 weeks
  • Follow-up until complete resolution

Complications

Increased HIV/STI Susceptibility
HIGH

Ulcerative nature augments HIV transmission risk

Reactive Arthritis
LOW

Association documented with L serovars

Fistulae/Sinus Tracts
HIGH

Bubo rupture leads to scarring & fibrosis

Chronic Lymphedema
LOW

Genital elephantiasis, esthiomene, saxophone penis

Stricture Formation
LOW

Anorectal strictures may require surgery. Rare: bowel perforation ‚Üí sepsis/death

Prognosis

Excellent with early treatment:

  • Full recovery expected
  • Symptoms resolve within 1-3 weeks
  • Microbiological cure rates >95% with doxycycline

Late-stage disease:

  • Chronic genital oedema ‚Üí permanent disfiguration
  • May require reconstructive surgery
  • Proctectomy/colostomy for severe anorectal disease
  • Healing can be prolonged

Prevention & Counseling

Abstinence Period
  • Avoid sexual contact until symptoms resolve
  • Complete full antibiotic course
  • Condoms may not prevent transmission from extensive lesions
No Immunity

Previous infection does not confer immunity

  • Reinfection possible
  • Safe sex practices essential
Partner Notification
  • All contacts within 60 days
  • Direct contact with lesions/discharge
  • Treatment required
