Safeguarding Children

Clinical Decision Support - Recognising Abuse or a Child at Risk

Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk assessment steps

Critical Reminder

Any doctor who suspects child maltreatment has a duty to act. If you believe a child is in immediate danger, act in the child's best interests.

General Principles

Approach

When alerted to possible child abuse:

  • Listen and observe: history, symptoms, signs, disclosures, appearance, behavior, interactions
  • Seek an open, non-judgmental explanation for injuries or concerns
  • Record everything: what was said, observed, by whom, and your concerns
  • Assess level of concern and immediate danger
  • Discuss with colleagues and refer appropriately
Unsuitable explanations include:
  • Inconsistent with child's age, development, or medical condition
  • Inconsistent between carers or changes over time
  • Cultural practices are NOT acceptable excuses for hurting a child

History Taking

Technique

Key principles:

  • Use open, non-judgmental questions ("What happened?") NOT leading questions ("Were you hit?")
  • Where possible, speak with child separately to build trust
  • Consider taking history directly from child if in their best interests (document reasons if without carer consent)
  • Listen to the child: "What is a day like in the life of this child?"
  • Use interpreters from outside the family if needed

Important Note

Children may show no outward signs of abuse and may hide what is happening. Trust your professional judgment.

Examination

Procedure

Examination requirements:

  • Document all findings systematically
  • Record signs on a body map (examples available from NHS Wales/Cardiff University)
  • Obtain consent for child protection examination (child if competent, parent, or court)
  • In emergencies, may examine without explicit consent if in child's best interests—document this decision
  • Assess the whole child and all injuries comprehensively

High-Risk Indicators

Alert

Red flags requiring immediate attention:

  • Frequent attendance or unusual patterns of attendance to healthcare services
  • Change to child's behavior or emotional state
  • Injury with features suggestive of maltreatment
  • Evidence of sexual activity in young children
  • Harmful interactions between child and carer
  • Failure to access medical care appropriately
  • Unsuitable explanations for injuries
  • Presence of risk factors (see below)

Child Abuse Risk Factors

Context

Family/environmental factors:

  • Previous history of child maltreatment in family
  • Domestic violence, marital conflict, history of violent offending
  • Mental health disorders, learning disability, physical illness/disability in carers
  • Drug or alcohol misuse (especially unstable/chaotic)
  • Housing or financial difficulties
  • Disability or chronic illness in child
  • Single parents (especially immature or unsupported)
  • History of animal/pet abuse
  • Children in care system
  • Vulnerable groups: homeless families, asylum seekers, young carers

Self-Reflection Questions

Critical Thinking

When concerns arise, ask yourself:

  • Why am I worried?
  • What is the perceived level of risk?
  • What are the implications of doing nothing or deferring action?
  • What should I do right now?
Remember: Taking action is justified even if abuse is not confirmed, as long as concerns are honestly held and reasonable.

Diagnosis

Recognizing specific types of child maltreatment

Legal Framework

From October 2015, health professionals have a statutory duty to report FGM in girls under 18 to police. Sexual activity with a child <13 years is automatically sexual abuse.

Neglect

Consider/Suspect

Failure to supply basic needs:

  • Frequent and/or severe infestations (scabies, head lice)
  • Regularly inappropriate clothing for weather
  • Evidence of failure to thrive
  • Persistently smelly or dirty child
  • Poor home hygiene affecting health
  • Inadequate provision of food or unsafe living environment

Supervision & Care:

  • Injuries suggestive of inadequate supervision (sunburn, ingestion, near drowning)
  • Cared for by person unable to provide adequate care
  • Failure to administer prescribed medication
  • Repeated failure to attend appointments
  • Persistent failure with immunisations or development reviews
  • Failure to seek treatment for dental pain

Physical Abuse

Suspect

Bruises (suspect unless specified otherwise):

  • Bruising in shape of object (fingertips, hand, ligature, belt, teeth marks)
  • ANY bruising on non-mobile baby (especially facial)
  • Bruising/petechiae without medical cause or suitable explanation
  • Multiple bruises of similar size/shape
  • Bruises at unusual sites: face, eyes, ears, neck 'safe triangle', inner arms, buttocks, abdomen, thighs
  • Bruising suggestive of strangulation
  • Bruising on child not independently mobile

Other injuries:

  • Human bites (other than from another young child)
  • Lacerations/abrations at unusual sites or with unsuitable explanations
  • burns/scalds with inconsistent explanations or recognisable object shapes
  • Fractures (especially different ages, occult fractures, inconsistent explanations)
  • Intracranial injuries in absence of major trauma (especially <3 years)
  • Retinal hemorrhages or eye injuries without major trauma
  • Spinal or visceral injuries without confirmed major accident
  • Oral injuries without adequate explanation
  • Delay in seeking medical care
Fabricated/Induced Illness: Symptoms only in presence of one person, multiple specialists involved, poor response to treatment, discrepancy in clinical picture.

Emotional Abuse

Consider/Suspect

Child behaviors:

  • Reported changes in behavior/emotional state without other cause
  • Recurring nightmares with similar themes
  • Extreme fearfulness, withdrawal, or low self-esteem
  • Aggressive behavior inconsistent with age
  • Affection-seeking or attention-seeking behaviors
  • Over-friendliness to professionals/strangers
  • Inappropriate interpersonal interactions
  • Extreme emotional responses (tantrums in school-aged children)
  • Dissociative behaviors
  • Self-harming behaviors
  • Running away from home
  • Inappropriate bedwetting/soiling (secondary)
  • Delayed development (physical, mental, emotional)

Parent/Carer behaviors:

  • Punishment for involuntary bedwetting
  • Rejecting or scapegoating a child
  • Inappropriate expectations or threats
  • Using child to fulfill adult needs
  • Refusing to allow child to speak to health professional alone
  • Hostility towards child
  • Exposure to frightening events (domestic abuse)
  • Failure to promote appropriate socialization

Sexual Abuse

Suspect/Consider

Physical indicators:

  • Genital or anal bleeding/discharge without suitable explanation
  • Recurrent dysuria without medical cause
  • Foreign bodies in vagina or anus
  • Gaping anus without medical explanation
  • Anogenital injury without suitable explanation
  • Anal fissure (unless medical reason like constipation/Crohn's)
  • Pregnancy in child <13 years (always abuse)
  • STI in child <13 years (except vertical transmission)

Infections and conditions:

  • Hepatitis B or anogenital warts in child <13 (unless clear non-sexual transmission)
  • Hepatitis B or anogenital warts in child 13-15 (unless from consensual peer sex)
  • STI or pregnancy in 16-17 year old if non-consensual, exploited, or power imbalance
  • Sexual activity with child <13 is automatically sexual abuse

Behavioral indicators:

  • Sexualized behavior in prepubertal child
  • Self-harm or running away behaviors
  • Secondary enuresis
IMPORTANT: Genital examination should only be performed by an expert. Immediate referral to specialist services required.

Differential Diagnosis

Exclude

Medical conditions that may mimic abuse:

  • Failure to thrive: Medical causes of growth failure
  • Bruising: Thrombocytopenia, hemophilia, Mongolian blue spots
  • Fractures: Osteogenesis imperfecta, metabolic bone disease, rickets, copper deficiency, osteomyelitis, leukemia
  • Intracranial injuries: Glutaric acidemia (rare cause of subdural hemorrhage)
  • Other: Accidental injuries, normal childhood falls
Always consider alternative explanations but do NOT exclude maltreatment without thorough investigation.

Investigations

When Indicated

Based on presentation:

  • Blood tests: FBC, clotting studies (for bruising/bleeding disorders)
  • Skeletal survey/bone scan: For suspected physical abuse in children <2 years (may be needed in older children)
  • Brain imaging: If head injury suspected clinically, or infants <1 year with any physical abuse injuries
  • Retinal examination: By ophthalmologist for head injury cases
  • Spinal imaging: Part of full skeletal survey; MRI if fracture or spinal cord injury suspected
  • Sexual health tests: STI screening if sexual abuse suspected (by trained clinician)
  • Forensic dentistry: For bite mark interpretation
  • Specific tests: To exclude differential diagnoses

Management

Immediate actions and referral pathways

GMC Guidance

All doctors have a duty to report concerns that a child may be at risk, including when working with adult patients where their children may be at risk.

Immediate Actions

Act Now

If immediate danger is present:

  • Ensure child's immediate safety
  • Do NOT leave child in dangerous situation
  • Contact police if urgent protection needed (can remove child to safety for 72 hours)
  • Gain consent for actions where possible, but act in child's best interests if necessary
  • Document all actions and decisions clearly
Statutory duty: Report FGM in girls <18 to police immediately.

Referral Pathways

Urgent Referral

Who to contact:

  • Social Services: Local authority social services officer on call 24/7 with child protection register access
  • Police: Child abuse investigation units for investigating abuse
  • Named professionals: Hospital/locality child protection leads (doctors/nurses/midwives)
  • Designated professional: Overall responsibility within clinical commissioning group
  • Safeguarding Partners: Multi-agency team (replacing LSCBs)
  • NSPCC: Can initiate child protection investigations

Multi-Agency Approach

Collaboration

Key principles:

  • Share information between agencies (e.g., domestic violence notifications)
  • Always try to gain consent for information sharing
  • Involve senior colleague in decision-making
  • If delayed reporting may benefit child, seek advice from experienced colleague (GP Lead for Child Protection or medical defense organization)
  • Document all advice received and decisions made
  • Follow local safeguarding partnership protocols
Remember: GMC guidance states actions will be justified if concerns are honestly held and reasonable, even if abuse is not confirmed.

Documentation

Essential

What must be documented:

  • Exact words used by child and carers (use quotations)
  • Full description of injuries on body maps
  • Photographs if consent obtained and policy followed
  • Your observations and concerns with reasoning
  • Actions taken and rationale
  • Advice received from senior colleagues or specialists
  • Referrals made and information shared
  • Time, date, and signatures for all entries
Legal requirement: Thorough documentation is essential for child protection investigations and may be used in court proceedings.

Specialist Referrals

Expert Input

When to refer to specialists:

  • Genital examination: Only by appropriately trained clinician
  • Sexual abuse: Specialist sexual assault referral center
  • Forensic dentistry: For bite mark interpretation
  • Ophthalmology: For retinal hemorrhages in head injury
  • Radiology: Skeletal surveys and expert interpretation
  • Child psychiatry/psychology: For emotional abuse assessment
  • Community pediatrics: For developmental assessment
  • Genetics: To exclude conditions like osteogenesis imperfecta

Legal Considerations

Framework

Key legal points:

  • Children Act 1989 & 2004: Child welfare paramount
  • Statutory duty to report FGM in under-18s to police
  • GMC duties apply to all doctors, including those working with adults whose children may be at risk
  • Police can remove child to safety for 72 hours
  • Local authorities have responsibility for safety and welfare
  • NSPCC authorized to initiate child protection proceedings
  • Different UK nations have slightly different agencies/protocols
Consent: May examine without consent in emergencies if in child's best interests. Always document decisions.

Follow-up

Ongoing monitoring, prevention, and support

Prognosis & Long-term Outcomes

Long-term Impact

Without intervention:

  • Recurrent or escalating abuse (can be fatal)
  • Adverse effects on health, well-being, and development
  • Physical, emotional, and social effects can be lifelong

Adult consequences of childhood abuse:

  • Anxiety and depression
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder
  • Substance misuse
  • Self-destructive, aggressive, or antisocial behavior
  • Poor parenting and relationship difficulties
  • Employment difficulties
  • Disability or physical scarring
  • Effects of STIs, including HIV
  • Increased risk of criminal involvement

Prevention Strategies

Proactive

Primary prevention:

  • Early identification and support of vulnerable children and families
  • Targeted early intervention services
  • Information sharing between agencies (e.g., domestic violence notifications)
  • Education and training of all healthcare staff
  • Assess family impact when parent/carer is ill; enlist extra support
  • For children with additional needs: use Early Assessment Framework (EAF) in England
Protective factors: Strong family bonds, social support, access to healthcare, parental resilience, and child development knowledge.

Ongoing Monitoring

Surveillance

After initial concern:

  • Regular review appointments
  • Monitor attendance patterns
  • Observe parent-child interactions over time
  • Track developmental milestones
  • Liaise with health visitors, school nurses, and teachers
  • Review emergency department attendances
  • Monitor siblings and other children in household
  • Follow up on referrals and ensure actions taken
Re-referrals: Highest level since 2015. Always review previous concerns when new issues arise.

Support Services

Resources

Key organizations:

  • NSPCC: National child protection helpline (0808 800 5000) and Childline (0800 1111)
  • Local Safeguarding Partners: Multi-agency coordination
  • Named/Designated Professionals: Clinical advice and support
  • Police: Child abuse investigation units
  • Social Services: 24/7 referral and child protection register access
  • Family support services: Early help and intervention
  • Mental health services: CAMHS for child and family
  • Specialist centers: For sexual abuse, fabricated illness

Staff Training & Development

Mandatory

Training requirements:

  • Level 1: All staff (basic awareness)
  • Level 2: Clinicians with child contact
  • Level 3: Paediatricians and GPs with child protection responsibilities
  • Level 4: Named and designated professionals
  • Regular updates and refresher training
  • Local safeguarding board training programs
  • BMJ Learning child protection modules
  • RCGP and RCPCH safeguarding courses
Stay updated: Guidelines reviewed regularly. Next review due November 2026.

When to Escalate

Red Flags

Immediate escalation triggers:

  • Child discloses significant abuse
  • Life-threatening injury or severe neglect
  • Non-mobile baby with any injury
  • Suspected sexual abuse with immediate risk
  • Parent preventing child protection examination
  • Child abandoned or parent absconding
  • Previous child death in family from abuse
  • Multiple concerns across agencies
  • Professional disagreement about risk
  • Regression after previous improvement
Emergency: Contact police (999) if child in immediate danger. Use local escalation policy for urgent concerns.
