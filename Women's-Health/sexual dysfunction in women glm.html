Diagnostic Rule of Thumb

Symptoms must persist minimum 6 months, occur in 75%-100% of encounters, and cause clinically significant distress.

üí° Clinical Key Points

Primary Correlates: Dysfunction correlates most strongly with poor mental health and negative feelings towards partner(s).

Hormones: Serum sex hormone levels do not reliably correlate with dysfunction.

Normal Aging: Do not mistake normal age-related or relationship-duration changes for disorder.

Desire vs. Arousal: Women often engage for intimacy; desire is often triggered during activity (responsive desire).

‚ö° FOD: Orgasmic Disorder

Must experience all or almost all (approx. 75%-100%) occasions:

  • Marked delay in, marked infrequency of, or absence of orgasm
  • OR Markedly reduced intensity of orgasmic sensations

Note: Anxiousness during sex is a major factor for FOD.

üö® Strong Risk Factors

High prevalence/impact associations:

  • Depression: Prevalence 50-80%. Past history predicts current issues even if treated.
  • Antidepressant Use: SSRIs can affect 70% of women (desire, arousal, delay orgasm).
  • Anxiety: Cognitive distraction is a major aetiology.
  • Cancer History: Especially breast/gynecologic. Chemotherapy and body image issues are key drivers.

  • Endometriosis (73% prev.)
  • Hyperprolactinaemia
  • Renal failure

Psychosocial

  • Relationship quality (primary driver)
  • Partner sexual dysfunction
  • Past trauma/abuse
  • Post-partum state
  • Low self-esteem
‚úÖ Management Approach

First-line treatment is typically non-pharmacological.

  • Psycho-education: Normalize responsive desire.
  • CBT & Sex Therapy: Address negative thoughts and distractions.
  • Mindfulness: Improve focus on sexual stimuli.
  • Mitigate Risk Factors: Review meds, treat depression.
