Dysmenorrhoea Management Algorithm

A concise clinical reference guide for the assessment, diagnosis, and management of dysmenorrhoea in primary care

Assessment & Diagnosis

History

  • Pain characteristics: Type, timing, duration, severity, relation to menstruation
  • Menstrual history: Cycle length, regularity, flow volume
  • Associated symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, fatigue
  • Gynaecological symptoms: Dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding, discharge
  • Risk factors: Family history, BMI <20, smoking, stress
  • Medical/obstetric history: Previous treatments, pregnancy plans

Examination

  • All women: Abdominal examination
  • Pelvic exam: Including speculum (except in young sexually inactive women with classic primary dysmenorrhoea)

Investigations (if indicated)

  • Ultrasound: If secondary causes suspected
  • Swabs: If STI risk (pain + discharge/abnormal bleeding)
  • Pregnancy test: If pregnancy possible

Differentiate Primary vs Secondary

Primary Dysmenorrhoea (likely if)

  • Pain starts 6-12 months after menarche
  • Cramping lower abdominal pain (may radiate to back/thighs)
  • Pain begins before menstruation, lasts â‰¤72 hours
  • Non-gynaecological symptoms present (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
  • Normal pelvic examination

Secondary Dysmenorrhoea (likely if)

  • Pain starts after years of painless periods
  • Pain not consistently related to menstruation
  • Other gynaecological symptoms present
  • Abnormal pelvic examination (though normal doesn't exclude)

Diagnostic Pathway

Primary Dysmenorrhoea

No identifiable pelvic pathology

Secondary Dysmenorrhoea

Underlying pelvic pathology present

Management

Primary Dysmenorrhoea

First-line

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400mg 8-hourly, naproxen 500mg 12-hourly) - start at onset
  • Paracetamol 1g 6-hourly if NSAIDs contraindicated

Second-line

  • Combined hormonal contraception (if contraception needed)
  • Progestogen-only contraceptives (if estrogen contraindicated)

Non-pharmacological

  • Heat therapy
  • Exercise
  • TENS machine
  • Dietary modifications

Secondary Dysmenorrhoea

Treat Underlying Cause

  • Endometriosis: Refer to gynaecology
  • Fibroids: Refer for management
  • PID: Antibiotics + follow-up
  • IUD-related: Consider removal if persistent

Symptomatic Relief

  • NSAIDs or paracetamol as for primary dysmenorrhoea

Referral Criteria

Refer to Specialist When:

  • Suspected secondary dysmenorrhoea
  • Inadequate response to treatment
  • Severe symptoms affecting quality of life
  • Abnormal pelvic examination
  • Diagnostic uncertainty

Follow-up

Recommended Follow-up Schedule:

  • Review response to treatment at 3 months
  • Reassess diagnosis if symptoms persist/change
  • Consider referral if inadequate response
