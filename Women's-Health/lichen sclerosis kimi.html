Lichen Sclerosus

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference Tool

PRO REFERENCE

Overview

Definition: Chronic inflammatory dermatosis affecting anogenital skin (women) / glans & prepuce (men). Old terms: BXO (men), kraurosis vulvae.

Aetiology: Likely autoimmune in genetically predisposed patients. Association with thyroid disease (women) and smoking/diabetes (men).

Epidemiology:
  • Women: ~3% prevalence (Peaks: Prepubertal & Postmenopausal).
  • Men: ~0.07% prevalence (Peaks: Young boys & Adults).
  • Boys with phimosis: 12-100% incidence.

Red Flags & Actions

  • Malignancy Risk (SCC): Lifetime risk of 4-5% for both sexes. Biopsy any persistent lump, thickening, or non-healing ulcer.
  • Biopsy Indications: Essential if lesions do not respond to treatment, presentation is atypical (young adults, pigmented, extragenital), or if malignancy is suspected.
  • Child Protection: Signs in girls can mimic sexual abuse (ecchymosis). Diagnosis of LS does NOT exclude abuse. Trauma can be a trigger.

Differential Diagnosis

Vitiligo Candida infection Lichen Planus Morphoea VIN / Bowen's Disease Graft-versus-host disease

Note: LS spares the vagina and cervix (unlike other conditions). Perianal involvement is common in women (~30%) but rare in men.

Clinical Presentation

Feature Female Male
Key Symptom Itch (worse at night), Pain/Dyspareunia Soreness, Phimosis, Poor urinary stream
Signs White "figure-of-eight" patches (vulva/anus). Ecchymosis. Labial shrinking/fusing. White patches on glans/prepuce. Meatal scarring/narrowing. Ballooning on urination.
Complications Scarring (introitus narrowing), Clitoral pseudocyst, Constipation (fissures). Phimosis, Urethral stricture (20%), Urinary retention.

Management Plan

Female Protocol
Male Protocol
First Line: Clobetasol Propionate 0.05%

(Use ointment base. 30g tube should last 12 weeks)

1 Month 1: Apply once daily (at night)
2 Month 2: Apply on alternate nights
3 Month 3: Apply twice weekly
4 Maintenance: As-required use
  • Review at 3 months.
  • If symptoms return, step back up to effective frequency.
  • AVOID: Oestrogen or Testosterone creams (ineffective/unsafe).
First Line: Ultra-potent topical steroids

(Clobetasol propionate 0.05% applied once daily)

1 Apply daily until remission achieved
2 Gradually reduce / intermittent use (e.g. weekly)
  • Circumcision: Recommended if phimosis exists and NO response to steroids after 1-3 months.
  • Treat secondary infection if present.
  • Urethral strictures may require graft repair.
General Advice:
  • Wash with bland emollients (e.g., aqueous cream).
  • Avoid irritants and tight clothing.
  • Use lubricants if necessary.

Prognosis & Follow-up

  • Women: Chronic condition. Symptom remission achievable (98% compliant), but maintenance often needed.
  • Men: Higher chance of cure. 50-60% cured by steroids, >75% cured by circumcision.
  • Girls: 75% prepubertal cases continue to need treatment after menarche.
  • Long-term: Annual review in primary care for stable patients.
