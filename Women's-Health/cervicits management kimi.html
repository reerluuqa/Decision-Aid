üìã Clinical History
Essential

Obtain comprehensive history focusing on:

  • Vaginal discharge characteristics (color, odor, consistency)
  • Presence of post-coital bleeding or intermenstrual bleeding
  • Dyspareunia or pelvic pain
  • Recent sexual activity and new partners
  • Recent antibiotic use or douching
  • Contraceptive method (IUD, spermicides)
  • Pregnancy status
  • History of STIs

Important: Offer STI screening to all sexually active patients with cervicitis symptoms

üî¨ Physical Examination
Required

Perform speculum examination to assess:

  • Mucopurulent discharge from cervical os
  • Cervical erythema and edema
  • Endocervical friability (bleeds on contact)
  • Presence of ectropion or Nabothian cysts
  • Cervical motion tenderness

Bimanual examination: Assess uterine/adnexal tenderness or masses

‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flag Assessment
URGENT

Immediate escalation required for:

  • Severe lower abdominal pain with fever
  • Signs of pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)
  • Pregnancy with significant bleeding or pain
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise)
  • Immunocompromised status with severe symptoms
üß™ Initial Investigations
Recommended
  • Pregnancy test (if reproductive age)
  • NAAT for Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae
  • Wet mount microscopy for trichomonas, BV, candida
  • Urinalysis if urinary symptoms present
  • HIV and syphilis screening (if indicated)

Diagnosis
‚ñº
üéØ Diagnostic Criteria
Clinical Diagnosis

Cervicitis is diagnosed when ONE or MORE of the following are present:

  • Mucopurulent discharge from cervix
  • Endocervical bleeding on contact
  • ‚â•10 WBC per high-power field on cervical secretions
üîç Microbiological Diagnosis
Test-Based

Common causative organisms:

  • Chlamydia trachomatis (most common bacterial cause)
  • Neisseria gonorrhoeae
  • Trichomonas vaginalis
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV)
  • Human papillomavirus (HPV)
  • Mycoplasma genitalium

Note: Up to 50% of cervicitis cases have no identifiable pathogen

ü§î Differential Diagnosis
Consider
  • Physiological ectropion (normal variant)
  • Cervical polyp
  • Cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN)
  • Cervical cancer (especially with post-coital bleeding)
  • Bacterial vaginosis
  • Vulvovaginal candidiasis
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
üí° Special Considerations
High-Risk Groups
  • Pregnancy: Urgent treatment needed to prevent complications
  • Adolescents: Higher risk for STIs, ensure confidential care
  • HIV positive: May have atypical presentation
  • Postmenopausal: Consider atrophic changes vs infection

Treatment
‚ñº
ü¶† Antimicrobial Therapy
Empiric Treatment

First-line regimen (cover chlamydia & gonorrhea):

  • Doxycycline 100 mg PO twice daily for 7 days
  • PLUS Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose

Alternative if chlamydia is excluded:

  • Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose only

For trichomonas (if identified):

  • Metronidazole 500 mg PO twice daily for 7 days
Note: Adjust ceftriaxone to 1g if patient >150kg
ü§∞ Pregnancy Management
Special Regimen

Pregnant patients require different treatment:

  • Azithromycin 1 g PO single dose PLUS
  • Amoxicillin 500 mg PO three times daily for 7 days PLUS
  • Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose

Avoid doxycycline in pregnancy - risk of fetal harm

üë• Partner Management
Required
  • Recommend STI screening for all sexual partners within last 60 days
  • Empiric treatment for chlamydia & gonorrhea recommended
  • Advise abstinence from sexual activity until treatment completed
  • Provide expedited partner therapy where legally permissible
üíä Adjunctive Therapy
Supportive
  • Analgesia for pain/discomfort (paracetamol/NSAIDs)
  • Avoid douching and irritants during treatment
  • Probiotics may help restore vaginal flora
  • Consider testing for other STIs (HIV, syphilis)
‚ö†Ô∏è Treatment Failure
Re-evaluate

If symptoms persist after treatment:

  • Review compliance and re-examine
  • Consider re-testing for chlamydia/gonorrhea
  • Test for less common causes (Mycoplasma, HSV, T. vaginalis)
  • Evaluate for PID or other complications
  • Consider gynecology referral

Follow-up
‚ñº
üìÖ Follow-up Timeline
Routine
  • 1-2 weeks: Clinical review if symptoms persist
  • 3 months: Test of cure for chlamydia/gonorrhea if symptoms persist
  • 3 months: Repeat STI screening (risk of reinfection)
  • 6-12 months: Routine cervical screening (if due)
üéØ Test of Cure
Recommended

Indications for test of cure:

  • Pregnancy
  • Persistent symptoms after treatment
  • Concern about compliance
  • Complicated infection

Timing: At least 3-4 weeks after treatment completion

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention Counseling
Education
  • Consistent condom use to reduce STI risk
  • Limit number of sexual partners
  • Regular STI screening if at risk
  • HPV vaccination (if eligible)
  • Avoid douching (disrupts normal flora)
  • Complete full antibiotic course
‚ö†Ô∏è Complications to Monitor
Watch For
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): 10-40% risk if untreated
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Increased risk after PID
  • Infertility: Tubal damage from infection
  • Chronic pelvic pain: Ongoing inflammatory process
  • Preterm delivery: If pregnant with infection
  • HIV transmission: Increased risk with STI co-infection
üè• Referral Criteria
Gynecology

Refer to specialist if:

  • Unclear diagnosis after initial evaluation
  • Severe or recurrent disease
  • Signs of cervical pathology (mass, atypical bleeding)
  • Recalcitrant symptoms despite treatment
  • Need for colposcopy or biopsies
  • Pregnant patients with complications
  • Persistent post-coital bleeding
üìä Documentation
Best Practice
  • Record symptoms and examination findings
  • Document STI screening and results
  • Note treatment provided and rationale
  • Partner management advice given
  • Follow-up plan established
  • Confidentiality and consent discussed
