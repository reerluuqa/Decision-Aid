History Taking

â–¶

Key elements to elicit:

  • Age at presentation (median age for breast cancer diagnosis is 63 years)
  • Duration and rate of growth of the mass
  • Associated symptoms: pain, nipple discharge, skin changes
  • Menstrual status and hormonal therapy use
  • Family history of breast/ovarian cancer, age at diagnosis
  • Prior breast biopsies or history of atypical hyperplasia
  • BRCA1/2 mutation status if known
High Risk BRCA+

Physical Examination

â–¶

Complete bilateral breast examination:

  • Inspect both breasts for size variation, skin changes (peau d'orange), nipple inversion/excoriation
  • Palpate mass characteristics: size, texture, mobility, tenderness, borders
  • Examine in both upright and supine positions
  • Assess axillary, supraclavicular, and infraclavicular lymph nodes
  • Check for chest wall involvement (hands-on-hips maneuver)

Suspicious features: Irregular, fixed masses with skin/nipple changes

Key Differential Diagnoses

â–¶

Common Benign Conditions:

  • Fibroadenoma: Smooth, rubbery, mobile mass in women <40 years
  • Fibrocystic changes: Multiple, tender masses fluctuating with cycle
  • Simple cyst: Anechoic, well-circumscribed on ultrasound
  • Fat necrosis: Firm mass after trauma/surgery, may mimic cancer
  • Breast abscess: Painful, fluctuant mass with fever in lactating women

Suspicious/Malignant Features:

Irregular shape Spiculated margins Height > width Microcalcifications

Treatment

Management pathways based on diagnosis

â–¼

Benign Lesions

â–¶

Management based on type:

  • Simple cysts: Observation or ultrasound-guided aspiration if symptomatic
  • Fibroadenomas: Observation if <3cm and stable; surgical excision if large, growing, or patient preference
  • Complex cysts: Core needle biopsy to rule out malignancy
  • Fat necrosis: Usually observation; biopsy if suspicious features
  • Atypical hyperplasia: Vacuum-assisted excision or surgical excision (15% upstaged to DCIS)
Observation Biopsy Excision

Malignant Lesions

â–¶

Multidisciplinary approach required:

  • Surgical options: Breast-conserving surgery (lumpectomy) vs mastectomy based on tumor size/location
  • Axillary staging: Sentinel lymph node biopsy or axillary dissection
  • Adjuvant therapy: Chemotherapy, radiation, hormone therapy based on receptor status and stage
  • Neoadjuvant therapy: Consider for large tumors to enable breast conservation
  • DCIS management: Surgical excision Â± radiation; tamoxifen to reduce recurrence risk

Special Considerations

  • Pregnant/Lactating: Ultrasound preferred; biopsy safe during pregnancy
  • Male breast cancer: Presents later; same diagnostic approach
  • Metastatic disease: Rare (0.5-2%); treat primary cancer
  • Inflammatory breast cancer: Urgent referral, skin changes without discrete mass

Follow-up

Surveillance and ongoing care protocols

â–¼

Post-Treatment Surveillance

â–¶

After benign diagnosis:

  • Clinical breast exam at 6 months if BI-RADS 3 or atypical features
  • Annual screening mammography if age-appropriate
  • Patient education on self-examination and when to return

After breast cancer treatment:

  • Clinical exam every 3-6 months for 3 years, then 6-12 months for next 2 years
  • Annual mammography of preserved/contralateral breast
  • Consider MRI for high-risk patients (dense tissue, BRCA+)
  • Monitor for recurrence: local, regional, distant
  • Manage treatment side effects: lymphedema, hormone therapy symptoms
Annual Mammo 3-6 Month Exams

Patient Education & Counseling

â–¶
  • Risk factor modification: Weight control, alcohol moderation, smoking cessation
  • BRCA testing: Offer to high-risk patients and families
  • Hormone therapy: Review risks/benefits with menopausal patients
  • Genetic counseling: For strong family history or atypical lesions
  • Psychosocial support: Anxiety, body image, survivorship issues
