IMMEDIATE REFERRAL REQUIRED

Refer urgently to Accident & Emergency if any of these features are present:

  • Altered level of consciousness
  • Focal neurological deficit
  • Midline cervical tenderness
  • Immediate onset of neck pain
  • Age 65 years or older
  • Dangerous mechanism of injury
  • Unable to walk or sit following injury
  • Significant head or facial injury
Initial Assessment
History
Take detailed medical history, including circumstances of injury
Assess pain intensity (Visual Analogue Scale)
Ask about occupational history and previous injuries
Screen for anxiety or depression symptoms
Physical Examination
Apply Canadian C-spine rule for people ≤65 years
Assess active range of motion (45° rotation)
Evaluate for neurological involvement
Palpate neck for tenderness
Quebec Task Force Classification (WAD Grades)
Grade I

Neck pain with no objective physical signs

Grade II

Neck pain with objective signs, no neurological involvement

Grade III

Neck pain with neurological involvement

Grade IV

Neck pain with fracture or dislocation

Common Signs & Symptoms
Neck pain (84-100%)
Headache (60-83%)
Reduced neck movement
Muscular spasm
Stiffness
Sleep disturbance
Fatigue
Dizziness
Paraesthesia
Memory loss
Impaired concentration
Temporomandibular joint pain
First-Line Management
Recommended Actions
Provide reassurance about expected recovery (2-3 months)
Encourage early return to normal activities
Promote early mobilization
Offer oral analgesics (ibuprofen, paracetamol, codeine)
Consider physiotherapy referral for multimodal treatment
What to Avoid
Rest and immobilization
Use of soft collars
Anti-seizure medications
Antidepressants
Muscle relaxants
When to Refer
Condition Action
Serious injury suspected
Pain intensity >5/10 on VAS
Disability >15/50 on NDI
Poor expectation of recovery
Post-traumatic stress symptoms
Follow-up Schedule
7 Days

Reassess VAS and NDI. If high or unchanged, review treatment and consider other options.

3 Weeks

If VAS and NDI unchanged, consider more detailed assessment and referral to specialist.

6 Weeks

If no resolution of symptoms, refer to specialist and consider psychologist referral if IES score >25.

12 Weeks

If symptoms resolved, stop treatment. If still improving, continue with focus on active participation.

6-12 Months

Review intermittently until symptoms resolved. If not improving, consider referral to pain clinic.

Prognosis
Predictors of Poor Recovery
  • High initial pain intensity
  • High frequency of healthcare utilization
  • Post-traumatic stress symptoms
  • Negative expectations of recovery
  • Self-rated collision severity
  • Decreased initial neck range of motion
  • Pain-related depression or kinesiophobia
Recovery Timeline
  • 40%: Some resolution by 6 weeks
  • 50%: Substantial improvement by 12 weeks
  • 40%: Complete resolution by 12 weeks
  • 50%: Full recovery within 6 months
  • 20-40%: Risk of developing chronic pain
