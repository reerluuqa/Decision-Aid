Acute Torticollis - Clinical Reference

Last updated: October 2023

RED FLAGS - URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED

Immediate specialist assessment needed for malignancy, infection, cervical myelopathy, or severe neurological symptoms

Red Flags & Emergency Signs

Fever, night sweats

Unexplained weight loss

Neurological deficits

Paresis, sensory changes, altered reflexes

History of trauma

Especially in osteoporosis risk

Severe headache

With photophobia or nausea/vomiting

History of cancer

Or inflammatory arthritis, immunosuppression

Age factors

Onset <20 years or >55 years

Assessment Approach

Clinical Assessment Steps

1
Take detailed medical history (symptoms, occupation, medical conditions)
2
Examine neck appearance, range of motion, and palpate for tenderness
3
Evaluate for neurological involvement (sensation, strength, reflexes)
4
Consider Spurling test (if no contraindications) to exclude radiculopathy
5
Check for nuchal rigidity (Kernig's/Brudzinski's signs) if meningitis suspected

Key Clinical Features

Typical Presentation
Atypical Features
Sudden onset

Severe unilateral pain with neck deviation to one side

Movement restriction

Painful and limited neck movements

Palpable findings

Diffuse tenderness with muscle spasm, possible trigger points

History

No trauma, but may have cold exposure or unusual positioning

Associated symptoms

Headache may be present in some cases

Duration

Usually resolves within 24-48 hours, up to a week in some cases

Recurrence

Common to experience recurrent episodes

Management Recommendations

Analgesia

NSAIDs (ibuprofen), paracetamol, or codeine as needed

Physiotherapy

Consider referral for specialized treatment

Temperature therapy

Heat or cold packs to reduce pain and spasms

Sleep position

Use a low, firm pillow

Gentle exercises

Neck rotations and stretches when pain allows

Avoid

Cervical collars (limit movement), driving/biking

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Distinguishing Features
Acute disc prolapse Most common cause of severe secondary torticollis
Acute trauma History of injury, whiplash-type presentation
Adverse drug reaction Recent use of antipsychotics, metoclopramide, etc.
Cervical radiculopathy Pain radiating to limb, positive Spurling test
Meningitis Fever, nuchal rigidity, positive Kernig's/Brudzinski's
Malignancy Weight loss, night sweats, severe unremitting pain

Patient Information

What to Explain
Recommended Exercises
Recovery timeline

Usually resolves within 24-48 hours, occasionally up to a week

Recurrence

Common to experience recurrent episodes

Follow-up

Return if symptoms don't improve or worsen

Neck rotation

Sit upright, slowly turn head toward left shoulder, hold 5 seconds, repeat to right. 3 rotations each side.

Neck stretch

Hold left shoulder with right hand, slowly tilt head to right, hold 5 seconds, repeat on opposite side. 3 stretches each side.
