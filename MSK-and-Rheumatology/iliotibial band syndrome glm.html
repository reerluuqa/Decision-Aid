Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS)

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference Tool
Red Flag: Consider surgical intervention for refractory cases that don't respond to 6+ weeks of conservative therapy.

Overview

Overuse injury resulting from excessive friction of the distal iliotibial band sliding over the lateral femoral epicondyle.

Most common cause of lateral knee pain in runners Affects 62% women, 38% men

Key Symptoms

Sharp or burning pain superior to the lateral joint line
Pain occurs several minutes into or after activity
Worsening symptoms can occur at rest

Diagnostic Tests

Noble's Test
Positive when pain occurs at ~30Â° knee flexion with pressure on lateral femoral epicondyle
Ober's Test
Positive when affected leg cannot be passively adducted to horizontal (indicates ITB tightness)
Modified Thomas's Test
Positive when angle of femur is below horizontal (assesses hip flexor flexibility)

X-ray: Usually normal but may show other knee pathologies

Ultrasound: May show soft tissue swelling and fluid collection between ITB and lateral femoral epicondyle

MRI: May show thickening of ITB over lateral femoral epicondyle and fluid collection

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

Athlete (runner/cyclist)
High weekly mileage
Interval training
Worn-out running shoes
Downhill running

Other Factors

Female sex
Lack of running experience
Leg-length discrepancies
Genu varum (bow leg)

Management Phases

Acute Phase

Reduce inflammation and provide pain relief with NSAIDs. Modify activity and apply ice to affected area.

Activity modification NSAIDs Ice application

Sub-acute Phase

Focus on stretching of the ITB and soft-tissue therapy for myofascial restrictions.

Stretching exercises Foam roll mobilization

Recovery & Strengthening

Begin once range of motion and myofascial restrictions are resolved. Focus on hip abductor strength.

Hip abduction exercises Movement pattern training

Return to Running

Gradually reintroduce running once exercises can be performed pain-free.

Alternate day running Avoid downhill Gradual progression

Prevention Strategies

Replace worn-out running shoes regularly
Avoid always running in same direction
Increase distance/frequency gradually
Strengthen hip abductor and external rotator muscles
Regular ITB stretching

Follow-up & Prognosis

Grade Symptoms
1 Pain 1-3 hours after activity
2 Minimal pain at end of activity
3 Pain interferes with training
4 Pain restricts training and daily activities
5 Pain interferes with all activities

Prognosis: Most patients recover with 6 weeks of conservative therapy, though relapses are common.
