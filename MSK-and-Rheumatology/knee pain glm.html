Red Flags & Emergency Actions
Signs of Serious Pathology
Infection (Septic Arthritis)
  • Redness, swelling, heat, reduced movement
  • Rapid onset, severe pain
  • Difficulty weight bearing
  • Fever or systemic illness
Tumour
  • Persistent, non-mechanical bone pain
  • Pain at night or at rest
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Previous cancer history
Emergency Actions
Immediate Referral to A&E
  • Inability to weight bear (4 steps) after injury
  • Suspected septic arthritis
  • Suspected fracture
  • Suspected vascular injury
Urgent Referral (2 weeks)
  • Suspected malignancy
  • Suspected inflammatory arthritis
  • Unexplained persistent symptoms
Diagnosis & Assessment
History Taking
  • Onset - Acute vs. gradual
  • Location - Anterior, medial, lateral, posterior
  • Mechanism - Trauma, twisting, direct blow
  • Night Pain - May indicate tumor/inflammation
  • Locking/Giving Way - Meniscal or ligament injury
Examination
  • Inspect - Swelling, erythema, deformity, muscle atrophy
  • Palpate - Warmth, tenderness, effusion
  • Range of Motion - Extension to flexion (0-135°)
  • Gait Assessment - Ability to weight bear
  • Special Tests - Ligament stability, meniscal injury
Ottawa Knee Rule (Excluding Fracture)
X-ray Required if ANY of the Following:
Inability to weight bear (4 steps) immediately AND during examination
Inability to flex knee to 90 degrees
Tenderness of the head of the fibula
Isolated tenderness of the patella
Age 55 years or older

By Age Group

Patellofemoral pain Osgood-Schlatter Referred hip pain Joint hypermobility
Adults (18-50)
Patellofemoral pain Trauma Ligament sprains Meniscal tears Inflammatory arthritis
Older Adults (>50)
Osteoarthritis Gout Baker's cyst Degenerative meniscus
By Anatomical Site
A
Anterior: Patellar subluxation, Osgood-Schlatter, Patellar tendonitis, Patellofemoral pain
M
Medial: Medial collateral ligament sprain, Medial meniscal tear, Pes anserine bursitis
L
Lateral: Lateral collateral ligament sprain, Lateral meniscal tear, Iliotibial band tendonitis
P
Posterior: Baker's cyst, Posterior cruciate ligament injury, Posterior horn meniscal tears
Special Tests
Common Diagnostic Tests
Lachman Test (ACL)
Patient supine, knee at 20-30° flexion. Anterior translation of tibia indicates ACL tear.
Posterior Drawer Test (PCL)
Patient supine, knee at 90° flexion. Posterior translation of tibia indicates PCL tear.
Valgus Stress Test (MCL)
Patient supine, knee at 30° flexion. Apply valgus force to test medial stability.
McMurray Test
Note: Not recommended in primary care due to poor diagnostic accuracy.
Thessaly Test
Patient stands on affected leg with knee flexed at 5° and 20°, rotates internally and externally. Joint line pain indicates meniscal tear.
Patellar Apprehension Test
Attempt to move patella laterally. Pain/anxiety indicates patellar instability.
Investigations
Blood Tests
Test Indication
Inflammatory Markers (CRP, ESR) Suspected infection/inflammation
Full Blood Count Suspected infection/systemic disease
Urate Suspected gout
Autoantibodies (RF, ANA) Suspected inflammatory arthritis
Imaging
Modality Indication
X-ray Suspected fracture, osteoarthritis
Ultrasound Baker's cyst, effusion, superficial ligaments
MRI Cruciate/meniscal injuries, malignancy (usually secondary care)
CT Complex fractures (usually secondary care)
