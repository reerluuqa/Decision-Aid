Clinical History
  • Pain location: Localized to tibial tuberosity (just below kneecap)
  • Onset: Gradual, initially mild & intermittent, may progress to severe & continuous
  • Laterality: Usually unilateral, but both knees affected in up to 30%
  • Activity-related: Exacerbated by running, jumping, kneeling; relieved by rest
  • Mechanism: Repetitive traction of quadriceps on patellar tendon during growth spurt
Physical Examination
Key Findings:
  • Tenderness over tibial tuberosity
  • Pain on resisted knee extension
  • Tight quadriceps & hamstrings
  • Swelling/bony enlargement of tibial tubercle
Reassuring Findings:
  • Normal passive range of movement
  • Absence of effusion
  • No systemic symptoms
  • No night pain or rest pain
Red Flag Features
  • Trauma: Sudden onset after injury
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • Abnormal examination: Joint effusion, limited ROM, bony tenderness elsewhere
  • Night pain: Persistent pain that wakes patient
  • Rest pain: Pain not relieved by rest
  • Referred pain: Hip pathology (especially in adolescents)
Action: If any red flags present → Investigate or refer for specialist assessment
Diagnosis & Differential
Clinical Diagnosis
Diagnosis: Osgood-Schlatter disease is a clinical diagnosis when typical features are present and red flags are absent.

No routine imaging required. X-ray may show soft tissue swelling, tendon thickening, or ossicles if performed.

Differential Diagnoses
Urgent Referral
Traumatic Knee Injury:
  • Meniscal injuries
  • Ligament injuries (ACL/PCL)
  • Stress fractures
  • Tibial tuberosity fracture
  • Bursitis
Infection
Septic Arthritis / Osteomyelitis:
  • Acute severe pain & swelling
  • Warmth & tenderness
  • Fever & systemic illness
Malignancy
Bone Tumours:
  • Persistent night pain
  • Pain at rest
  • Weight loss, malaise
  • Bony tenderness elsewhere
Hip Pathology
Referred Pain from Hip:
  • Slipped upper femoral epiphysis
  • Transient synovitis (<10 years)
  • Perthes' disease
Inflammatory
Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis:
  • Single or multiple joints
  • Morning stiffness
  • Joint swelling
Overuse
Other Overuse Injuries:
  • Osteochondritis dissecans
  • Patellofemoral pain syndrome
  • Chondromalacia patellae
  • Patellar dislocation
Imaging: Consider X-ray or MRI if loose ossicles suspected or diagnostic uncertainty exists. Seek specialist advice if uncertain.
Management Strategy
Self-limiting
Activity Modification
Prognosis: Symptoms typically resolve over weeks to months, coinciding with end of growth spurt. Complete recovery expected when tibial growth plate closes (14-18 years).
Patient Education & Reassurance
  • Explain condition is benign and self-limiting
  • Provide written information from Association of Paediatric Chartered Physiotherapists
  • Emphasize typical resolution timeline
  • Discuss importance of activity modification
Activity Modification (Based on Pain)
Minimal Pain

Continue exercise. Seek medical advice if symptoms worsen.

Activities Not Tolerated

Reduce intensity, frequency, or duration, or switch to low-impact activities (swimming, cycling).

Persistent Pain Despite Modification

Rest until symptoms improve, then gradually introduce low-impact quadriceps exercises (isometric contractions, straight-leg raises, cross-training).

Recurrent Pain

Reduce or stop activity until symptoms settle, then gradually re-establish exercise according to tolerance.

Pain Relief Strategies
  • Ice packs: 10-15 minutes up to 3 times daily, especially after exercise
  • Protective knee pads: To relieve pain on kneeling
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol and/or NSAID if appropriate (check contraindications)
Preventative Strategies
  • Regular stretching: Quadriceps and hamstrings to reduce muscle tightness
  • Cross-training: Maintain fitness while reducing repetitive stress
  • Gradual progression: Increase intensity slowly as tolerated
Referral Pathways
Physiotherapy

Indication: Symptoms worsen or don't improve despite appropriate management. For stretching, strengthening, and activity modification advice.

Specialist Assessment

Indications: Diagnostic uncertainty → Paediatrician or Orthopaedic Surgeon

Orthopaedic Surgery

Indications: Persistent disease into adulthood with significant functional impact. Consider surgical treatment for severe cases.

Reassessment Triggers
  • Worsening symptoms despite appropriate management
  • New red flag features (systemic symptoms, night pain, etc.)
  • Persistent pain beyond expected timeline
  • Functional limitation affecting daily activities or sports
  • Diagnostic uncertainty - consider alternative diagnoses
Persistent Disease in Adulthood

Risk factors for persistence:

  • Marked enlargement of tibial tuberosity
  • Ossicle formation in patellar tendon
  • Early severe symptoms
  • Incomplete rehabilitation

Consequences of persistent disease:

  • Chronic anterior knee pain
  • Pain on kneeling
  • Knee pain after activity
  • Reduced sports participation
  • Decreased lower-body strength
  • Lower health-related quality of life
Expected Timeline: Complete recovery expected when tibial growth plate closes, typically between 14-18 years of age.
Long-term Follow-up

If symptoms persist into adulthood and significantly affect functioning, refer to orthopaedic surgeon for consideration of surgical intervention (e.g., ossicle excision, tibial tuberosity reshaping).
