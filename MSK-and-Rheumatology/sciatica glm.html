URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED

Sudden onset bilateral radicular pain or unilateral radicular pain progressing to bilateral pain requires urgent referral to a musculoskeletal triage service (within 2 weeks)

Cauda Equina Syndrome (CES)

  • Saddle anaesthesia - perianal, perineal, or genital sensory loss
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction - urinary retention, difficulty initiating micturition
  • Progressive neurological weakness in legs
  • Gait disturbance or difficulty walking
  • Laxity of the anal sphincter
  • Erectile dysfunction
Emergency Referral

Spinal Fracture

  • Major trauma at any age
  • Mild trauma in people aged over 70 years
  • Prolonged corticosteroid use
  • History of osteoporosis
  • Structural deformity of the spine
  • Point tenderness over vertebrae
Urgent Imaging

Infection

  • Fever or chills
  • Recent infection or immunosuppression
  • IV drug use
  • Unrelenting night pain
  • Incapacitating pain

Cancer/Malignancy

  • History of malignancy
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Age > 50 years with new onset back pain
  • Pain not improving with rest
  • Night pain that disturbs sleep

When to Suspect Sciatica

  • Unilateral leg pain radiating below the knee to the foot or toes
  • Low back pain (if present) which is less severe than any leg pain
  • Numbness, tingling (paraesthesia) in the distribution of a nerve root (dermatome)
  • Weakness or reflex changes in a myotomal distribution
  • Positive straight leg raise test (pain reproduced below 60Â° of hip flexion)

Definition

Radiating leg pain caused by inflammation or compression of the lumbosacral nerve roots (L4-S1) forming the sciatic nerve.

Alternative names: 'radicular pain', 'lumbar radiculopathy', and 'lumbosacral radicular syndrome'.

Prevalence

  • Lifetime prevalence: 13-40%
  • Period prevalence: 2-34% per year
  • Point prevalence: 2-13%
  • 5-10% of people with non-specific low back pain also have sciatica
  • Peak incidence in 5th decade of life

Common Causes

  • Herniated intervertebral disc (90% of cases)
  • Spondylolisthesis
  • Spinal stenosis
  • Infection (rare)
  • Cancer (rare)

Risk Factors

  • Smoking
  • Obesity
  • Occupational factors (whole body vibration, strenuous physical activity)
  • Older age (45-64 years)
  • Genetic influences

Assessment Approach

History Taking

  • Pain characteristics (onset, duration, location, radiation)
  • Sensory and strength changes
  • Other symptoms (e.g., urinary retention)
  • History of back pain or sciatica
  • History of malignancy
  • Recent trauma
  • Comorbidities and current medication

Physical Examination

  • Observe spine for deformity and abnormal curvatures
  • Palpate to localize tenderness
  • Observe gait and ability to walk
  • Assess passive and active range of motion
  • Examine hips and assess passive range of motion
  • Assess neurological signs
  • Perform straight leg raising test

Investigations

  • Do not routinely X-ray the spine to confirm diagnosis
  • Consider investigations if infection, malignancy, or inflammatory conditions are suspected:
    • Full blood count
    • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
    • C-reactive protein (CRP)
    • Blood cultures
    • Urinalysis and culture

Psychosocial Assessment

Consider using the StarT Back Tool to inform shared decision-making about stratified management:

  • Divides people into low, medium, and high risk of persistent back pain-related disability
  • Quality of life, pain severity, function, and psychological distress are key factors
  • People at higher risk may require more complex and intensive support

Management Goals

  • Identify and manage potentially serious alternative causes of back pain
  • Provide symptomatic relief
  • Provide appropriate advice and information
  • Arrange referral for exercise therapy, physiotherapy, cognitive behavioural therapy
  • Refer to specialist low back pain and sciatica service for assessment for surgical options where appropriate

Pharmacological Management

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen up to 1200mg/day or naproxen up to 1000mg/day)
  • Weak opioids if NSAIDs ineffective or contraindicated
  • Consider neuropathic pain agents at follow-up if symptoms persist
  • Avoid paracetamol alone for low back pain without sciatica
  • Avoid anticonvulsants or antidepressants for low back pain without sciatica

Non-Pharmacological Management

  • Advice to remain active and continue normal activities as much as possible
  • Exercise therapy
  • Physiotherapy
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy
  • Manual therapy
  • Acupuncture (may be considered)

Referral Criteria

  • Red flag symptoms (see Red Flags tab)
  • Persistent symptoms (>6-12 weeks) despite conservative management
  • Severe or worsening symptoms
  • Significant functional limitation
  • Psychosocial factors suggesting poor prognosis

What NOT to Do

  • Do not routinely request imaging unless serious underlying pathology is suspected
  • Do not offer paracetamol alone for low back pain without sciatica
  • Do not offer anticonvulsants or antidepressants for low back pain without sciatica
  • Do not offer opioids for chronic low back pain without sciatica
  • Do not offer spinal injections for low back pain without sciatica (except radiofrequency denervation)

General Prognosis

  • Episodes of sciatica are usually transient, with rapid improvements in pain and disability within a few weeks to months
  • Half of people recover spontaneously within 6 weeks
  • Most people experience improvement in symptoms over time with either conservative treatment or surgery
  • In a 5-year follow up, 8% of people showed no recovery and 23% reported ongoing symptoms that fluctuated over time

Favorable Prognostic Factors

  • Shorter pain duration
  • Fewer other symptoms associated with back and leg pain
  • Belief that the problem will be short-lived
  • Initially having myotomal weakness
  • Younger age
  • Better general health
  • Lower level of low back pain or sciatica bothersomeness
  • Better physical function
  • Negative straight leg raise test
  • Better pain coping strategies
  • Lower levels of depression and psychological distress
  • Less fear of movement
  • Low physical workload

Poor Prognostic Factors

  • Workplace factors:
    • Time off work
    • Problems or dissatisfaction at work
    • Heavy work
    • Working unsociable hours
  • Psychosocial factors:
    • Low or negative moods
    • Stress
    • Overprotective family
    • Lack of support
    • Social withdrawal
    • Belief that pain and activity are harmful
    • Belief that the problem will last a long time
    • Inappropriate expectations of treatment

Recurrence

  • Recurrence of symptoms is common
  • 54% of people had experienced at least one recurrence of low back pain at 6 months follow up
  • 47% at 2 years follow up
  • Up to one third of people report persistent back pain of at least moderate intensity 1 year after an acute episode requiring care

Surgical Outcomes

  • People with sciatica experience a rapid reduction in pain and disability in the first 3 months after surgery
  • Still may have mild to moderate symptoms 5 years later
  • Microdiscectomy shows larger improvements than other surgical techniques
