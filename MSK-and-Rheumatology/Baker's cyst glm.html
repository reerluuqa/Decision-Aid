Baker's Cyst Clinical Reference

Quick-scan guide for primary care management of popliteal cysts
URGENT: Arrange same-day assessment in secondary care if any red flags are identified or if there is suspicion of DVT, compartment syndrome, or septic arthritis.

What is a Baker's Cyst?

A distension of the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa behind the knee. Primary (idiopathic, mainly in children) vs secondary (associated with underlying knee disorders, mainly in adults).

Red Flags
  • Sudden increase in swelling or pain
  • Change in consistency of swelling
  • Neurovascular symptoms
  • Signs of rupture: severe calf pain, swelling, warmth, tenderness, erythema
  • Fever or systemic signs of infection
Management Approach
1
Identify and manage any underlying condition (osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, etc.)
2
If asymptomatic: no treatment required, reassure patient
3
If symptomatic: simple analgesia and physiotherapy
4
Avoid direct aspiration in primary care
Diagnosis
Assessment Key Findings
History Asymptomatic swelling, posterior knee pain, feeling of tightness
Examination Bulge in medial popliteal fossa, Foucher's sign (cyst tense in extension, softens in flexion)
Investigations Ultrasound (children), MRI for adults to identify underlying conditions
Management in Children
  • Admit to paediatrics/A&E if red flags present
  • Refer to paediatric orthopaedic surgeon if symptomatic or cyst is very large
  • Refer to paediatric rheumatologist if underlying condition suspected
  • Reassure that primary cysts usually resolve without treatment
Management in Adults
  • Arrange same-day assessment if red flags present
  • Identify and optimize management of underlying condition
  • If asymptomatic: no treatment required
  • If symptomatic: simple analgesia and physiotherapy
  • Refer to specialist if diagnosis unclear or symptoms persist
Differential Diagnosis
Deep vein thrombosis
Superficial thrombophlebitis
Abscess
Tumors (benign/malignant)
Ganglion cyst
Popliteal artery aneurysm
