Assessment

History, examination & red flags

‚ñº
‚ùì When to Suspect TMD
‚ñº

Suspect TMD in people with one or more of the following:

  • Pain in/around TMJ and muscles of mastication
    Pre-auricular region, may radiate to ear, temple, teeth, cheek, or angle of jaw. Provoked by palpation or jaw movement.
  • Reproducible joint noise
    Clicking, popping, or crepitus on jaw movements, with or without restricted movement or locking.
  • Headache in temporal region
    Limited to temporal area and exacerbated by jaw function.
  • Otalgia
    Ear pain in the absence of ear disease.
üí°

3-Question TMD Screening Tool

Any single positive response suggests TMD is more likely:

  • Do you have pain in the temple, face, TMJ, or jaws once a week or more?
  • Do you have pain or stiffness in your jaws when you wake?
  • Do you have pain when chewing or opening wide?

üëÅÔ∏è Physical Examination
‚ñº
  • Facial Assessment
    Assess for facial/mandible asymmetry or swelling
  • Cranial Nerve Examination
    Check for any cranial nerve deficits
  • Oral Examination
    Examine hard/soft palate, teeth, and gums to exclude dental pathology
  • TMJ Palpation
    Press anteriorly to tragus bilaterally. Assess tenderness, range of movement (normal 35-55mm), pain on movement, joint noises/crepitus
  • Muscle Palpation
    Palpate masseter and temporalis muscles (with teeth clenched) for tenderness, trigger points, or hypertrophy
  • Lymph Nodes
    Check for cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Over 50 years
    Palpate superficial temporal vessels and assess appearance (exclude GCA)
üö® Red Flag Symptoms & Signs Critical
‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è

Urgent Action Required

If any red flags present, exclude serious pathology and manage appropriately using clinical judgement.

  • Previous history of malignancy
    May indicate new primary, recurrence, or metastases
  • Persistent/unexplained neck lump or cervical lymphadenopathy
    May indicate neoplastic, infective, or autoimmune cause
  • Persistent and worsening pain
  • Pain with exertion, coughing, or sneezing
    Potential raised intracranial pressure
  • Jaw pain in patients taking bisphosphonates
    Osteonecrosis risk
  • Neurological symptoms/signs
    Cranial nerve abnormalities, sensory/motor changes, unilateral hearing loss, vestibular dysfunction
  • Facial asymmetry, mass, swelling, or profound trismus
    May indicate neoplastic, infective, or inflammatory cause
  • New-onset unilateral headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication (>50 years)
    May indicate Giant Cell Arteritis
  • Unexplained fever or weight loss
    May indicate malignancy, infection
  • Occlusal (bite) changes
    May indicate neoplasia, RA, trauma
  • Persistent hoarseness, dysphagia, or mouth ulcers >3 weeks

Diagnosis

Classification & differential diagnosis

‚ñº
üìä TMD Classification
‚ñº

Note: A person may have more than one type of TMD simultaneously.

üí™ Masticatory Muscle Disorders

Myalgia Common
  • Pain provoked on palpation of masseter/temporalis muscles
  • Pain within the body of the muscle or radiating beyond
  • Pain with maximum mouth opening
  • Pain in jaw, temples, pre-auricular area, or inside the ear
  • Myofascial pain with referral may have active trigger points

ü¶¥ TMJ Disorders

Disc Displacement with Reduction Common
  • Reproducible joint noise (clicking, popping, snapping) during mouth opening/closing
  • Usually without significant restriction in jaw movement
  • May have deviation of mandible during opening that coincides with click
  • May progress to 'closed lock' state in minority
Disc Displacement without Reduction ('Closed Lock') Refer
  • Persistent, markedly limited mouth opening with sudden onset
  • Deviation of mandible towards affected side
  • Pain if forced mouth opening attempted
  • May be preceded by period of clicking
Degenerative Joint Disease
  • Crepitus with any jaw movement (often not associated with pain)
  • Pain from TMJ on jaw movement
  • Point tenderness of TMJ
  • May have restricted movement, deviation, grating noises
Subluxation
  • History of locking open and need for self-manipulation to close
  • Often in people with hypermobility
  • Deviation of mandible on mouth opening

ü§ï Headache Attributable to TMD

  • Headache in temporalis area modified with jaw movement/function
  • Reproduced with palpation of temporalis muscle or jaw movements

Treatment

Primary care management options

‚ñº
üè• Immediate Referral to OMFS Urgent
‚ñº
üöë

Refer to Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery if:

  • History of trauma or fracture to TMJ complex
  • Markedly limited mouth opening (closed lock) suggesting disc displacement without reduction
  • Pain or reduced jaw function in people with known rheumatic joint disease
  • Recurrent dislocation of TMJ and/or associated syndromes
üè† Conservative Management First Line
‚ñº

üì¢ Education & Reassurance

  • Reassure the patient
    Condition is usually non-progressive; symptoms may fluctuate but should improve
  • Educate about the condition
    Nature of disorder, anatomy, predisposing/precipitating/perpetuating factors, management options, therapy goals

üèÉ Self-Management Advice

  • Soft diet & rest
    If acute pain present
  • Avoid parafunctional activities
    Wide yawning, teeth grinding/clenching, chewing gum/pencils, nail biting
  • Local measures
    Apply covered ice or warm flannel/heat pad; massage affected muscles
  • Positive lifestyle strategies
    Gentle regular exercise, maintain social interactions, relaxation, stress management, positive sleep habits

Follow-up

Monitoring & specialist referral

‚ñº
üìà Prognosis
‚ñº
üìä

Expected Outcomes

  • Symptoms resolve spontaneously in up to 40% of people
  • With conservative treatment, 50-90% of people's symptoms improve
  • Only 5-10% require specialist treatment
  • Single episode in 12%, recurrent in 65%, persistent in 19%

Factors Associated with Worse Prognosis:

  • Being female
  • Increasing age at presentation
  • Higher reported pain intensity
  • More widespread non-specific symptoms
  • Myogenous TMD
  • Comorbid psychosocial factors (anxiety, depression)
üè• Specialist Referral Criteria If Not Improving
‚ñº

Refer to OMFS, ENT, Neurology, or Multidisciplinary Pain Clinic if:

  • Worsening symptoms
  • Persistent symptoms despite 6-8 weeks of primary care treatment
  • Persistent inability to manage a normal diet
  • Severe pain and dysfunction from internal derangement not responding to conservative measures
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Other chronic pain-related comorbidities

    For recurrent TMJ dislocation
  • Total prosthetic TMJ replacement
