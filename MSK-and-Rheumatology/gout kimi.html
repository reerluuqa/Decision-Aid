Gout Management Algorithm

Based on NICE Clinical Knowledge Summaries (June 2023)

Assessment

Septic Arthritis
CKD/Elderly

History

  • Rapid onset (<24hrs) severe pain, swelling, redness, warmth
  • Typical joints: 1st MTP (50% first attack), midfoot, ankle, knee, fingers, wrist, elbow
  • Frequency and duration of attacks
  • Dietary habits (alcohol, sugary drinks, meat, seafood)
  • Family history of gout/hyperuricaemia
  • Comorbidities: CKD, hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidaemia
  • Medications: diuretics, low-dose aspirin

Examination

  • Warm, red, swollen joints (any joint possible)
  • Tophi: firm white nodules on extensor surfaces, Achilles tendon, ear helix
  • Tenderness and limited range of movement
  • Assess for septic arthritis, pseudogout, inflammatory arthritis

Investigations

Serum Urate Level

  • ≥360 μmol/L (6 mg/dL) confirms diagnosis
  • If <360 μmol/L but suspected, repeat 2-4 weeks after flare settles
  • Do not diagnose based on hyperuricaemia alone (95% never develop gout)

Secondary Care Investigations (if uncertain)

  • Joint aspiration and synovial fluid microscopy for crystals
  • Imaging: X-ray, ultrasound, or dual-energy CT
Immediate Referral: Suspect septic arthritis if systemically unwell with hot swollen joint. Refer for emergency joint aspiration & IV antibiotics.

Diagnosis

Crystal Confirmation
Uncertain

When to Suspect

  • Rapid onset severe pain with redness/swelling in 1st MTP joint
  • Tophi present (suggests longstanding untreated gout)
  • Consider other joints: midfoot, ankle, knee, hand, wrist, elbow
  • Consider chronic gouty arthritis with chronic inflammatory joint pain

ACR Diagnostic Criteria

Confirmed if:

  • Monosodium urate crystals in joint fluid, OR
  • Monosodium urate crystals in tophus, OR
  • ≥6 of the following criteria:
  • More than one attack of acute arthritis
  • Maximum inflammation developed within 1 day
  • Monoarthritis attack, redness observed over joints
  • 1st MTP joint painful or swollen
  • Unilateral 1st MTP joint attack
  • Unilateral tarsal joint attack
  • Tophus (confirmed or suspected)
  • Hyperuricaemia
  • Asymmetrical swelling within a joint on X-ray
  • Subcortical cyst without erosions on X-ray
  • Joint culture negative for organism during attack

Differential Diagnosis

  • Septic arthritis (must be excluded)
  • Psoriatic arthritis
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate deposition)
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Bursitis/tenosynovitis/cellulitis
  • Haemochromatosis
  • Trauma
Refer if: Diagnosis uncertain, young patient (<30), pregnancy, or suspected systemic illness.

Treatment

Acute
Prevention

Acute Gout Flare

Treat as soon as possible

First-line Options (choose based on comorbidities):

NSAID: Naproxen at maximum dose until 1-2 days after resolution
  • Consider PPI for gastric protection
  • Monitor renal function if heart failure
Colchicine: 500 micrograms 2-4 times daily
  • Max 6 mg per course, don't repeat within 3 days
  • Reduce dose in elderly/CKD (eGFR 10-50)
  • Contraindicated if eGFR <10
Oral Corticosteroid: Prednisolone 30-35 mg once daily for 3-5 days
  • Off-label use
  • Consider if NSAIDs/colchicine contraindicated
Intra-articular/IM Corticosteroid: If oral therapy ineffective/not tolerated
Adjuncts: Ice packs, rest and elevate limb, keep joint cool, paracetamol for pain
Continue ULT: Do not stop allopurinol or febuxostat during acute flare

Prevention (Urate-Lowering Therapy)

Indications for ULT:

  • Multiple or troublesome flares
  • CKD stages 3-5
  • Diuretic therapy
  • Tophi or chronic gouty arthritis

Treat-to-Target Strategy:

  • Start 2-4 weeks after flare settles (or during flare if frequent)
  • Start low, increase monthly based on serum urate
  • Target: <360 μmol/L (<300 if tophi or frequent flares)
  • Lifelong treatment
Allopurinol: First-line (especially if cardiovascular disease)
  • Start 100 mg daily, increase by 100 mg every 4 weeks
  • Mild: 100-200 mg, Moderate: 300-600 mg, Severe: 700-900 mg
  • Screen for HLA-B*5801 in high-risk ethnicities (Han Chinese, Thai, Korean)
Febuxostat: Alternative if allopurinol not tolerated/ineffective
  • Start 80 mg daily, increase to 120 mg if urate >6 mg/dL
  • Caution: increased cardiovascular mortality risk
  • Avoid if major cardiovascular disease (MI, stroke, unstable angina)

Prophylaxis during ULT initiation:

  • Colchicine 500 micrograms twice daily for at least 6 months
  • If colchicine contraindicated: NSAID or corticosteroid
  • Continue prophylaxis for 3 months after reaching target urate

Follow-up & Monitoring

On Target
Monitoring

Post-Flare Follow-up (4-6 weeks)

  • Measure serum urate level
  • Assess lifestyle and comorbidities (CV risk, CKD)
  • Offer CKD testing: eGFR and urine ACR
  • Review medications (consider stopping diuretics if hypertension controlled)
  • Discuss long-term ULT benefits/risks
  • Provide patient education materials

Ongoing Monitoring

During ULT Titration:

  • Check serum urate monthly until target reached
  • Monitor kidney function in parallel
  • Screen CV risk factors annually

After Target Reached:

  • Annual serum urate monitoring
  • Annual kidney function and CV risk assessment
  • Liver function if on febuxostat
  • Monitor for allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (first 3 months)

When to Refer

Immediate: Suspected septic arthritis
Urgent/Routine:
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Treatment failure or intolerance
  • Young patient (<30) or pregnancy
  • CKD stages 3b-5 (eGFR <45)
  • Organ transplant recipients
  • Need intra-articular injection
  • Suspected systemic illness (RA, connective tissue disease)

Lifestyle Advice

  • Healthy balanced diet (no specific diet proven)
  • Weight reduction if obese
  • Limit alcohol (especially beer), sugary drinks
  • Reduce meat and seafood intake
  • Ensure adequate hydration
  • At least 2 alcohol-free days per week

Patient Education

Explain to patients:

  • Gout is a lifelong condition due to urate crystal deposition
  • ULT prevents flares, shrinks tophi, prevents joint damage
  • Continue ULT during acute flares
  • Recurrent flares will decrease with effective ULT
  • Provide written information (UK Gout Society: www.ukgoutsociety.org)
