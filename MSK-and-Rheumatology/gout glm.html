Gout Clinical Reference Tool

Last updated: June 2023

Definition

Gout is a type of arthritis caused by monosodium urate crystals forming inside and around joints, causing sudden flares of severe pain, heat, and swelling.

Any joint can be affected, but it most commonly affects the distal joints, such as toes, knees, ankles, and finger joints.

Clinical phases:

  • Phase 1 Asymptomatic hyperuricaemia
  • Phase 2 Acute gout
  • Phase 3 Intercritical gout
  • Phase 4 Chronic tophaceous gout

Key Facts

  • Most common inflammatory arthritis
  • Prevalence in UK: 2.49% (increased from 1.4% in 1999)
  • Incidence: 1.77 per 1000 person-years
  • Men to women ratio: 4.3:1
  • More common in men >30 years and postmenopausal women

Complications

  • Chronic arthritis (2% after 20 years)
  • Joint damage
  • Renal stones (14% of patients)
  • Tophi (50% after 10 years, 72% after 20 years)
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Chronic kidney disease (25% have CKD stages 3-5)

Risk Factors

Comorbidities

  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hyperlipidaemia
  • Osteoarthritis

Other Risk Factors

  • Excess alcohol consumption
  • High-purine diet (meat, seafood)
  • Obesity
  • Family history
  • Male sex
  • Older age
  • Certain medications (diuretics, low-dose aspirin)

When to Suspect Gout

Classic Presentation

  • Rapid onset (often overnight) of severe pain
  • Redness and swelling in one or both metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joints
  • First MTP joint affected in 50% of initial cases
  • Maximum inflammation within 24 hours

Other Presentations

  • Midfoot, ankle, knee, hand, wrist or elbow joints
  • Tophi (hard cutaneous nodules of sodium urate crystals)
  • Chronic inflammatory joint pain

Assessment

History

  • Joints affected
  • Symptom severity and speed of onset
  • Frequency and duration of attacks
  • Previous attacks and treatments
  • Dietary habits
  • Impact on work and functioning
  • Family history
  • Comorbidities and risk factors

Examination

  • Warm, red and swollen joints
  • Tophi (firm, white nodules under translucent skin)
  • Tenderness and limited range of movement
  • Assess for septic arthritis
  • Measure serum urate level

Investigations

Primary Care

  • Measure serum urate level
  • A serum urate level of 360 micromol/L (6 mg/dL) or more confirms the diagnosis
  • If below 360 micromol/L but gout strongly suspected, repeat after flare has settled

Secondary Care

  • Joint aspiration and microscopy of synovial fluid
  • Imaging (X-ray, ultrasound, or dual-energy CT) if diagnosis uncertain

Differential Diagnosis

  • Bursitis, tenosynovitis, cellulitis
  • Haemochromatosis
  • Pseudogout
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Psoriatic arthritis
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Septic arthritis (emergency)

Emergency Management

If septic arthritis is suspected (systemically unwell with acutely painful, hot, swollen joint), refer immediately according to local care pathway.

First-Line Treatment Options

Acute episodes should be treated as soon as possible. Offer one of the following first-line, taking into account the person's preferences, comorbidities and concurrent medication:

Treatment Dose Duration Considerations
NSAID (e.g., naproxen) Maximum dose Until 1-2 days after attack resolved Consider PPI for gastric protection
Colchicine As per protocol As per protocol Adjust dose in renal impairment
Oral corticosteroid (e.g., prednisolone) 30-35 mg once daily 3-5 days Off-label use

Additional options:

  • Intra-articular or intramuscular corticosteroid injection if NSAIDs and colchicine are not tolerated or ineffective (off-label)
  • Ice packs applied to affected joint
  • Paracetamol as adjunct for pain relief
  • Combining treatments if response to monotherapy is inadequate

Treatment Considerations

  • Do not offer an interleukin-1 (IL-1) inhibitor unless NSAIDs, colchicine and corticosteroids are contraindicated, not tolerated or ineffective
  • Refer to rheumatology service before prescribing an IL-1 inhibitor
  • Aspirin is not indicated for gout
  • Provide tailored information about gout, its causes, and treatment options

Lifestyle Modifications

Dietary Recommendations

  • Limit alcohol intake
  • Reduce consumption of sugary drinks
  • Limit purine-rich foods (meat, seafood)
  • Increase fluid intake
  • Maintain a healthy weight

General Advice

  • Regular exercise
  • Weight management
  • Review medications that may increase urate levels
  • Treat comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes, etc.)

  • Tophi
  • Joint damage
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Urolithiasis
  • Use of diuretics

First-Line ULT

  • Allopurinol - starting dose 100mg daily, titrated to target serum urate <360 micromol/L
  • Febuxostat - for people intolerant of allopurinol or when allopurinol is contraindicated

Prophylaxis During ULT Initiation

  • NSAID or colchicine for first 3-6 months of ULT
  • Start ULT 2-4 weeks after acute attack has resolved

Medication Safety

Febuxostat

  • Use with caution in people with cardiovascular disorders
  • Particularly cautious in those with evidence of high urate crystal and tophi burden
  • Be cautious when initiating urate-lowering therapy

Allopurinol

  • Screen before starting treatment
  • Dose adjustment in renal impairment
  • Risk of hypersensitivity reactions

Urgent Referral

Immediate referral according to local care pathway is required for:

  • Suspected septic arthritis
  • Systemically unwell patient with acutely painful, hot, swollen joint

Routine Referral

Consider referral to rheumatology for:

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Recurrent attacks despite optimal treatment
  • Tophaceous gout
  • Renal impairment (eGFR <30 ml/min)
  • Contraindications or intolerance to standard therapies
  • Consideration for IL-1 inhibitor therapy

Follow-up

After Acute Attack

  • Review within 2 weeks
  • Assess response to treatment
  • Check serum urate level
  • Consider need for urate-lowering therapy

On Urate-Lowering Therapy

  • Review every 3-6 months initially
  • Check serum urate level
  • Titrate dose to target serum urate <360 micromol/L
  • Monitor for adverse effects
