Clinical Assessment

History, examination & risk stratification

‚ñº
üî¥ Urgent: Neurovascular compromise, severe pain, infection signs
üü° Caution: Painful cyst, functional limitation, volar location
üü¢ Stable: Asymptomatic, dorsal cyst, cosmetic concern only

Key History & Examination Findings

Typical Presentation: Smooth, soft, subcutaneous mass (1-4cm) on wrist, mobile, non-tender, transilluminates with penlight
  • Symptoms: Cosmetic concern, wrist aching, paresthesia, weakness, coolness of hand/fingers
  • Location:
    • Dorsal (60-70%): Over scapholunate interval
    • Volar (30-40%): Between FCR tendon & radial artery
  • Physical signs:
    • Increases size with activity, decreases with rest
    • Neurovascular compression rare but serious
Risk Factors & Epidemiology
  • Demographics: Female:male ratio 3:1 (adults), 1.4-1.8:1 (children)
  • Age: Peak incidence 2nd-4th decade, but can occur at any age
  • Trauma: 10-40% recall preceding injury, but definitive link unconfirmed
  • Scapholunate instability: Hypothesized but not definitively linked
Neurovascular Compression Signs (Red Flags)
  • Median nerve: Numbness thumb/index/long finger (mimics carpal tunnel)
  • Ulnar nerve: Numbness ring/small fingers
  • Radial nerve: Numbness dorsoradial hand
  • Vascular: Coolness, pallor, blue discoloration, sluggish capillary refill

Diagnostic Approach

Is the diagnosis uncertain or atypical?

‚úì Typical presentation
  • Clinical diagnosis only
  • No investigations needed
‚ö† Atypical/uncertain
  • Ultrasound or MRI
  • Assess size, location, relation to artery
  • Identify ligamentous injury

Special Diagnostic Procedures

Aspiration (Dorsal Cysts Only)
  • Indication: Diagnostic & potentially therapeutic (dorsal cysts)
  • Contraindication: Volar cysts (risk of radial artery injury)
  • Technique: Sterile prep, 18-gauge needle, aspirate viscous mucin
  • Fluid: Clear, jelly-like, viscous, mucinous material = diagnostic
  • Risk: Rare septic arthritis (communicates with joint space)
Differential Diagnoses
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome: No mass, positive Tinel's, no transillumination
  • Lipoma: Does not transilluminate, non-cystic on US/MRI
  • Giant cell tumor of tendon sheath: Solid, fixed to tendon, no fluid on aspiration
  • Tenosynovitis: Diffuse swelling along tendon, not discrete mass
  • Radial artery aneurysm: Pulsatile, Doppler flow, no transillumination
  • Sarcoma: Poorly circumscribed, immobile, MRI concerning features

Treatment Algorithm

Stepwise management approach

‚ñº
Core Principle: Conservative first-line approach. Surgery reserved for failed conservative therapy or neurovascular compromise.

Primary Decision Point

Neurovascular Compromise Present?

Paresthesia, weakness, coolness, color change

‚úó No Compromise ‚Üí Conservative Pathway

1st: Observation

  • Spontaneous resolution: 58% adults, 93% children (9-12 months)
  • Activity modification, wrist supports

2nd: Aspiration ¬± Corticosteroid

  • Dorsal cysts only - 13% success (single)
  • 40% success with 3-week splint
  • 85% success with up to 3 treatments
  • Recurrence rate: 59%

3rd: Surgical Excision

  • Open or arthroscopic
  • Dorsal recurrence: 3-9% (open), 7-11% (arthroscopic)
  • Volar recurrence: 7-19%
‚úì Compromise Present ‚Üí Surgical Pathway
  • 1st line: Surgical resection
  • Non-emergent but timely intervention
  • Open or arthroscopic excision
  • Remove cuff of normal tissue with stalk
  • Avoid scapholunate ligament injury

Analgesia Options

NSAID Dosing (for activity-related pain)
  • Naproxen: 250-500mg PO BID PRN, max 1250mg/day
  • Diclofenac potassium: 50mg PO BID/TID PRN, max 150mg/day
  • Ibuprofen: 300-400mg PO q6-8h PRN, max 2400mg/day
  • Adjuncts: Compressive wraps, wrist supports during activity
Surgical Considerations
  • Open excision: Lower recurrence with cuff of normal tissue
  • Arthroscopic: Treats intra-articular pathology, comparable outcomes
  • Volar cysts: Higher recurrence (7-19%), risk of radial artery injury
  • Complications: Stiffness, instability (scapholunate diastasis), neurovascular injury, neuroma, scar

Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring and recurrence prevention

‚ñº
Prognosis: No malignant transformation reported. Recurrence rates vary by treatment modality.

Monitoring Requirements

  • No specific schedule needed - no malignant potential
  • Re-evaluate if growing, painful, or neurovascular symptoms develop
  • Patient education on warning signs for complications

Recurrence Rates by Treatment

Detailed Recurrence Data
  • Observation: Spontaneous resolution in 58% adults, 93% children (9-12 months)
  • Aspiration: 59% recurrence rate overall
  • Open surgery: Dorsal 3-9%, Volar 7-19%
  • Arthroscopic: 7-11% recurrence
  • Pediatric: Higher recurrence in teenagers

Potential Complications

Treatment-Related Complications
  • Aspiration/Injection: Radial artery injury, median nerve injury, septic arthritis, fatty atrophy, skin depigmentation
  • Surgical: Wrist stiffness, scapholunate instability, neurovascular injury, neuroma formation, cosmetic scar
  • All rates: Generally low with proper technique
Patient Counseling Points: Trade bump for scar; surgery not guaranteed cure; recurrence possible; volar cysts have higher recurrence and vascular risk
