Key Diagnostic Factors
1 Common Symptoms
Ill-defined ache localized to anterior knee behind patella
Pain aggravated by activities increasing patellofemoral compressive force:
  • Ascending/descending stairs
  • Squatting
  • Prolonged sitting with knee flexed
Insidious onset without history of trauma
2 Physical Examination
Q angle - Measure patellar tendency to move laterally
Pain on palpation of patellar retinaculum
Patellar tilt test - Lateral patellar tilt suggests tight lateral structures
Patellar mobility test - Assess mediolateral movement
Patellar apprehension test - Patient becomes apprehensive with lateral movement
Patellar maltracking test - "J sign" indicates tracking abnormalities
Treatment Algorithm
1 Initial Management
Activity modification - Relative rest and reduce activities exerting compressive force
Cold application - 10-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily during acute phase
NSAIDs - Limited evidence for short-term pain reduction
Patellar taping - May reduce symptoms and increase quadriceps activity
Patellar bracing - May benefit patients with increased patellar displacement
2 Specific Mechanical Problems
Abnormal Patellofemoral Mechanics
Quadriceps weakness - Open/closed kinetic chain exercises
Tight soft tissues - Deep longitudinal massage + passive stretch
Decreased patellar mobility - Patellar mobilization techniques
Lower Extremity Alignment
Subtalar joint pronation - Orthotics
Hip internal rotation - Strengthening hip extensors, abductors, external rotators
Gait deviations - Normalization of gait mechanics
3 Ongoing Management
Comprehensive home exercise program - Essential for long-term improvement
Surgery - Consider for persistent symptoms with structural abnormalities
Physical Examination Tests
Patellar Assessment
Q Angle

Line connecting ASIS to patella center and patella center to tibial tuberosity

Normal: 10-15° (men: 14±3°, women: 17±3°)
Patellar Tilt Test

Compare medial and lateral border height in supine position

Positive if medial border more anterior than lateral
Mediolateral Glide Test

Measure distance from mid-patella to femoral epicondyles

Patella should sit equidistant (±5mm) from each epicondyle at 20° flexion
Patellar Mobility Test

Assess passive mediolateral range of motion

Divide patella into 4 quadrants and measure displacement
Dynamic Assessment
Patellar Apprehension Test

Apply lateral pressure to patella at 30° flexion

Positive if patient becomes apprehensive at maximum displacement
Patellar Tracking Test

Observe patellar movement during knee extension from 90°

"J sign" indicates maltracking (lateral deviation at terminal extension)
Muscle Flexibility

Assess quadriceps, hamstring, and iliotibial band flexibility

Decreased flexibility associated with PFPS
Muscle Strength

Test quadriceps and hip abduction/external rotation strength

Functional performance tests preferred for athletes
Risk Factors
Strong Risk Factors
Bony and structural abnormalities

Patella alta, trochlear dysplasia, femoral anteversion, knee valgus, laterally displaced tibial tuberosity

Iliotibial band tightness

Affects normal patella excursion and contributes to lateral patellar tilt

Abnormal patellar mobility

Hypomobility associated with tight ITB; hypermobility with lateral glide associated with medial patellofemoral ligament laxity

Quadriceps muscle weakness

Common in PFPS patients; activation imbalance due to delayed vastus medialis activation

Moderate Risk Factors
Subtalar joint pronation

Hyperpronation causes tibial internal rotation, preventing full knee locking

Hip internal rotation

Increased femoral internal rotation leads to increased contact pressure between patella and lateral trochlear groove

Gait deviations

Hyperextension and decreased knee flexion reduce shock absorption

Overuse

Training errors including increasing intensity too quickly, inadequate recovery, excessive hill work

Quick Actions
Treatment Plan for Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome
Acute Phase (Weeks 1-2)
  • Activity modification - reduce activities that increase patellofemoral compressive forces
  • Cold application - 10-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily
  • NSAIDs as needed for pain control
  • Patellar taping if pain limits exercise ability
  • Gentle range of motion exercises
Recovery Phase (Weeks 2-6)
  • Quadriceps strengthening - closed kinetic chain exercises (wall slides, mini-squats)
  • Hip abductor and external rotator strengthening
  • Stretching of tight structures (IT band, hamstrings, quadriceps)
  • Patellar mobilization if hypomobile
  • Gradual return to activity with modified training program
Maintenance Phase (Weeks 6+)
  • Progressive strengthening program
  • Plyometric and sport-specific exercises
  • Continued flexibility program
  • Address gait abnormalities if present
  • Consider orthotics if subtalar pronation is significant

Patient Education Handout
What is Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome?

Patellofemoral pain syndrome is pain around the kneecap (patella) where it meets the thigh bone (femur). It's one of the most common causes of knee pain, especially in athletes and young adults.

What Causes It?

The exact cause is often multifactorial, but may include:

  • Overuse from sports or training activities
  • Muscle weakness or imbalance (especially quadriceps)
  • Alignment problems of the kneecap or leg
  • Tight muscles or soft tissues around the knee
  • Foot problems (flat feet, excessive pronation)
What Can You Do?
  • Activity modification: Reduce activities that cause pain, especially those that involve deep knee bending, stairs, or hills.
  • Ice: Apply ice for 15-20 minutes after activities to reduce pain and inflammation.
  • Exercises: Perform specific strengthening and stretching exercises as prescribed by your healthcare provider.
  • Footwear: Wear appropriate shoes with good support. Consider orthotics if recommended.
  • Gradual return: Return to sports gradually, following your rehabilitation plan.
Follow-up Plan
Initial Follow-up (2 weeks)
  • Assess pain level and functional improvement
  • Review exercise compliance and technique
  • Modify treatment plan as needed
  • Consider referral to physical therapy if not already done
Progress Assessment (6 weeks)
  • Evaluate progress toward functional goals
  • Advance exercise program if appropriate
  • Discuss gradual return to sport/activity
  • Consider imaging if symptoms persist despite appropriate treatment
Long-term Follow-up (3-6 months)
  • Assess maintenance of improvements
  • Review home exercise program compliance
  • Address any recurrent symptoms
  • Consider specialist referral for persistent symptoms
