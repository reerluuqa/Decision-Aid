Assessment

When to Suspect RA

Suspect rheumatoid arthritis (RA) in anyone with persistent synovitis where no other underlying cause is obvious.

  • Symmetrical synovitis of small joints (hands/feet)
  • Pain worse at rest or during inactivity
  • Swelling giving a "boggy" feel on palpation
  • Morning stiffness lasting over 1 hour
  • Rheumatoid nodules over extensor surfaces
  • Systemic features: malaise, fatigue, fever, weight loss
Urgent referral needed: Small joints affected, multiple joints involved, or >3 months delay in seeking care

Initial Investigations

While diagnosis is primarily clinical, consider these tests to speed up the process:

  • Rheumatoid factor (present in 60-70% of RA cases)
  • Anti-CCP antibodies (if RF negative)
  • X-rays of hands and feet
  • FBC, renal/liver function tests
  • Inflammatory markers (CRP/ESR)
Caution: Do not delay referral for specialist assessment while awaiting test results

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnosis is primarily clinical based on:

  • Pattern of joint involvement
  • Serological markers (RF, anti-CCP)
  • Acute phase reactants (CRP, ESR)
  • Symptom duration
Stable: Early diagnosis leads to better outcomes

Differential Diagnosis

Consider these conditions that may mimic RA:

  • Psoriatic arthritis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Gout/pseudogout
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica
  • Viral/bacterial arthritis
  • Fibromyalgia
Caution: Septic arthritis requires immediate intervention

Treatment

Pharmacological Management

Follow a treat-to-target strategy aiming for remission or low disease activity:

  • cDMARDs: Methotrexate (first-line), leflunomide, sulfasalazine
  • Glucocorticoids: Short-term bridging therapy
  • bDMARDs/tsDMARDs: For inadequate response to cDMARDs
  • NSAIDs: Symptom relief (lowest effective dose, shortest duration)
Urgent: Start cDMARDs within 6 weeks of referral

Non-Pharmacological Approaches

  • Physical and occupational therapy
  • Joint protection strategies
  • Smoking cessation
  • Mediterranean diet
  • Regular exercise program
  • Patient education programs
Stable: Multidisciplinary approach improves outcomes

Follow-up

Monitoring & Review

  • Monthly monitoring until treatment target achieved
  • Comprehensive annual review coordinated by rheumatology
  • Assess disease activity, functional ability, comorbidities
  • Medication reviews for adherence and adverse effects
  • Vaccinations (influenza, pneumococcal)
Stable: Regular monitoring prevents complications

Managing Flares

For worsening symptoms:

  • Exclude septic arthritis (single hot/swollen joint + fever)
  • Consider intra-articular glucocorticoid injection
  • Short course of oral glucocorticoids if needed
  • Contact rheumatology within 1 working day
Urgent: Seek specialist advice within 1 working day for flares
