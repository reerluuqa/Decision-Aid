Key Symptoms to Ask About

‚ö†Ô∏è Atypical Presentations

Some patients may present with sensory changes in all fingers, or pain radiating to wrist, forearm, or shoulder.

Risk Factors to Identify

  • Repetitive hand/wrist activities or vibrating tool use
  • Obesity
  • Pregnancy
  • Osteoarthritis of thumb MCP joint
  • Rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory conditions
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Diabetes mellitus
üîç Physical Examination
‚ñº

General Examination

  • Examine entire upper limb including neck, shoulder, elbow, and wrist
  • Look for thenar eminence wasting
  • Assess sensory loss in median nerve distribution
  • Test thumb abduction and opposition strength
  • Check grip and pinch strength

Provocation Tests

Test Method Positive Finding
Phalen's Test Flex wrist fully for 60 seconds Pain/paraesthesia in median nerve distribution
Tinel's Test Tap over median nerve at volar wrist Paraesthesia/pain in median distribution
Durkan's Test Direct thumb pressure over carpal tunnel Reproduces/worsens paraesthesia
üö® Signs of Severe Disease
  • Constant sensory deficit
  • Thenar muscle wasting and weakness
  • Trophic ulceration at digit tips (rare)

Step 2: Diagnosis

Confirming CTS and excluding differentials

‚ñº

Clinical Diagnosis Criteria

‚úÖ CTS is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on:
  • Typical symptom pattern (intermittent paraesthesia in median nerve distribution)
  • Night-time predominance with "flick sign" relief
  • Positive provocation tests
  • Exclusion of alternative diagnoses
‚ö° Nerve Conduction Studies
‚ñº

Not routinely needed for typical presentations

Consider NCS When:

  • Diagnosis is uncertain
  • Surgical referral is being considered
  • Need to exclude polyneuropathy or radiculopathy
  • Atypical presentation
‚ö†Ô∏è Note

NCS have moderate sensitivity and specificity. Patients may benefit from surgery even with normal NCS results.

Carpal Tunnel Decompression

üìä Prognosis Information
‚ñº
üìà Natural History
  • 34% - symptoms resolve spontaneously within 6 months
  • Spontaneous recovery less likely with increasing age
  • Good prognostic factors: short symptom duration, young age, pregnancy-related
  • Poor prognostic factors: bilateral symptoms, positive Phalen's test

Untreated Follow-up Studies

At 2-year follow-up of untreated patients:

  • 47.6% symptoms resolved
  • 28.8% symptoms stable
  • 23.4% symptoms worsened
