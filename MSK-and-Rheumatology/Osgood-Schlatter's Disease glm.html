Definition

Overuse syndrome of the paediatric population resulting in traction apophysitis of the tibial tubercle.

Self-limiting condition that resolves after activity modification, with ultimate resolution at skeletal maturity.

Epidemiology
  • Athletic adolescents 21%
  • Non-athletic adolescents 4.5%
  • Bilateral cases 20-30%
Key Risk Factors
HIGH RISK
  • Adolescent males
  • Athletic participation
  • History of OSD in contralateral knee
LOWER RISK
  • High patella (patella alta)
  • Proximal/broader tibial attachment
  • Increased external tibial torsion
Pathophysiology
Stage 1: Cartilaginous (0-11 years)
Stage 2: Apophyseal (12-14 years) - Disease Process

Secondary ossification center unable to withstand forces from patellar tendon

Stage 3: Epiphyseal (15-18 years)
Stage 4: Bony (>18 years) - Resolution
Key Diagnostic Factors
Pain at tibial tubercle

Worsened by forceful extension, relieved by rest

Localized tenderness

Palpation directly over tibial tubercle elicits pain

Risk factors present

Adolescence, male sex, athletic participation

Other Diagnostic Factors
Activity limitation

Inability to maintain desired athletic performance

Localized swelling

Distinguishable from knee effusion

Localized warmth

Directly over tubercle, not whole knee

Prominence of tibial tubercle

Common later in disease as apophysis matures

Differential Diagnosis
Condition Key Differentiating Features Diagnostic Tests
Tibial tubercle fracture Sudden onset with trauma, unable to extend knee X-ray shows fracture line
Hoffa's disease Tenderness at anterior joint line, not tibial tubercle Normal x-ray
Sinding-Larsen-Johansson Tenderness at inferior pole of patella X-ray shows patellar changes
Osteomyelitis Systemic symptoms, pain at rest Elevated ESR/CRP, MRI changes
Osteosarcoma Systemic symptoms, pain at rest X-ray shows radiolucent lesion
Early Stage Management
Activity Modification

Abstain from sports that provoke pain. Continue if mild pain with no weakness.

Ice & Compression

Apply cold pack to anterior knee as needed. Use compressive bandage if swelling present.

NSAIDs

Ibuprofen 5-10 mg/kg every 6-8 hours (children) or 300-400 mg every 6-8 hours (adults)

Physiotherapy

Start after acute symptoms abate. Focus on quadriceps/hamstrings strengthening and flexibility.

Severe or Prolonged Pain
Consider knee immobilization and bracing for several days to 1 week.

Full weight-bearing should be allowed and encouraged while in brace.

Prolonged immobilization (e.g., cylinder cast) is no longer recommended due to quadriceps atrophy.

Surgical Intervention

Consider for 10-12% of patients with prolonged symptoms after skeletal maturity:

  • Partial resection of tibial tubercle
  • Excision of separated ossicle
  • Drilling of tibial tubercle
Surgery should NOT be performed until after patient reaches skeletal maturity.
PREVENTION: Regular stretching of quadriceps, hamstrings, and iliotibial band. Gradual increase in intensity and duration of athletic activity.
Potential Complications
Recurvatum deformity

Short-term, low likelihood

Early fusion of anterior portion of proximal tibial epiphysis can lead to hyper-extension at knee.

Persistent bony deformity

Long-term, medium likelihood

May be cosmetic concern or cause pain with kneeling on prominent tibial tubercle.

Prognosis
>90%
Excellent prognosis

With non-operative management

Self-limiting course with resolution after activity modification and reaching skeletal maturity.

Excellent relief of symptoms can be expected after surgical treatment of refractory cases.

Follow-up

No routine monitoring necessary once OSD resolves.

Return to sport when:

  • Patient is pain-free
  • Recovers at least 90% of normal strength in affected lower extremity
Quick Scan: Osgood-Schlatter's Disease
RED FLAGS
  • Unilateral symptoms
  • Severe or persistent pain
  • History of trauma
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
TYPICAL SIGNS
  • Bilateral symptoms (20-30%)
  • Pain with activity, relief with rest
  • Tenderness at tibial tubercle
  • Age 8-15 years, athletic
Immediate Actions
Order X-rays
If red flags present

Anteroposterior, lateral, and oblique views of knee
Conservative Management
If typical presentation

Activity modification, ice, NSAIDs, physiotherapy
