Step 1: Assessment

History taking and physical examination

‚ñº

üìù History Taking

  • Onset: Acute, chronic, recurring, or sudden? Related to trauma or specific activity?
  • Nature of pain: Shooting or aching? Severity, timing, provoked/relieved by movement?
  • Location: Where is the pain and does it radiate?
  • Occupational history: Work posture, sedentary position, repetitive tasks?
  • Medical history: Previous neck pain, cancer history, inflammatory conditions?
  • Psychological factors: Anxiety, depression, stress levels?
  • Fever: If present, assume infection until proven otherwise
  • Cord compression symptoms: Lower limb weakness, bowel/bladder disturbance?

üîç Physical Examination

  • Assess neck appearance and range of motion
  • Inspect skin for rash, petechiae, or purpura
  • Palpate neck for tenderness (midline tenderness suggests other pathology)
  • Check for cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Neurological examination: power, tone, reflexes, sensation in arms and legs
  • Consider Spurling test for cervical radiculopathy
  • If meningitis suspected: Kernig's and Brudzinski's signs
‚ÑπÔ∏è Cervical X-rays and imaging are NOT routinely required

Red flags, grading, and differential diagnosis

URGENT Red Flag Features

‚ö†Ô∏è Cervical Myelopathy

‚ñ∂
  • Paresis, sensory changes or loss
  • Clumsy or weak hands, gait disturbance
  • Babinski's sign (up-going plantar reflex)
  • Hoffman's sign, Lhermitte's sign
  • Bowel or bladder dysfunction

‚ö†Ô∏è Malignancy, Infection, Inflammation

‚ñ∂
  • Fever, night sweats, unexplained weight loss
  • Excruciating or intractable night pain
  • Pain that is increasing, generalised stiffness
  • Exquisite tenderness over vertebral body
  • Morning stiffness (inflammatory)
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy
  • History of immunosuppression, TB, or IV drug abuse

‚ö†Ô∏è Other Concerning Features

‚ñ∂
  • History of violent trauma or fall from height
  • Minor trauma in person at risk of osteoporosis
  • History of cancer (assume metastases until excluded)
  • History of neck surgery or inflammatory arthritis
  • Vascular features: blackouts, dizziness, pulsatile neck mass
  • Congenital disorders (e.g., Down's syndrome)

‚úì Clinical Features of Non-Specific Neck Pain

  • Pain aggravated by particular movements, posture, and activities
  • Pain radiating in non-segmental distribution (arm, head, shoulder, scapulae)
  • Pain with paraesthesia/hyperaesthesia BUT no objective sensory/motor loss
  • Positional asymmetry, limited range of movements (often asymmetric)
  • Tenderness in intervertebral joints and/or hypertonic muscles

  • Drug reactions ‚Äì antipsychotics, metoclopramide
  • Infections ‚Äì meningitis, herpes zoster, osteomyelitis
  • Malignancy ‚Äì primary or metastatic
  • Vascular ‚Äì carotid or vertebral artery dissection

üó£Ô∏è Reassurance & Education

‚ñ∂
  • Neck pain is common and usually resolves within a few weeks
  • A firm pillow may provide comfort at night (lateral support, one pillow only)
  • Encourage activity and return to normal lifestyle/work ASAP
  • Do not drive if neck range of motion is restricted
  • Discourage cervical collars ‚Äì restricts mobility and may prolong symptoms
  • Signpost to Versus Arthritis for patient information

Treatment Options

üíä Oral Analgesics

Consider ibuprofen, paracetamol, or codeine based on pain severity, preferences, tolerability and risk of adverse effects.

First-line

üß¥ Topical NSAID

May be appropriate for localised pain. Lower systemic exposure than oral NSAIDs.

Alternative

üèÉ Physiotherapy

Multimodal treatment: stretching, strengthening, range of motion exercises, and manual therapy.

Recommended

üßò Exercise Options

Yoga, Pilates, Tai Chi, or Qigong. Simple neck exercises from Versus Arthritis or Chartered Society of Physiotherapy.

Self-management

‚ö° Additional Options in Specific Situations

  • Occupational health referral: For work-related neck pain
  • Neuropathic pain medication: If radiation present without features requiring investigation (see NICE CKS on Neuropathic pain)

üìà Prognosis Information

  • Most cases of acute neck pain resolve within 2 months
  • Around 50% continue to have low-grade symptoms or recurrences >1 year
  • Non-specific neck pain usually resolves within days/weeks but can recur or become chronic

üìâ Factors Associated with Poorer Prognosis

‚ñ∂
  • Previous episodes of neck pain
  • Poor general health
  • Concurrent low back pain or headaches
  • Psychological factors (anxiety, depression, worry)
  • Work dissatisfaction
