Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA)

Interactive management algorithm. Click sections to expand.

Assessment & Suspicion
Screening

When to Suspect PsA

PsA occurs in around 25% of patients with psoriasis [cite: 12]. Recognition of inflammatory joint symptoms is the first step.

  • Key Symptoms: Prolonged morning stiffness (>30 mins), first-step foot pain, tender/swollen joints [cite: 120-123].
  • Dactylitis: "Sausage digit" swelling, present in 40-50% of patients [cite: 220].
  • Nail Changes: Pitting or onycholysis (80-90% of patients) [cite: 224].

Screening Tools

Use questionnaires like PEST, PASE, or ePASQ to identify PsA in psoriasis patients [cite: 117].

Diagnosis
Clinical

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis is made clinically based on history and physical exam [cite: 115]. Most patients are seronegative.

  • Current psoriasis (2 pts) or history/family history (1 pt).
  • Nail dystrophy (1 pt).
  • Negative Rheumatoid Factor (1 pt) [cite: 85].
  • Dactylitis (current or history) (1 pt).
  • Juxta-articular new bone formation on X-ray (1 pt) [cite: 93].

Investigations

  • Labs: ESR/CRP (markers of inflammation), negative RF/anti-CCP (to exclude RA) [cite: 254].
  • Imaging: Plain X-rays of hands/feet (erosions, periostitis) [cite: 237]. X-rays of spine/pelvis if axial symptoms suspected.
Management Algorithm
Active Disease

Treatment depends on the dominant clinical manifestation. Select a presentation below:

Peripheral Arthritis

Step 1: Treatment Naive

Conventional Synthetic DMARD (csDMARD): Usually Methotrexate (preferred), Leflunomide, or Sulfasalazine [cite: 288, 348].

Adjunct: NSAIDs for symptoms [cite: 285]. Intra-articular steroid injections for oligoarthritis [cite: 401].

Step 2: Inadequate Response

If response is inadequate (3-6 months), escalate therapy [cite: 288].

Biological Agents (bDMARDs):

  • TNF-alpha inhibitors: (e.g., Adalimumab, Etanercept) often first-line biologic [cite: 292].
  • IL-17 inhibitors: (e.g., Secukinumab, Ixekizumab) if relevant skin involvement or TNF contraindication [cite: 381].
  • IL-23 inhibitors: (e.g., Guselkumab).
  • JAK inhibitors: (e.g., Tofacitinib) usually reserved for after biological failure [cite: 306].

Axial Disease

Note csDMARDs (Methotrexate) are generally not effective for axial disease [cite: 312].

Step 1: Initial Therapy

NSAIDs and physiotherapy [cite: 314].

Step 2: Inadequate Response

Escalate directly to Biological Agents:

  • TNF-alpha inhibitors or IL-17 inhibitors (Secukinumab/Ixekizumab) are recommended [cite: 309, 310].
  • JAK inhibitors are an option if NSAIDs fail [cite: 314].
  • IL-12/23 and IL-23 inhibitors are not recommended for axial disease [cite: 312].

Enthesitis & Dactylitis

Dactylitis

Associated with severe disease and radiographic progression [cite: 316].

  • Initial: NSAIDs + Physiotherapy.
  • Escalation: Biologics (TNF, IL-17, IL-12/23, JAK) are effective. Methotrexate may show some efficacy [cite: 580].

Enthesitis

  • Initial: NSAIDs are first-line [cite: 516]. Physiotherapy.
  • Escalation: If NSAIDs fail, switch to Biologics (TNF, IL-17, IL-12/23, JAK) or Apremilast [cite: 534].
Follow Up & Prevention
Long-term

Monitoring

Disease and drug monitoring recommended on a regular basis. Assess CRP, joint counts, and skin extent (BSA) at visits [cite: 611].

Complications & Comorbidities

  • Cardiovascular Disease: Medium likelihood. Risk assessment recommended every 5 years [cite: 614, 618].
  • Metabolic Syndrome: Obesity and dyslipidaemia are common [cite: 616].
  • Infection: Biologicals increase risk. Screen for TB/Hepatitis B prior to initiation [cite: 613].

Supportive Measures

Smoking cessation, weight loss, and low-impact exercise are strongly recommended [cite: 390].
