Osteochondritis Dissecans

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm | BMJ Best Practice Guidelines

Assessment

Initial evaluation and clinical examination

Critical Assessment Factors

  • Patient age (adolescent/young adult athletes most common)
  • Joint-specific symptoms (knee, ankle, elbow)
  • Activity-related pain pattern
  • Presence of mechanical symptoms (locking, catching)
  • Stability assessment is paramount for management decisions

History & Physical Examination

Key Presenting Features

Vague joint pain exacerbated by activity, mechanical symptoms (locking/catching), recurrent effusion.

Common
Knee-Specific Evaluation

• Wilson's test (limited sensitivity/specificity)
• Joint line vs femoral condyle tenderness
• Extension loss from large effusion/loose bodies
• Antalgic or external rotation gait pattern

Special Tests
Ankle-Specific Evaluation

• Tenderness in posteromedial (dorsiflexion) or anterolateral (plantarflexion) talar dome
• Previous sprain history in 90% of cases

6.5% of ankle sprains
Elbow-Specific Evaluation

• Lateral elbow pain in throwing/overhead athletes
• Loss of full extension common
• Active radiocapitellar compression test

Ages 11-23

Imaging Requirements

Initial Imaging

Plain Radiographs: Minimum 2 views of involved joint

X-rays may be normal in early lesions

Advanced Imaging

CT: Best for loose body identification

MRI: Optimal for lesion characterization & stability

MR Arthrogram

Enhances cartilage defect detection when effusion absent

Weight-Bearing Films

Full-length alignment views for lower extremity

Imaging Protocol Note: Knee MRI should include cartilage-sensitive and fluid-sensitive sequences. Lesion stability is determined by presence of high T2 signal (fluid) behind fragment.

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

  • • Repetitive throwing/valgus stress
  • • Gymnastics/weight-bearing on upper extremity
  • • Ankle sprain/instability
  • • Competitive athletics

Weak Risk Factors

  • • Family history
  • • Genetic predisposition (controversial)
Insufficient Data

Diagnosis

Classification and stability assessment

Critical Diagnostic Principle

  • The most critical factor influencing management is lesion stability assessment
  • Stable lesions: maintain subchondral stability, patient denies mechanical symptoms
  • Unstable lesions: subchondral stability lost, mechanical symptoms may be present
  • MRI staging determines prognosis and guides treatment approach

MRI Staging System (Guhl)

Osteochondritis Dissecans MRI Classification
Stage I

Cartilage thickening, no break

Stable
Stage II

Cartilage breached, low signal rim (fibrous attachment)

Stable
Stage III

High T2 signal (fluid) behind fragment

Unstable
Stage IV

Loose body with articular surface defect

Unstable
Accuracy Note: MRI accuracy improves from 45% to 85% when high T2 signal is interpreted as instability only when accompanied by cartilage breach on T1-weighted images.

Juvenile Classification (Hefti)

Stage 1: Small signal change, no clear fragment margins Early
Stage 2: Clear fragment margins, no fluid between fragment and bone Stable
Stage 3: Partial fluid visible between fragment and underlying bone Partially Unstable
Stage 4: Fluid completely surrounding fragment (still in situ) Unstable
Stage 5: Fragment completely detached and displaced Loose Body

Site-Specific Classifications

Capitellum Classification (Baumgarten)

Ia - Intact/Stable: Intact cartilage, no loss of subchondral stability
Ib - Intact/Unstable: Intact cartilage, loss of subchondral stability
II - Open/Unstable: Cartilage fracture with partial displacement
III - Detached: Loose cartilaginous fragment

Treatment

Management strategies based on lesion characteristics

Treatment Principles

  • Stable lesions: Conservative management first line
  • Unstable lesions: Surgical intervention indicated
  • Skeletally immature: Better healing potential
  • Malalignment: Consider osteotomy to unload compartment
  • Duration: Minimum 6 months conservative trial before surgical consideration

Conservative Management Protocol

Phase 1: Initial (4-6 weeks)

  • Activity modification to pain-free level
  • Protected weight-bearing with crutches
  • Short-term immobilization (cast/knee immobilizer)
  • NSAIDs for pain relief

Phase 2: Intermediate (6 weeks)

  • Weight-bearing as tolerated
  • Gentle strengthening program
  • No sport or repetitive impact activities
  • Maintain ROM

Phase 3: Return to Sport

  • Supervised sport readiness program
  • Gradual return to full activity
  • Monitor for symptom recurrence
3-6 months
Analgesia Options:
NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 300-400mg q6-8h (max 2400mg/day) or Naproxen 250-500mg q12h (max 1500mg/day)
Paracetamol: 500-1000mg q4-6h (max 4000mg/day)
Caution: Large stable lesions (>288 mm²) are less likely to heal with conservative care alone. Consider earlier surgical intervention in skeletally mature patients.

Special Considerations

Skeletally Immature

50% heal within 10-18 months with conservative care. Better drilling success rates. Children <12 years show more spontaneous healing.

Better prognosis
Skeletally Mature

Lower healing rates, progressive course. Majority require surgical intervention. Stable lesions may still trial conservative management.

Worse prognosis
Malalignment

If weight-bearing line passes through involved compartment, consider osteotomy to unload area and enhance healing.

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications, and long-term outcomes

Follow-up Principles

  • All patients require orthopedic surgeon follow-up
  • Conservative cases: Monitor until radiographic healing confirmed
  • Post-surgical cases: Per surgeon protocol until symptom resolution/plateau
  • Ongoing assessment for complications essential

Complications

Persistent Pain (Medium Likelihood)

  • Knee: ~67% post-surgical patients
  • Elbow: ~40% post-surgical patients
  • May limit return to sport
Short-term

Articular Incongruity & Early DJD

  • Primary concern in OCD
  • Elbow: >60% show degenerative changes on x-ray
  • Worse outcomes expected in weight-bearing joints
Long-term

Joint Stiffness

  • Especially with immobilization
  • Emphasize ROM maintenance during conservative treatment
Preventable

Prognostic Factors

Favorable Prognosis

• Early detection
• Stable lesions
• Skeletally immature patients
• Smaller lesions
• Compliance with conservative protocol

Positive Predictors
Poor Prognosis

• Unstable lesions
• Skeletally mature patients
• Large lesions
• Perilesional sclerotic rim (in children)
• Previous surgical intervention

Negative Predictors
Special Consideration - Children

Perilesional sclerotic rim predicts worse evolution. Children <12 years show less sclerosis and more spontaneous healing than those >15 years.

Patient Education Points

  • Explain variable healing timeline (6-18 months)
  • Emphasize compliance with activity modification
  • Discuss risk of persistent symptoms even with optimal treatment
  • Address long-term joint health concerns
  • Provide realistic return-to-sport expectations
  • Encourage cross-training and avoiding year-round single sport participation (prevention)

Primary Prevention

Prevention Strategies for Young Athletes

• Encourage cross-training and avoid early sport specialization
• Limit weekly training hours
• Ensure adequate rest periods between seasons
• Monitor for overuse symptoms
• Educate coaches about early warning signs
