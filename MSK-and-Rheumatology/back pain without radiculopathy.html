Low back pain (non‑specific; without radiculopathy)

Adult (≥18y typical) No sciatica / radiculopathy Fast triage → self‑management
Urgency: triage‑based
Content seeded from UK primary-care guidance; tailor to local pathways.

REFER IMMEDIATELY (same day / emergency pathway)

  • Cauda equina features: new urinary retention/overflow, new saddle anaesthesia, new faecal incontinence, rapidly progressive bilateral radicular pain/deficit, unexpected anal sphincter laxity, gait disturbance.
  • Possible fracture: sudden severe central pain relieved by lying down; major trauma; minor trauma/strenuous lift with osteoporosis risk; step deformity; point vertebral tenderness.
  • Possible malignancy: age ≥50, progressive/unremitting pain, thoracic pain, night pain preventing sleep, pain worse on straining, weight loss, prior cancer, no improvement after 4–6 weeks conservative management.
  • Possible infection: fever/systemically unwell; recent infection; diabetes; IVDU; immunocompromise (incl. HIV / immunosuppressants).

SAFETY‑NET (warn / re‑access)

  • Return urgently if any red‑flag symptom develops or neurological deficit progresses.
  • Arrange review if symptoms persist or worsen after 3–4 weeks (clinical judgement).

ROUTINE MANAGEMENT (typical uncomplicated episode)

  • Reassure: acute non‑specific LBP usually self‑limiting; most recover within weeks.
  • Encourage activity and early return to usual activities/work; avoid prolonged bed rest.
  • Do not routinely order imaging for non‑specific LBP in primary care.
Assessment: history, exam, imaging
triage + rule out serious cause
  • History: onset, pattern, duration; aggravating/relieving factors; radiation; neuro symptoms; systemic symptoms; prior episodes; psychosocial impact and work/sleep.
  • Examination: gait/posture; deformity; localized tenderness; ROM; neurological exam for deficits/upper motor neuron signs. Consider perianal sensation/anal tone if clinically indicated.
  • Risk stratification: use a tool (e.g., STarT Back) to identify risk of prolonged/complex recovery and guide intensity of support.
  • Imaging: avoid routine X‑ray/CT/MRI in non‑specific LBP; consider only if serious pathology suspected (e.g., fracture, malignancy).
Initial management: self‑management + analgesia
start simple; short courses
  • Core advice: keep active; resume normal movement; gradual pacing; return to work/study early; discourage bed rest.
  • Non‑pharmacological: consider short‑term local heat; advise regular exercise to reduce recurrence.
  • Analgesia (symptom relief):
    • Do not use paracetamol alone as a recommended strategy for non‑specific LBP.
    • NSAID first‑line (e.g., ibuprofen) if no contraindications: lowest effective dose, shortest duration; consider gastroprotection if indicated.
    • If NSAID contraindicated/not tolerated/ineffective: consider short‑term codeine ± paracetamol, balancing adverse effects and dependence risk.
    • Do not use benzodiazepines for muscle spasm in acute LBP.
    • Do not use opioids for chronic non‑specific LBP; avoid gabapentinoids/antiepileptics/antidepressants for LBP without radiculopathy.
  • Adjunct pathway options (risk factors for poor recovery): group exercise; manual therapy as part of a package including exercise; CBT as part of a package when psychosocial barriers prominent.
Follow‑up & referral
3–4 weeks trigger
  • If persistent/worsening after 3–4 weeks: reassess for evolving underlying cause, adherence, and response.
  • Escalate if red flags/new concerning features.
  • For chronic LBP: consider combined physical + psychological approach.
  • When non‑invasive measures fail in chronic LBP: consider specialist back pain service (e.g., radiofrequency denervation; spinal cord stimulation in selected neuropathic pain phenotypes).
Differentials and mimics
consider alternate sources
  • Neurological: sciatica/radiculopathy; myelopathy/higher cord lesion; peripheral neuropathy (e.g., peroneal palsy); spinal stenosis; deep gluteal syndrome.
  • Systemic/inflammatory: sacroiliitis/axial spondyloarthropathy; polymyalgia rheumatica.
  • Vascular: claudication; abdominal aortic aneurysm.
  • MSK/other: hip pathology (OA, AVN), greater trochanteric pain syndrome, shingles; intra‑abdominal/urological causes (renal colic, pyelonephritis, pancreatitis, prostatitis, PID).
What to say (high‑yield patient messaging)
micro‑script
  • Reassure: movement‑related pain is common and not necessarily harmful; aim is gradual return to function.
  • Plan: keep active; short course analgesia if needed; heat and gentle exercise.
  • Work: early return with adjustments is typically beneficial.
  • Safety‑net: seek urgent care for new bladder/bowel symptoms, saddle numbness, progressive weakness, fever, unexplained weight loss, new night pain, or major trauma.
