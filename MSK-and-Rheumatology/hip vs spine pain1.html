🩺 Hip vs Spine Pain - Rapid Decision Aid

Quick reference for differentiating hip and lumbar spine pathology based on history & examination

Core Principle: Scans can lie; history & examination won't. Treat the patient, not the test result.

🦴 Hip Origin Clues

  • Pain: Groin (60-70%), upper thigh, lateral hip
  • Radiation: Stops at or before knee
  • Pattern: Worse with twisting, loading, first steps after sitting
  • Function: Difficulty with shoes/socks, giving way, stiffness
  • Exam: Restricted internal rotation, muscle wasting (thigh/gluteal)

🦴 Spine Origin Clues

  • Pain: Buttock, radiates below knee (calf/ankle)
  • Pattern: Claudication (walk→pain, rest→relief) in stenosis
  • Posture: Worse sitting/slouched (disc), better leaning forward (stenosis)
  • Neuro: Numbness, weakness, objective power loss
  • Exam: Positive straight leg raise, abnormal neurology

⚠️ Overlap & Confusion

  • L1/L2 radiculopathy → groin pain (mimics hip)
  • Isolated nerve fiber irritation → buttock/hip pain only
  • Pelvic tilt from hip OA → back pain
  • Co-existing pathology very common >50 years
  • Normal MRI findings over 40; correlate with symptoms
📋 History & Symptom Patterns ▼

Hip-Related Pain

  • Location: Groin (most common), anterior thigh, lateral hip, buttock
  • Radiation: Down to knee but NOT beyond
  • Onset: Twisting movements, weight-bearing, getting out of chair (stiff first steps)
  • Relief: Rest, avoiding painful movements
  • Function: Difficulty with socks/shoes, giving way/locking sensation

Spine-Related Pain

  • Sciatica (Disc): Buttock → posterior thigh → calf/ankle; worse sitting, bending forward; better walking initially
  • Stenosis: Buttock/thigh pain; worse walking/standing; better leaning forward, sitting, rest (claudication)
  • Neurology: Numbness, tingling, weakness in specific dermatomes/myotomes
  • L1/L2: Can cause groin pain (hip mimic)
🔍 Physical Examination ▼

Hip Examination (Look → Feel → Move)

  • Look: Gait from waiting room, limp, muscle wasting (thigh/gluteals), leg length discrepancy
  • Move: Compare both sides
    • Internal rotation (first to go in hip pathology)
    • External rotation at 90° flexion
    • Abduction/adduction range
  • Special: Pain reproduced with hip movements, not with straight leg raise

Spine Examination

  • Look: Gait pattern (antalgic in sciatica, stooped in stenosis), muscle wasting
  • Move: Straight leg raise test
    • Positive: Pain radiates down leg
    • Hip vs Spine: Pain eases when knee flexed (spine), not with hip pathology
  • Neurological: Mandatory if suspect spine
    • Power: Heel/toe walking, ankle/knee/hip movements
    • Sensation: Dermatomal testing
    • Reflexes: Knee/ankle
🩻 Investigations & Imaging ▼

Hip Imaging

  • X-ray (ESSENTIAL): Pelvis AP within 1 year
    • Compare both hips, assess joint space, bone quality
    • May include lower lumbar spine for co-existing disease
    • Look for subtle dysplasia (may be missed on reports)
  • MRI: If X-ray normal but high suspicion, or for soft tissue pathology (labral tears, FAI)

Spine Imaging

  • MRI (ESSENTIAL for nerves): Shows disc, stenosis, nerve compression
    • CAVEAT: >40 years, degenerative changes are NORMAL and may be asymptomatic
    • Correlate imaging with clinical findings
  • X-ray (Dynamic): Standing AP/lateral + flexion/extension views
    • Assesses instability (spondylolisthesis)
⚠️ Critical: Over-interpretation of scans is common. A report of "degenerative changes" does NOT equal diagnosis. Always correlate with clinical picture.
💉 Diagnostic Injections ▼

Hip Block

  • Indication: Diagnostic uncertainty, mixed pathology, normal imaging with high suspicion
  • Interpretation: >50% pain relief = hip is source
  • Therapeutic use: Temporary relief for events (holidays, weddings)
  • Rules: Max 3 injections in 2 years; avoid surgery within 3 months (infection risk)
  • Risk: Rarely accelerates underlying arthritis

Nerve Block (Epidural/Root Block)

  • Indication: Confirm spinal nerve root as pain source
  • Interpretation: Complete, even temporary, relief confirms spine origin
  • Therapeutic: NERVE trial: 2/3 patients with herniated disc got long-term relief equal to surgery
  • Best for: Sciatica without progressive weakness; reduces need for surgery
  • Mechanism: Reduces nerve inflammation, allows natural disc resorption
🛤️ Management Pathways ▼

Hip Pathology

20s-30s

FAI/Labral Tear: Activity-related pain, often post-sport. Refer to hip preservation specialist if refractory.

40s-50s (Perimenopausal)

Early OA: Estrogen-related acceleration. Trial injection + physiotherapy.

50+

Established OA: Failed conservative measures → total hip replacement.

Spine Pathology

Majority

Non-operative: 90% improve within 3 months. Simple analgesia, physiotherapy, injection for pain window.

Surgical

Indications: Progressive weakness, severe/lasting symptoms, failed conservative care.

  • Microdiscectomy: Minimally invasive, remove disc fragment/ligament
  • Decompression: For stenosis
  • Fusion (rare): For instability with metalwork
Referral Trigger: If you cannot confidently differentiate after history/exam → refer to both spinal AND hip specialists simultaneously. Multidisciplinary discussion is key.
🎯 Key Principles & Red Flags ▼
  • Diagnosis is clinical: History + examination > imaging. Scans guide, not dictate.
  • Co-existence is common: >50 years, expect both spine and hip degenerative changes. Determine primary pain generator.
  • When in doubt, block it: Diagnostic injections are the most reliable differentiator.
  • Patient expectations: Most want a clear diagnosis and care pathway, not necessarily immediate surgery.
  • Red Flags (urgent referral): Cauda equina symptoms, progressive motor weakness, severe unremitting night pain, systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss), recent major trauma.
  • Multidisciplinary care: Direct communication between hip/spine surgeons and physios improves outcomes.
