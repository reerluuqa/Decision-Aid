Back Pain
Red Flags: Fever, night pain, systemic symptoms, neurological signs, progressive deformity
Urgent Referral

Systemic symptoms, neurological signs, persistent pain, functional limitation

Routine Referral

Non-specific mechanical pain not responding to conservative measures

Reassurance

Short-lived, non-specific pain without red flags

Common in school-age and adolescents

Most back pain in children is non-specific and self-limiting. However, serious underlying pathology must be excluded. Management includes rest from exacerbating activities and possibly physical therapy.

Bow Legs
Normal Finding: Common in children 10-14 months, usually resolves by 18-24 months
Routine Referral

Persistent after 2 years, unilateral, asymmetrical, progressive worsening

Reassurance

Bilateral, symmetrical, painless in children under 2 years

Common in children 10-14 months

Physiological bow legs are common and self-limiting. Pathological causes include rickets, skeletal dysplasia, trauma, infection, tumours or Blount's disease.

Clumsy Child
Red Flags: Regression of skills, asymmetry, focal neurological signs
Urgent Referral

Regression of skills, progressive worsening, asymmetry, abnormal neurological exam

Routine Referral

Persistent coordination problems affecting daily activities and school performance

Consider

Developmental coordination disorder, visual impairment, ADHD, autism spectrum disorder

Common in school-age children

Developmental coordination disorder is a common neurodevelopmental disorder. It may co-exist with ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, and developmental dyslexia.

Curly Toes
Normal Finding: Congenital, affects 3% of people, often noticed in infancy
Routine Referral

Persistent pain, problems with footwear, rigid deformity after 6 years

Reassurance

Asymptomatic, flexible deformity, often self-corrects by age 6

Often noticed in infancy

Underlapping toe ('curly' toe) deformity is thought to be congenital, most commonly affecting the fourth and fifth toes. Most cases are passively correctable and rarely rigid.

Flat Feet
Normal Finding: Common in babies and toddlers, usually resolves by 6 years
Routine Referral

Pain, rigid deformity, asymmetry, lack of arch on tiptoes, abnormal neurology

Reassurance

Flexible, painless flat feet in otherwise healthy children under 6 years

Normal in babies and toddlers

Flat feet (pes planus) is normal in babies and toddlers due to fatty tissue on the insoles. It usually resolves by 6 years. Pathological causes include juvenile idiopathic arthritis, cerebral palsy, or Down's syndrome.

Growing Pains
Typical Features: Symmetrical lower limb pain, evening/night pain, normal exam
Red Flags: Joint swelling, systemic symptoms, morning stiffness, limp, fever
Routine Referral

Atypical presentation, persistent symptoms, parental concern despite reassurance

Reassurance

Typical presentation with normal examination and no red flags

Usually between 3-12 years

Growing pains is a benign, non-inflammatory syndrome of recurrent discomfort. The cause is unknown, but there seems to be a genetic susceptibility. Physical examination is usually normal.

Heel Pain
Common Cause: Calcaneal apophysitis (Sever's disease) in active children 8-15 years
Red Flags: Fever, inability to bear weight, swelling, redness, systemic symptoms
Urgent Referral

Signs of infection (osteomyelitis), suspected fracture, tumour, inability to bear weight

Routine Referral

Severe or persistent pain not responding to conservative measures

Reassurance

Typical Sever's disease with activity-related pain relieved by rest

Usually 8-15 years

Calcaneal apophysitis (Sever's disease) is the most common cause of heel pain in children. Management includes reassurance, avoiding exacerbating activities, anti-inflammatory medication, ice, Achilles stretching, and heel cup shoe inserts.

Hypermobility
Common Finding: Joints moving beyond normal range, often decreases with age
Routine Referral

Pain affecting function, recurrent joint instability, significant fatigue

Reassurance

Asymptomatic hypermobility without pain or functional limitation

Consider

Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan syndrome, hypermobility spectrum disorder

All ages, more common in females

Joint hypermobility is common in children and may be a normal variant. It can be asymptomatic, part of a syndrome (like Ehlers-Danlos), or symptomatic but not meeting criteria for a syndrome (hypermobility spectrum disorder).

In-toeing
Common Finding: Up to 30% of children under 6 may exhibit in-toeing as normal development
Routine Referral

Persistent after 8 years, functional limitation, asymmetry, worsening over time

Reassurance

Typical presentation with normal development, usually resolves over time

Common Causes

Internal tibial torsion (resolves by age 5), femoral anteversion (resolves in 80% by age 8), metatarsus adductus

Common in children under 6 years

In-toeing is usually due to normal developmental variation. The three main causes are internal tibial torsion, femoral anteversion, and metatarsus adductus. Most cases resolve spontaneously.

Knock Knees
Normal Finding: Usually bilateral between 3-6 years, resolves by 7 years
Routine Referral

Persistent after 7 years, unilateral, asymmetrical, progressive worsening

Reassurance

Bilateral, symmetrical in children 3-6 years without functional limitation

Common between 3-6 years

Knock knees (genu valgum) is usually a self-limited, asymptomatic condition. It's exacerbated by obesity, ligamentous laxity, and flat feet. Pathological causes include trauma, fracture, and previous osteomyelitis.

Late Walking
Normal Range: Most children walk independently by 18 months
Red Flags: Regression of motor skills, asymmetry, abnormal neurological exam
Urgent Referral

Regression of skills, abnormal neurological signs, asymmetry, not walking by 18 months with other developmental concerns

Routine Referral

Not walking independently by 18 months with otherwise normal development

Most walk by 12-14 months with assistance

Most children start walking with assistance by 12-14 months. Late walking may be normal variation or indicate underlying pathology. Assessment should include developmental history and physical examination.

Out-toeing
Normal Finding: Less common than in-toeing, often associated with bow legs
Routine Referral

Persistent after 8 years, functional limitation, asymmetry, worsening over time

Reassurance

Typical presentation with normal development, usually resolves over time

Can occur at any age

Out-toeing is less common than in-toeing and is often associated with bow legs in young children. It usually resolves spontaneously but may require assessment if persistent or associated with functional limitations.

Tip Toe Walking
Normal Finding: Common in toddlers learning to walk, usually resolves by age 3
Red Flags: Asymmetry, regression of skills, abnormal neurological exam, progressive contractures
Urgent Referral

Asymmetrical toe walking, regression of skills, abnormal neurological signs, progressive worsening

Routine Referral

Persistent after age 3, bilateral but with tight Achilles tendons, functional limitation

Reassurance

Intermittent toe walking in children under 3 years with normal development

Common in toddlers, usually resolves by age 3

Tip toe walking is common in toddlers learning to walk. Persistent toe walking may be idiopathic, habitual, or associated with cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy, or autism spectrum disorder.
