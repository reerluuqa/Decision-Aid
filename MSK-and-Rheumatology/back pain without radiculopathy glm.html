Assessment Approach
1
Ask about onset, type, site, pattern of pain, radiation, duration, aggravating/relieving factors
2
Screen for red flags (see Red Flags tab)
3
Assess impact on daily functioning and psychological wellbeing
4
Examine gait, posture, spine for deformity, localized tenderness, range of movement
5
Perform neurological examination if symptoms suggest nerve involvement
6
Use risk stratification tool (e.g., Keele STarT Back) to guide management
Key Questions
Is pain mechanical (varies with posture/movement)?
Any history of previous episodes?
Any associated symptoms (morning stiffness, weakness, numbness)?
What medications have been tried?
Examination Focus
Inspect posture and gait
Palpate for tenderness and deformity
Assess range of movement
Consider neurological examination if indicated
Non-Specific Low Back Pain Characteristics
Pain varies with posture and time
Exacerbated by movement
No red flags present
No specific underlying cause identified
Initial Management
Reassure that most back pain improves within weeks
Advise to stay active and continue normal activities
Offer self-management information and resources
Consider NSAIDs if not contraindicated
Pharmacological Options
First-line: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
Weak opioids only if NSAIDs ineffective
Avoid paracetamol alone
Avoid benzodiazepines for muscle spasm
Avoid gabapentinoids for non-radicular pain
Non-Pharmacological Options
Group exercise programs
Physiotherapy
Cognitive behavioral therapy
Manual therapy (short-term only)
Acupuncture (short-term only)
What to Avoid
Routine imaging for non-specific back pain
Bed rest
Spinal injections (except radiofrequency denervation)
Opioids for chronic back pain
Referral Considerations
Specialist assessment if symptoms persist > 6 weeks with risk factors
Urgent referral if red flags present
Consider referral for multidisciplinary assessment if chronic pain
Consider psychological assessment if significant yellow flags
Risk Stratification Approach

Use a validated tool like the Keele STarT Back to identify patients at risk of prolonged recovery and guide management decisions.

Risk Category Characteristics Management Approach
Low Risk No risk factors for chronicity Self-management, reassurance, simple analgesia
Medium Risk Some psychosocial risk factors Physiotherapy, more intensive support
High Risk Multiple psychosocial risk factors, high distress Specialist referral, multidisciplinary approach

Key Differentiating Features
Radiculopathy: Pain radiating below knee, dermatomal distribution, neurological deficit
Spinal stenosis: Pain worsens with walking, improves with sitting/forward flexion
Ankylosing spondylitis: Morning stiffness > 30 min, improves with activity, age < 40
Malignancy: Night pain, weight loss, history of cancer
Infection: Fever, systemic symptoms, recent infection
