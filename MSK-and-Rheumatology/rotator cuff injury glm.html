Rotator Cuff Injury - Clinical Reference

Last updated: Sep 19, 2025

Quick Summary

Red Flags: Acute traumatic tears in younger patients, significant weakness, or failure of conservative treatment after 10-12 weeks may require surgical intervention.
Prevalence: Affects 6.8%-22.4% of people over 40, increasing with age (up to 50% in those over 80)
Causes: Traumatic or attritional (repetitive overhead activity, age-related degeneration)
Key Symptoms: Anterolateral shoulder pain, night pain, weakness with overhead activities
Diagnostic Tests: Empty-can test, External rotation test, Lift-off test, Belly-press test

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Shoulder pain: Most common complaint, worse at night, aggravated by overhead activities
  • Shoulder weakness: Especially with resisted external rotation, abduction, or internal rotation
  • Loss of active range of motion: Highly suggestive of rotator cuff tear
  • Risk factors: Age >60 years, history of repetitive overhead movement

Differentiating Factors

  • Rotator cuff impingement: Weakness due to pain, strength improves after pain-relieving injection
  • Greater tuberosity fracture: Identical presentation to acute tear, but X-ray shows fracture
  • Glenohumeral osteoarthritis: Painful stiffness with limitation of both active and passive motion
  • Adhesive capsulitis: Symmetrical loss of both passive and active motion due to soft-tissue contracture

Initial Imaging

X-rays should be obtained in the presence of trauma or if there is concern about tumor or infection.
  • Standard views: True anteroposterior, axillary lateral, outlet/scapular Y views
  • Typical findings: Usually normal in acute non-traumatic injury
  • Large tears: May show superior migration of humeral head
  • Acute massive tears: May show pseudosubluxation of humeral head

Advanced Imaging

  • MRI without contrast: 91% sensitivity, 97% specificity for full-thickness tears
  • Ultrasound: 95% sensitivity, 96% specificity for full-thickness tears; operator-dependent
  • MR arthrography: More effective for both partial and full thickness tears
  • CT arthrography: Comparable to MR arthrography for full-thickness tears but inferior for partial-thickness tears

Management

General Approach: Treatment is based on degree of dysfunction, pain, quality of tendons and muscles, location of tear, and patient goals and activity level.

Treatment Algorithm

Surgical Indications

  • Younger, active patients
  • Higher activity level desired
  • Weakness and substantial functional disability
  • Pain continuing despite several months of physiotherapy
  • Full use of arm required for vocational or recreational needs

Conservative Management

  • Older and sedentary patients
  • Small tears with mild loss of ROM and strength
  • Low functional demands
  • Ice, stretching, and NSAIDs
  • Single subacromial corticosteroid injection if needed
  • Range of motion and cuff/scapular strengthening exercises

Considerations for Chronic Tears

  • Time since injury: Function and appearance of torn rotator cuff deteriorates with time
  • Muscle atrophy: Chronic tears can lead to muscle atrophy and fatty replacement
  • Risk factors for re-tear: Fatty infiltration and symptom duration
  • Patients over 60: Favorable outcome can still be expected if tissue quality remains sufficient
