Rehabilitation after Traumatic Injury
NICE Guideline [NG211] – Primary Care Algorithm (Feb 2022)

Interactive card-based algorithm for healthcare professionals

1. Assessment
On track with plan

At discharge the hospital multidisciplinary team must provide an individualised rehabilitation plan (sometimes called a rehabilitation prescription) that includes:

  • Details of injuries
  • Short- and long-term goals
  • Patient needs & preferences
  • Programme of therapies/treatments and how they will be delivered
  • Return to work/education/leisure advice
  • Driving advice & DVLA guidance
  • Follow-up arrangements and key contacts

In primary care you are part of the MDT supporting the plan:

  • Monitor progress against goals using PROMs/CROMs (adults) or child/parent-reported measures
  • Encourage the person to keep a diary of symptoms, treatments and progress
  • Assess psychological and cognitive functioning at every contact
  • Act as (or liaise with) the rehabilitation coordinator/key worker
Note: Severity of injury does NOT correlate with complexity of rehabilitation needs – always use a person-centred holistic approach.
2. Identifying Needs & Complications
Caution

Actively screen at every contact for:

  • Ongoing pain, mobility problems, sensory loss
  • Anxiety, depression, PTSD symptoms (can occur or recur at any time)
  • Acute stress reaction (common in first 4–6 weeks: sleep disturbance, flashbacks, low mood, nightmares)
  • Cognitive impairment (common even without TBI)
  • Thoughts of self-harm or suicide
  • Social/educational/vocational isolation

Red flags requiring urgent action:

  • Urgent referral to psychology – psychological problems are preventing engagement with rehabilitation
  • Urgent safeguarding / mental health review – suicidal ideation or risk to others
3. Management & Support
Stable / progressing

Core actions in primary care:

  • Provide education about the injury, expected recovery trajectory and self-management (sleep, pacing, pain)
  • Offer tailored psychological & emotional support as part of the rehabilitation programme
  • Consider a guided self-managed programme with online resources covering:
    • Movement & exercise
    • Energy conservation
    • Sleep hygiene
    • Nutrition
    • Pain management
    • Mental health
    • Wound care & peer support
  • Support return to work/education – advise employers have duty to make reasonable adjustments
  • Offer information about falls prevention, safeguarding, domestic abuse or violence prevention services as appropriate
4. Follow-up & Referral
Review required

Follow-up frequency:

  • According to the rehabilitation plan
  • Review & update the rehabilitation prescription at least every 4–6 weeks (BSRM recommendation)
  • Patients discharged from critical care → functional reassessment 2–3 months post-discharge (physical, sensory, communication, psychological, social dimensions)

Refer or escalate if:

  • Urgent psychology – rehabilitation engagement blocked by psychological issues
  • Specialist rehabilitation team – recovery slower than expected or new morbidity identified
  • Specific services as required (orthogeriatrician, spinal cord injury centre, peripheral nerve service, falls clinic, etc.)
