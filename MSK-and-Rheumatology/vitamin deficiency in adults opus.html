1. Assessment

Identify when to test and recognize risk factors

When to Test for Vitamin D

Key Decision
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Do NOT routinely test for vitamin D deficiency in asymptomatic people.

Check serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] if a person has:

  • Musculoskeletal symptoms that may be attributable to vitamin D deficiency (e.g., suspected osteomalacia, chronic widespread pain with proximal muscle weakness)
  • Suspected bone disease that may be improved with vitamin D treatment (osteomalacia or osteoporosis)
  • Prior to specific treatment: Before starting potent antiresorptive agents (zoledronate, denosumab, teriparatide) or bisphosphonates for Paget's disease

Risk Factors for Vitamin D Deficiency

Identify

Adults at higher risk of vitamin D deficiency include:

  • Aged 65 years and over
  • Low/no sun exposure: Cover skin for cultural/religious reasons, housebound, or confined indoors
  • Darker skin pigmentation: African, African-Caribbean, or South Asian origin
  • Malabsorption disorders: Coeliac disease, Crohn's disease, cystic fibrosis, or post-bariatric surgery
  • Severe liver disease or end-stage CKD
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Obesity (BMI >30 kg/m¬≤): Vitamin D may be sequestered in adipose tissue
  • Drugs increasing deficiency risk: Antiepileptics, corticosteroids, orlistat, rifampicin, HAART
üí° Note: Asymptomatic people at higher risk do NOT need routine testing but should be advised on maintenance vitamin D supplementation.

If vitamin D deficiency or insufficiency is confirmed, consider:

  • Bone profile: Calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase
  • Parathyroid hormone (PTH) level

üî¨ Assess for Underlying Cause

  • FBC, ferritin, B12, folate
  • Renal and liver function tests
  • Thyroid function tests
  • Coeliac serology if malabsorption suspected

Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that may present similarly with bone pain and/or muscle weakness:

  • Malignancy: Bone cancer, soft tissue sarcoma, myeloma
  • Fibromyalgia: Myofascial trigger points and somatic symptoms
  • Hyperparathyroidism: Bone pain, muscle weakness, hypercalcaemia symptoms
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: Bilateral shoulder/pelvic pain, age >50
  • Thyroid disease: Fatigue and muscle weakness
  • Paget's disease: Dull weight-bearing pain, bowing of bones, elderly men

3. Treatment

Loading doses, maintenance therapy, and referral criteria

When to Refer Before Starting Treatment

Urgent

Seek Specialist Advice BEFORE Starting Vitamin D

  • Hypercalcaemia risk: Granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, TB), metastatic bone disease, some lymphomas, primary hyperparathyroidism
  • GI/Malabsorption disorders: May need intensive high-dose replacement under specialist supervision
  • Active renal stones or history: Risk of vitamin D toxicity causing hypercalciuria
  • Severe liver disease or end-stage CKD: May need activated vitamin D metabolites
  • Pregnancy: Specialist advice required

Lifestyle Advice

  • Safe sun exposure: Short periods (not burning/tanning) with forearms, hands, or lower legs exposed, especially April-September
  • Dietary sources: Oily fish, egg yolk, fortified cereals/spreads, red meat, liver (limited sources)
  • Avoid sunbeds: Ineffective for vitamin D synthesis and potentially harmful
  • Long-term supplementation: Advise continuation to maintain bone health

Long-term
