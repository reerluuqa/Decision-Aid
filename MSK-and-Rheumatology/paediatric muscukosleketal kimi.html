üìã Key History Points

  • Parental concerns: Pain, gait, function, duration
  • Pain characteristics: Site, timing, radiation, severity
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • Development: Motor milestones, regression
  • Family history: Arthritis, musculoskeletal conditions
  • Trauma history: Recent injuries, sports activities

ü©∫ Examination Framework

Use pGALS assessment:

  • Gait observation (walking, running)
  • Spine examination (curvature, tenderness)
  • Joint assessment (swelling, warmth, ROM)
  • Postural assessment (standing, sitting)
  • Neurological screen (tone, reflexes, power)
  • Growth parameters (height, weight)

üî¥ Red Flag Screening

RED FLAG Systemic Features
  • Prolonged morning stiffness (>30 min)
  • Night pain (not growing pains)
  • Fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly
Local Features
  • Joint swelling, warmth, erythema
  • Limp or refusal to bear weight
  • Single joint involvement
  • Neurological deficit
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction
ü©∫ Differential Diagnosis
‚ñº

ü¶¥ Back Pain

Common

Usually non-specific and self-limiting

Common Causes:
  • Muscular strain (poor posture)
  • Overuse injuries (sports)
  • Scheuermann's disease
  • Spondylolysis/spondylolisthesis
Serious Causes:
  • Discitis, osteomyelitis
  • Spinal tumors
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis
  • Ankylosing spondylitis

ü¶µ Lower Limb Variants

Bow Legs (Genu Varum): Normal <3 years
  • Physiological: bilateral, symmetrical, painless
  • Resolves by 18 months-2 years
  • Pathological: rickets, Blount's disease, dysplasia
Knock Knees (Genu Valgum): Normal 3-6 years
  • Physiological: bilateral, symmetrical
  • Resolves by 7 years
  • Exacerbated by obesity, ligamentous laxity
Flat Feet (Pes Planus): Normal in toddlers
  • Flexible: arch present on tiptoe, normal variant
  • Resolves by 6 years
  • Rigid: suggests pathology
Toe Walking: Normal <3 years
  • Idiopathic: diagnosis of exclusion
  • 80% resolve by 10 years
  • Rule out: CP, muscular dystrophy, ASD

üîÑ Rotational Variants

In-toeing: Common <6 years
  • Metatarsus adductus (most common 0-1 year)
  • Internal tibial torsion (1-3 years)
  • Femoral anteversion (3-8 years, W-sitting)
Out-toeing: Normal early infancy
  • External tibial torsion
  • Femoral retroversion
  • Usually resolves by 18-24 months

üò¥ Growing Pains

Benign 3-12 years

Diagnosis of exclusion

Typical Features:
  • Symmetrical lower limb pain
  • Evening/night pain, morning-free
  • Normal examination
  • No limp, normal activity
  • Systemically well
Atypical Features ‚Üí Refer:
  • Unilateral or asymmetric pain
  • Persistent/increasing severity
  • Morning pain
  • Joint swelling/stiffness
  • Systemic symptoms

ü§∏ Hypermobility

Common variant 34% prevalence
Benign Hypermobility:
  • Asymptomatic, no functional impairment
  • Common in children, decreases with age
  • May be associated with flat feet
Pathological Hypermobility:
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
  • Marfan syndrome
  • Joint hypermobility spectrum disorder
  • Associated pain, fatigue, dysautonomia

ü§π Clumsy Child

Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD): 5-6% prevalence
  • Motor skills below expected level
  • Interferes with daily activities
  • Co-occurs with ADHD, autism, dyslexia
  • Male predominance
Other Causes:
  • Visual impairment
  • Orthopaedic disorders
  • Mild cerebral palsy
  • Hereditary ataxia

üë∂ Delayed Walking

Normal range

Girls: by 15 months | Boys: by 18 months

Physiological Delay:
  • Bottom shuffling (common)
  • Family history of late walking
  • Otherwise normal development
Pathological Causes:
  • Cerebral palsy
  • Muscular dystrophy
  • Spina bifida
  • Rickets
  • Developmental hip dysplasia

üè° Community Management

PRIMARY CARE
Reassurance & Advice:
  • Physiological variants (bow legs, knock knees, flat feet)
  • Growing pains with typical features
  • Mild hypermobility without symptoms
  • Idiopathic toe walking <3 years
  • Simple back pain without red flags

  • Back pain with normal examination
  • Hypermobility with mild symptoms
  • Flexibility exercises for toe walking
  • Postural advice for growing pains
  • Sever's disease (heel pain)

üè• Specialist Referral Criteria

SECONDARY CARE
Orthopaedics:
  • Bow legs >3 years or severe deformity
  • Knock knees <2 or >5-6 years
  • Rigid flat feet/asymmetry
  • Persistent rotational deformities
  • Fixed metatarsus adductus
  • Sever's disease unresponsive to conservative treatment
Rheumatology:
  • Inflammatory arthritis features
  • Hypermobility with significant symptoms
  • Suspected connective tissue disorders
  • Joint pain with swelling/stiffness
  • Complex regional pain syndrome
Paediatrics:
  • Delayed walking with other concerns
  • Suspected neurological disorders
  • Systemic symptoms
  • Developmental regression
  • Failure to thrive

üéØ Condition-Specific Management

Sever's Disease (Heel Pain)

  • Avoid aggravating activities
  • Ice, NSAIDs for pain relief
  • Heel cups/orthoses
  • Achilles stretching exercises
  • Self-limiting, resolves with growth

Growing Pains

  • Parental reassurance
  • Massage and heat application
  • Simple analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen)
  • Consistency in bedtime routine
  • Monitor for atypical features

Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder

  • Strengthening exercises
  • Proprioceptive training
  • Activity modification
  • Pain management strategies
  • Multidisciplinary approach

üìÖ Monitoring Schedule

Physiological Variants:
  • Review in 6-12 months
  • Photo documentation of progression
  • Measure intercondylar/intermalleolar distances
  • Document gait patterns
  • Parental reassurance at each visit
Pathological Conditions:
  • More frequent monitoring (1-3 months)
  • Specialist follow-up as advised
  • Multidisciplinary team involvement
  • Parental education and support
  • School liaison when needed
Red Flag Monitoring:
  • Immediate action if red flags develop
  • Parental awareness of warning signs
  • 24-hour contact advice
  • Timely specialist referral
