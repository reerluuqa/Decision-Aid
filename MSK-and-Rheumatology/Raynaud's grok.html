Raynaud’s Phenomenon
Interactive Management Algorithm

NICE CKS – Last revised October 2024 | From age 5 years onwards

Assessment

History – key questions

  • Episodic, clearly demarcated colour change (blanching essential) triggered by cold or stress?
  • Symmetrical attacks, sparing thumbs?
  • Duration (typical 15–20 min after rewarming)?
  • Numbness/paraesthesia → pain on rewarming?
  • Age at onset (primary usually teens/20s; secondary often >30)?
  • Family history of Raynaud’s?
  • Symptoms suggestive of CTD (fatigue, joint stiffness/swelling, rash, dry eyes/mouth, reflux, etc.)?
  • Smoking, occupational vibration, drugs (beta-blockers, ergot, etc.)?
  • Impact on daily function?

Examination

  • Digits: colour, temperature, critical ischaemia signs (persistent pallor, severe pain)
  • Digital pitting, ulceration, gangrene
  • Nailfold capillaroscopy (dermatoscope in primary care) – abnormal = early secondary marker
  • Sclerodactyly, puffy fingers, telangiectasia, calcinosis
  • Peripheral pulses (asymmetrical → consider large-vessel disease)

Investigations (primary care)

  • FBC, CRP/ESR, thyroid function
  • ANA (titre ≥1:160 or any positive centromere/ nucleolar pattern → high risk secondary)
  • If ANA positive or high suspicion → ENA ± specific autoantibodies
Diagnosis & Classification

Primary Raynaud’s (80–90%)

  • Onset <30 years, symmetrical, reversible episodes
  • No tissue injury, no underlying disease
  • Normal capillaroscopy, negative/low-titre ANA
  • Green – Manage in primary care

Secondary Raynaud’s – Red flags

  • Onset >30 years
  • Asymmetrical or very painful attacks
  • Persistent ischaemia, digital pitting/ulceration
  • Abnormal nailfold capillaries
  • Positive ANA (especially ≥1:160 or specific pattern)
  • Clinical features of CTD
  • Amber – Refer rheumatology (urgent if ulcers or abnormal capillaries)

Critical ischaemia

RED – Urgent hospital admission or same-day vascular/rheumatology review
Persistent pallor/cyanosis, severe pain, ulceration, necrosis, gangrene

Treatment & Management

Urgent/Emergency pathway

  • Critical ischaemia → Admit or urgent specialist review (same week)
  • Digital ulceration/gangrene/infection → Urgent rheumatology/vascular

Referral indications

  • Suspected secondary Raynaud’s (especially abnormal capillaries or positive ANA)
  • Frequent severe attacks despite maximum primary care treatment
  • Child ≤12 years with suspected Raynaud’s
  • Age 13–17 years requiring drug treatment → seek paediatric/paediatric rheumatology advice
  • Occupational vibration exposure → refer occupational medicine

Primary care management (primary Raynaud’s)

First-line: General measures (all patients)

  • Keep whole body & extremities warm (layered clothing, hats, gloves, heated gloves/feet warmers)
  • Avoid sudden cold exposure
  • Stop smoking, reduce caffeine
  • Stress management/relaxation techniques
  • Patient information: NHS Raynaud’s | SRUK charity

Drug prophylaxis (if symptoms persist & troublesome)

First-line: Nifedipine sustained-release (off-label but preferred)

  • Adults ≥18 years: 10 mg twice daily → titrate up to 40 mg twice daily
  • Immediate-release (licensed): 5 mg three times daily → up to 20 mg three times daily (more side-effects)
  • Intermittent use acceptable (cold weather only)
  • Caution: hypotension, headache, flushing, ankle swelling

Second-line (if nifedipine not tolerated): Amlodipine 5–10 mg once daily (off-label)

Follow-up & Monitoring
  • Review response to lifestyle measures and drug treatment (if prescribed)
  • Trial withdrawal of nifedipine annually or after warm season – many enter remission
  • Reassess promptly if:
    • New persistent ischaemia or ulceration
    • New features suggesting secondary cause
    • Poor control despite maximum primary care treatment
  • Safety-net: advise patient to seek urgent review if digital pain/ischaemia worsens
