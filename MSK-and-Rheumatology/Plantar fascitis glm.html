Plantar Fasciitis Quick Reference

Last updated: December 2024
REFER URGENTLY if: Severe symptoms, diagnostic uncertainty, or persistent symptoms for 3-6 months despite management

Red Flags

  • Sudden onset pain with swelling and inability to weight bear (possible calcaneal fracture)
  • History of systemic inflammatory conditions (e.g., spondyloarthritis)
  • Persistent symptoms for 3-6 months despite management
  • Severe symptoms impacting daily function
  • Atypical clinical features suggesting alternative diagnosis

Typical Presentation

  • Inferomedial heel pain, insidious onset
  • "First step pain" - intense pain in morning or after rest
  • Pain improves with activity but worsens with prolonged standing
  • Usually unilateral (but may be bilateral in 30%)
  • Point tenderness at medial calcaneal tuberosity

Assessment

History

  • Onset and timing
  • Activity level
  • Risk factors
  • Previous treatments

Examination

  • Gait assessment
  • Footwear evaluation
  • Palpation of heel
  • Windlass test
When to arrange imaging

Consider imaging if there are atypical clinical features and/or persistent symptoms following initial management:

  • Weight-bearing foot X-ray: assess foot alignment, exclude bony lesions
  • Foot ultrasound: may show plantar fascia thickening and rule out other pathology
  • MRI: may be arranged if considering surgical intervention

Management

  • Rest foot and avoid prolonged standing
  • Wear comfortable shoes with good arch support
  • Consider over-the-counter insoles and heel pads
  • Weight loss if overweight/obese
  • Simple analgesia (paracetamol/NSAIDs)
  • Icepack application (15-20 min every few hours)
  • Regular plantar fascia and gastrocnemius stretching
  • Podiatrist/orthotist for orthoses and/or night splints
  • Physiotherapist for supervised exercises
  • Consider corticosteroid injection (ultrasound-guided)
  • Orthopaedic surgeon if symptoms persist 3-6 months
  • Consider extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT)

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Achilles tendonosis Insertional tenderness at posterior calcaneus, pain radiating up calf
Calcaneal fracture Swelling, heat, inability to weight bear, pain on pressing calcaneum
Fat pad atrophy Centralized heel pain, flattened atrophied plantar surface
Sub-calcaneal bursitis Swelling and pain of retrocalcaneal region, not worsened by toe dorsiflexion
Plantar fascia tear/rupture Sudden-onset pain and bruising, often after a 'pop' sound
Tarsal tunnel syndrome Burning pain and paraesthesia along plantar aspect of foot
Spondyloarthropathy History of peripheral enthesitis, especially at multiple sites

Prognosis

  • Usually self-limiting
  • 80% improve within 12 months with non-surgical treatment
  • Mean duration of symptoms: 13.3-14.1 months
  • Risk of ongoing symptoms higher in women and bilateral cases

Epidemiology

  • Most common cause of plantar heel pain in adults
  • Lifetime incidence: ~10%
  • Affects 4-7% of general population
  • Most common in people aged 40-60 years
  • Slightly higher incidence in women
