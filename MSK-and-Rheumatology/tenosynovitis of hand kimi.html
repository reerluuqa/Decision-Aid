Assessment
üìù Key Diagnostic Factors
‚ñº

Core Clinical Features

  • Location: Pain/swelling over retinacular sheath (tendon tunnel)
  • Motion-related pain: Pain increases with active/passive movement
  • Response to injection: Dramatic improvement with lidocaine + corticosteroid confirms diagnosis
  • Symptom duration: Typically develops over weeks to months

Condition-Specific Findings

  • Trigger Finger: Painful popping with flexion/extension, palpable nodule at metacarpal head
  • de Quervain's: Radial wrist pain 1-2cm proximal to radial styloid, positive Finkelstein test
  • Intersection Syndrome: Pain/swelling 4cm proximal to wrist, crepitus with resisted wrist extension
  • EPL Tenosynovitis: Pain at Lister's tubercle with thumb IP joint motion
  • ECU Tenosynovitis: Ulnar-sided wrist pain, worse with extension/ulnar deviation
  • FCR Tenosynovitis: Pain at palmar wrist crease over scaphoid tubercle

‚ñº

Strong Risk Factors

  • Age 50-60 years: Peak incidence in sixth decade
  • Female sex: 5-6x more common in women
  • Diabetes mellitus: 5x higher risk (especially insulin-dependent)
  • Pregnancy & lactation: Strong risk for de Quervain's disease
  • Dominant hand involvement: Related to degenerative changes
  • Concomitant stenosing conditions: Similar pathology elsewhere

Weaker Associations

  • Degenerative joint disease or trauma (specific conditions)
  • Hyperlipidaemia (population-based association)

Not Associated

  • Repetitive keyboard use (no clear occupational link)

üëÜ Types of Tenosynovitis
‚ñº
Trigger Finger/Thumb Most Common

Digital flexor tendonitis at A1 pulley ‚Üí catching/locking. Grades: 0-4 (pre-trigger to fixed contracture)

de Quervain's Disease Common

APL & EPB tenosynovitis in first dorsal compartment. Radial styloid pain, positive Finkelstein test

Intersection Syndrome

Second dorsal compartment friction. Pain 4cm proximal to wrist with resisted extension

Extensor Pollicis Longus (EPL) Urgent

Rare but high risk of rupture. Pain at Lister's tubercle, requires early diagnosis

Extensor Carpi Ulnaris (ECU)

Ulnar-sided wrist pain, increased with all wrist motions

Flexor Carpi Radialis (FCR)

Pain at palmar wrist crease over scaphoid tubercle

üî¨ Investigations
‚ñº

Primary Investigation

  • High-resolution ultrasound: Most useful & accurate for all stenosing tendinopathies
  • Findings: Effusion, sheath thickening, hyperaemia

Consider if Suspected

  • Plain X-ray: Fractures, arthritis, calcific tendonitis
  • MRI: Occult masses, cysts, tendon rupture, TFCC tear
  • CT: Bony issues, loose bodies
  • Blood tests (FBC, ESR, CRP): Rule out infection/inflammatory arthritis
üîÑ Differential Diagnoses
‚ñº

Key Differentials to Exclude

Osteoarthritis (de Quervain's/FCR)

Tenderness over joint (not tendon), positive grind test, X-ray shows arthritic changes

Septic Tenosynovitis/Arthritis Emergency

Erythema, fusiform swelling, elevated WBC/ESR/CRP, positive cultures

Radial Sensory Neuritis (Wartenberg's)

Sensory changes, positive Tinel's sign over radial sensory nerve

TFCC Tear (ECU)

MRI/CT to differentiate from ECU tenosynovitis

Dupuytren's Contracture

Painless nodules, palpable cord in palm (vs. locked trigger digit)

Injection Technique Guidelines

  • Volume: 1-3 mL total (corticosteroid + 1% lidocaine)
  • Needle: 25-27 gauge
  • Corticosteroids: Methylprednisolone 20mg, Triamcinolone 5mg, or Betamethasone 6mg
  • Repetition: May repeat, but caution after multiple injections (skin thinning, fat atrophy, tendon rupture)

Diclofenac potassium 50 mg TID
Ibuprofen 300-400 mg Q6-8H (max 2400 mg/day)

Pregnancy: NSAIDs contraindicated, splinting usually sufficient

2nd Line: First Dorsal Compartment Injection Next Step

Success rate: 50-80% with one or two injections. Must identify separate EPB subsheath.

3rd Line: Surgery Failed Conservative

Longitudinal incision of first dorsal compartment. Identify and release EPB subcompartment to prevent recurrence.

Follow-up & Monitoring
üìÖ Monitoring Schedule
‚ñº

Post-Injection Follow-up

  • 1 month: Repeat examination after trial injection
  • If failed: Consider second injection or surgical referral
  • If successful but recurrent: Re-evaluate and consider surgery

Surgical Follow-up

  • Post-op: Wound check at 10-14 days
  • Rehabilitation: Gradual return to activity at 4-6 weeks
  • Final assessment: 3 months post-op
‚ö†Ô∏è Potential Complications Monitor For
‚ñº
Contracture (Trigger Finger) High Risk

Prolonged neglect of locked digit ‚Üí flexion contracture of PIP joint

Tendon Rupture High Risk

EPL and FCR at high risk. Attritional rupture from prolonged neglect.

Injection-Related Skin Changes Low Risk

Superficial injection may cause: skin thinning, fat atrophy, depigmentation, necrosis

Surgical Nerve Injury Low Risk

Digital nerves at risk during trigger finger release; radial sensory nerve in de Quervain's

Tendon Bow-stringing/Subluxation Low Risk

Release of A2 pulley increases bow-stringing risk; ECU instability if retinaculum over-released

Neuritis Low Risk

Prolonged inflammation may involve nearby sensory nerves

üìà Prognosis & Outcomes
‚ñº

Trigger Finger

  • Splinting: 55-66% success rate
  • Corticosteroid injection: 48-93% success
  • Diabetes: Higher recurrence rate, immediate surgery may be cost-effective
  • Open release: Definitive treatment, high success rate

de Quervain's Disease

  • Splinting alone: Not highly effective
  • Injection: 50-80% success with 1-2 injections
  • Injection + splinting: More effective than injection alone
  • Pregnancy: Resolves after lactation cessation

Other Tenosynovitides

  • Most respond to conservative measures
  • Surgical treatment highly effective for resistant cases
üí¨ Patient Counseling Points
‚ñº

Activity Modification

  • Avoid positions/functions that exacerbate symptoms
  • Continue assisted motion with opposite hand to prevent stiffness

Recurrence Management

  • Recurrence after successful injection ‚Üí re-evaluate for surgery
  • Multiple recurrences may indicate need for earlier surgical intervention

Expectations

  • Conservative treatment: 4-6 weeks trial period
  • Injection: May take 1-2 weeks for full effect
  • Surgery: High success rate, low complication risk
