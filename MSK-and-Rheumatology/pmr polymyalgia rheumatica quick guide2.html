Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Fast Decision Aid for Primary Care | EULAR 2015 / BSR Guidance
Lifetime risk: 2.4% (♀) 1.7% (♂) | Peak age 70-80y | No diagnostic test
✓ Diagnostic Criteria (Clinical)
  • Age >50 years (if <60, refer)
  • Bilateral shoulder and/or pelvic girdle pain >2 weeks
  • Morning stiffness >45 minutes (or all-day stiffness)
  • Functional impact: difficulty rising from chair/turning in bed
  • Systemic symptoms: fatigue, weight loss, feeling unwell
  • Elevation of inflammatory markers common but NOT universal
25-50% of PMR diagnoses are incorrect. Most commonly late-onset RA or GCA.
⚠️ Key Red Flags / Differentials

Rule out before confirming PMR:

  • Active malignancy (weight loss, night pain)
  • Infection (fever, localizing symptoms)
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (see GCA tab)
  • Late-onset Rheumatoid Arthritis (consider anti-CCP)
  • Statin-induced myopathy (check CK)
  • Hypovitaminosis D (isolated knee/thigh pain)
  • Local shoulder/hip pathology
  • Fibromyalgia (normal inflammatory markers)
Physical Exam: Exclude infection/malignancy. May see morning stiffness on mobilizing. No single test confirms PMR.
🎯 When to Refer (Rheumatology)
  • Age <60 years
  • Chronic or atypical onset
  • Absence of typical proximal symptoms
  • Normal or very high ESR/CRP (>100)
  • Incomplete response to steroids
  • Difficulty withdrawing steroids
  • Overlapping GCA symptoms
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
If diagnosis unclear → Refer to rheumatology
🧪 Baseline Investigations (EULAR 2015)

Use clinical judgment—screen for alternative diagnoses and steroid comorbidities:

Test Rationale / Notes
FBC Anaemia of chronic disease? WCC abnormal?
ESR & CRP Elevated in most, but not universal. Normal ESR/CRP → reconsider diagnosis
U&E, LFT, Calcium Baseline renal/hepatic function; bone metabolism
Glucose / HbA1c Baseline glycemic status (steroids will worsen). Note: Random glucose often better than fasting on steroids
RhF, anti-CCP Exclude late-onset RA
Creatine Kinase (CK) Exclude myopathy (especially if on statins)
Urinalysis Protein/haematuria → alternative pathology
CXR If systemic symptoms, smoker, or suspicion of malignancy
Consider: TFT, Vit D, Myeloma screen If clinically indicated
Documentation: EULAR suggests documenting as "minimum dataset" but this is for research. Use clinical judgment for the individual patient.
💉 Starting Steroids
Dose: Prednisolone 12.5–25mg daily
Use minimum effective dose (often 15mg)

Tapering Regimen:

  1. Maintain initial dose for 2–4 weeks
  2. Reduce by 2.5mg every 4 weeks until 10mg
  3. After 1 month at 10mg, reduce by 1mg monthly
Expected response within 24–72 hours.
If no response → reconsider diagnosis.
If need >25mg to control symptoms → suspect GCA, not PMR.
🛡️ Protection & Safety
Protection Details
Gastroprotection PPI (e.g., omeprazole) for duration of steroids
Bone Protection Assess FRAX/QFracture. Usually bisphosphonate + Ca/Vit D
Steroid Card Record of tapering regimen
Emergency Card Required for all taking ≥5mg prednisolone (adrenal insufficiency risk)
Comorbidity Check: Review hypertension, diabetes, osteoporosis, obesity, cataracts/glaucoma before starting. Consider lower starting dose if multiple risks.
📉 Relapse Management

Relapse rate: ~50% during treatment

Median duration: 1–2 years

Prolonged need: 40% need >2 years, 20-33% need >5 years

If relapse:

  • Increase to previous effective dose
  • Taper more slowly
  • Review diagnosis if multiple relapses
  • Consider DMARD (methotrexate) or IL-6 inhibitors (tocilizumab/sarilumab) via secondary care
Good news: Fracture risk returns to baseline ~12 months after stopping treatment.
📅 Follow-up Schedule
  • First year: Every 4–8 weeks
  • Second year: Every 8–12 weeks
  • 3-monthly Bloods: FBC, ESR/CRP, U&E, Glucose/HbA1c

At Each Visit:

  • Assess clinical remission (symptoms + function)
  • Check BP and weight (steroid side effects)
  • Screen for GCA symptoms (headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms)
  • Review bone protection adherence
⚠️ Safety Monitoring

Watch for steroid side effects:

  • Hypertension
  • Hyperglycemia/diabetes
  • Weight gain
  • Osteoporosis
  • Cataracts
  • Adrenal suppression
Patient Education: Ensure they know GCA warning symptoms requiring same-day review (see GCA tab).

CRITICAL SAFETY ALERT
20% of PMR patients have GCA. 50% of GCA patients have PMR.
Each time you see a PMR patient, ask: "Could this be GCA?"
🚨 GCA Symptoms to Check
  • New-onset headache (often temporal)
  • Scalp tenderness (especially when combing hair)
  • Jaw/tongue claudication (pain when chewing)
  • Visual disturbance (diplopia, amaurosis, field defects)
  • Limb claudication
  • Fever, weight loss, fatigue
👁️ Examination Findings
  • Tender or thickened temporal artery
  • Scalp tenderness on palpation
  • Visual field defects
  • Asymmetry of pulses
  • Optic disc pallor (late sign)
No single sign/symptom rules GCA in or out.
⚡ Immediate Action Required

Visual Disturbance Present

IMMEDIATE ADMISSION to ophthalmology for IV steroids.

Risk of irreversible blindness. Do not wait for blood tests.

No Visual Disturbance

SAME DAY DISCUSSION with rheumatology/ophthalmology.

Start high-dose steroids (40-60mg prednisolone) immediately if GCA suspected while awaiting assessment.

💡 Key Clinical Pearls
  • Imaging studies in "relapsing PMR" often reveal subclinical GCA masked by current steroids
  • GCA can appear weeks, months, or years after PMR diagnosis
  • ESR/CRP may be normal in 10-20% of GCA cases (especially if already on steroids for PMR)
  • Temporal artery biopsy remains gold standard but treat immediately if clinical suspicion high
