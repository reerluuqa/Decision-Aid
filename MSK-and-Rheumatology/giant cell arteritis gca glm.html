Giant Cell Arteritis

Quick Reference for Clinicians
Medical Emergency Last Updated: Sept 2025
IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED for any patient with visual symptoms - vision loss is irreversible and may affect the second eye within days.

Red Flags

  • New visual loss (partial or complete)
  • Diplopia or visual field defects
  • Jaw claudication (pain with chewing)
  • New onset headache (usually temporal)
  • Temporal artery tenderness/thickening
  • Scalp tenderness (especially when brushing hair)

Emergency Actions

Visual symptoms? Arrange urgent same-day ophthalmology assessment
Start treatment immediately if GCA strongly suspected - do not delay for tests
Standard dose: 40-60mg oral prednisolone daily
With visual loss: IV methylprednisolone or 60-100mg oral prednisolone for 3 days
Refer urgently via fast-track pathway (within 3 working days)

Key Investigations

Primary Care

  • CRP (usually elevated)
  • ESR (often >50mm/hr)
  • FBC (anemia, thrombocytosis)

Secondary Care

  • Temporal artery ultrasound
  • Temporal artery biopsy
  • MRI/CT if needed
Do not delay treatment while waiting for investigations

Management Overview

Phase Treatment Duration
Initial Prednisolone 40-60mg daily Until symptoms resolve
Tapering Gradual reduction Over 12-18 months
Adjunct Methotrexate or Tocilizumab If high steroid toxicity risk

Follow-up Schedule

  • Every 2-8 weeks for first 6 months
  • Every 12 weeks for second 6 months
  • Every 12-24 weeks during second year
Monitor for relapse, steroid side effects, and complications

Complications

Immediate

  • Permanent vision loss
  • Second eye involvement

Long-term

  • Aortic aneurysm
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Steroid-induced osteoporosis
Patient Advice: Provide blue steroid card, educate about symptoms of relapse, and advise to seek immediate medical attention for visual symptoms.
