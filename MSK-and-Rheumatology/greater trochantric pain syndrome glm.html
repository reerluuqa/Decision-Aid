Diagnosis

  • Chronic lateral hip/thigh/buttock pain (intermittent or persistent)
  • Usually gradual onset, may progressively worsen over time
  • Pain may radiate down the lateral aspect of the thigh, but rarely below the knee
  • Pain aggravated by physical activity (walking) and pressure on affected side
  • Tenderness on palpation of the greater trochanter

Physical Examination Tests

Trendelenburg's Test

Observe from behind while lifting each foot off the ground. Positive if pelvis dips on lifting the unaffected leg.

Single Leg Stance

Stand on affected leg with contralateral knee flexed to 90° for 30 seconds. Positive if lateral hip pain within 30 seconds.

FABER Test (Flexion, Abduction, External Rotation)

Place lateral malleolus of test leg above patella of contralateral leg, lower knee passively. Positive if lateral hip pain.

Resisted Active Abduction

With person supine, abduct affected hip by 45° while person resists. Positive if lateral hip pain.

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Iliotibial band syndrome Lateral hip pain on walking/running, symptoms reproduced by Ober's test
Iliopsoas bursitis Painful snapping sensations in anterior hip, tenderness over femoral triangle
Hip osteoarthritis Limited range of motion, especially internal rotation with hip and knee flexed
Avascular necrosis History of corticosteroid use or alcohol misuse, confirmed by MRI
Meralgia paraesthetica Numbness, tingling in anterolateral hip and thigh, aggravated by hip extension

Management

1

Reassurance & Education

Explain that greater trochanteric pain syndrome is usually self-limiting and resolves in over 90% of people with conservative treatment. Provide verbal and written information.

2

Conservative Treatment

  • Rest the affected hip by avoiding aggravating activities
  • Apply ice pack for 10 minutes at a time every few hours
  • Offer analgesia (paracetamol or NSAID such as ibuprofen)
  • Weight loss advice if overweight or obese
  • Smoking cessation advice if appropriate
  • Assess need for aids and devices (walking aid)
3

Secondary Treatment Options

If conservative measures fail:

  • Peri-trochanteric corticosteroid injection
  • Referral to physiotherapy for quadriceps strengthening, iliotibial band stretching, or hip abduction exercises

Prognosis

Self-limiting:
90%+

Most cases resolve with conservative treatment. A few cases persist despite treatment and may require surgical intervention.

Poorer Prognosis Factors:

  • High pain intensity
  • Long pain duration
  • Widespread pain
  • High functional disability
  • Previous pain episodes
  • Depression/anxiety

Key Points

Definition

Greater trochanteric pain syndrome is a regional pain syndrome with chronic intermittent pain around the greater trochanter. The term 'trochanteric bursitis' was previously used, but 'greater trochanteric pain syndrome' is now preferred as inflammation is not always present.

Common Causes

  • Tendinopathy/muscular tear of gluteus medius
  • Tendinopathy/muscular tear of gluteal minimus
  • Trochanteric bursitis
  • Iliotibial band thickening
