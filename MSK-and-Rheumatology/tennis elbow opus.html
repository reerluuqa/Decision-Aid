1. Assessment
History Taking & Red Flag Identification
‚ñº
üö®
Red Flags - Exclude Alternative Diagnoses
  • History of trauma with significant swelling or deformity
  • Hot, tender, swollen joint (consider septic/inflammatory arthritis)
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats)
  • Progressive bone pain or unexplained mass

üìù History Checklist

  • Pain characteristics Severity, onset, duration, and exacerbating features
  • Location & radiation Lateral epicondyle with radiation down extensor aspect of forearm
  • Occupational/recreational activities Construction, assembly line, typing, racquet sports, kayaking
  • Grip weakness Difficulty with daily tasks requiring grip strength
  • Functional impact Cup raising, shaking hands, shaving, lifting bags
  • Associated symptoms Sleep disturbance, anxiety, depression
  • Previous history Prior episodes, treatments tried
üí° Typical Presentation

Insidious onset pain (or burning sensation) in dominant arm at lateral epicondyle. Symptoms exacerbated by repetitive wrist movements, especially wrist extension and power gripping palm-down.

üìä Key Epidemiology

  • Prevalence: 1-3% of population
  • Peak age: 35-54 years
  • Affects men and women equally
  • Dominant arm involved in 75% of cases
  • Racquet sports cause <10% of cases

2. Diagnosis
Clinical Examination & Differential Diagnosis
‚ñº

ü©∫ Clinical Examination

  • Point tenderness Over and/or distal to lateral epicondyle, along common extensor tendon
  • Maudsley's test Resisted middle finger extension causes pain
  • Cozen's test Dorsiflex wrist against resistance (elbow at 90¬∞) - extending elbow increases pain
  • Range of motion Full active/passive ROM at elbow and wrist usually preserved
  • Tinel's sign Negative (tap medial elbow over ulnar nerve - positive if paraesthesia without pain)
  • Neck/shoulder assessment Exclude referred pain from cervical spine

Diagnosis is Clinical

Investigations are not usually needed in primary care. Diagnosis based on history and examination findings.

üî•
Fire Safety Warning - Topical Ibuprofen Gel

Advise patients to avoid smoking and naked flames. Fabrics in contact with gel burn more easily. Washing may reduce but not totally remove product buildup.

Second-Line (if symptoms persist)
Oral NSAIDs

Topical NSAIDs preferred over oral due to lower GI side effect risk

‚è±Ô∏è If No Response After 6 Weeks

  • Reassess and consider alternative diagnoses
  • Consider physiotherapy referral Strong evidence for short-term benefit. Includes stretching, strengthening, eccentric training, massage, ultrasound therapy.
‚õî
Do NOT Routinely Offer Corticosteroid Injection

May provide short-term relief but associated with worse outcomes at 6 months.

If offered, discuss possible adverse effects and long-term implications:

  • Tendon weakening or rupture
  • Post-injection pain flare
  • Skin changes (fat atrophy, hypopigmentation)
  • Up to 50% symptom recurrence

Prognosis

Self-limiting condition. 80-90% improve within 12-24 months. Pain relief typically occurs within 6-12 months with conservative treatment.

üìÜ Follow-up Timeline

6 Weeks

6-12 Months

12-24 Months

If refractory: consider orthopaedic referral

üè• Orthopaedic Referral Criteria

  • Diagnosis uncertain Consider imaging (USS or MRI) only if diagnostic uncertainty persists
  • Refractory pain or severe functional impairment
  • Symptoms persist despite 12-24 weeks of optimal management
‚ö†Ô∏è
Surgery - Manage Expectations

Current evidence does not support surgery as more effective than placebo. The only placebo-controlled trial showed no clear benefit. Surgery may be considered in selected cases after 3-6 months of failed non-surgical treatment, but expectations should be managed.

üìâ Factors Associated with Worse Outcome

  • High baseline pain intensity
  • Longer symptom duration before treatment
  • Coexisting neck or shoulder pain
  • Female gender / Older age
  • Recurrent episodes
  • Involvement of dominant arm
  • Manual work or repetitive strain occupations
  • Low social support
  • Smoking and heavy alcohol consumption
