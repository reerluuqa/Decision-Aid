Definition

OA is a clinical diagnosis of joint degeneration involving the entire joint structure:

  • Articular cartilage loss
  • Subchondral bone changes (sclerosis, cysts)
  • Osteophyte formation
  • Synovial inflammation
  • Periarticular muscle weakness

Classic Presentation

  • Pain: Activity-related, worse with weight-bearing
  • Stiffness: Morning stiffness ≤30 minutes
  • Age: Typically >45 years
  • Joints: Knee, hip, hand (PIP/DIP), spine
  • Signs: Bony enlargement, crepitus, limited ROM

Key Point

OA is essentially a clinical diagnosis. Imaging is NOT routinely required if presentation is typical. Order imaging only if diagnosis is unclear or atypical features present.

Kellgren-Lawrence Radiographic Classification

Grade Findings
0No radiographic OA
1Doubtful: possible osteophytic lipping
2Minimal: definite osteophytes, possible JSN
3Moderate: multiple osteophytes, definite JSN, sclerosis
4Severe: large osteophytes, marked JSN, severe sclerosis

Red Flag Features

ATYPICAL FEATURES → Consider Alternative Diagnosis

  • Prolonged morning stiffness (>30 minutes) → Consider inflammatory arthritis
  • Night pain (pain present at night) → Consider malignancy, infection, or advanced disease
  • Rapid worsening of symptoms → Consider infection, malignancy, or inflammatory flare
  • Recent trauma with acute onset → Consider fracture, internal derangement
  • Palpable warmth over joint → Consider septic arthritis, gout, inflammatory arthritis
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, malaise) → Consider infection, malignancy, inflammatory arthritis

Red Flag Joint Patterns

  • MCP joint involvement → Consider RA, CPPD
  • Symmetrical small joint polyarthritis → Consider RA
  • Ankle/wrist involvement (without trauma) → Consider secondary OA
  • Acute monoarthritis with erythema → Consider gout, septic arthritis

Laboratory Red Flags

  • Elevated CRP/ESR → Not typical for OA alone
  • Positive RF or anti-CCP → Suggests RA
  • Abnormal LFTs/Renal function → Affects medication choices

Diagnostic Approach

Clinical Diagnosis of OA

ALL 3 required: Activity-related joint pain + Morning stiffness ≤30 min + Age >45 years

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Activity-related joint pain
  • Morning stiffness ≤30 minutes
  • Age >45 years
  • Bony enlargement (Heberden/Bouchard nodes)
  • Crepitus on motion
  • Limited ROM with pain
  • Typical joint distribution (knee, hip, hand DIP/PIP)

Physical Exam Findings

  • Observation: Bony deformities, malalignment (varus/valgus), effusion
  • Palpation: Joint line tenderness, crepitus, bony enlargement
  • ROM: Limited active/passive range, pain with movement
  • Gait: Antalgic limp, slow steady gait
  • Special tests: McMurray's for meniscal involvement

When to Order Imaging

✓ ROUTINELY indicated:

  • Diagnosis unclear after clinical exam
  • Atypical features present
  • Considering alternative diagnoses
  • Planning surgical intervention

✗ NOT routinely indicated:

  • Typical OA presentation
  • Monitoring disease progression
  • Routine follow-up without change

Differential Diagnosis

Key Differentiating Features

Condition Key Differentiating Signs/Symptoms Diagnostic Test
Gout Acute onset (hours), erythematous hot joint, often 1st MTP Joint fluid: urate crystals
Pseudogout Acute onset, CPPD risk factors, often knee/wrist Joint fluid: CPPD crystals, X-ray chondrocalcinosis
Rheumatoid Arthritis Symmetrical MCP/PIP involvement, prolonged AM stiffness, systemic symptoms Elevated CRP/ESR, +RF, +anti-CCP
Psoriatic Arthritis Asymmetric DIP involvement, skin psoriasis, enthesitis X-ray erosions, MRI enthesitis
Bursitis Localized tenderness over bursa (trochanteric, pes anserine) Diagnostic injection
Meniscal Tear Acute locking, giving way, preceding trauma MRI
Avascular Necrosis Subacute onset, steroid use risk factor, hip/knee MRI (most sensitive)

Septic Arthritis

Emergency! Hot, swollen, painful joint with fever. Requires urgent arthrocentesis and antibiotics.

Lyme Disease

Consider in endemic areas. Monoarthritis, often knee. History of tick bite, erythema migrans.

Non-Pharmacological Management

First-Line Treatment

All patients should start with non-pharmacological approaches. These are foundational and should continue alongside pharmacological therapy.

Exercise & Physical Therapy

  • Quadriceps strengthening - Evidence-based for knee OA
  • Hip strengthening - Effective for knee OA
  • Low-impact aerobic - Swimming, cycling, walking
  • Balance exercises/Tai chi - For knee/hip OA
  • Yoga - Alternative for knee OA
  • Water-based exercise - Beneficial for all joints

Reassure: Exercise is NOT harmful to joints and is strongly recommended.

Weight Management & Self-Care

  • Weight loss - Strongly recommended if overweight (especially knee OA)
  • Diet + exercise - Best combined approach
  • Patient education - Self-management programs
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy - Adjunct to physiotherapy
  • Assistive devices - Walking stick for knee/hip OA

Supplements & Alternative Therapies

GLUCOSAMINE & CHONDROITIN

  • Not routinely recommended (ACR, NICE)
  • Modest pain reduction possible
  • Low risk, patient may request
  • Discuss with patient

OTHER

  • Acupuncture: ACR recommends, NICE does not
  • TENS: Not recommended (insufficient evidence)
  • Arnica gel: May be as effective as topical NSAID

Pharmacological Management

⚠️ OPIOID WARNING

  • Opioids provide minimal benefit for OA pain
  • Number needed to treat for benefit: 10 | NNH for harm: 5
  • Benefits do NOT outweigh harms regardless of preparation or dose
  • Use only when all other treatments ineffective/contraindicated
  • Tramadol: modest benefit, generally well tolerated

Treatment Hierarchy

1st LINE
  • TOPICAL NSAIDs - Hand/knee OA (ACR, NICE)
  • ORAL NSAIDs - Lowest effective dose, shortest duration
2nd LINE
  • Duloxetine - Antidepressant with analgesic properties
  • Intra-articular corticosteroids - For acute flares or NSAID contraindication
3rd LINE
  • Tramadol - Consider if other options failed
  • COX-2 inhibitors - For GI risk patients (with PPI)
4th LINE
  • Other opioids - Last resort, significant risks
  • Arthroscopic surgery - NOT effective for OA

Topical Therapies

  • Topical NSAIDs - First-line for hand/knee OA. Effective within 2 weeks.
  • Topical Capsaicin - ACR recommends for knee OA
  • Arnica gel - May be comparable to topical NSAIDs

Oral NSAID Considerations

  • GI risk: Add PPI for gastroprotection
  • CV risk: Avoid diclofenac, ibuprofen if CVD history
  • Renal risk: Avoid in CKD, elderly
  • Naproxen may have better CV safety profile

Paracetamol Role

Limited Efficacy

Paracetamol has small to modest benefit and is statistically inferior to all other drug categories for OA pain. May be used for rescue analgesia or if local therapies alone are insufficient.

Injections & Procedures

Intra-Articular Corticosteroids

ACR Recommended

For knee and hip OA. Consider for acute flares or when NSAIDs contraindicated.

  • Effective for pain reduction up to 6 weeks
  • Greater benefit in severe baseline pain
  • Unclear duration of benefit (1-12 weeks reported)
  • May increase risk of rapidly destructive hip disease
  • Avoid within 3 months of joint replacement surgery (infection risk)

Viscosupplementation (Hyaluronic Acid)

Not Recommended by Guidelines

ACR and NICE do not recommend. Commonly used despite recommendations.

  • Modest benefit reported in some studies
  • Effect size varies by product
  • May increase risk of serious adverse effects
  • Consider patient phenotype and preferences

ARTHROSCOPIC SURGERY - NOT EFFECTIVE

Clinical guidelines (ACR, NICE) DO NOT recommend arthroscopic surgery for knee OA. Multiple RCTs show no benefit over placebo/sham surgery.

Surgical Options

Referral Criteria

Consider surgery for patients with persistent pain despite multiple treatment modalities, significantly impacting quality of life, who are surgical candidates.

Total Knee Replacement

  • Significant pain relief and functional improvement
  • KOOS4 score: 32.5 vs 16.0 (non-surgical)
  • Consider for moderate-to-severe knee OA

Partial Knee Arthroplasty

  • For younger patients (≤60 years)
  • 96.5% implant survival at 10 years
  • Pain relief and satisfactory activity level

Total Hip Replacement

  • Effective for end-stage hip OA
  • Significant pain relief and functional improvement
  • High patient satisfaction

Thumb OA Surgery

  • Arthroplasty, trapeziectomy, arthrodesis options
  • Consider patient needs and preferences
  • Uncertainty about best procedure

Pre-Surgical Optimization

Intervention Benefit
Weight loss Better post-surgical outcomes, reduced harm risk
Pre-op exercise Reduces pain, may improve activity after hip replacement
Avoid Delay surgery ≥3 months after intra-articular steroid (infection risk)

Procedures NOT Recommended

Partial meniscectomy - No role for meniscal tear in knee OA (RCT evidence)
Arthroscopic surgery - Not effective for knee OA

ACR Diagnostic Criteria

Knee OA Criteria

Clinical + Laboratory (5 of 9):

  • Age >50 years
  • Stiffness <30 minutes
  • Crepitus
  • Bony tenderness
  • Bony enlargement
  • No palpable warmth
  • ESR <40 mm/hour
  • RF <1:40
  • Synovial fluid OA signs

Clinical + Radiographic:

  • Knee pain PLUS
  • Osteophytes PLUS (1 of 3):
    • Age >50 years
    • Stiffness <30 minutes
    • Crepitus

Hand OA Criteria

Clinical criteria: Hard tissue enlargement of ≥2 of 10 selected joints (DIP, PIP, 1st CMC) PLUS:

  • Swelling in <3 MCP joints, OR
  • Hard tissue enlargement of ≥2 DIP joints

If <2 enlarged DIP joints → require deformity of ≥1 of 10 selected joints.

Hip OA Criteria

Clinical + Laboratory (1 of 2):

  • Hip internal rotation ≥15°
  • Pain on internal rotation
  • Morning stiffness ≤60 minutes
  • Age >50 years

OR

  • Hip internal rotation <15°
  • ESR ≤45 mm/hour

Clinical + Radiographic:

  • Hip pain PLUS 2 of 3:
    • Osteophytes (femoral/acetabular)
    • JSN (superior/axial/medial)
    • ESR <20 mm/hour

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

Factor Impact
Age >50 Strongest risk factor; incidence peaks at 50+
Female sex 2:1 female:male ratio; sharp increase after menopause
Obesity Strongest association with knee OA (RR 2.07 for women)
Genetics 40-50% heritability; family history significant
Joint malalignment Increases risk and disease progression
Physical labor/sport Miners (knee), agricultural workers (hip), athletes
Joint trauma Post-traumatic OA common (e.g., post-ACL reconstruction)

Weak/Other Risk Factors

Factor Notes
High BMD Higher bone density associated with OA
Low estrogen Post-menopausal status increases risk
Low income Higher prevalence in low/middle-income countries
Joint anatomy Congenital deformities, dysplasia
Muscle weakness Periarticular muscle weakness facilitates progression
