Axial Spondyloarthritis

Interactive Clinical Algorithm for Primary Care | Based on NICE CKS (April 2024)

Assessment

When to Suspect

Suspect axial spondyloarthritis if low back pain + spinal stiffness starting before age 45 and lasting >3 months, PLUS ≥4 of the following:

  • Onset before age 35 years
  • Symptoms waking patient during second half of night
  • Buttock pain (variable location, may alternate with hip pain)
  • Improvement with movement/exercise, NOT relieved by rest
  • Improvement within 48 hours of NSAID use
  • Family history of spondyloarthritis in first-degree relative
  • Current/past history of peripheral arthritis
  • Current/past history of enthesitis
  • Current/past history of psoriasis (including nail changes)

Additional Features to Consider

Acute anterior uveitis
Urgent referral
Inflammatory bowel disease
Consider referral
Dactylitis
Specialist referral
Recent GI/GU infection
Consider reactive arthritis

Initial Assessment

Ask about:

  • Axial, peripheral, and extra-musculoskeletal symptoms
  • Back/neck pain characteristics (onset, duration, pattern)
  • Response to NSAIDs
  • Family history and risk factors (HLA-B27, smoking)
  • Impact on mobility and daily functioning

Examine for:

  • Inflammatory arthritis, enthesitis, dactylitis
  • Psoriasis skin/nail lesions
  • Spinal movement limitation (later stage)
Diagnosis & Investigation

Primary Care Investigations

Blood tests: FBC, CRP, ESR, HLA-B27
Do NOT rule out if CRP/ESR normal
HLA-B27: Arrange if ≥3 additional criteria present
Do NOT rule out if negative
Imaging: X-ray sacroiliac joints
Normal X-ray does NOT exclude non-radiographic disease

Differential Diagnosis

  • Mechanical back pain
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Spinal fracture
  • Infectious sacroiliitis
  • Bone metastasis
  • Primary bone tumors
  • Hypermobility/fibromyalgia

Rheumatology Referral Criteria

Refer if: Low back pain starting before age 45 lasting >3 months AND:

  • ≥4 additional diagnostic criteria, OR
  • Exactly 3 additional criteria + positive HLA-B27

Do not rule out diagnosis based on individual symptom/sign/test

Management & Treatment

Scenario: Suspected Disease

Analgesia while awaiting diagnosis:

  • NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) at lowest effective dose
  • If max tolerated dose for 2-4 weeks ineffective → switch NSAID
  • If NSAIDs contraindicated: short-term paracetamol ± codeine

Referral Pathways

Rheumatology: If referral criteria met Urgent if dactylitis

Ophthalmology: Suspected acute anterior uveitis Same-day urgent

Gastroenterology: Suspected IBD Consider referral

Dermatology: Suspected psoriasis with significant skin involvement Consider referral

Scenario: Confirmed Disease

Patient Education & Support:

NASS: www.nass.co.uk
Exercise, flare management, lifestyle advice

Analgesia:

Same principles as suspected disease. Individualize based on comorbidities.

Follow-up & Monitoring

Primary Care Review

Symptom control: Pain, stiffness, fatigue, flare episodes
Quality of life: Psychosocial impact, mental health
Complications: Extra-musculoskeletal manifestations
Medication: Adverse effects, adherence

Risk Assessment

Cardiovascular Risk:

Assess and manage appropriately (CVD risk, hypertension, diabetes, lipids, smoking cessation)

Osteoporosis Risk:

Screen every 2 years. Be aware: spinal DEXA may be falsely elevated due to syndesmophytes. Hip measurements more reliable.

When to Escalate

Uncontrolled Symptoms Action required

  • Ensure physiotherapy referral for structured exercise program
  • Check NSAID compliance → switch if ineffective
  • Consider additional analgesia (paracetamol/codeine)
  • Seek specialist rheumatology advice
  • Consider pain management service referral

Urgent Referral Indications

Acute anterior uveitis Same-day ophthalmology

Painful red eye, photophobia, blurred vision

Cauda equina syndrome Emergency spinal surgery
Suspected spinal fracture Urgent specialist imaging

Minimal trauma + increased pain

Refractory hip pain Consider arthroplasty

Structural damage on X-ray

Severe spinal deformity Spinal surgery assessment

Progressive, affecting QoL despite optimal management

Multidisciplinary Team

  • Rheumatologist
  • Specialist physiotherapist
  • Specialist rheumatology nurse
  • Occupational therapy
  • Orthotics/podiatry
  • Pain management service

Key Messages
