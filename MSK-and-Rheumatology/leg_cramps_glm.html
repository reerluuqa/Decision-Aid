Leg Cramps - Quick Reference

NICE CKS Last revised: Dec 2022
Definition

Sudden, involuntary, painful muscle contraction, usually in the posterior calf, but sometimes involving the foot or thigh.

  • Usually lasts seconds to several minutes
  • Most common at rest during the night
  • Pain and tenderness may persist for hours
Prevalence
  • Common, especially in older people
  • ~33% of people over 60 experience rest cramps
  • 40% report 3+ attacks per week
  • ~31% report being woken by cramps
Common Causes
Medical Conditions
  • Peripheral arterial disease
  • Venous insufficiency
  • Neurological disease
  • Metabolic/endocrine disease
  • Electrolyte imbalance
  • Vitamin B or D deficiency
Other Factors
  • Pregnancy
  • Strenuous exercise
  • Medications:
    • Diuretics
    • Statins
    • Beta-2 agonists
    • Imatinib
Complications
  • Sleep disturbance and daytime somnolence
  • Distress from pain
  • Rarely: blood vessel avulsion with severe, intense cramps
Emergency Admission Required: Severe cramps with altered consciousness, anuria, or hyper/hypopyrexia
Typical Features of Idiopathic Leg Cramps
  • Sudden intense pain in calf/foot (rarely thigh)
  • Most often at night or when resting
  • Duration: seconds to <10 minutes
  • Single muscle involvement
  • Unilateral symptoms
  • No abnormal findings on physical exam
  • Good response to stretching
Red Flag Features
  • Duration >10 minutes
  • Widespread muscle cramps
  • Calf pain with exercise
  • Urge to move legs
  • Leg numbness or weakness
  • Muscle wasting/fasciculations
  • Altered reflexes
  • Calf swelling, varicose veins
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Key Distinguishing Features
Restless Legs Syndrome Urge to move legs, no pain or muscle tightening
Claudication Pain with exercise, relieved by rest
Peripheral Neuropathy Numbness, tingling, neuropathic pain
Deep Vein Thrombosis Pain, swelling, redness, warmth
Myalgia/Myositis Deep aching pain without contraction
Self-Care Advice
During an attack: Stretch and massage affected muscle. For calf cramps, straighten leg and dorsiflex ankle.
Prevention: Stretch exercises of affected muscles 3 times daily. Combined calf and hamstring stretching may be more effective.
Sleep position: Prevent toes from pointing downwards. Prop feet up with pillow or hang feet over bed end. Keep blankets loose.
Analgesia: Consider for muscle tenderness after cramp.
When to Refer
Admit to hospital: Severe cramps with altered consciousness, anuria, or hyper/hypopyrexia
Refer to specialist: When underlying cause suspected that cannot be managed in primary care
Consider referral: Diagnosis in doubt or primary care treatment fails with quality of life impact
Quinine Use
Generally NOT recommended due to poor benefit-to-risk ratio
Consider only if:
  • Treatable causes excluded
  • Cramps are very painful and frequent
  • Quality of life is affected
  • Self-care measures have failed
  • No medical conditions or drug interactions increasing risk
Prescribing approach:
  • 200-300 mg at bedtime for 4 weeks
  • Monitor benefit with sleep/cramp diary
  • Stop if no benefit after 4 weeks
  • If beneficial, continue for 3 months then reassess
  • Review every 3 months if continued treatment needed
WARNING: Quinine is extremely toxic in overdose and can cause irreversible vision loss and life-threatening effects
Contraindications
  • Previous adverse reaction to quinine
  • Haemolysis or haemoglobinuria
  • Optic neuritis
  • Tinnitus
  • Myasthenia gravis
  • Pregnancy
Use with Caution
  • Older people
  • Cardiac disease
  • Risk factors for QT prolongation
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Conduction defects
  • Electrolyte disturbance
  • G6PD deficiency
  • Breastfeeding (only if benefits outweigh risks)
Adverse Effects
Seek medical advice if signs of thrombocytopenia occur: unexplained petechiae, bruising, or bleeding
Common Adverse Effects
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Headache
  • Tinnitus
  • Visual disturbance
  • Abdominal pain
  • Dizziness
Serious Adverse Effects
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Intravascular coagulation
  • Pancytopenia
  • Arrhythmias
  • Convulsions
  • Respiratory depression
  • Renal failure
  • Irreversible vision loss
Drug Interactions
Increased Quinine Toxicity
  • Azole antifungals
  • HIV protease inhibitors
Reduced Quinine Levels
  • Rifampicin
  • Carbamazepine
  • Phenytoin
Quinine Increases Plasma Concentration Of:
  • Flecainide
  • Digoxin and cardiac glycosides
  • Mefloquine
  • Amantadine
Dangerous Combinations:
  • Drugs that increase QT interval (amiodarone, clarithromycin, etc.)
  • Anticoagulants
  • Hypoglycaemics
