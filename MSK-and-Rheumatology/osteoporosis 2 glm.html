Definition

Osteoporosis is a complex skeletal disease characterised by low bone density and micro-architectural defects in bone tissue, resulting in increased bone fragility and susceptibility to fracture.

Key Points

  • Asymptomatic until fracture occurs
  • Diagnosis based on prior fragility fracture or BMD T-score ≤-2.5
  • Women have increased risk (23.1% general population, 35.3% in older women)
  • Prevalence increases with age
  • Vetebral and hip fractures comprise 24% of all fractures in women aged 60-69, but 67% for women aged 80+

Emergency Situations

  • Acute vertebral fracture: Severe back pain, may require hospitalization
  • Hip fracture: Surgical emergency, high mortality risk
  • Multiple fractures: May indicate underlying malignancy (myeloma)
Refer to Emergency Department

Prevention Basics

  • Adequate calcium intake (1000-1200 mg/day)
  • Vitamin D supplementation (800-1000 IU/day)
  • Weight-bearing exercise
  • Fall prevention strategies
  • Smoking cessation
  • Limit alcohol intake

Diagnostic Criteria

Category T-score Clinical Interpretation
Normal ≥-1.0 Within normal range
Osteopenia -1.0 to -2.5 Low bone mass
Osteoporosis ≤-2.5 High fracture risk
Severe/Etab. ≤-2.5 + fracture Very high fracture risk

Imaging

DXA (Dual-energy X-ray Absorptiometry)

Gold standard for measuring bone mineral density (BMD)

  • Measures BMD at hip and lumbar spine
  • T-scores determined by measuring bone density at the hip
  • Accuracy at hip >90%
  • Spine DXA may be misleading in older patients due to artifacts

Vertebral Fracture Assessment (VFA)

  • Can be performed during same session as DXA
  • Moderate sensitivity and high specificity for detecting vertebral fractures
  • Reduced radiation exposure compared to spinal radiography

Trabecular Bone Score (TBS)

  • Measure of bone microarchitecture derived from lumbar spine DXA images
  • Independent predictor of incident fracture
  • Useful for patients with BMD T-score or FRAX score close to intervention threshold

Laboratory Evaluation

Initial Tests for All Patients

  • Serum calcium
  • Serum albumin
  • Serum phosphate
  • Serum creatinine
  • 25-hydroxy vitamin D
  • Parathyroid hormone

Additional Tests Based on Clinical Suspicion

  • Testosterone (men)
  • Thyroid function tests
  • Urinary free cortisol
  • Serum/urine protein electrophoresis

Biochemical Markers

  • Serum procollagen type I N propeptide (PINP) - bone formation
  • Serum C-terminal telopeptide of type I collagen (CTX) - bone turnover

Red Flag Findings

  • Vitamin D deficiency: <20 nanograms/mL
  • Testosterone deficiency: in men
  • Abnormal thyroid hormone levels: could indicate hyperthyroidism
  • High parathyroid hormone: could indicate hyperparathyroidism
  • High urinary free cortisol: could indicate Cushing's syndrome
  • Abnormal serum/urine protein electrophoresis: could indicate myeloma

FRAX Assessment Tool

The FRAX tool integrates clinical risk factors for fracture, with or without BMD scores at the femoral neck, to calculate a 10-year fracture probability for men and women.

  • Can help assess need for BMD testing or pharmacological treatment
  • Limitations: doesn't incorporate recent falls history or spine BMD
  • FRAXplus calculator addresses some limitations with additional variables

Treatment Goals

  • Prevention of fractures (particularly hip, vertebral, and radial)
  • Preservation of bone mass
  • Reduction of pain
  • Maintenance of mobility and independence

Lifestyle Interventions

Calcium and Vitamin D

  • Recommended daily calcium intake: 1000-1200 mg
  • Recommended daily vitamin D intake: 800-1000 IU
  • Higher doses may be needed in deficient patients

Exercise

  • Weight-bearing exercises (walking, jogging, dancing)
  • Resistance training
  • Balance exercises to reduce fall risk

Fall Prevention

  • Home safety assessment
  • Balance training
  • Review of medications that increase fall risk
  • Vision correction

Pharmacological Treatment

First-line Therapy

Bisphosphonates are first-line pharmacological therapy for most postmenopausal women and men:

  • Alendronate
  • Risedronate
  • Ibandronate
  • Zoledronic acid (IV)

Second-line/Alternative Therapy

  • Denosumab (RANKL inhibitor)
  • SERMs (Raloxifene)
  • Hormone Replacement Therapy (considered only for high-risk patients for whom non-estrogen drugs are inappropriate)

Anabolic Therapy

For severe osteoporosis or those who fail other treatments:

  • Teriparatide (PTH analog)
  • Abaloparatide (PTHrP analog)
  • Romosozumab (sclerostin inhibitor)

Important: All patients treated with teriparatide, abaloparatide, denosumab, or romosozumab should receive sequential therapy with antiresorptive osteoporosis treatment to prevent rapid bone loss.

Screening Recommendations

ACOG (American College of Obstetrics and Gynecology)

  • Screen all postmenopausal patients aged ≥65 years with DXA
  • Screen patients <65 years with increased risk factors
  • Repeat BMD testing no sooner than 2 years after initial screening for patients near treatment thresholds

USPSTF (US Preventive Services Task Force)

  • Screen women aged 65 years and older
  • Screen postmenopausal women under 65 at increased risk
  • No upper age limit at which screening should not be performed

American College of Preventive Medicine

  • Evaluate all adult patients (50+) for osteoporosis risk factors
  • BMD testing in women 65+ and men 70+
  • BMD testing in younger postmenopausal women or men 50-69 with at least 1 major or 2 minor risk factors

Screening Tools

Dual-energy X-ray Absorptiometry (DXA)

Gold standard for measuring bone mineral density (BMD)

  • Measures BMD at hip and lumbar spine
  • T-scores determine osteoporosis status
  • Recommended for high-risk patients

FRAX Assessment Tool

Calculates 10-year fracture probability based on clinical risk factors

  • Can be used with or without BMD values
  • Helps determine need for treatment
  • Available online

Quantitative Ultrasound (QUS)

Alternative if DXA unavailable

  • Can be used for heel measurements
  • Cannot use same diagnostic criteria as DXA
  • May predict population-based fracture risk

Emergency Actions

Acute Fracture Management

Hip Fracture

  • Immediate surgical consultation
  • Pain management
  • Preoperative assessment
  • Early mobilization post-surgery

Vertebral Fracture

  • Assess for neurological compromise
  • Pain management (may include opioids)
  • Consider vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty for severe pain
  • Physical therapy

Multiple Fractures

  • Investigate for underlying malignancy
  • Order serum/urine protein electrophoresis
  • Consider bone scan

Prevention Strategies

Primary Prevention

Nutrition

  • Calcium intake: 1000-1200 mg/day
  • Vitamin D: 800-1000 IU/day
  • Adequate protein intake
  • Limit sodium and caffeine

Exercise

  • Weight-bearing activities (30 minutes, 3-5 times/week)
  • Resistance training (2-3 times/week)
  • Balance exercises (especially for older adults)

Lifestyle

  • Smoking cessation
  • Limit alcohol to ≤1 drink/day for women, ≤2 for men
  • Maintain healthy weight (BMI 20-25)

Secondary Prevention

For Patients with Osteoporosis

  • Pharmacological treatment as appropriate
  • Continued calcium and vitamin D supplementation
  • Regular weight-bearing exercise
  • Home safety modifications
  • Regular vision and hearing checks
  • Medication review to minimize fall risk

Treatment Algorithm

Pharmacological Treatment Approach

Initial Assessment

  • Confirm diagnosis with DXA (T-score ≤-2.5)
  • Assess for secondary causes
  • Evaluate fracture risk (FRAX)
  • Check calcium and vitamin D levels

First-line Treatment

  • Calcium (1000-1200 mg/day) and Vitamin D (800-1000 IU/day)
  • Oral bisphosphonate (alendronate, risedronate, ibandronate)
  • IV bisphosphonate (zoledronic acid) if unable to tolerate oral

If First-line Fails or Contraindicated

  • Denosumab (RANKL inhibitor)
  • SERMs (raloxifene) for women with high breast cancer risk
  • Hormone replacement therapy (for women <60 or <10 years postmenopause)

For Severe Osteoporosis

  • Teriparatide (PTH analog) - maximum 2 years
  • Abaloparatide (PTHrP analog) - maximum 2 years
  • Romosozumab (sclerostin inhibitor) - maximum 1 year
  • Follow with antiresorptive therapy to maintain gains

Monitoring

  • Repeat DXA after 1-2 years
  • Monitor for adherence and side effects
  • Consider drug holiday after 5 years of bisphosphonate therapy for low-risk patients

Risk Assessment Tools

FRAX Tool

The FRAX tool calculates the 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture.

Clinical Risk Factors Included:

  • Age
  • Sex
  • Weight
  • Height
  • Previous fracture
  • Parent fractured hip
  • Current smoking
  • Glucocorticoids
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Secondary osteoporosis
  • Alcohol (3+ units/day)
  • Femoral neck BMD (optional)

Garvan Fracture Risk Calculator

The Garvan calculator estimates fracture risk based on age, sex, fracture history, and falls history.

Key Features:

  • Includes number of falls in the past year
  • Provides 5 and 10-year fracture risk
