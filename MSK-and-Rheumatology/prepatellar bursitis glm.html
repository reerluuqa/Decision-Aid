Pre-patellar Bursitis

Quick Clinical Reference Tool
URGENT: Admit immediately if suspected septic joint (limited range of movement), severe infection, signs of sepsis, or systemic toxicity including high fever.

Definition

Inflammation of the pre-patellar bursa, a fluid-filled sac between the patella and skin that reduces friction. Also known as "housemaid's knee" or "carpet layer's knee".

Type Characteristics
Non-septic Sterile inflammation from trauma, overuse, crystal deposition, or systemic conditions
Septic Bacterial infection (usually Staphylococcus aureus or streptococci)

Who's at Risk?

  • Men aged 40-60 years
  • Occupations involving prolonged kneeling (cleaners, miners, carpet layers)
  • Sports with repetitive knee movement (basketball, wrestling)
  • Immunocompromised patients (diabetes, corticosteroid use)

Septic vs Non-septic

Feature Septic Non-septic
Fever Common Rare
Pain Severe, progressive Moderate, stable
Fluid appearance Purulent Straw-colored or bloody
CRP/ESR Elevated Normal

Complications

  • Septic arthritis
  • Skin necrosis and soft tissue infection
  • Sepsis
  • Osteomyelitis of the patella
  • Patellar tendon rupture (after aspiration/injection)

History

  • Pain, swelling, redness of knees
  • Difficulty kneeling or walking
  • Fever (more likely in septic)
  • History of trauma
  • Occupational/recreational activities involving kneeling
  • Associated conditions (RA, gout)
  • Immunocompromised status

Examination

  • Localized swelling and erythema over patella
  • Warmth (even in non-septic cases)
  • Tenderness (may be painless in chronic cases)
  • Fluctuance (movable and compressible)
  • Assess range of motion (usually preserved)
  • Check for signs of infection (fever, tachycardia)

Investigations

Test Indication Findings
Bursal aspiration To differentiate septic vs non-septic Pus (septic), straw-colored (non-septic), bloody (trauma/gout)
Gram stain/culture If septic suspected Staphylococcus aureus (80-90%), streptococci
Crystal analysis If gout suspected Urate crystals (gout)
CRP/ESR If septic suspected Elevated in septic bursitis
X-ray If bony pathology suspected Fracture, calcification, joint disease

Septic Bursitis Management

Immediate Action

Aspirate bursal fluid using aseptic technique before starting antibiotics

First-line Antibiotics

  • Flucloxacillin: 500mg four times daily (1000mg if >70kg)
  • Clarithromycin: 500mg twice daily (if penicillin allergic)
  • Erythromycin: 500mg four times daily (pregnancy/breastfeeding)

Follow-up

  • Review every few days
  • Treatment for 1-4 weeks depending on response
  • Adjust antibiotics according to sensitivities
  • Consider repeat aspiration if symptoms recur

Non-septic Bursitis Management

Conservative Measures

  • Rest: Avoid activities that worsen symptoms
  • Ice: Apply for 10 minutes every few hours
  • Activity modification: Use kneepads if kneeling required
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol or NSAIDs

Additional Options

  • Aspiration: If effusion is large
  • Corticosteroid injection: Only if confident it's non-septic
  • Refer: If no response after 2 months

Special Cases

  • Gout/RA: Manage underlying condition
  • Overuse: Modify activities, use protective devices

When to Refer or Admit

Urgent Admission

  • Suspected septic joint (limited range of movement)
  • Severe infection with signs of sepsis
  • Extensive cellulitis
  • Pointing abscess requiring drainage

Consider Admission

  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Patients with comorbidities (diabetes, RA)
  • Inadequate response to antibiotics
  • No response to primary care treatments after 2 months
Condition Distinguishing Features
Patellar dislocation Obvious deformity, acute trauma, inability to extend knee
Osgood-Schlatter disease Pain at tibial tubercle, common in adolescents
Septic arthritis Limited range of movement, severe pain, systemic illness
Gout/pseudogout Crystal deposition in fluid, may affect multiple joints
Cellulitis Spreading redness, no fluctuance, no localized swelling
