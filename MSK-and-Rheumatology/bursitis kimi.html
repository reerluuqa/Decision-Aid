Bursitis Management Algorithm

Interactive clinical decision support tool based on BMJ Best Practice guidelines. Last updated: May 10, 2024

Assessment & History
Key Diagnostic Factors
Localized Pain Tenderness on Palpation Decreased Active ROM Septic Features

Acute Presentation

  • Pain localized to bursa site, worse on movement
  • History of trauma or repetitive activity
  • Swelling and erythema (if superficial)
  • Crystal deposition: marked erythema, warmth, pain

Chronic Presentation

  • Symptoms lasting months with flares
  • Limited active movement if near joint
  • Passive range typically preserved
  • Recurrent swelling episodes
Septic Bursitis Red Flags

Urgent Assessment Required

Most common in prepatellar and olecranon bursae due to superficial position. Look for:

  • Low-grade temperature
  • Local erythema and warmth
  • Significant swelling
  • Recent penetrating injury or foreign body
Site-Specific Findings

Subacromial Bursitis

  • Painful arc on shoulder abduction (60-120°)
  • Impingement under coracoacromial arch
  • May coexist with rotator cuff tears

Trochanteric Bursitis

  • Lateral hip/thigh pain with limp
  • Tenderness over greater trochanter
  • Pain at extremes of rotation/abduction
  • Pseudoradiculopathy down lateral thigh

Knee Bursitis

  • Prepatellar: Anterior knee swelling, patella may be impalpable
  • Infrapatellar: Pain below patella over tibial tuberosity
  • Anserine: Medial knee tenderness 3-5cm distal to joint line

Olecranon Bursitis

  • Posterior elbow swelling/fluctuance
  • Usually minimal pain unless infected
  • High risk of recurrence
Risk Factors

Occupational

Repetitive mechanical stress (kneeling, carpet laying, "housemaid's knee")

Biomechanical

Leg length discrepancy, IT band contracture, valgus knee deformity

Systemic Disease

Rheumatoid arthritis, gout, CPPD, hip osteoarthritis

Footwear

Poorly fitting shoes causing retrocalcaneal bursitis

Diagnosis & Investigations
Clinical Diagnosis

Key Principle

Imaging studies are NOT usually required. Diagnosis can be made clinically based on localized pain and tenderness over a bursa, with swelling if superficial.

Clinical Confirmation Aspirate if Septic X-ray for Chronic Cases
Investigations

Bursal Aspiration

  • Indication: Suspected septic bursitis
  • Analysis: Gram stain, culture, WBC count
  • Crystals: Urate (gout) or CPPD crystals
  • Note: WBC <20,000/mm³ may still be septic

Imaging

  • X-ray: Exclude tumors, osteoarthritis
  • Ultrasound: Evaluate fluid collection
  • MRI: Deep bursae or associated pathology
  • CT: Rarely needed, for complex cases
Trochanteric Bursitis Diagnostic Criteria

Proposed Criteria (Not Validated)

Required: Lateral hip pain AND tenderness around greater trochanter

PLUS at least ONE of:

  • Pain at extremes of rotation, abduction, or adduction
  • Pain with hip abductor contraction against resistance
  • Pseudoradiculopathy (pain radiating down lateral thigh)
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Differentiating Features Tests
Septic Joint Severe pain with any movement, systemic symptoms Joint aspiration, WBC >50,000/mm³
Meniscal Tear Joint line pain, positive McMurray test MRI, Apley grind test
Osteoarthritis Joint space narrowing, crepitus, stiffness Weight-bearing X-rays
Rotator Cuff Tear Weakness, positive drop arm test MRI, ultrasound
Lumbar Radiculopathy Back pain radiation, neuro signs Lasegue test, MRI spine
Treatment & Management
Treatment Algorithm Overview
1
First-Line

Conservative Management & Analgesia

Activity modification, rest, ice, NSAIDs for all non-septic cases

2
Second-Line

Corticosteroid Injection

For cases refractory to conservative treatment. Avoid in septic bursitis.

3
Last Resort

Surgical Bursectomy

When all other modalities fail to control symptoms

Conservative Management
Rest & Modify Activity Ice Application Compression Assistive Devices

Activity Modification

  • Avoid activities that worsen symptoms
  • Protective padding (kneepads for prepatellar)
  • Crutches or walking stick for lower limb bursitis
  • Correct biomechanical risk factors

Ice & Physiotherapy

  • Ice topically for 10 minutes every few hours (first 24h)
  • Gentle mobilization after 24-48 hours
  • Physiotherapy to maintain ROM
  • Duration is patient-dependent
Pharmacological Management
Drug Class Example Dosage Notes
First-Line Paracetamol 500-1000mg q4-6h (max 4g/day) Safe initial analgesic
Topical NSAID Diclofenac gel Apply up to 4x daily Preferred over systemic NSAIDs
Oral NSAID Ibuprofen 200-400mg q4-6h (max 2.4g/day) If topical insufficient
Injection Methylprednisolone 40mg into bursa Repeat q1-5 weeks if needed

Steroid Injection Cautions

  • Retrocalcaneal: High risk of tendon rupture
  • Septic bursitis: Absolute contraindication
  • Technique: Aseptic technique, ultrasound guidance for deep bursae
  • Response: 2/3 excellent response in trochanteric bursitis
Septic Bursitis Management
URGENT Requires Aspiration Antibiotic Therapy
1
Immediate

Bursal Aspiration

Provides fluid for culture and reduces bacterial load. May need repeat aspiration.

2
Empiric

Antibiotic Therapy (1-4 weeks)

IV Options: Cefazolin 500-1500mg q6-8h or Oxacillin 500-1000mg q6-8h

Oral Options: Dicloxacillin 125-250mg q6h or Clarithromycin 500mg q12h

MRSA: Vancomycin 1g IV q12h

3
If Needed

Surgical Debridement

Indicated if inadequate drainage, abscess formation, or sinus present

Surgical Management
Last Resort Rarely Needed Effective if Indicated

Subacromial Bursitis

  • Bursectomy with subacromial decompression
  • Arthroscopic or open approach
  • Consider for refractory cases

Trochanteric Bursitis

  • Endoscopic bursectomy preferred
  • No detrimental effect from removal
  • Rarely recommended as isolated procedure

Retrocalcaneal Bursitis

  • Debridement of calcific portion
  • Bursal excision
  • Haglund deformity resection if present
  • Consider after 3-6 months conservative failure

Olecranon Bursitis

  • Open bursectomy for recurrent cases
  • High recurrence risk with conservative treatment
  • Compression bandaging + NSAIDs may be safer
Follow-up & Prognosis
Monitoring Schedule

Septic Bursitis

  • Review every 2-3 days during acute phase
  • Monitor for symptom resolution
  • Orthopedic follow-up to ensure resolution
  • Investigate underlying risk factors if recurrent

Non-Septic Bursitis

  • Follow-up only if symptoms persist
  • Review if no improvement after 4-6 weeks
  • Consider escalation to injection or surgery
Complications
Complication Timeframe Likelihood Management
Infection post-procedure Variable Low Antibiotics for up to 4 weeks (staph/strep cover)
Tendon rupture post-injection Variable Low Surgical repair, avoid repeat injection
Septic arthritis Variable Low Emergent joint washout + IV antibiotics
Recurrence Months-Years Moderate Address risk factors, consider surgery
Prognosis

Generally Excellent

  • Most patients respond to conservative management
  • Complete recovery is common
  • Corticosteroid injection success: 67% excellent response in trochanteric bursitis
  • At 5 years, 29% of trochanteric cases may have persistent symptoms
  • Injections increase recovery likelihood by 2.7x for trochanteric pain
Secondary Prevention

Patient Education Points

  • Maintain healthy weight to reduce joint pressure
  • Ensure proper posture and technique for work/sport
  • Use padding for activities involving joint pressure
  • Take regular breaks during repetitive tasks
  • Avoid knocking or banging joints
  • Promptly clean and dress any cuts near bursae
Patient Discussion Points

Activity Modification

  • Avoid overhead lifting (subacromial)
  • Gentle mobilization after 24-48 hours
  • Physiotherapy importance
  • Signs of infection post-injection

Septic Bursitis Care

  • Complete antibiotic course
  • Monitor for worsening symptoms
  • Seek urgent care if no improvement
  • Follow-up appointments critical
