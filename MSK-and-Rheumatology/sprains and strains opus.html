Step 1: Assessment

History taking and clinical examination

‚ñº
üìù History Taking
‚ñ∂

‚ö° Mechanism of Injury

Ask about how and when the injury occurred. Sudden inversion/internal rotation suggests lateral ankle sprain; sprinting suggests hamstring strain.

üìä Symptom Assessment

Evaluate pain severity, swelling, bruising, weight-bearing ability, and functional limitation. A 'pop' may suggest ACL injury or tendon tear.

üîÑ Duration & History

Symptoms >few days may indicate severe injury. Ask about previous injuries, chronic instability, or recurrent problems.

üèÉ Activity Level

Assess baseline physical activity, sport participation, use of bracing/taping, and risk factors for re-injury.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Consider Non-Accidental Injury

Where appropriate, consider non-accidental injury or domestic abuse, especially if mechanism doesn't match injury pattern.

ü©∫ Physical Examination
‚ñ∂
  • Observation: Look for swelling, bruising, limb deformity, or asymmetry (bruising may take 24hrs to appear)
  • Palpation: Check for point tenderness and joint line tenderness; replication of 'known pain' indicates ligament injury
  • Range of Motion: Assess active and passive ROM, strength, and flexibility
  • Stability Testing: Perform specific tests (e.g., anterior drawer test for ankle instability)
  • Functional Assessment: Evaluate coordination, balance, gait, and weight-bearing ability
  • Neurovascular: Check peripheral pulses are intact and symmetrical; assess for nerve injury
‚ÑπÔ∏è

Examination Challenges

Examination of an acutely injured joint can be difficult due to pain, swelling, and guarding. Consider repeat examination after initial swelling subsides.

üìã X-ray Decision Rules (Ottawa Rules) Clinical Tool

ü¶¥ Ottawa Clinical Decision Rules

Highly sensitive and cost-effective method for reducing unnecessary X-rays. Apply to patients aged >5 years.

ü¶∂ Ankle X-ray if:

  • Pain in malleolar zone AND:
  • Inability to bear weight (4 steps) immediately and at exam
  • OR bone tenderness at posterior edge of distal 6cm of fibula/tip of lateral malleolus
  • OR bone tenderness at posterior edge of distal 6cm of tibia/tip of medial malleolus

ü¶∂ Foot X-ray if:

  • Pain in midfoot zone AND:
  • Inability to bear weight (4 steps) immediately and at exam
  • OR bone tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal
  • OR bone tenderness of navicular bone

Step 2: Diagnosis

Classification of injury severity and differential diagnosis

‚ñº
üîó Sprain Classification (Ligament Injury)
‚ñ∂

A sprain is a stretch and/or tear of a ligament, occurring from abnormal or excessive forces applied to a joint. Commonly affects ankles, knees, wrists, and thumbs.

Grade I - Mild
Mild stretching of the ligament complex without joint instability. Tenderness present but joint stable.
Grade II - Moderate
Partial rupture of the ligament complex without joint instability. Moderate pain and swelling.
Grade III - Severe
Complete rupture of the ligament complex with joint instability. May require specialist referral.
üí™ Strain Classification (Muscle/Tendon Injury)
‚ñ∂

A strain ('pull') is a stretch and/or tear of muscle fibres and/or tendon, occurring when a muscle is stretched beyond limits or forced to contract too strongly. Typically affects foot, thigh muscles, and back.

First-Degree (Mild)
Only a few muscle fibres stretched/torn. Muscle tender and painful but has normal strength. Power may be limited by pain.
Second-Degree (Moderate)
Several injured muscle fibres. More severe pain, mild swelling, noticeable loss of strength, possible visible bruise.
Third-Degree (Severe)
Muscle tears completely through ('pop' sensation). Total loss of muscle function, severe pain/swelling, difficulty weight-bearing.
üîÄ Differential Diagnosis
‚ñ∂
Condition Key Features
Tendinitis Inflammation of tendon/sheath from repeated stress. Pain worsens with activity.
Bursitis Inflamed bursa from overuse. Localized swelling over bony prominence.
Tendon Rupture Inability to move area, palpable gap in tendon. Urgent
Meniscus Injury Twisting injury with tearing sensation, possible joint locking, swelling within 24hrs.
Stress Fracture May mimic hamstring strain. Point tenderness over bone, pain with activity.
Achilles Tendinopathy Degenerative condition. Pain, swelling, stiffness of Achilles tendon.
Joint Hypermobility/EDS Frequent dislocations, hyperextensibility, early-onset OA.
Referred Pain From lumbar spine, hip, or SI joint. Radicular symptoms may be present.

Step 3: Treatment

Management plan including referral criteria and self-care

‚ñº
üö® Urgent Referral to Emergency Department Urgent
‚ñ∂
üöë

Arrange Emergency Referral If Any Present or Suspected:

  • Fracture or dislocation (use Ottawa rules for imaging decision)
  • Damage to nerves or circulation
  • Wound penetrating the joint or known bleeding disorder
  • Serious complication: haemarthrosis or septic arthritis
  • Tendon rupture
  • Complete tear or tear of >50% muscle belly
  • Large intramuscular haematoma

Severe Injuries

For severe sprains and strains, a short period of immobilization for a few days after injury may be needed before commencing mobilization.

üèÉ Return to Activity Advice

  • Return to sport: Athletes may return when there is full, painless range of movement and muscle strength is restored
