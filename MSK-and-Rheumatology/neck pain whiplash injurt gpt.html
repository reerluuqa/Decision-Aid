1) Triage: exclude serious head/neck pathology first

Prioritise immediate risks. If serious injury suspected: urgent ED/A&E referral. Avoid ROM testing until red flags have been excluded.

Visual flag: serious injury features
FAST SCAN
  • Altered consciousness
  • Focal neurological deficit / limb paraesthesia
  • Midline cervical tenderness
Visual flag: serious non-MSK pathology
RED FLAGS
  • Infection/malignancy/inflammation: fever, night sweats, weight loss, escalating pain, intractable night pain
  • New/severe headache, photophobia/phonophobia, visual loss
  • Skin findings: erythema, wounds/exudate, petechiae/purpura, papulovesicular rash
  • Marked vertebral body tenderness / generalized stiffness

2) Assessment: structured history + focused examination

Capture mechanism, symptom profile, disability burden, and neuro/systemic features. Build the pathway before imaging.

1 History essentials
Use VAS
Ask about:
  • Injury circumstances (collision type, mechanism)
  • Symptoms: localisation, onset timing, trajectory, intensity (ideally VAS)
  • Occupational context + functional limitations
  • Medical history incl. previous injury/infection
  • Psychological symptoms (anxiety/depression)
  • Cancer history (assume cancer aetiology until excluded)
  • Fever (assume infectious aetiology until excluded)
2 Examination sequence
Safety first
  • Do not assess neck movements until serious injury has been excluded
  • Screen for neurological involvement (sensation/strength/reflexes)
  • If meningitis suspected: consider Kernig/Brudzinski
  • Palpate for cervical tenderness; inspect skin (rash, petechiae/purpura)
  • Assess associated injuries; exclude alternative causes of neck pain
3 Measure disability + expectations
NDI
  • Quantify disability using Neck Disability Index (NDI)
  • Ask expectation of recovery (‚ÄúDo you think you‚Äôll get better soon?‚Äù)
  • Negative expectation ‚Üí monitor closely; if not improving by 3‚Äì6 weeks, consider referral to WAD-experienced clinician

4) Classify WAD (Quebec Task Force)

Use WAD grade to standardise severity and guide escalation.

WAD I
Neck pain + associated symptoms without objective physical signs
WAD II
Neck pain + associated symptoms with objective physical signs, no neurological involvement
WAD III
Neck pain + associated symptoms with neurological involvement (reflex/sensation/weakness changes)
WAD IV
Neck pain + associated symptoms with fracture or dislocation

5) Management: active recovery + targeted supports

Core principle: reassure + keep active. Avoid immobilisation and soft collars unless another diagnosis dictates.

1 Self-care and behaviour
First-line
  • Reassure: recovery often within 2‚Äì3 months
  • Encourage early return to usual, non-provocative activities + early mobilisation
  • Frame symptoms as a normal reaction; focus on functional gains
  • Discourage rest, immobilisation, and soft collars
2 Analgesia (oral)
Symptom control
Consider oral analgesics (e.g., ibuprofen, paracetamol, or codeine) depending on severity, preference, tolerability, and adverse-effect risk.
3 Physiotherapy (consider)
Multimodal
Consider referral for a multimodal programme: ROM exercises, strengthening/stretching, and selected manual therapy.
4 Psychological support (consider early)
Risk stratify
Consider psychology referral where there is poor expectation of recovery / high disability expectation, or acute stress/PTSD symptoms, depressed mood, anxiety/fear about pain, high anger/frustration, passive coping, kinesiophobia, or avoidance behaviours.
5 Avoid ineffective options
Not recommended
Treatments described as ineffective/not recommended for whiplash include: anti-seizure medications, antidepressants, and muscle relaxants.
