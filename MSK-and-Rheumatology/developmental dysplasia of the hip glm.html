Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

What is DDH?

A spectrum of conditions affecting the proximal femur and acetabulum, ranging from:

  • Acetabular immaturity
  • Hip subluxation
  • Frank hip dislocation

In true DDH, the femoral head has a persistently abnormal anatomical relationship with the pelvic acetabulum, leading to abnormal bony development.

Key Statistics
Prevalence: 1.5-20/1000 births
Gender: 4x more common in females
Risk Factor: More common in breech presentation
Classification
Hip Dysplasia

Radiographic finding alone showing imperfect coverage of femoral head by acetabulum

Hip Subluxation

Partial articulation with greater than normal movement of femoral head within acetabulum

Hip Dislocation

Femoral head sits fully outside acetabulum, either at rest or with provocative testing

Fixed Antenatal Dislocation

Typically associated with multiple deformations, neurological conditions, or syndromes

Diagnosis

Key Diagnostic Factors
Positive Ortolani Test
Used in infants <6 months, loses sensitivity around 3-6 months due to increased musculature
Positive Barlow Test
Used in infants <6 months, loses sensitivity around 3-6 months due to increased musculature
Limited Hip Abduction
Most important screening method in infants around 3 months of age
Risk Factors
Female sex (4:1 ratio compared to males)
Positive family history (relative risk: 3.4-24.9)
Breech presentation (relative risk: 1.3-11.1)
Postural deformity (congenital muscular torticollis)
Restricted intrauterine space
Incorrect lower-extremity swaddling
Investigations
Ultrasound of Hips
Preferred for infants 6 weeks to 6 months to confirm clinical findings
First-line Low specificity
Hip X-ray
Preferred beyond 6 months when ossific nucleus has started to ossify
After 6 months Better specificity
Screening Recommendations
Physical Examination
  • Barlow and Ortolani tests until 3-6 months of age
  • Hip abduction assessment after 3-6 months
  • Routine examination at all well-child checks
Ultrasound Scanning
  • Controversial as universal screening tool
  • Recommended for high-risk infants (selective screening)
  • Delay until ~6 weeks to reduce false positives

Management

Treatment Algorithm by Age
< 6 months
Hip Dysplasia: Observation → Pavlik harness
Hip Subluxation: Observation (up to 3 weeks)
Non-teratological Dislocation: Pavlik harness → Closed reduction → Open reduction
Teratological Dislocation: Open reduction with spica casting
6-18 months
Non-teratological Dislocation: Closed reduction with spica casting → Open reduction
Teratological Dislocation: Open reduction with spica casting
>18 months to 6 years
Hip Dislocation: Open reduction with spica casting ± femoral/pelvic osteotomy
> 6 years
Hip reconstruction (pelvic/femoral osteotomies)
Salvage osteotomies if concentric reduction cannot be achieved
Observation may be considered in select cases
Non-Surgical Treatment
Pavlik Harness
  • Achieves closed reduction >90% of the time
  • Frequent clinical follow-up for at least 3 months
  • Discontinue if no reduction after 3-4 weeks
  • Complications: osteonecrosis (0-7%), nerve palsy
Rigid Abduction Brace
  • Used if Pavlik harness fails
  • Successful in >80% of cases that fail Pavlik harness
Surgical Treatment
Closed Reduction with Spica Casting
  • General anesthesia with arthrographical confirmation
  • Adductor tenotomy frequently performed
  • Cast immobilization for 12 weeks or until stability
Open Reduction with Spica Casting
  • Removal of intra-articular obstacles
  • Concentric reduction and capsulorrhaphy
  • May include femoral/pelvic osteotomies in older children
Treatment Goals & Considerations
Treatment Goals
  • Obtain and maintain stable, concentric reduction
  • Optimize functional and anatomical results
  • Avoid complications
Key Considerations
  • Early diagnosis leads to better outcomes
  • Most stable, radiologically dysplastic hips improve spontaneously
  • Upper age limit for open reduction of asymptomatic bilateral dislocations is ~6 years

Follow-up & Monitoring

Monitoring Recommendations
During Treatment

Clinical and radiographic follow-up is required until skeletal maturity is reached

First 12-24 Months

More frequent imaging may be required during this period

After Initial Treatment

Radiographs suggested every 1-3 years

Long-term Follow-up

Follow-up well into adulthood is required to assess functional outcome

Patient Discussions
Parent Education
  • Explain reasons for referral to pediatric orthopedist
  • Discuss potential evaluation and treatment interventions
  • Emphasize compliance with Pavlik harness or brace wear
Long-term Counseling
  • Reinforce necessity of routine follow-up
  • Explain that follow-up is needed even when asymptomatic
  • Discuss potential complications and long-term outcomes
Complications to Monitor
Osteonecrosis
Can occur secondary to suboptimal positioning within brace or forced abduction
Nerve Palsy
Can occur secondary to suboptimal positioning within brace
Recurrent Instability
Requires ongoing monitoring through radiographic evaluation
Residual Dysplasia
May require additional intervention if severe
Quick Reference: DDH Critical Points
Red Flags
  • Positive Ortolani/Barlow test in infants <6 months
  • Limited hip abduction in infants >3 months
  • Asymmetric skin folds or leg length discrepancy
  • Late presentation with abnormal gait or limp
Immediate Actions
  • Refer to pediatric orthopedist for confirmed instability
  • Initiate Pavlik harness for dislocated hips in infants <6 months
  • Arrange ultrasound for infants with risk factors
  • Parent education on treatment compliance
Key Tests
  • Ortolani test (relocatable dislocation)
  • Barlow test (dislocatable hip)
  • Hip abduction assessment (>3 months)
  • Ultrasound (6 weeks to 6 months)
  • X-ray (>6 months)
Timeline
  • Screening: All well-child checks in first year
  • Observation period: Up to 3 weeks for subluxation
  • Pavlik harness: Minimum 3 months with follow-up
  • Spica cast: Typically 12 weeks
  • Long-term: Follow-up to skeletal maturity
