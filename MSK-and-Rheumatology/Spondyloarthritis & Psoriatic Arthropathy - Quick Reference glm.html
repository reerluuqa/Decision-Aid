Spondyloarthritis & Psoriatic Arthropathy

Last revised: March 2024

Red Flag: Urgent Referral Required

Any person with suspected axial or peripheral spondyloarthritis should be referred to a rheumatologist for confirmation of diagnosis and treatment implementation.

When to Suspect Axial Spondyloarthritis

  • Chronic or recurrent low back pain, fatigue, and stiffness
  • Onset of pain at age 45 or younger
  • Pain present for more than 3 months
  • Inflammatory back pain (worse in morning, >30 min, improves with movement)
  • Current or previous buttock pain
  • Pain in thoracic or cervical spine
  • Asymmetric peripheral arthritis
  • Enthesitis (insertional pain at tendon/ligament attachments)
  • Symptoms waking in night (especially second half)
  • Response to NSAIDs within 48 hours
  • Family history of spondyloarthritis

When to Suspect Psoriatic Arthropathy

  • Current or previous psoriasis or family history
  • Peripheral arthritis (distal interphalangeal joints commonly affected)
  • Dactylitis - "sausage-like" swelling of digits
  • Enthesitis
  • Nail pitting and onycholysis
  • Monoarticular or oligoarticular involvement
  • Symmetrical polyarticular involvement
  • Morning stiffness >30 min, improves with use
  • Morning first-step foot pain
  • Lower extremity symptoms worse than upper
  • Heel pain, elbow pain, lateral hip pain (enthesitis)

Important Notes

  • Spondyloarthritis may be present despite no evidence of sacroiliitis on X-ray
  • Can occur in people who are HLA-B27 negative
  • There may be no correlation between severity of skin and joint manifestations in psoriatic arthritis
  • The Psoriasis Epidemiological Screening Tool (PEST) is validated to assess adults with psoriasis for psoriatic arthritis

Assessment Approach

History Taking

  • Pain and stiffness characteristics
  • Whether symptoms are relieved by rest or activity
  • Extra-articular symptoms (uveitis, etc.)
  • Family history of spondyloarthritis, psoriasis, IBD

Physical Examination

  • Assess affected joints for swelling
  • Check for impaired mobility
  • Inspect skin (scalp, limbs, trunk) if psoriatic arthritis suspected
  • Examine nails for pitting and onycholysis

Special Considerations

Skin Assessment

For people with features consistent with psoriatic arthritis but no prior history of psoriasis, inspect the skin and nails.

Age Considerations

Axial spondyloarthritis usually manifests between ages 16-45. Psoriatic arthritis peaks in the sixth decade in women.

Comorbidities

Look for established comorbidities: uveitis, psoriasis, inflammatory bowel disease, recent GI/GU infection.

Joint Patterns

Note pattern of joint involvement: asymmetric, monoarticular, oligoarticular, or symmetrical polyarticular.

Investigations to Consider

Blood Tests

  • ESR and/or CRP (may be elevated, but normal in many)
  • HLA-B27 (if suspected axial spondyloarthritis with 3/4 referral criteria)
  • Rheumatoid factor (to differentiate from RA)
  • Vitamin D, uric acid, calcium, thyroid function (to rule out differentials)

Imaging

  • X-ray of sacroiliac joints and spine (for suspected axial)
  • X-rays of hands and feet (for suspected psoriatic)
  • X-ray of pelvis (all suspected psoriatic arthritis)
  • Ultrasound of joints and enthesitis sites (if X-ray inconclusive)

Important Considerations

  • No single test can reliably diagnose or rule out spondyloarthritis
  • Normal ESR/CRP does not exclude diagnosis
  • Spondyloarthritis may be present despite no evidence of sacroiliitis on X-ray
  • Do not let investigations delay referral for clinically suspected cases

Suspected Spondyloarthritis

Immediate Actions:

Refer to Rheumatologist

Any person with suspected axial or peripheral spondyloarthritis should be referred to a rheumatologist for confirmation of diagnosis and treatment implementation.

While Awaiting Referral:

  • Consider NSAIDs for symptom relief
  • Document symptoms and examination findings
  • Arrange baseline investigations if not already done

Confirmed Spondyloarthritis

Shared Care Management:

  • Follow-up as part of shared-care arrangement with specialist rheumatology
  • Monitor for complications (uveitis, IBD, cardiovascular disease)
  • Assess and manage cardiovascular risk factors
  • Monitor for medication side effects

For People with Psoriasis:

  • Annual assessment for psoriatic arthritis
  • Monitor systemic therapy in accordance with local protocols

NICE Quality Standards

Referral

Adults with suspected axial or peripheral spondyloarthritis are referred to a rheumatologist.

Imaging

Adults with suspected axial spondyloarthritis and an X-ray that does not show sacroiliitis have an MRI using an inflammatory back pain protocol.

Physiotherapy

Adults with axial spondyloarthritis are referred to a specialist physiotherapist for a structured exercise programme.

Information

Adults with spondyloarthritis are given information about their condition, healthcare professionals involved, and how to contact them.

Cardiovascular Risk

Adults with severe psoriasis are offered a cardiovascular risk assessment at diagnosis and at least once every 5 years.

Annual Assessment

People with psoriasis having treatment are offered an annual assessment for psoriatic arthritis.

Medication Updates (March 2024)

Imraldi 40 mg

Indicated for active and progressive psoriatic arthritis in adults when response to previous DMARD therapy has been inadequate.

Steqeyma (ustekinumab)

Biosimilar for plaque psoriasis, paediatric plaque psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis and Crohn's disease.

WEZENLA

Indicated for active psoriatic arthritis in adults when response to previous non-biological DMARD therapy has been inadequate.

gobivaz

Newly licensed for the treatment of psoriatic arthritis.
