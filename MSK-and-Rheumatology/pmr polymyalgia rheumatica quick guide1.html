🔑 Key Principles

▼
⚠ Every time you think PMR → also think GCA
⚠ 25–50% initially diagnosed with PMR later found to have a DIFFERENT diagnosis
⚠ 20% of PMR patients have concurrent GCA
⚠ There is NO diagnostic test for PMR — it is a clinical diagnosis
  • Inflammatory disorder → proximal muscle pain & stiffness
  • Almost always age >50y (peaks 70–80y)
  • Predominantly managed in primary care
  • No NICE/SIGN guidance — BSR 2009 + EULAR 2015 available
  • Raised inflammatory markers common but not universal

🎯 Typical Symptoms (BSR/BHPR)

▼
Diagnosis = clinical history + examination + elevated inflammatory markers (usually)
  • Painful stiffness >2 weeks in:
    • Upper arms / shoulders (bilateral)
    • Lateral hips
    • Buttocks / thighs
  • Worse in morning — eases over first few hours
  • Some describe all-over stiffness lasting all day
  • Functional impact: difficulty getting out of bed, turning in bed, getting up from chair
  • Feeling systemically unwell
  • Patients may describe disability rather than localised symptoms
Good response to steroids within 24–72h supports diagnosis — but do NOT rely on steroid response alone for diagnosis

🔍 Examination

▼
  • Directed at excluding other diagnoses — especially infection or cancer
  • If examined in morning → may be very stiff on mobilising
  • Always check for GCA signs at every visit — temporal arteries, scalp tenderness, visual fields, pulse symmetry
  • Full joint examination to exclude inflammatory arthritis

⚠️ Diagnostic Pitfalls

▼
25–50% of PMR diagnoses turn out to be WRONG
Most commonly: late-onset RA or GCA
  • Normal inflammatory markers do NOT exclude PMR — but should raise suspicion of alternative dx
  • Very high ESR/CRP (>100) → consider GCA or other pathology
  • Age <60y → think hard about alternatives, lower threshold for referral
  • If diagnosis unclear → refer to rheumatology

🚨 GCA — NEVER MISS DIAGNOSIS

▼
🚨 EARLY TREATMENT PREVENTS SIGHT LOSS
🚨 50% of GCA patients have PMR symptoms
🚨 20% of PMR patients have concurrent GCA
🚨 GCA may appear weeks, months or YEARS after PMR diagnosis
🚨 Relapsing PMR — imaging frequently finds GCA partially masked by steroids

🔴 GCA Symptoms

  • New-onset headache
  • Scalp tenderness
  • Jaw/tongue claudication (pain on chewing)
  • Visual disturbance
  • Fever / weight loss / fatigue
  • Limb claudication

🔴 GCA Signs

  • Tender/thickened temporal artery
  • Scalp tenderness
  • Visual field defect
  • Asymmetry of pulses
🏥 GCA + VISUAL DISTURBANCE → ADMIT/REFER IMMEDIATELY for ophthalmology review — IV steroids may be needed

📞 Suspected GCA without visual symptoms → SAME-DAY discussion (usually rheumatology)
⚡ No single sign, symptom or test can rule GCA in or out — act on clinical suspicion
⚡ If needs >25mg pred for response → re-evaluate — could this be GCA?
✅ Educate ALL PMR patients about GCA symptoms that should prompt urgent (same-day) medical advice

🔀 Differential Diagnoses — Ask: Is This More Likely?

▼
  • Active cancer — weight loss, night sweats, new lumps
  • Infection — fever, localised signs
  • Giant cell arteritis (GCA)
  • Late-onset rheumatoid arthritis — small joints, RhF/anti-CCP positive
  • SLE / connective tissue disease / myopathy
  • Local shoulder/hip pathology — unilateral, mechanical features
  • Fibromyalgia / chronic pain syndromes — widespread tender points, normal bloods
  • Hypovitaminosis D — knee and thigh pain alone?
  • Drug-induced myopathy — statins! Check medication history
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Myeloma
💡 Always ask yourself: Are any of these more likely than PMR?
💡 Age <50y → PMR very unlikely — investigate thoroughly for alternatives

🧪 Baseline Investigations (EULAR 2015)

▼
Use clinical judgement — these are a pragmatic guide to screen for alternatives & assess comorbidities before long-term steroids

Essential Bloods

  • FBC
  • ESR + CRP — normal does not exclude PMR; very high (>100) → think GCA/other pathology
  • U&E
  • LFTs
  • Calcium
  • Glucose / HbA1c — essential pre-steroids
  • Rheumatoid Factor + anti-CCP

Consider Additionally

  • TFTs
  • Creatine kinase (if myopathy suspected)
  • Myeloma screen (protein electrophoresis, BJP)
  • Vitamin D

Other

  • Urinalysis
  • CXR — consider if prominent systemic symptoms / smoker
📋 Document this "minimum dataset" at diagnosis

💊 Pre-Steroid Checklist

▼
Before starting steroids — assess comorbidities that increase glucocorticoid side-effect risk:
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes / prediabetes
  • Obesity
  • Osteoporosis
  • Cataracts / glaucoma
  • Review medications for interactions / increased toxicity risk
Multiple comorbidities → trial LOWER dose first + lower threshold for referral if higher doses needed or relapses occur

🔄 Relapses & Difficulty Weaning

▼
~50%
Relapse during treatment
40%
Need >2y steroids
  • Relapse → step up steroids briefly → taper more slowly
  • Persistent symptoms / multiple relapses → REVIEW DIAGNOSIS
  • May indicate undiagnosed GCA or other inflammatory arthritis → refer
Steroid-sparing therapy (secondary care decision):
• Methotrexate (DMARD) — most commonly used
• Tocilizumab (IL-6 blocker) — trial evidence for refractory PMR (JAMA 2022)
• Sarilumab — reduced relapse rate & steroid burden (NEJM 2023)

🛡️ Bone & Gastroprotection

▼
Protect the patient's bones and stomach — do this from the START
ProtectionDrugNotes
Gastroprotection PPI (omeprazole or lansoprazole) — standard dose For duration of steroid treatment
Bone protection Bisphosphonate ± calcium/vitamin D FRAX/QFracture at start — bone loss occurs quickly. Majority will need bone protection.
  • Fracture risk returns to baseline ~12 months after stopping steroids
  • Audits show GPs under-prescribe both gastroprotection and bone protection — don't forget!

▼
PeriodFrequency

BloodsFBC, ESR/CRP, U&E, glucose — every 3 months
💡 Tip: HbA1c + random glucose together may be most useful — fasting glucose misses steroid-related post-meal hyperglycaemia

✅ At EVERY Follow-up Visit — Do This

▼
  • Assess clinical remission (symptoms & signs)
  • Check FBC, ESR/CRP, U&E, glucose
  • Screen for GCA symptoms/signs — new headache, jaw claudication, visual changes, temporal artery tenderness
  • Ensure patient is aware of GCA symptoms needing URGENT (same-day) medical review
  • Monitor steroid side-effects: weight, BP, glucose, mood, sleep
  • Confirm bone & gastroprotection in place
  • Check steroid card is up to date

📤 When to Refer (BSR/EULAR)

▼
Consider referral to rheumatology if:
  • Age <60 years
  • Chronic or atypical onset
  • Absence of typical symptoms
  • Normal or very high ESR/CRP (>100) — may suggest GCA or other pathology
  • Incomplete / non-response to steroids
  • Difficulty withdrawing steroids / multiple relapses
  • Overlapping GCA symptoms
🏥 GCA + visual disturbance → IMMEDIATE admission/referral (ophthalmology) — IV steroids may be required

📞 Suspected GCA → SAME-DAY discussion (usually rheumatology)
If diagnosis is unclear at any point → refer to rheumatology rather than persisting with empirical steroid treatment
