Assessment of Inflamed Joint

Interactive clinical decision support algorithm for evaluating and managing patients presenting with inflammatory arthritis. Based on BMJ Best Practice guidelines (Last updated: Oct 23, 2025).

Step 1: Clinical Assessment

Focused history and physical examination to identify red flags and narrow differential diagnosis

URGENT PRIORITY

In any patient with acute hot swollen joint, septic arthritis must be excluded first. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention.

History of Onset
Critical
  • Acute onset (< 1 week): Suggests infection, crystal arthropathy, or trauma
  • Subacute (1-4 weeks): Consider reactive arthritis, Lyme disease
  • Chronic (> 6 weeks): RA, psoriatic arthritis, osteoarthritis
  • Migratory pattern: Acute rheumatic fever, gonococcal arthritis
Joint Involvement Pattern
Important
  • Monoarthritis (1 joint): Septic arthritis until proven otherwise
  • Oligoarthritis (2-4 joints): Reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis
  • Polyarthritis (≥5 joints): RA, SLE, viral arthritis
  • Axial involvement: Ankylosing spondylitis, enteropathic arthritis
Risk Factor Assessment
High Risk
  • IV drug use: Increased risk of septic arthritis
  • Diabetes/Immunosuppression: Suspect infection
  • Prosthetic joint: Prosthetic joint infection risk
  • Recent surgery/procedure: Iatrogenic infection
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Increased infection susceptibility
Physical Examination
Essential
  • Classic signs: Pain, erythema, warmth, swelling, stiffness
  • Range of motion: Limited and painful in infection
  • Palpation: Identify joint line vs periarticular structures
  • Systemic signs: Fever, malaise, rash, tenosynovitis
Red Flags - Immediate Action
EMERGENCY
  • Fever > 39°C with hot joint: Septic arthritis likely
  • Severe pain with minimal movement: Suspect infection
  • Rapidly progressive swelling: Hemarthrosis or infection
  • Neurovascular compromise: Compartment syndrome risk
  • Immunocompromised with joint symptoms: Low threshold for infection
Travel & Exposure History
Epidemiologic
  • Tick exposure: Lyme disease endemic areas
  • Travel to tropics: Chikungunya, arbovirus infections
  • Sexual history: Gonococcal arthritis risk
  • Food poisoning: Reactive arthritis (Salmonella, Campylobacter)
  • Day care/school: Parvovirus B19 exposure

Initial Assessment Flow

1
Assess onset and pattern: Acute vs chronic, mono- vs polyarticular
2
Identify red flags: Fever, immunosuppression, prosthetic joint, severe pain
3
Risk stratification: High risk ( septic arthritis) vs low risk
4
Immediate action: If high risk → urgent joint aspiration + empiric antibiotics

Step 2: Diagnostic Investigation

Joint aspiration, laboratory tests, and imaging to establish definitive diagnosis

URGENT: Joint Aspiration

All patients with suspected septic arthritis require immediate joint aspiration for synovial fluid analysis. Do not delay empiric antibiotics while awaiting results.

Synovial Fluid Analysis
Critical
Interpretation Guidelines
  • WBC > 100,000/mm³:Septic arthritis (high specificity)
  • WBC 50,000-100,000/mm³: Indeterminate - consider infection
  • PMNs > 90%: Strongly suggests bacterial infection
  • Crystals present: Gout (negatively birefringent) or pseudogout (weakly positive)
Essential Tests
  • Gram stain and bacterial culture
  • Cell count with differential
  • Crystal analysis (polarized microscopy)
  • AFB smear/culture if TB suspected
  • Fungal culture if immunocompromised
Blood Cultures
High Yield
  • Positive in 50% of septic arthritis cases
  • Obtain before antibiotics when possible
  • Two sets from different sites recommended
  • Helps identify organism and antibiotic susceptibility
  • Crucial for prosthetic joint infections
Inflammatory Markers
Supportive
  • ESR/CRP: Elevated in infection and inflammation
  • Procalcitonin: More specific for bacterial infection
  • Serum urate: > 360 μmol/L (6 mg/dL) supports gout
  • Ferritin: > 3000 ng/mL suggests AOSD
  • ACE level: Elevated in sarcoidosis
Autoimmune Serology
Specific
  • RF and anti-CCP: Rheumatoid arthritis
  • ANA (≥1:160): SLE screening (high sensitivity)
  • Anti-dsDNA, Anti-Sm: SLE specific
  • HLA-B27: Ankylosing spondylitis (95% in white patients)
  • ANCA: Vasculitis workup
Imaging Modalities
Essential
Indications & Findings
  • X-ray: Fracture, OA changes, chondrocalcinosis
  • Ultrasound: Joint effusion, synovitis, guided aspiration
  • MRI: Early sacroiliitis, soft tissue pathology
  • DECT: Urate deposition in gout
  • CT: Complex fractures, osteochondral lesions
Microbiologic Tests
Critical
  • Gonococcal cultures: Thayer-Martin medium (genital, pharyngeal, rectal)
  • NAAT for Chlamydia: First-catch urine or swabs
  • Parvovirus IgM: Recent infection (7-10 days post-exposure)
  • Lyme serology: 2-tier testing (EIA + Western blot)
  • TB cultures: 6-8 weeks on Lowenstein-Jensen media

Diagnostic Approach Flow

1
Joint aspiration: Immediate in all suspected septic arthritis cases
2
Synovial fluid analysis: Gram stain, culture, cell count, crystals
3
Blood tests: Cultures, inflammatory markers, serology as indicated
4
Imaging: X-ray ± ultrasound ± MRI based on differential
5
Special tests: PCR for atypical infections, biopsy if needed

Step 3: Treatment & Management

Evidence-based treatment strategies based on definitive diagnosis

SEPTIC ARTHRITIS - EMERGENCY TREATMENT

Start empiric IV antibiotics immediately after obtaining cultures. Coverage must include Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA) and gram-negative organisms.

Septic Arthritis Treatment
URGENT
Empiric Antibiotic Regimens
  • Vancomycin + 3rd gen cephalosporin (e.g., ceftriaxone)
  • Add pseudomonal coverage if risk factors present
  • Adjust based on culture results and susceptibilities
  • Duration: 2 weeks IV + 4-6 weeks oral
Supportive Measures
  • Joint drainage (repeated aspiration or arthroscopy)
  • Immobilization initially, then early mobilization
  • Analgesia with NSAIDs or opioids as needed
  • Consider surgical washout if inadequate response
Crystal Arthropathies
Acute Management
Gout Treatment
  • NSAIDs: First-line (e.g., naproxen, indomethacin)
  • Colchicine: Effective if early in attack
  • Steroids: Oral, intra-articular, or IM if NSAIDs contraindicated
  • Urate-lowering therapy: Start after acute attack resolves
Pseudogout Treatment
  • NSAIDs: First-line treatment
  • Joint aspiration: Reduces intra-articular pressure
  • Steroids: Intra-articular or oral if NSAIDs contraindicated
  • Colchicine: Prophylaxis for recurrent attacks
Reactive & Infectious Arthritis
Targeted Therapy
  • Gonococcal: Ceftriaxone + azithromycin (cover Chlamydia)
  • Lyme disease: Doxycycline or amoxicillin for 28 days
  • Reactive arthritis: NSAIDs ± steroids, treat triggering infection
  • Parvovirus: Supportive care, usually self-limited
  • TB arthritis: Standard anti-TB regimen for 6-18 months
Inflammatory Arthritis (RA/PA)
Long-term
Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • NSAIDs: Symptom control only
  • Steroids: Bridge therapy, intra-articular injection
  • DMARDs: Methotrexate first-line (start early)
  • Biologics: Anti-TNF, anti-IL-6 for refractory disease
Psoriatic Arthritis
  • NSAIDs: First-line for mild disease
  • Steroids: Intra-articular injections
  • DMARDs: Methotrexate, sulfasalazine
  • Biologics: Anti-TNF, anti-IL-17, anti-IL-12/23
Hemarthrosis Management
Urgent Drainage
  • Immediate aspiration: Reduces cartilage damage from blood
  • Factor replacement: Hemophilia patients (raise to 50-100%)
  • Tranexamic acid: Adjunctive hemostatic agent
  • Treat underlying cause: Coagulopathy, trauma, tumor
  • Immobilization: Short-term to prevent re-bleeding
Osteoarthritis Management
Conservative
  • NSAIDs: First-line for pain and inflammation
  • Intra-articular steroids: Short-term relief for flares
  • Hyaluronic acid: Controversial benefit
  • Physical therapy: Strengthening, ROM exercises
  • Weight loss: Critical for weight-bearing joints

Treatment Algorithm

1
Start empiric antibiotics if septic arthritis suspected (do not wait for culture results)
2
Drain infected joint (repeated aspiration or surgical washout)
3
Tailor therapy based on culture results and clinical response
4
Supportive care: Analgesia, physical therapy, treat underlying cause

Step 4: Monitoring & Follow-up

Response assessment, complication monitoring, and long-term management planning

Follow-up Principles

All patients require close monitoring for treatment response and complications. Frequency of follow-up depends on diagnosis severity and treatment intensity.

Septic Arthritis Follow-up
Frequent Monitoring
  • Daily initially: Monitor temperature, pain, range of motion
  • Repeat aspiration: If ongoing effusion or poor response
  • CRP/ESR: Should decrease within 3-5 days of effective therapy
  • IV to oral switch: When clinically improved and afebrile
  • Total duration: 6 weeks minimum, longer if slow response
Crystal Arthritis Monitoring
Routine
  • Acute attack: Follow until resolution (usually 5-10 days)
  • Urate-lowering therapy: Monitor serum urate every 2-4 weeks initially
  • Prophylaxis: Colchicine or NSAIDs during initiation of ULT
  • Lifestyle: Diet modification, weight management, alcohol reduction
  • Joint protection: Avoid triggers for recurrent attacks
Inflammatory Arthritis Monitoring
Regular Assessment
Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • DAS28: Every 1-3 months to assess disease activity
  • CRP/ESR: Monitor treatment response
  • X-rays: Annually to assess progression
  • DMARD monitoring: LFTs, CBC every 8-12 weeks
  • Biologics: Screen for TB, HBV before starting
Psoriatic Arthritis
  • PASI score: Monitor skin disease activity
  • Joint counts: Swollen/tender joint assessment
  • Enthesitis/dactylitis: Assess targeted therapies
  • HRQoL measures: Impact on daily functioning
When to Refer
Important
  • Rheumatology: Inflammatory arthritis, diagnostic uncertainty
  • Orthopedics: Septic arthritis, suspected prosthetic infection
  • Infectious disease: Atypical infections, complex cases
  • Oncology: Suspected paraneoplastic or metastatic arthritis
  • Physiotherapy: Rehabilitation, joint protection strategies
Complication Monitoring
Vigilant
  • Septic arthritis: Osteomyelitis, septicemia, chronic joint damage
  • RA: Cervical spine instability, interstitial lung disease, vasculitis
  • Gout: Tophi formation, nephrolithiasis, chronic kidney disease
  • PsA: Ocular involvement (uveitis), cardiovascular comorbidities
  • Infection: Recurrence, secondary infections during immunosuppression
Long-term Management
Ongoing
  • Patient education: Disease understanding, self-management
  • Lifestyle modification: Exercise, weight control, smoking cessation
  • Vaccinations: Up-to-date before immunosuppression
  • Comorbidity screening: Cardiovascular, osteoporosis, depression
  • Multidisciplinary care: Coordinate with primary care, specialists

Follow-up Schedule Guide

1
High-risk patients (septic arthritis): Daily monitoring until stable, then weekly
2
Acute inflammatory arthritis: Review at 1-2 weeks, then monthly until controlled
3
Stable chronic disease: Every 3-6 months with laboratory monitoring
4
Remission: Consider tapering therapy with close monitoring
