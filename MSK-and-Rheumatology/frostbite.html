Assessment

Emergency Priorities

Life-Threatening Conditions First

Assess and treat hypothermia, major trauma, or other life-threatening injuries before focusing on frostbite.

Urgent

History & Examination

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Cold exposure history: Duration, temperature, wind chill, altitude
  • Early symptoms: Cold sensation → numbness/loss of sensation
  • Rewarming pain: Extreme pain reported by 76% of patients during rewarming
  • Skin discoloration: Purplish, white, or yellowish waxy appearance
  • Bister formation: Pattern indicates severity (distal vs proximal)
  • Neurovascular status: Document sensation and perfusion

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

  • Cold exposure (temperatures ≤ 0°C/32°F)
  • Previous frostbite (prolonged reduced cold tolerance)
  • Vascular insufficiency/poor circulation

Moderate Risk Factors

  • High altitude exposure
  • Alcohol consumption (impairs judgment & causes heat loss)
  • Tobacco use (reduces peripheral circulation)
  • Dehydration
  • Extremity trauma
  • Constrictive clothing/equipment
  • Hypoxia

Physical Findings by Severity

Degree Findings Prognosis Badge
1st Degree Erythema, numbness, white/yellow waxy discoloration, mild edema Good Stable
2nd Degree Clear/milky fluid blisters, surrounded by erythema and edema Good Stable
3rd Degree Deep purple blood-filled blisters within 24h, reticular dermis injury Poor Caution
4th Degree Full thickness injury: subcutaneous tissue, muscle, nerve, bone Very Poor Urgent
Field Tip Superficial: 1st-2nd degree (no/minimal tissue loss) Deep: 3rd-4th degree (anticipated tissue loss)
Diagnosis

Diagnostic Approach

Primary: Clinical diagnosis based on history and physical examination

Imaging: Not required for diagnosis, but used for prognosis assessment

Timing: Severity classification should be applied AFTER rewarming

Diagnostic Investigations

Key Imaging Modalities

Technetium-99m Pertechnetate Scintigraphy

  • Measures tissue perfusion
  • Most sensitive for deep tissue/bony injury
  • Best correlation at 48 hours with prognosis
  • Can assess treatment response
Gold Standard

Magnetic Resonance Angiography (Emerging)

  • More available than Technetium scans
  • Direct vessel and tissue visualization
  • Predicts tissue viability
  • Guides surgical amputation timing

Plain Radiography

  • Not for frostbite diagnosis
  • Only for suspected fractures/dislocations

Emerging Modalities

  • Thermography
  • Duplex imaging

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Features Management
Frostnip Mildest form; rewarming restores function without damage Preventive action needed
Pernio (Chilblains) Non-freezing cold injury; tender pruritic lesions Avoid active rewarming >30°C
Immersion Foot Prolonged wet cold exposure; slow rewarming required Do NOT rapid rewarm
Raynaud's Phenomenon Reversible skin changes; no tissue damage Vascular assessment
Cutaneous Burns Heat/chemical exposure history Burn management protocol

Prognostic Indicators

Good Prognostic Signs Positive

  • Early pinprick sensation recovery
  • Healthy-appearing skin
  • Clear (non-hemorrhagic) blebs

Poor Prognostic Signs Negative

  • Hemorrhagic blebs not extending distally
  • Persistent cyanosis
  • Tissue remains frozen/wooden
Treatment

Immediate Priorities

Critical Actions

If risk of amputation is present, urgent transfer to major trauma center or vascular unit with modern treatment capabilities is essential. Time is critical!

Time-Critical

Rapid Rewarming Protocol

Timing: Initiate as soon as possible, BUT only when safe from refreezing
Method: Water bath at 37-39°C (98.6-102.2°F)
  • May use up to 40°C per AHA/Red Cross
  • Gently circulated water
  • Duration: 15-30 minutes until soft and pliable
Precautions:
  • NO dry heat or rubbing
  • Remove jewelry early (swelling risk)
  • Antibacterial soap can be added
  • Elevate extremity above heart
Caution
Completion: Tissue flushes red/purple and becomes soft/pliable

Pharmacological Management

Essential Medications All Patients

NSAIDs (Start in field)

  • Ibuprofen: 6 mg/kg PO twice daily initially, max 2400 mg/day
  • Aspirin: 325-650 mg PO q4h PRN if ibuprofen unavailable
  • Purpose: Analgesia + anti-inflammatory (blocks prostaglandin/thromboxane)

Analgesia

  • Opioids: Morphine sulfate 10-30 mg PO q4h PRN for severe pain

Tetanus Prophylaxis Required

  • All patients require tetanus vaccination
  • Previously immunized: Booster dose
  • Not immunized: Tetanus immunoglobulin

Adjunctive Therapies Selective

Antibiotics

  • Not routinely recommended
  • Indications: Grade 3-4 injury, severe edema, obvious infection
  • Cover: Strep, Staph, Pseudomonas, gram-negatives, anaerobes

Iloprost (Prostacyclin Mimetic) First-Line

  • Indication: Grade 2-4 frostbite with amputation risk
  • Timing: Within 72 hours post-rewarming (sooner better)
  • Dose: 0.5 ng/kg/min IV, titrate to 2 ng/kg/min
  • Duration: 6 hours/day for 5-8 days
  • Advantage: Lower adverse effects than r-tPA
FDA Approved (US)

Recombinant tPA (Alteplase) Second-Line

  • Indication: Grade 3-4 with likely amputation
  • Timing: Within 24 hours post-thawing
  • Route: IV or intra-arterial (better efficacy)
  • Contraindications: Trauma, recent surgery, recent stroke
  • Note: Use only if iloprost unavailable
High Risk

Wound Care Protocol

Bister Management:
  • Clear blisters: Debride in hospital only (not in field)
  • Hemorrhagic blisters: Keep intact to prevent infection
Topical Therapy: Aloe vera cream/gel applied before dressings
Dressings: Loose circumferential dressings with cotton/gauze padding between digits
Hydrotherapy: Daily 45-minute sessions at 37-39°C with antiseptic solution
Positioning: Elevate and splint affected parts

Hydration

  • Warmed fluids: 37-42°C (98.6-107.6°F)
  • Route: IV or oral depending on condition
  • Field IV: Small rapid boluses (250 mL) to prevent cooling
  • Purpose: Maintain perfusion and prevent thrombosis
Adjunctive

Surgical Management

Timing Principle

Early surgery is rarely indicated. Delay amputation 6-12 weeks to allow demarcation.

Delay Surgery

Early Surgery Indications Exceptions

  • Compartment syndrome (requires fasciotomy)
  • Septicemia/sepsis from infected necrotic tissue
  • Constricting eschar causing ischemia

Delayed Surgery (6-12 weeks) Standard

  • Elective amputation after tissue demarcation
  • Skin grafting for selected severe injuries
  • Reconstructive procedures
Follow-up

Monitoring Protocol

Initial: High suspicion for compartment syndrome
Weekly: Follow-up until wounds stable
Rehab: Consult orthotist, podiatrist, or physiatrist for tissue loss
Imaging: Technetium-99m scans to assess perfusion and response

Complications

Complication Timeframe Likelihood Management
Wound infection Short-term High Broad-spectrum antibiotics
Compartment syndrome Short-term High Emergent fasciotomy
Gangrene (dry/wet) Short-term High Amputation if wet/septic
Joint stiffness/pain Long-term High PT/OT, analgesics
Complex regional pain Long-term High Amitriptyline, gabapentin
Amputation Long-term Medium Delayed 1-3 months
Recurrent frostbite Variable High Strict cold protection
Squamous cell carcinoma Long-term Low Surveillance of chronic ulcers

Prognosis Determination

Timeline: 1-3 months for viability assessment

  • 1st-2nd degree: Typically heal well without hospitalization
  • 3rd-4th degree: May require amputation months later
  • Risk factors for amputation: Late presentation, lower extremity involvement, infection

Secondary Prevention

Patient Education Essential

  • Protect from refreezing and trauma
  • Wear warm, non-restrictive layered clothing
  • Keep extremities dry (avoid perspiration)
  • Avoid alcohol and tobacco in cold environments
  • Trim nails to prevent injury
  • Recognize early frostnip signs
  • Prophylactic NSAIDs may be protective

Rehabilitation

Early Intervention Critical

  • Start early to prevent joint stiffness
  • Physical and occupational therapy
  • High-protein, high-calorie diet for healing
  • Pain management (conventional + amitriptyline/gabapentin for CRPS)
  • Psychological support for amputation patients

Follow-up Schedule

Weekly visits: Wound assessment, dressing changes, therapy adjustment
Bi-weekly: Monitor demarcation, plan surgical interventions if needed
Monthly: Long-term outcomes, rehabilitation progress, complication surveillance
