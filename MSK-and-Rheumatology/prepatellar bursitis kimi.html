Pre-patellar Bursitis

Interactive Management Algorithm for Healthcare Professionals

Last updated: May 2024 Adults 16+ years
Initial Clinical Assessment
Key Information

History Taking

  • Ask about pain, swelling, and redness of the knees
  • Assess difficulty with kneeling or walking
  • Check for fever (more likely in septic bursitis)
  • Evaluate risk factors:
    • History of trauma
    • Occupational/recreational kneeling activities
    • Associated conditions (RA, gout)
    • Immunocompromised state

Physical Examination

Visual Inspection:
Localized swelling over patella, erythema
Palpation:
Warmth, tenderness, fluctuant swelling
Range of Motion:
Generally preserved (unlike arthritis)
Infection Signs:
Fever, tachycardia, progressive worsening
Red Flag: Suspect alternative diagnosis if there is generalized joint swelling or limited/painful knee movement
Investigations & Aspiration
Laboratory Tests

Bursal Fluid Analysis

Fluid Appearance & Interpretation

  • Pus: Likely septic bursitis (but not specific)
  • Straw-coloured: Suggests non-septic bursitis
  • Blood-stained: Trauma, gout, RA, or septic
  • Milky: Tophaceous gout/pseudogout

Laboratory Tests

CRP/ESR:
Usually elevated in septic bursitis
WBC Count:
May not differ between septic/non-septic
Uric Acid:
If crystal arthropathy suspected
Blood Glucose:
Rule out diabetes

Aspiration Guidelines

Aspirate before starting antibiotics to maximize culture yield. May be done in primary care if expertise available.

Diagnostic Criteria

Confirmatory Tests

  • Gram Stain: Immediate bacterial identification
  • Culture: Definitive organism identification
  • Crystal Analysis: Gout/pseudogout confirmation
  • Differential Cell Count: Inflammatory assessment
Septic Bursitis:
Positive culture or Gram stain
Non-septic Bursitis:
Negative cultures, no crystals
Crystal-induced:
Presence of urate/CPPD crystals
Hemorrhagic:
Blood-tinged fluid, trauma history
Important: Coexistence of sepsis and gout is possible. Always send fluid for both culture and crystal analysis.
Infection Risk Assessment
High Risk

Predisposing Factors for Septic Bursitis

Trauma:
Minor or repeated trauma to knees
Pre-existing Disease:
Chronic bursal inflammation
Previous Procedures:
Prior aspiration or injection
Immunocompromise:
Diabetes, steroids, HIV, alcohol

Common Pathogens

Primary: Staphylococcus aureus (80-90%)
Secondary: Streptococci, E. coli, Pseudomonas
Chronic: Atypical mycobacteria, fungi

Conservative Management (Non-septic)
First Line
Non-invasive 2-4 weeks

Initial Conservative Measures

  • Rest: Avoid activities that worsen symptoms
  • Ice: 10 minutes every few hours (use towel barrier)
  • Activity Modification: Avoid kneeling, use protective padding
  • Analgesia: Start with paracetamol, consider NSAIDs
Ice Application:
10 min intervals, several times daily
Protection:
Kneepads, foam cushions
Simple Analgesia:
Paracetamol first line
NSAIDs:
Topical preferred initially

NSAID Prescribing Guidance

QIPP Recommendations: Review NSAID appropriateness, especially in high-risk patients. Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration. Consider PPI co-prescription.
Bursal Aspiration
Procedure Expertise Required

When to Consider Aspiration

  • Large effusion causing discomfort
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Need for pain relief and improved function
  • Reduce bacterial load in suspected infection

Contraindications

Overlying cellulitis, skin lesion, or abrasion may increase infection risk. Ensure aseptic technique.

Septic Bursitis Management
Priority
Antibiotics Required 1-4 weeks

Empirical Antibiotic Therapy

Antibiotic Dosage Duration Notes
Flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS
(1g QDS if >70kg)		 7 days initial First line (covers Staph/Strep)
Clarithromycin 500 mg BD 7 days initial Penicillin allergy alternative
Erythromycin 500 mg QDS 7 days initial Preferred in pregnancy

Management Steps

  • Aspirate bursal fluid using aseptic technique before antibiotics
  • Start empirical antibiotics covering Staph/Strep species
  • Provide conservative measures (rest, ice, protection)
  • Review every few days to monitor response
  • Adjust based on culture sensitivities when available
  • Total duration: 1-4 weeks based on response
Important: If aspiration not possible, start antibiotics and arrange for aspiration at specialist center
Corticosteroid Injection
Caution Specialist Only

When to Consider

  • No response to conservative measures ± aspiration
  • Non-septic bursitis confirmed (2 months)
  • Available expertise and experience
  • No contraindications present

Contraindications

  • Suspected or confirmed sepsis
  • Signs of inflammation over injection site
  • Surgical scar or abnormal skin
  • Lack of sterile technique capability
Not recommended in primary care due to significant risk of introducing infection
Follow-up Schedule
Regular Monitoring Primary Care

Review Timeline

  • Septic Bursitis: Review every few days initially
  • Initial Assessment: 7 days after starting treatment
  • Non-septic: Re-assess at 2 weeks if no improvement
  • Referral Threshold: No response after 2 months conservative treatment
Improvement Signs:
Reduced swelling, pain, erythema
Warning Signs:
Increasing pain, spreading inflammation
Repeat Aspiration:
If swelling recurs
Resolution:
Typically 1-4 weeks for septic cases
Referral Criteria
Urgent Specialist

Urgent Admission Criteria

Same Day Admission

  • Suspected septic joint (limited ROM)
  • Sepsis or systemic toxicity signs
  • High fever with severe infection
  • Extensive cellulitis
  • Pointing abscess requiring drainage

Specialist Referral

  • No response to antibiotics: Consider IV antibiotics or drainage
  • No aspiration expertise: Refer for bursal fluid analysis
  • Recurrent septic bursitis: May need surgical excision
  • Non-septic not responding: Corticosteroid injection or surgery
  • Immunocompromised patients: Lower threshold for referral
