Red Flags & Emergency Actions
Immediate Concerns
  • Severe hypocalcaemia - Monitor ECG for QT prolongation
  • Pathological fractures - Immobilize and refer to orthopedics
  • Severe muscle weakness - Risk of falls, especially in elderly
  • Renal failure - Check for CKD-MBD complications

Key Facts
Definition

Metabolic bone disease characterised by incomplete mineralisation of the underlying mature organic bone matrix (osteoid) following growth plate closure in adults.

Epidemiology
  • Vitamin D deficiency in >40% of adults in US, Europe, and East Asia
  • Post-mortem prevalence up to 25% in European adults
  • Up to 33% of patients develop osteomalacia after gastrectomy
Primary Causes
Vitamin D deficiency

At-Risk Populations
  • Elderly with limited sunlight exposure
  • Post-gastrectomy or bariatric surgery patients
  • Patients on anticonvulsant therapy
  • Those with malabsorption syndromes

Additional Tests
  • Bone X-rays - Look for Looser pseudofractures (milkman fractures)
  • DXA scan - Low bone mass (cannot distinguish from osteoporosis)
  • 24-hour urinary phosphate - High in patients with renal phosphate wasting
  • Bone biopsy - Definitive test but rarely necessary
Diagnostic Threshold

A 25-hydroxyvitamin D concentration of <25 nanomol/L (<10 nanogram/mL) is the threshold regarded internationally as defining the risk of osteomalacia.

Treatment
1
First-line: Calcium plus Vitamin D

Ergocalciferol or Colecalciferol: 50,000 units orally once or twice weekly for 6-12 weeks; or 5000 to 10,000 units orally once daily for 6-12 weeks

Calcium carbonate or citrate: 1-2 g/day orally given in 3-4 divided doses

2
Second-line: Calcium plus Vitamin D Analogue

Calcitriol: 0.25 to 1 microgram orally once daily

Calcium carbonate or citrate: 1-2 g/day orally given in 3-4 divided doses

Special considerations: Patients taking anticonvulsant medications, glucocorticoids, or other drugs that activate steroid and xenobiotic receptors require higher doses of vitamin D. The goal is to achieve concentrations of 25-hydroxyvitamin D at about the 75-150 nmol/L (30-60 ng/mL) range.
1
X-linked Hypophosphataemia or Oncogenic Osteomalacia

Burosumab: X-linked hypophosphataemia: 1 mg/kg subcutaneously every 4 weeks initially, adjust dose according to serum phosphate levels

Oncogenic osteomalacia: 0.5 mg/kg subcutaneously every 4 weeks initially, adjust dose according to serum phosphate levels

2
Alternative Treatment

Calcitriol: X-linked hypophosphataemia: 0.5 to 0.75 micrograms/day orally given in 2 divided doses

Sodium phosphate/potassium phosphate: 250-1000 mg/day orally given in 3-4 divided doses

Calcium carbonate or citrate: 1-2 g/day orally given in 3-4 divided doses

Management of CKD-MBD
  • Maintenance of normal calcium and phosphate levels
  • Treatment with calcitriol
  • Use of phosphate-binding agents
  • Avoidance of aluminium-containing phosphate binders
Warning: Hyperphosphataemia directly induces hypocalcaemia and decreases the efficacy of 1-alpha-hydroxylase in the kidney, which decreases the levels of the active vitamin D metabolites.
Follow-up & Monitoring
Monitoring Parameters
  • Serum calcium concentration
  • 25-hydroxyvitamin D concentration
  • Urinary calcium excretion
  • Bone density (DXA)
  • Symptom improvement
Expected Timeline
  • Muscle strength improvement: Within weeks
  • Bone tenderness reduction: Within weeks
  • Radiographic healing: 6-12 months
  • Bone density improvement: 1-2 years

Quick Reference Summary
Key Points
  • Osteomalacia is most commonly caused by vitamin D deficiency
  • Patients frequently complain of diffuse bony pain with limited sunlight exposure
  • Diagnosis: Low 25-hydroxyvitamin D with low/normal calcium and elevated PTH
  • Treatment: Increased sunlight exposure, oral vitamin D, and calcium replacement
Remember
Severe 25-hydroxyvitamin D deficiency <25 nanomol/L (10 nanogram/mL) is the threshold for osteomalacia risk
Successful treatment is possible once the underlying cause is addressed
