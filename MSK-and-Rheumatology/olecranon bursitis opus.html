Step 1: Assessment

Clinical history and physical examination

Clinical History

Key Questions
  • Onset & Duration: When did symptoms begin? Acute (hours to days) or chronic?
  • Trauma History: Direct blow to elbow? Repeated minor trauma? Skin abrasion/laceration?
  • Occupation: Gardener, mechanic, plumber, student? Prolonged elbow pressure?
  • Sports Activity: Gymnastics, weightlifting, rugby, football, hockey?
  • Systemic Conditions: Gout, rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes, immunocompromised state?
  • Prior Episodes: Previous bursitis, aspiration, or corticosteroid injection?
Risk Factors for Septic Bursitis
  • Trauma to the elbow (including minor or repeated trauma)
  • Pre-existing bursal disease
  • Prior aspiration and infiltration of the bursa
  • Impaired immunity (systemic corticosteroids, diabetes, HIV, alcohol abuse)
  • Skin abrasion or penetrating injury

Clinical Note

S. aureus causes 80-90% of septic olecranon bursitis cases. Streptococci are the second most common causative organism.

Physical Examination

Classic Presentation
  • Localized swelling over the olecranon process (posterior elbow)
  • Fluctuant mass â€“ movable and compressible
  • May be tender or warm, but can be painless
  • Movement at elbow joint is painless except at full flexion (when bursa compressed)
  • Swelling develops over hours to days
  • Evidence of local skin abrasion may be present
Red Flags for Septic Bursitis

Suspect Septic Bursitis If:

These features indicate possible infection requiring prompt assessment and treatment.

  • Increased tenderness or painful, red, hot swelling that is progressively worsening
  • Local cellulitis â€“ spreading erythema around the bursa
  • Abrasion or laceration over the bursa
  • Fever â€“ systemic signs of infection
  • Immunocompromised state
  • Tachycardia, low blood pressure, change in mental status
  • Seeking medical help soon after onset of swelling

Step 2: Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis and differentiate septic from non-septic

Differential Diagnosis

Conditions to Consider
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Whole joint inflamed, nodules may be firm without fluctuance, other joints affected
  • Septic arthritis: Joint swollen (no localized bursal swelling), limited painful ROM on examination
  • Gout/Pseudogout: History of other joint involvement, crystals on aspiration
  • Cellulitis: May coexist with septic bursitis
  • Tennis elbow: Tenderness over lateral epicondyle at extensor carpi radialis brevis origin
  • Trauma: Fracture, tendon or ligament injury â€“ history usually indicative
  • Bone/soft tissue tumours: Rapid expansion, invasive skin changes, weight loss (rare)

Key Differentiator

Suspect an alternative diagnosis if there is generalized joint swelling, restricted range of elbow movement, or the arm is held in flexion.

When to Aspirate

Indications for Aspiration
  • If septic bursitis is suspected from clinical features
  • To rule out infection in persistent cases of aseptic bursitis
  • To provide pain relief by reducing bursal pressure
  • Ideally done before starting antibiotics
Fluid Appearance Interpretation
Fluid Appearance Likely Diagnosis
Pus Septic bursitis likely (but RA can also produce pus)
Straw-coloured Non-septic bursitis more likely
Blood-stained Septic bursitis, trauma, gout, or RA
Milky Tophaceous gout/pseudogout; rarely RA
Laboratory Analysis
  • Send bursal fluid in a sterile container for:
    • Gram staining
    • Culture and sensitivity
    • Crystal examination (urate/calcium pyrophosphate)
  • Consider differential white cell count and glucose levels if Gram stain/culture negative
  • Urate crystals = definitive diagnosis of gout (but sepsis and gout can coexist)

Consider X-ray If:

Bony pathology suspected (fracture, underlying joint disease, significant trauma history, or very rapid swelling)

Step 3: Treatment

Management of non-septic and septic bursitis

Non-Septic Bursitis STABLE

Conservative Measures (First-Line)
  • Rest & Reduced Activity: Avoid trauma and direct pressure to elbows
  • Protective Elbow Pads: Use if activity cannot be avoided
  • Ice: Apply for 10 minutes every few hours (not directly on skin â€“ use thin towel)
  • Compressive Bandaging: Elasticated tubular bandage if tolerated
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol or NSAID (e.g., ibuprofen) for pain relief

Reassurance

Most patients respond well to conservative treatment without the need for surgery. Advise to re-attend if symptoms worsen.

Aspiration (Second-Line)
  • Consider if expertise and equipment are available
  • Improves function and comfort, particularly for large effusions
  • No response to conservative measures alone
Corticosteroid Injection (Third-Line)
  • Only if confident bursitis is non-septic
  • Requires expertise and experience with the procedure
  • No response to conservative measures and/or aspiration

Contraindications to Injection

Signs of inflammation, surgical scar, or abnormal skin at the injection site. Refer to specialist if skills not available or infection risk present.

Management Steps
  1. Aspirate bursal fluid using aseptic technique
  2. Start empirical antibiotics (cover Staph and Strep)
  3. Send fluid for Gram stain, culture, and sensitivities
  4. Conservative measures and analgesia
  5. Review every few days to monitor effectiveness
  6. Adjust antibiotic according to sensitivities
  7. Consider repeated aspiration if symptoms recur

Step 4: Follow-up & Referral

Monitoring, admission criteria, and specialist referral

Urgent Admission Criteria IMMEDIATE

Admit Urgently If:

  • Suspected septic joint (limited ROM of elbow unlike septic bursitis)
  • Septic bursitis with severe infection or systemic toxicity including high fever
Same-Day Admission

Admit same day if septic bursitis AND:

  • Extensive cellulitis
  • Pointing abscess requiring incision and drainage (if expertise unavailable in primary care)
  • Immunocompromised patient
  • Other comorbid conditions (diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis)

Referral Indications

Urgent/Specialist Referral
  • No response to antibiotic in septic bursitis â€“ may need change in antibiotic, IV antibiotic, or incision and drainage
  • Aspiration needed but no expertise in primary care
  • Recurrent septic bursitis â€“ surgical excision may be required (only after infection cleared)
Routine Referral
  • Non-septic bursitis not responding after 2 months of conservative measures (refer sooner if significant discomfort)
  • Corticosteroid injection or surgical management may be beneficial

Prognosis & Prevention

Expected Outcomes
  • Most cases resolve without complications
  • Non-septic bursitis: typically benign course with conservative treatment
  • Septic bursitis: usually resolves with aspiration and antibiotics (average ~5 weeks, can take up to 20 weeks)
  • Aspiration alone: 50% settle in 2 weeks, 25% have effusion at 8 weeks, 10% persistent at 6 months
  • Recurrence possible especially with recurrent minor trauma

Prevention Advice

Modify occupational/sports activities and use protective elbow pads to prevent recurrence. Pain settles faster with traumatic bursitis than with crystal-induced or septic bursitis.

Potential Complications

Septic olecranon bursitis poses greatest complication risk:

  • Fistulae after spontaneous rupture or I&D
  • Sinus tract development after aspiration
  • Secondary septic arthritis (rare â€“ documented in RA patients)
  • Bacteraemia and toxic shock syndrome
  • Sepsis in immunocompromised patients
  • Osteomyelitis of olecranon process (persistent infection)
  • Necrotizing fasciitis
