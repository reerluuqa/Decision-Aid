üö® Immediate Assessment
üö®
EMERGENCY REFERRAL REQUIRED IF: Unconscious, reduced consciousness, respiratory depression, hypotension, tachycardia/bradycardia, seizures, hypothermia/hyperthermia, or deteriorating condition.

ABCDE Approach

  • Airway: Remove obstructions, assess for corrosive injury
  • Breathing: Measure respiratory rate, SpO‚ÇÇ (note: may be unreliable with poor perfusion or COHb/MetHb)
  • Circulation: Pulse, BP, temperature
  • Disability: Glasgow Coma Scale assessment
  • Exposure: Look for track marks, skin blisters, evidence of ingestion
üìã Detailed History

Substance Details

  • What substance was taken?
  • Amount ingested (estimate in tablets/strips if possible)
  • Timing of ingestion (critical for paracetamol)
  • Route (oral, inhalation, injection)
  • Other substances/alcohol taken?

Context & Risk

  • Accidental vs deliberate?
  • Risk of repetition?
  • Psychiatric history?
  • Suicidal intent?
  • Safeguarding concerns?
‚ö†Ô∏è
NOTE: History may be unreliable. People may estimate quantities as "handfuls" or "mouthfuls" leading to inconsistencies with lab tests.
ü©∫ Examination

Neurological

  • Pupil size & reaction (dilated = anticholinergic/sympathomimetic; pinpoint = opioid/cholinergic)
  • Eye movements (strabismus, nystagmus)
  • Movement disorders (dystonia, hypertonia, hyperreflexia)
  • Level of consciousness

General Observations

  • Track marks (IV drug use)
  • Breath odor (alcohol/solvents)
  • Skin blisters (prolonged unconsciousness)
  • Signs of liver disease
  • Atypical bruising (safeguarding)
üß† Capacity Assessment
‚öñÔ∏è
Mental Capacity Act (2005) - 5 Key Principles: Presumption of capacity | Support decision-making | Unwise decisions allowed | Best interests | Least restrictive option
Capacity Assessment Questions ‚ñº
  • Does the person have impairment of mind/brain function?
  • Are they unable to make the specific decision needed?
  • Is the inability due to the impairment?
  • Can they understand relevant information?
  • Can they retain that information?
  • Can they weigh information in decision-making?
  • Can they communicate their decision?
‚è±Ô∏è
EMERGENCY: In life-threatening emergencies, capacity assessment can be brief. It's almost always in best interests to give urgent treatment without delay.

üéØ Common Presentations
üí°
Think poisoning in: Altered consciousness, acute confusion, hypoglycemia, seizures, abnormal LFTs, unexplained bleeding, multiple people with similar symptoms (CO poisoning), recurrent unexplained symptoms in children.

üë∂ Children

  • Usually accidental exploration (<6 years)
  • Found with tablets/chemicals nearby
  • May present with altered behavior before diagnosis known
  • Adolescents: often disclosure to others
  • Unwitnessed: present when symptoms develop

üë¥ Adults

  • Most common: deliberate self-harm
  • Circumstantial evidence (empty containers, suicide note)
  • Drug misuse experimentation (adolescents/young adults)
  • Accidental overdose (medication errors)
  • Occupational exposure

Treatment
‚ñº
üö® When to Admit to Hospital
üè•
ALWAYS ADMIT: Deliberate self-poisoning, unconscious/reduced consciousness, symptomatic patients, unknown substances, delayed-action drugs (paracetamol, aspirin, iron, TCAs, modified-release preparations)

RED FLAG Immediate Transfer

  • Unconscious/reduced consciousness
  • Respiratory depression (RR <12)
  • SpO‚ÇÇ <94% on air
  • Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)
  • Significant tachycardia/bradycardia
  • Irregular pulse
  • Hypothermia/hyperthermia
  • Seizures
  • Deteriorating condition

CAUTION Consider Admission

  • Symptomatic (beyond mild GI upset)
  • Delayed-action drugs ingested
  • High-risk medications (even if asymptomatic)
  • Children, pregnant women
  • Significant comorbidities
  • Multiple drug ingestion
  • Unknown substance
‚ö†Ô∏è
HIGH-RISK MEDICATIONS IN CHILDREN: Methadone, opioids, TCAs, antipsychotics, calcium-channel blockers, beta-blockers, flecainide, clonidine, oral hypoglycemics - ingestion of 1-2 adult doses can be fatal in toddlers.
üè† Managing Without Admission
‚úÖ
Only if LOW TOXICITY substance + Asymptomatic/Mild GI upset only

Home Management

  • Drink small glass of water/milk/juice for mild GI upset
  • Provide NPIS "Low toxicity substances" leaflet
  • Clear return advice: worsening or new symptoms ‚Üí seek care
  • Follow TOXBASE guidance for specific substance

Accidental Extra Tablet

  • Assess total dose vs maximum/toxic dose
  • Consider renal/hepatic impairment
  • Consult BNF/TOXBASE for individual drug advice
  • Seek UKMI/SPS advice if uncertain
  • Restart medication when appropriate
üíä
IMPORTANT: If >2 cardiotoxic agents taken (e.g., beta-blocker + calcium-channel blocker) ‚Üí hospital assessment regardless of dose.
üö´ Managing Refusal of Admission
‚öñÔ∏è
Capacity Assessment Essential: If lacks capacity AND has mental disorder ‚Üí consider Mental Health Act. If has capacity ‚Üí right to refuse, but document thoroughly and seek advice.
Refusal Management Steps ‚ñº
  1. Determine reason for refusal
  2. Explain potential consequences
  3. Assess capacity using Mental Capacity Act principles
  4. If lacks capacity + mental disorder: consider MHA assessment (Section 2/3)
  5. If has capacity: respect autonomy but:
    • Persuade to attend/treat
    • Involve family/carers (with consent)
    • Seek NPIS/A&E/psychiatry advice
    • Document clearly against medical advice
    • Follow up and reassess
üè• Secondary Care Management

Investigations

  • Bloods: FBC, U&E, LFT, glucose, clotting, ABGs
  • Toxicology: Paracetamol, salicylate levels
  • Drug levels: Lithium, digoxin, iron, theophylline
  • ECG: For cardiotoxic drugs
  • Imaging: If indicated

Elimination Techniques

  • Activated charcoal: Within 1 hour (up to 2 hours for some drugs)
  • Multiple-dose charcoal: Carbamazepine, phenobarbital, theophylline
  • Alkalinization: Salicylate poisoning
  • Hemodialysis: Lithium, ethylene glycol, methanol, salicylates
  • Whole bowel irrigation: Sustained-release preparations, iron, lithium
üíâ
ANTIDOTES: N-acetylcysteine (paracetamol) | Naloxone (opioids) | Flumazenil (benzodiazepines) | Glucagon (beta-blockers/CCBs) | Desferrioxamine (iron)

Follow-up
‚ñº
üß† Psychiatric Assessment
üß†
ALL deliberate self-poisoning requires psychosocial assessment by trained specialist (mental health worker/social worker) after physical treatment.

Assessment Components

  • Events preceding self-harm
  • Reasons for overdose
  • Suicidal intent assessment
  • Current difficulties & life issues
  • Substance/alcohol misuse
  • Psychiatric disorder screening
  • Risk factors (socioeconomic, employment)
  • Psychiatric & self-harm history
  • Social supports & coping resources

Admission Criteria

  • Serious psychiatric disorder present
  • High suicide risk identified
  • Requires treatment of psychiatric condition
  • Children <16 years: admit overnight to pediatric ward
  • Age 16-17: individual assessment for overnight admission
üìã Post-Discharge Follow-up
üìÖ
Mutually agreed follow-up plan essential: Ensure ongoing management of psychosocial needs and risk of further self-harm.

Follow-up Plan

  • Primary care review within 1 week
  • Mental health follow-up as indicated
  • Substance misuse services if needed
  • Social services involvement
  • Clear safety planning
  • Crisis contact numbers provided

Prevention Strategies

  • Medication review (dose, quantity prescribed)
  • Supervised medication administration if needed
  • Consider dosette boxes or blister packs
  • Address underlying causes of error (poor memory, confusion)
  • Education about drug interactions
  • Limit access to lethal means

üõ°Ô∏è
Vulnerable Groups: Consider safeguarding for all ages - children, adolescents, vulnerable adults, older people.

üßí Children

  • Repeated accidental ingestions ‚Üí consider neglect
  • Deliberate poisoning in children ‚Üí consider fabricated/induced illness
  • Adolescent self-harm ‚Üí assess family dynamics
  • Ensure safe home environment

üßë Vulnerable Adults

  • Assess capacity and support needs
  • Consider carer stress/burnout
  • Adult social care referral if needed
  • Address domestic abuse if suspected
üìä Quality Standards (NICE)
‚≠ê
People who self-harm are cared for with compassion, respect, and dignity, receiving comprehensive psychosocial assessment.

Key Quality Points

  • Initial assessment of physical health, mental state, safeguarding, social circumstances
  • Risk assessment for repetition/suicide
  • Comprehensive psychosocial assessment by specialist
  • Respect, dignity, and compassion in all interactions
