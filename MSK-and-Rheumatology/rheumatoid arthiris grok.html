Assessment & Red Flags

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Medical Emergency: GCA is a medical emergency. Early treatment prevents vision loss.
Core Clinical Features Urgent ‚ñº

Suspect GCA if age ‚â•50 years with ONE or more of:

  • New-onset headache: Temporal, occipital, or generalized (occurs in ~2/3 of patients)
  • Temporal artery abnormality: Tenderness, thickening, nodularity, reduced/absent pulse (present in ~30%)
  • Visual disturbances: Transient/permanent vision loss, diplopia, field defects (occurs in up to 30%)
  • Scalp tenderness: Especially temporal/occipital areas (~50%)
  • Jaw claudication: Pain on chewing (nearly 50%)
  • Systemic features: Low-grade fever, fatigue, weight loss, depression
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica features: Proximal muscle pain/stiffness (~40%)
Additional Features Caution ‚ñº
  • Neurological features (30%): Mononeuropathy, TIA/stroke, cranial nerve palsies
  • Large vessel involvement: Bruits, limb claudication, unequal BP between arms
  • Respiratory/ENT: Cough, sore throat, hoarseness, audio-vestibular symptoms

Diagnosis & Differential

‚ñº
Primary Care Investigations ‚ñº

Do NOT delay referral for tests. Perform simultaneously:

  • Full Blood Count: Normocytic anemia, thrombocytosis common
  • CRP: Typically elevated
  • ESR: Often >50mm/hr (but may be normal)
  • Additional if indicated: U&E, HbA1c, LFTs, bone profile

Treatment & Referral

‚ñº
With Visual Symptoms Same-Day ‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Emergency Protocol:
  • Same-day ophthalmology assessment
  • IV Methylprednisolone 1g daily for 3 days (if available)
  • OR Oral Prednisolone 60-100mg daily for 3 days if IV not possible
  • DO NOT delay treatment for investigations
Without Visual Symptoms Within 3 Days ‚ñº
  • Urgent specialist discussion (rheumatology)
  • Start Prednisolone 40-60mg daily immediately
  • Specialist assessment: Same day if possible, within 3 working days
  • Continue prednisolone until reviewed
‚ö†Ô∏è Fast-track referral pathway required
Adjunctive Therapy Consider ‚ñº

Consider if high risk of glucocorticoid toxicity:

  • Methotrexate: Glucocorticoid-sparing agent
  • Tocilizumab: IL-6 inhibitor (specialist initiated)
  • Aspirin: Consider low-dose for vascular protection

Follow-up & Monitoring

‚ñº
Review Schedule Routine ‚ñº
First 6 months: Every 2-8 weeks
Second 6 months: Every 12 weeks
Second year: Every 12-24 weeks
Post-treatment: As needed

Each review includes:

  • Symptoms of disease control/relapse
  • Targeted examination
  • FBC, ESR, CRP monitoring
  • Adverse effects assessment
  • Blood pressure & glucose monitoring
Relapse Management Action Required ‚ñº

Relapse = Return of symptoms ¬± raised inflammatory markers

Major Relapse (Ischemic): Jaw/tongue claudication, visual symptoms
  • Urgent specialist discussion
  • Increase to high-dose steroids (similar to initial treatment)
  • Consider adjunctive therapy
Minor Relapse (Non-ischemic): Headache recurrence only
  • Increase steroid dose to last effective dose
  • Discuss with specialist
Steroid Tapering Gradual ‚ñº

Typical Duration: 1-2 years (may be longer)

Example Tapering Schedule:
Weeks 1-4: 40-60mg daily
Weeks 5-8: Reduce by 10mg every 2 weeks
Weeks 9-12: Reduce by 5mg every 2-4 weeks
Weeks 13+: Slower reduction (1mg/month) below 10mg
üí° Patient Advice: Provide steroid card, warn about abrupt cessation, infection risk, and symptoms requiring urgent review
