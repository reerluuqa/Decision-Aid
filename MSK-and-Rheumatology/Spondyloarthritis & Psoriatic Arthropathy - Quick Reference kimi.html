Osgood-Schlatter Disease

Interactive Clinical Algorithm for Primary Care Management

Assessment

Patient History
  • Age: 10-15 years (boys), 8-13 years (girls)
  • Activity: High-impact sports (running, jumping)
  • Symptoms: Anterior knee pain, swelling
  • Onset: Gradual, activity-related
  • Duration: Weeks to months
Typical Age Range
Physical Examination
  • Tibial tubercle: Tenderness, prominence, swelling
  • Range of motion: Usually normal, pain with resisted extension
  • Gait: Antalgic if severe
  • Quadriceps: Tightness common
Atypical Exam → Consider imaging

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

Osgood-Schlatter disease is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on:

  • Typical age and activity level
  • Characteristic anterior knee pain
  • Tibial tubercle tenderness/swelling
  • Symptoms exacerbated by activity
Clinical Dx Usually Sufficient
Imaging Indications

X-ray if atypical features or diagnostic uncertainty:

  • Soft tissue swelling over tibial tubercle
  • Fragmentation of tubercle apophysis
  • Irregularity of ossification center

Consider ultrasound for soft tissue assessment

Red Flags → Immediate Imaging
Differential Diagnosis
  • Sinding-Larsen-Johansson: Inferior patellar pole pain
  • Patellar tendinopathy: Older adolescents/adults
  • Hoffa's fat pad syndrome: Infrapatellar tenderness
  • Septic arthritis: Systemic symptoms, inability to bear weight
  • Tumor: Night pain, systemic signs
  • Fracture: History of trauma

Treatment

Activity Modification
Mild: Continue activity with pain monitoring Moderate: Reduce high-impact activities Severe: Temporary cessation of sports
  • Avoid activities that cause significant pain
  • Cross-training with low-impact exercises (swimming, cycling)
  • Gradual return-to-play protocol
  • Pain monitoring scale (0-10)
Pain Management

First-line: NSAIDs

  • Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg every 6-8 hours (max 2400 mg/day)
  • Naproxen 10 mg/kg every 12 hours (max 1000 mg/day)
  • Take with food, use shortest effective duration

Alternative: Topical NSAIDs or acetaminophen

NSAIDs Caution: GI, renal, cardiovascular risk
Physical Therapy
  • Stretching: Quadriceps, hamstrings, hip flexors
  • Strengthening: Eccentric quads, core stability
  • Manual therapy: Soft tissue mobilization
  • Modalities: Ice post-activity (15-20 min)
  • Taping/bracing: Patellar tendon unloading
PT is Mainstay of Treatment
Interventional Options

Rarely required, consider only if refractory:

  • Corticosteroid injection: NOT recommended (risk of tendon rupture, skin atrophy)
  • Platelet-rich plasma: Limited evidence in adolescents
  • Surgery: Refractory cases after skeletal maturity
Avoid Steroid Injections

Follow-up

Expected Course
  • Self-limited condition: Resolves with skeletal maturity
  • Duration: 12-24 months typical
  • Recurrence: Common with growth spurts
  • Residual: Prominent tibial tubercle may persist
Benign, Self-Limiting Course
Complications to Monitor
  • Avulsion fracture: Sudden severe pain, inability to extend knee
  • Chronic pain: Persistent beyond skeletal maturity
  • Genu recurvatum: Growth disturbance (rare)
  • Tibial tubercle ossicle: Persistent prominence
When to Refer
Immediate: Acute avulsion fracture Routine: Refractory cases >12 months Orthopedics: Persistent pain after maturity
  • Atypical presentation or diagnostic uncertainty
  • Failure of conservative management after 6-12 months
  • Severe functional limitation
  • Neurological signs or symptoms
Patient Education
  • Reassurance: Benign condition with excellent prognosis
  • Activity: Pain-guided activity modification, not total rest
  • Timeline: Symptoms may persist 1-2 years
  • Return to sport: When pain-free with daily activities
  • Stretching: Daily quadriceps flexibility program
Education is Key to Compliance
