Achilles Tendinopathy

Quick Clinical Reference Tool

RED FLAG: Achilles Tendon Rupture

Arrange admission or same-day referral to orthopaedics if rupture suspected

Signs of Achilles Tendon Rupture

  • Sudden pain in back of leg with audible snap
  • Difficulty weight bearing
  • Positive Simmonds triad:
    • Abnormal angle of declination
    • Gap in tendon
    • Positive calf squeeze test (no foot movement)
Immediate Referral

Diagnosis of Achilles Tendinopathy

Mid-portion (60% of cases)

  • Pain 2-6 cm above insertion
  • Tenderness on palpation
  • Thickening or nodularity

Insertional (25% of cases)

  • Pain at calcaneus insertion
  • Swelling at insertion
  • More common in older people
VISA-A Questionnaire

Risk Factors

Intrinsic Factors

Biomechanical abnormalities Diabetes Hypercholesterolaemia Hyperuricaemia Inflammatory disorders Age

Extrinsic Factors

Previous injuries Inappropriate footwear Training errors Corticosteroids Quinolone antibiotics Statins

Primary Care Management

  • Address underlying causes
  • Cold packs for acute injury
  • Paracetamol for pain relief
  • NSAIDs for acute phase only
  • Rest and reduce aggravating activities
  • Refer to physiotherapy if no improvement in 7-10 days
Do NOT inject corticosteroids

When to Refer

Condition Specialist Timeline
Achilles tendon rupture Orthopaedics (emergency) Immediate
Symptoms not improving after 7-10 days Physiotherapy Within 2 weeks
Chronic tendinopathy or failure of conservative measures Sports medicine or Orthopaedics After 3 months of conservative treatment

Differential Diagnosis

  • Retrocalcaneal bursitis
  • Plantaris tendinopathy
  • Peroneal tendon dislocation
  • Posterior ankle impingement
  • Haglund's deformity
  • Os trigonum syndrome
  • Calcaneal fracture
  • Sural nerve irritation
