Ganglion Cyst Clinical Reference

Red Flag: Neurovascular compromise requires immediate surgical resection. Look for paraesthesia, muscle weakness, or vascular insufficiency.

Key Facts

Most common benign lesion of the hand/wrist
Typically insidious onset with no predisposing conditions
Usually cosmetic problem but may cause local pain and neurovascular compression
No reported malignant transformation

Epidemiology

Female to male ratio: 3:1 (adults), 1.4:1 to 1.8:1 (children)
Usually affects patients in 2nd to 4th decades
Dorsal ganglions: 60-70% of cases
Volar ganglions: remainder of cases

Classification

Volar: Between flexor carpi radialis tendon and radial artery
Dorsal: Usually connected to scapholunate ligament
Occult: Vague dorsal wrist pain with wrist flexion

Red Flags

Neurovascular compression
Paraesthesia in hand/fingers
Muscle weakness
Coolness of hand/fingers

Key Diagnostic Factors

Subcutaneous wrist mass
Wrist pain
Non-tender mass
Increased mass size after activity
Trans-illuminating mass
Risk factors present

Investigations

Test Indication Result
Clinical diagnosis Initial assessment Usually sufficient
Cyst aspiration Dorsal ganglia only Thick, clear, jelly-like fluid
Ultrasound Uncertain diagnosis Cystic structure
MRI Occult ganglia Fluid-filled cyst

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiators
Carpal tunnel syndrome Night-time paraesthesia, positive Tinel sign
Lipoma Does not trans-illuminate, no fluid on aspiration
Giant cell tumour Solid mass, fixed to tendon sheath
Radial artery aneurysm Pulsatile mass, Doppler flow on ultrasound

Diagnostic Tips

Trans-illumination with a penlight often reveals a reddish glow
Dorsal cysts are usually located near and distal to the Lister tubercle
Volar cysts are usually located between the flexor carpi radialis tendon and the radial artery
Mass is typically 1-4 cm in diameter, but can reach up to 8 cm

Observation

Most appropriate initial line of treatment
Spontaneous resolution in up to 58% of adults and 93% of children
Activity modification to avoid painful wrist positions
Volar wrist splinting for pain control

Analgesia

NSAIDs for discomfort or aching
Compressive wraps or wrist supports during activities
Options: Naproxen 250-500mg twice daily, Diclofenac 50mg 2-3x daily, Ibuprofen 300-400mg every 6-8 hours

Cyst Aspiration

Primary form of closed management for dorsal ganglia
Not recommended for volar ganglia (risk of radial artery injury)
Success rate: 13% with single puncture, 40% with splinting, 85% with multiple treatments
Recurrence rate: 59%

Surgical Excision

Most successful treatment option
Recommended for neurovascular compromise
Options: Open excision vs. arthroscopic decompression
Recurrence rate: 3-9% (dorsal), 7-19% (volar)

Treatment Algorithm

1
Initial Assessment
Evaluate for neurovascular compromise. If present, proceed directly to surgical resection.
Diagnosis Red Flag Assessment
2
Conservative Management
Observation with activity modification and analgesia as needed. Spontaneous resolution occurs in up to 58% of adults.
First-line Non-invasive
3
Cyst Aspiration (Dorsal Ganglia Only)
Puncture and drainage with or without corticosteroid injection. Success rate increases with multiple treatments.
Second-line Not for volar cysts
4
Surgical Excision
Indicated for persistent symptoms, cosmetic concerns, or neurovascular compromise. Options include open excision or arthroscopic decompression.
Definitive treatment Low recurrence
5
Follow-up
Monitor for recurrence. No specific schedule required unless symptoms persist or worsen.
Long-term

Complications

Complication Risk Prevention
Radial artery injury Low (volar aspiration) Avoid volar ganglion aspiration
Septic arthritis Low Sterile technique during aspiration
Wrist stiffness Low Early mobilization post-surgery
Wrist instability Low Careful resection of scapholunate ligament

Prognosis

No reported cases of malignant transformation
Conservative management: spontaneous resolution in up to 58% of adults and 93% of children
Cyst aspiration: success rate up to 85% with multiple treatments
Surgical excision: recurrence rate 3-19% depending on location
