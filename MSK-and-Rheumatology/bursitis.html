Bursitis Quick Reference

Clinical Decision Support Tool • Updated: May 10, 2024
RED FLAGS: SUSPECT SEPTIC BURSITIS
  • Low-grade fever + local erythema/warmth/swelling
  • Purulent fluid on aspiration
  • History of penetrating injury
  • Immunocompromised patients

Common Presentation

  • Localized pain over bursa
  • Tenderness to palpation
  • Decreased active ROM (passive preserved)
  • Swelling if superficial bursa
Prepatellar ("Housemaid's Knee")
Olecranon ("Student's Elbow")
Trochanteric
Subacromial
Retrocalcaneal

Septic Indicators

Low-grade fever
Erythema
Warmth
Purulent fluid
Diagnostic Actions:
  • Aspirate for Gram stain & culture
  • Crystal analysis if gout history
  • X-ray to rule out osteomyelitis

Treatment Algorithm

1
Non-septic bursitis:
Activity modification, rest, ice
Paracetamol/NSAIDs, topical NSAIDs
2
Non-septic refractory:
Corticosteroid injection
(Avoid in retrocalcaneal bursa)
1
Septic bursitis:
Aspiration + antibiotics
(Dicloxacillin/Cefazolin/Vancomycin)
3
Refractory cases:
Bursectomy (last resort)

Antibiotic Therapy

  • Dicloxacillin 125-250mg QID
  • Cefazolin 500-1500mg IV/IM Q6-8H
  • Oxacillin 500-1000mg IV/IM Q6-8H
  • Clarithromycin 500mg BID (Penicillin allergy)
    • Duration: 1-4 weeks
    MRSA: Vancomycin 1g IV Q12H

    Risk Factors

    Repetitive trauma Kneeling occupations Gout/CPPD RA Hip OA Leg length discrepancy Poor footwear

    Key Differentials

    Condition Differentiating Feature
    Medial meniscopathy Positive McMurray test
    Knee osteoarthritis Joint space narrowing on X-ray
    Rotator cuff tear Weakness with movement
    Lumbar radiculopathy Pain radiates below knee
    Haglund's deformity Posterior calcaneal spur
