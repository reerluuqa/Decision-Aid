Critical Information

Urgent Referral Required: Refer people with Dupuytren's contracture and/or significant loss of function to a hand surgeon or specialist in plastic or orthopaedic surgery.

When to Refer Immediately

  • Contracture causing functional limitations
  • Inability to lay palm flat on tabletop (positive tabletop test)
  • Rapid progression of disease
  • Significant pain affecting daily activities
  • Ectopic lesions (knuckle pads, feet, penis)

Red Flags

  • Early onset (before age 40)
  • Bilateral involvement
  • Radial digit involvement
  • Family history of aggressive disease
  • Multiple affected digits
Note: These factors suggest aggressive disease (Dupuytren's diathesis)

Assessment

Key Clinical Features

  • Skin thickening, tethering, puckering, pitting, or dimpling on the palm
  • Reduced range of motion or loss of function in hands or fingers
  • Firm nodules fixed to skin and deep fascia of palm or fingers
  • Cords — longitudinal thickenings extending toward fingers
  • Contractures at MCP and PIP joints (fourth and fifth fingers most common)
  • Bilateral involvement (one hand usually more severely affected)
  • Examine both hands for thickening and pitting of palmar skin
  • Identify firm, painless nodules fixed to skin and palmar fascia
  • Check for cords (one or more)
  • Perform tabletop test: inability to lay palm flat indicates contracture
  • Assess for ectopic lesions on knuckles, feet, or penis
  • Evaluate functional limitations affecting work or leisure activities
Condition Distinguishing Features
Callus Thickened skin in response to friction or pressure
Ganglion Fluid-containing mass attached to tendon sheath or joint
Stenosing tenosynovitis (trigger finger) Tender nodule at metacarpophalangeal joint, intermittent locking
Diabetic cheiroarthropathy All digits affected except thumb, waxy skin appearance
Rheumatoid arthritis Joints usually enlarged and painful

Management

Primary Care Management

No treatment necessary for people without contracture or significant loss of function
  • Explain the condition and its progressive nature
  • Reassure that it's not cancerous
  • Advise that surgery may be required in the future
  • Reassure that pain associated with nodules will resolve gradually
  • Consider simple analgesia if needed
  • Advise return for review if contracture develops
  • Consider referral for painful nodules (without contracture)

Follow-up Plan

Initial Assessment

Diagnose Dupuytren's disease, assess functional impact

No Contracture

Monitor annually or sooner if symptoms worsen

Early Contracture

Refer to specialist for evaluation of treatment options

Post-treatment

Monitor for recurrence (common with certain procedures)

Treatment Options in Secondary Care

Surgical Procedures

Procedure Indications Pros Cons
Minimally Invasive Needle Fasciotomy Early stage disease, mild contractures Outpatient, local anesthesia, rapid recovery High recurrence rate (~50% at 3-5 years)
Minimally Invasive Segmental Aponeurotomy Moderate contractures Small incisions, removes segments of cord Doesn't remove all pathological tissue
Standard Limited Fasciectomy More severe contractures Lower recurrence than needle fasciotomy Longer recovery, higher complication rate
Complex Dermofasciectomy Skin involvement, aggressive recurrence, extensive disease in younger people Lowest recurrence risk Highest complication rate, requires skin graft
Complications: Overall complication rate is approximately 19% for needle fasciotomy, 17% for fasciectomy, and 12% for dermofasciectomy. Serious complications are rare (1.2% serious local complications, 0.78% serious systemic complications).

Background Information

Definition & Pathophysiology

Dupuytren's disease is a benign, progressive fibroproliferative disorder of the fascia of the hands and fingers, which can lead to disabling contractures.

  • Benign fibroproliferative disorder of hand fascia
  • Myofibroblast proliferation increases type III collagen
  • Nodules form from myofibroblast influx and collagen deposition
  • Progresses to pathologic cord formation
  • Autosomal dominant inheritance with variable penetrance
  • Heritability approximately 80%

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Risk Factor Impact
Age Predominantly affects people over 40
Sex 7x more common in men
Family History Autosomal dominant inheritance
Ancestry Most common in Northern Europeans
Diabetes 31% prevalence in people with diabetes
Smoking Up to 3x higher prevalence
Alcohol Dose-dependent association

Natural History & Prognosis

Early Stage

Skin thickening and pitting, small firm nodules develop

Progression

Cords form and extend toward fingers

Contracture

Cords mature and thicken, causing permanent flexion deformity

Long-term

75% develop more advanced disease, 35% progress to contracture over 18 years

Prognostic Factors: Early onset (before 40), bilateral involvement, radial digit involvement, and ectopic disease suggest aggressive disease (Dupuytren's diathesis).
