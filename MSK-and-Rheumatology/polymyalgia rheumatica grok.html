Assessment – When to Suspect PMR

Suspect PMR if age ≥50 AND ≥2 weeks of:

  • Bilateral shoulder ± pelvic girdle aching (often worse with movement, disturbs sleep)
  • Morning stiffness ≥45 minutes (classic – difficulty rising, dressing, turning in bed)

Common additional features (40–70%):

  • Constitutional: fatigue, low-grade fever, weight loss, anorexia
  • Bilateral upper arm tenderness
  • Peripheral arthritis (knees/wrists) or pitting oedema of hands/feet
RED FLAG – SUSPECT GIANT CELL ARTERITIS (GCA)

Urgent same-day rheumatology / ophthalmology referral if ANY of:
  • New headache (especially temporal)
  • Scalp tenderness / temporal artery abnormality
  • Jaw claudication
  • Visual symptoms (amaurosis, diplopia, vision loss)
  • Limb claudication
→ Start prednisolone 40–60 mg immediately if GCA suspected
Diagnosis
Core diagnostic requirements (age ≥50):
  • Bilateral shoulder ± pelvic girdle pain
  • Morning stiffness ≥45 min
  • Rapid response to prednisolone (≥70% improvement within 7 days)
  • Raised ESR/CRP (normal possible in 1–20%)

Investigations before starting steroids:

  • ESR / PV and/or CRP (usually >40 mm/hr)
  • FBC, U&E, LFT, bone profile, TSH, CK
  • Rheumatoid factor, ACPA (if suspicion of RA)
  • Protein electrophoresis ± urine BJP (exclude myeloma)
  • Consider chest X-ray

Trial of prednisolone 15 mg daily

  • Review at 1 week: ≥70% global improvement → highly likely PMR Confirmed PMR
  • Partial response → increase to 20 mg and reassess
  • Poor response or atypical features → reconsider diagnosis / refer Consider early referral
Atypical features → early rheumatology referral:
  • Age <60
  • Chronic onset (>2 months)
  • Normal or very high ESR/CRP (>100)
  • Predominant peripheral arthritis or distal swelling
  • Poor or transient response to steroids
  • Red flags (weight loss, night pain, neurological signs)
Treatment

Initial treatment (confirmed PMR):

  • Prednisolone 15 mg once daily (12.5–20 mg range acceptable)
  • Continue 15 mg until symptoms controlled (usually 3–4 weeks)
  • Give steroid card + written information
  • Advise chickenpox/shingles avoidance
Suggested tapering schedule (adjust slower if relapse-prone):
  • Weeks 0–3–4: 15 mg
  • Weeks 4–7: 12.5 mg
  • Weeks 7–11: 10 mg
  • Then reduce by 1 mg every 4–8 weeks (or slower)
  • Typical duration 12–24 months (some lifelong low dose)

Bone protection (mandatory if ≥1 risk factor):

  • Age ≥65 or previous fragility fracture → start bisphosphonate + calcium/vit D
  • Age <65 → calculate FRAX with steroid adjustment

Steroid-sparing agents: Consider methotrexate or tocilizumab early if high relapse risk or steroid side effects (specialist initiation).

Follow-up & Relapse Management

Monitoring schedule:

  • 1 week after any dose change
  • Every 3 months in first year
  • Check: symptoms of GCA, relapse, steroid side effects
  • Bloods: FBC, ESR/CRP, glucose, BP
Relapse (proximal pain + stiffness + ↑ESR/CRP):
  • Increase prednisolone to previous effective dose (usually 15–20 mg)
  • Symptoms usually settle within days
  • Once controlled → taper more slowly (e.g., 1 mg every 6–8 weeks below 10 mg)
Refer to rheumatology if:
  • Cannot reduce below 10 mg after 6–12 months
  • Repeated relapses (≥2 per year)
  • Steroid side effects or high risk (diabetes, osteoporosis, etc.)
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
