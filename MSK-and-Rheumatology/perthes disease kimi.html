Legg-Calvé-Perthes Disease

Interactive Management Algorithm - BMJ Best Practice

Assessment

Initial evaluation and risk factor assessment

1 History Taking
Essential
  • Painless limp (most common presentation)
  • Pain radiation pattern (knee, thigh, groin, buttocks)
  • Age range: typically 4-8 years (range 2-12 years)
  • Short stature compared to peers
  • History of passive smoking exposure
  • Family history of skeletal dysplasias
  • Assess for hyperactivity/ADHD
  • Document any minor trauma history
2 Physical Examination
Critical
  • Gait assessment: painless limp, gluteus medius lurch
  • Trendelenburg test (usually positive)
  • Quadriceps and gluteal muscle wasting
  • Range of motion limitations
    • Early: flexion deformity
    • Progressive: internal rotation, abduction limitation
    • Late: global reduction in all ranges
  • Apparent limb length discrepancy
  • Short stature phenotype
3 Risk Factor Assessment
Important
Male Sex
4-5 times more common in males
Age 4-8 years
Peak incidence, vulnerable vascular anatomy
Passive Smoking
Household/maternal smoking exposure
Hyper-coagulable States
Protein C deficiency, Factor V Leiden
Low Socio-economic Status
Urban deprivation association
Geography & Race
Higher incidence at northern latitudes, more common in Caucasians
Key Insight: Girls affected tend to have more severe femoral head involvement compared to boys of the same age.

Diagnosis

Imaging and classification systems

1 Initial Imaging
First Test
Bilateral Hip X-rays: AP and frog lateral views
  • Determine disease stage
  • Assess femoral head collapse and fragmentation
  • Identify subchondral fracture (crescent sign)
  • Evaluate joint space widening (Waldenström's sign)
2 Additional Investigations (if needed)
Consider
Laboratory Tests
FBC, ESR, CRP (to exclude other conditions)
Bone Scintigraphy
"Cold spot" in affected hip during ischaemic stage
MRI
Early diagnosis, perfusion index for prognosis
3 Classification Systems
Staging

A. Catterall Classification (Epiphyseal Involvement)

Group Involvement Features
I <25% Anterior head only, no collapse
II <50% Fragmentation, limited metaphyseal reaction
III <100% Extensive fragmentation, collapse, metaphyseal osteolysis
IV 100% Total head involvement, severe collapse

B. Herring Lateral Pillar Classification

Group A: No lateral pillar involvement, original height maintained Good
Group B: Lateral pillar lucency, <50% height loss Moderate
Group B/C: Exactly 50% height loss (borderline) Borderline
Group C: >50% height loss, increased lucency Poor

C. Stulberg Criteria (Prognosis)

Class Description Prognosis
I & II Spherical congruency Good (no/minimal arthritis risk)
III Aspherical congruency (>2mm loss) Mild-moderate arthritis
IV Flat head, abnormal neck/acetabulum Mild-moderate arthritis
V Flat head, incongruous joint Severe early arthritis
4 Differential Diagnosis
Exclude
Septic Arthritis

Acute onset, systemic symptoms, elevated WBC, positive cultures

Transient Synovitis

Acute onset, viral prodrome, resolves in 24-48h, normal labs

Skeletal Dysplasias

Bilateral symmetrical involvement, other epiphyses affected

Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

Multiple joints, systemic symptoms, positive autoantibodies

Treatment

Age-based management algorithm

1 Acute Pain Management
Immediate
First-line Options:
Paracetamol:
Children <12y: 10-15mg/kg q4-6h (max 75mg/kg/day)
Adults: 500-1000mg q4-6h (max 4g/day)
Ibuprofen:
Children <12y: 10mg/kg q6-8h (max 40mg/kg/day)
Adults: 300-400mg q6-8h (max 2.4g/day)

Short period of rest recommended during acute exacerbations

2 Ongoing Management by Age Group
Age-based

Children under 5 years

Mobilisation + Monitoring
Healing potential is very good. Maintain full ROM and monitor.
WITH lateral extrusion:
Non-surgical containment
  • Abduction cast/splint/brace
  • Daily ROM exercises essential
  • Continue until lateral epiphyseal re-ossification

Children 5-7 years

Epiphyseal involvement <50%:
Mobilisation + Monitoring

Maintain hip movement, symptomatic treatment

Epiphyseal involvement ≥50%:
Surgical Containment

Critical period - intervention improves outcome

Children 7-12 years

Catterall Stage I or II:
Surgical containment

Early containment crucial for remodelling

Catterall Stage III or IV:
Salvage procedure

Containment contraindicated

Children over 12 years

Early disease:
Salvage procedure

Limited remodelling potential

With arthritis:
Replacement arthroplasty after skeletal maturity

50% develop disabling arthritis by 6th decade

3 Containment Procedures
Technique

Non-surgical Containment:

  • Dynamic arthrogram under general anesthesia to determine optimal position
  • Abduction cast, splint, or brace with adductor tenotomy if needed
  • Daily ROM exercises to prevent stiffness
  • Continue until lateral epiphyseal re-ossification evident

Surgical Containment:

Proximal Femoral Osteotomy
Lateral varus opening wedge osteotomy - versatile procedure for hip containment
Innominate Osteotomy
Pelvic osteotomy for selected cases with >50% involvement without head deformity
Combined Procedures
Severe cases may require both femoral and pelvic osteotomies
Age consideration: Under 6 years - pelvic osteotomies have better outcomes. Over 6 years - femoral and pelvic osteotomies equally effective.
4 Salvage Procedures
Advanced Disease

For patients where containment is no longer appropriate (Catterall stages III/IV or age >12 years):

Shelf Arthroplasty
Extends acetabular roof coverage. Better outcomes in early stages (I/II). Caution in children >10-11 years.
Chiari Osteotomy
Pelvic osteotomy to deepen acetabulum. Suitable for older children with minimal remodelling potential.
Valgus Femoral Osteotomy
Improves femoral head orientation to increase weight-bearing area
5 Surgical Containment Postoperative Care
Recovery
  • Wound care: keep dry and clean until fully healed
  • Non-weight-bearing for 6 weeks or until union evident
  • Implant removal typically required 1 year post-surgery
  • Activity modification during fragmentation phase to avoid head deformity
  • Gradual return to high-impact activities after re-ossification confirmed

Follow-up

Long-term monitoring and prognosis

1 Monitoring Protocol
Regular
  • Clinical evaluation: Hip pain assessment, ROM measurement
  • Radiographic assessment: AP and frog lateral views of both hips
  • Timing: During growth spurts, acute painful episodes, and at regular intervals based on disease stage
  • Focus: Monitor evolution of femoral head deformity and intervene appropriately
2 Complications
Monitor
Osteoarthritis
Long-term complication. Risk factors: poor sphericity, incongruence, older age at onset. Medium Risk
Limb Length Inequality
From proximal femoral growth plate damage causing premature closure. Medium Risk
Stiffness & Loss of Rotation
Depends on hip position during disease. Maintain mobility to prevent. High Risk
3 Prognostic Factors
Outcome

Poor Prognosis Indicators:

Recurrent Synovitis

Chronic inflammation indicates persistent issues

Lateral Subluxation

Femoral head extrusion from acetabulum

>50% Femoral Head Involvement

Extensive necrosis leads to worse outcomes

Collapsed Lateral Pillar

Weight-bearing support compromised

Female Sex

More severe involvement than males at same age

Older Age at Onset

Less remodelling potential, worse prognosis

Good Prognosis Indicators:

  • Catterall Group I or II
  • Salter-Thompson Group A
  • Herring A or B (not B/C borderline)
  • Age under 7 years
  • Spherical femoral head with good acetabular congruence
Long-term Outlook: >50% of patients develop osteoarthritis between 4th-5th decades. Congruence is more important than sphericity for predicting future arthritis.
4 Patient & Parent Counseling
Education
  • Activity modification: Avoid high-impact activities during fragmentation phase until re-ossification confirmed
  • Pain management: NSAIDs and paracetamol for acute episodes, rest during exacerbations
  • Treatment expectations: Explain age-based approach and rationale for surgical vs non-surgical management
  • Long-term monitoring: Emphasize importance of regular follow-up through skeletal maturity
  • Complication awareness: Discuss potential for arthritis, limb length discrepancy, and stiffness
