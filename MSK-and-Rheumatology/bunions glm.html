Bunions (Hallux Valgus) Clinical Reference

Quick-Scan Tool for Primary Care Assessment & Management

Last Revised: August 2021
URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED for: Impending or non-healing foot ulcer OR suspected peripheral limb ischaemia
REFER TO DIABETIC FOOT PROTECTION SERVICE for patients with diabetes mellitus (urgency based on clinical judgement)

Quick Definition

Bunion (hallux valgus): Lateral deviation of the great toe away from the midline, causing medial prominence of the first metatarsal head.

Diagnostic criteria: Hallux valgus angle > 15° and intermetatarsal angle > 9° on weight-bearing X-rays.

Prevalence 23% (adults 18-65)
35.7% (adults 65+)
Gender Higher in women (30% vs 13%)

When to Suspect Bunion Deformity

Progressive pain at medial aspect of first MTP joint
Difficulty wearing shoes
Lateral deviation of the hallux at first MTP joint
Medial prominence of first metatarsal head

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Features
Hallux rigidus Osteoarthritis causing joint pain, swelling, stiffness, rigidity
Hallux interphalangeus Tip of great toe deviated laterally
Gout Acute inflammatory arthritis, often affecting MTP joint
Chronic sesamoiditis Gradual onset toe pain below first MTP joint, worse on weight-bearing

Assessment Approach

1
Ask about: Symptoms (pain, duration), impact on walking/lifestyle, footwear difficulties, family history, comorbidities, previous treatments
2
Observe: Gait, lower leg alignment
3
Examine:
  • Foot/feet in standing and seated position
  • Degree of hallux deformity and presence of pes planus
  • Skin quality and complications (calluses, corns, bursitis)
  • Palpate first MTP joint, lesser toes, forefoot for pain/tenderness
  • Assess neurovascular status
  • Range of movement of first MTP joint
  • Examine ligamentous laxity or contractures
  • Footwear type and wear patterns
Do not routinely arrange foot X-ray in primary care

Management Options

Self-Care Measures

Wear low-heeled, wide-fitting shoes with soft soles and toe boxes
Consider insoles, toe spacers, and/or bunion pads
Use ice for pain and inflammation relief
Analgesia: paracetamol and/or NSAID if needed

Professional Interventions

Refer to podiatry for footwear assessment and/or consideration of night splint or orthosis
Refer to musculoskeletal service for balance, proprioception, core stability advice, calf stretches, or corticosteroid injection (if unfit for surgery)

Surgical Management

Procedures: First metatarsal osteotomy, proximal phalanx osteotomy, soft tissue procedures, arthrodesis of first MTP or TMT joint

Complications: Infection, scar sensitivity, joint stiffness, transfer metatarsalgia, hallux varus, recurrence, bony non-union, nerve damage, prolonged swelling, chronic pain

High risk of recurrence: People with hypermobility, ligamentous laxity, or neuromuscular disorders

Urgent Referral Criteria

Impending or non-healing foot ulcer
Suspected peripheral limb ischaemia

Routine Referral Criteria

Consider referral to orthopaedic ankle and foot specialist or podiatric surgery if:

Progressive symptoms and/or deformity
Symptoms persist after three months of conservative treatment
Second toe is involved
Significant impact on daily functioning
Unable to wear suitable footwear
Specialist referral is not indicated for cosmetic or prophylactic reasons alone
