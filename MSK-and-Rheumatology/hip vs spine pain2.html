🦴 Hip vs Spine Pain — GP Quick Decision Aide

Based on expert discussion: Mr Anthony Gosh (Spinal Neurosurgeon) & Mr Jeremy Lane (Hip Surgeon)

⚡ Why This Matters

▼
  • High co-incidence of lumbar spine degeneration + hip OA, especially in older adults
  • Nerves from lumbar spine (sciatic, femoral, obturator) cross the hip → Hilton's Law: nerve crossing a joint will supply it
  • Referred pain patterns overlap significantly — pain can be referred down from spine or up from hip
  • Misattribution leads to wrong treatment and prolonged suffering
💡 "We treat the patient, not the test result." History & examination are far more meaningful than scans alone. Scans can lie; a correct history won't.

🟢 Hip Pain Symptoms

▼
  • Groin pain — ~60-70% of symptomatic hip OA patients point to groin
  • Buttock & lateral thigh pain
  • Pain refers to knee but NOT beyond — key differentiator
  • Painful to lie on affected side at night
  • Worse with weight-bearing, twisting under load
  • Functional clues:
    • Stiff getting out of chair; first few steps awkward/painful then eases
    • Limp worsens with walking distance
    • Feeling of giving way or locking (rough articular surfaces)
    • Difficulty with dressing — putting on trousers, socks, shoes
    • Pain in groin when leaning forward to reach feet

🟣 Spine Pain Symptoms

▼
  • Pain radiates below the knee → calf, ankle, foot
  • Associated numbness, tingling, weakness — dermatomal distribution
  • Spinal Stenosis pattern (typically >50-60 yrs):
    • Walking brings on leg pain (neurogenic claudication)
    • Stopping, sitting, or leaning forward relieves pain
    • Patients walk stooped with knees slightly bent
  • Disc herniation pattern:
    • Sitting/slouching worsens pain (pushes disc onto nerve)
    • Leaning forward to stand triggers pain
    • Once walking, may ease initially (disc material shifts)
    • Prolonged standing/walking → pain returns + numbness
  • Partial nerve compression: only some fibres affected → may cause buttock/lateral hip pain only (can mimic hip pathology)
  • Upper lumbar nerve (L1/L2) compression → groin/anterior hip pain (rare, confusing)

⚠️ Overlap & Pitfalls

▼
  • Partial sciatic nerve compression can mimic hip/buttock pain
  • L1/L2 radiculopathy mimics groin/hip pain
  • A painful hip can cause secondary back pain (altered biomechanics)
  • Both conditions cause limping — but different gait patterns
  • Both common in same demographic (>50 yrs, degenerative)
🔑 Ask: "Does the pain go below the knee?" and "Is there any numbness or weakness?" — Two questions that most reliably separate hip from spine.

👁️ Observation (Starts in Waiting Room)

▼
  • Call patient from waiting room yourself — observe gait, pain behaviour, general health
  • Hip clues: antalgic limp, Trendelenburg gait
  • Spine clues: stooped posture (stenosis), antalgic stepping (sciatica), guarded movement
  • General fragility / unwell appearance → consider other pathology

🦵 Hip Examination — Look, Feel, Move

▼

Expose: shoes, socks, trousers off

  • Look:
    • Muscle wasting — thigh (VMO above knee) and gluteal muscles
    • Leg length discrepancy — knees & ankles together lying flat
    • Pelvic tilt from unloading painful hip
  • Move (compare both sides):
    • Hip flexion to 90°, knee at 90°
    • Internal rotation — often first sign of hip trouble; restricted & painful = strong hip clue
    • External rotation (foot towards opposite side)
    • Abduction & adduction
    • Reduced ROM + reproduces pain = hip is likely source
⭐ Internal rotation restriction with pain is the most sensitive early clinical sign of hip joint pathology.

🔬 Spine / Neurological Examination

▼
  • Straight Leg Raise (SLR): leg straight, lift → stretches nerve against point of impingement → reproduces radicular pain
    • Key differentiator: bending knee (removing nerve stretch) relieves symptoms, whereas hip pain persists with knee bent and hip moved
  • Gait testing: heel walk (L5), toe walk (S1) — tests power
  • Muscle power: test each myotome systematically
  • Sensation: check each dermatome
  • Muscle wasting: from chronic denervation (calf, quads, gluteals)
  • Reflexes: knee jerk (L3/4), ankle jerk (S1)

📸 Imaging Summary

▼
InvestigationHipSpine
Plain X-ray AP pelvis — most important for hip; compare both hips; look for joint space narrowing, osteophytes, dysplasia. Valid within 1 year. Standing + flexion views → assess for spondylolisthesis/instability; dynamic info that MRI cannot provide
MRI If symptoms present but X-ray near-normal → order MRI to assess soft tissue (labral tears, early OA, AVN) Investigation of choice for nerve assessment; shows disc herniations, canal stenosis, foraminal stenosis
⚠️ MRI Over-interpretation Warning: No such thing as a "normal" MRI spine over age 40. Degenerative disc changes seen in the majority of asymptomatic patients >40. Findings must correlate with clinical picture. Do not treat the scan — treat the patient.
📝 Quality of radiology request matters. Detailed clinical info on the request form signals the radiologist to look for specific pathology. Under-reporting (e.g., subtle hip dysplasia missed) and over-reporting are both common problems.

💉 Diagnostic Injections — Key Decision Tool

▼
  • When mixed pathology exists (degenerative spine + arthritic hip) → diagnostic injection is often the most useful tool to determine the main pain source
  • Nerve root block (image-guided): switches off nerve pain → confirms spinal origin
  • Hip joint injection (image-guided): switches off hip pain → confirms hip origin
  • Even temporary relief is diagnostically valuable

Hip Injection Caveats:

  • Rule of thumb: max 3 injections in 2 years — beyond that, consider surgery
  • No hip replacement within 3 months of injection (infection risk)
  • Rarely can accelerate underlying arthritis
  • Can be used therapeutically short-term (e.g., bridging to a holiday/event, months of relief possible)

Spine Injection Evidence (NERVE Trial):

  • Disc herniation + sciatica: targeted nerve root injection vs microdiscectomy
  • ~⅔ of injection patients achieved long-term relief, comparable to surgery
  • Mechanism: reduces inflammation/swelling around nerve; body reabsorbs disc over time
  • If no motor weakness/progressive deficit → try injection first (may avoid surgery)

🏥 Spine Management Principles

▼
  • Majority managed non-operatively: even herniated discs often reabsorb within weeks to ~3 months
  • Simple analgesics + physiotherapy ± injection for pain window to enable rehab
  • Surgery indications: primarily to protect nerve function
    • Most surgery = minimally invasive decompression (small incision, microscope)
    • Removing thickened ligament, excess bone, or herniated disc material
    • Metalwork/fusion rarely needed — only for documented instability (spondylolisthesis with nerve compression)
🤝 MDT approach is key. When in doubt, the spine surgeon and hip surgeon should have a direct conversation rather than just cross-referring. Include physiotherapists in the team.

🚩 When to Refer / Red Flags

▼
  • Progressive neurological deficit (weakness, numbness worsening) → urgent spinal referral
  • Cauda equina symptoms (bladder/bowel disturbance, saddle anaesthesia) → emergency
  • Significant functional impairment despite conservative Rx → specialist assessment
  • Symptoms not matching imaging → further workup / second opinion
  • Patient looking unwell / systemic features → exclude malignancy, infection, inflammatory arthropathy
  • Mixed pathology not resolving → consider diagnostic injection pathway

📌 Top 5 Takeaways for GP

▼
  1. Pain below the knee + numbness/weakness = think spine. Pain stops at/before knee + groin pain = think hip.
  2. Internal rotation of hip is the single most useful quick clinical test for hip pathology.
  3. MRI findings over 40 are almost never "normal" — don't over-interpret degenerative changes. Correlate clinically.
  4. When in doubt between hip and spine, diagnostic injection (nerve block or hip block) is the best differentiator.
  5. Most spine conditions resolve conservatively. Refer urgently only for progressive neurological deficit or cauda equina.
