Temporomandibular Disorders (TMDs)
Interactive Management Algorithm

NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary — Last revised May 2024

Assessment & Red Flags

History (biopsychosocial approach)

  • Pain location, radiation, triggers (chewing, yawning, stress), relieving factors
  • Joint clicking/popping/crepitus, locking, limited opening
  • Impact on sleep, mood, work, eating
  • Previous trauma, dental treatment, parafunction (clenching/grinding)
  • Comorbidities (fibromyalgia, migraine, IBS, anxiety/depression)

Examination

  • Palpate TMJ (anterior to tragus) and masticatory muscles (masseter/temporalis)
  • Measure mouth opening (normal ≥35–55 mm)
  • Listen/palpate for joint noises
  • Check for occlusal changes, muscle hypertrophy

Red Flags → Urgent Referral / Investigation

  • Previous head/neck cancer
  • Persistent unexplained neck lump / lymphadenopathy
  • Sudden severe limitation of opening (closed lock with trauma history)
  • New neurological signs (cranial nerve deficit, unilateral tinnitus, etc.)
  • Jaw pain + bisphosphonate/denosumab use (risk of ONJ)
  • Age >50 with new headache, jaw claudication, temporal tenderness → Giant Cell Arteritis
  • Recurrent TMJ dislocation
Diagnosis

Suspect Pain-related TMD if ≥1 of:

  • Pain in TMJ/masticatory muscles modified by jaw movement/function/parafunction
  • Reproducible joint noise (clicking/popping/crepitus)
  • Restricted or altered jaw movement (including locking)
  • Temporal headache worsened by jaw function

DC/TMD Classification (most common in primary care)

  • Myalgia — pain on palpation of masseter/temporalis, worsened by function
  • Arthralgia — TMJ pain on palpation/movement
  • Disc displacement with reduction — clicking + ability to open wide
  • Disc displacement without reduction (closed lock) — sudden severe limitation (<25–30 mm)
  • Degenerative joint disease — crepitus ± pain
Note: Joint noise alone (no pain or limitation) is normal in up to 50% of population — reassure, no treatment needed.
Initial Treatment (Primary Care)

First-line — Conservative (≥95% of cases improve)

  • Reassure: Usually self-limiting, fluctuates, excellent prognosis with conservative measures
  • Education & Self-care: Soft diet, rest jaw, avoid wide opening/clenching, heat/ice pack, gentle massage
  • Simple analgesia: Paracetamol ± ibuprofen (lowest dose, shortest duration)
  • Acute severe myogenous pain: Diazepam 2 mg tds × max 5–7 days (muscle relaxant)
  • Chronic pain: Consider low-dose amitriptyline (10–30 mg nocte) or gabapentin
  • Address psychosocial factors — sleep hygiene, stress management, consider CBT if significant distress

Consider additional primary care options

  • Refer to dentist for occlusal splint (if bruxism confirmed or severe)
  • Physiotherapy — jaw exercises, acupuncture, posture advice
  • Psychology — CBT for pain-related anxiety or significant distress

Referral Indications

Urgent (within 2 weeks) — Red flags above, trauma, closed lock with trauma, suspected GCA

Routine Oral & Maxillofacial Surgery — Persistent symptoms >6–8 weeks despite conservative treatment, severe functional impairment, recurrent dislocation, known rheumatic disease with TMJ involvement

Stable / Continue primary care — Most patients — review 4–6 weeks

Follow-up & Safety-netting
  • Review at 4–6 weeks (phone or face-to-face)
  • If improving → continue self-care, wean analgesia
  • If static or worsening after 6–8 weeks conservative treatment → refer to Oral & Maxillofacial Surgery
  • Persistent severe pain/dysfunction → consider multidisciplinary pain clinic
  • Safety-net: return if red flags develop or unable to eat normal diet
