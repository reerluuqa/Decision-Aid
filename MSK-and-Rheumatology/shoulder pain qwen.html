Shoulder Pain Clinical Algorithm

Evidence-based guidance for assessment, diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up of shoulder pain in primary care

Algorithm Progress

  • Assessment
  • Diagnosis
  • Treatment
  • Follow-up

Assessment

Key Assessment Components

  • Pain characteristics: onset, duration, site, radiation, aggravating/relieving factors
  • Functional impact: activities of daily living, work, sports
  • Red flags: trauma, systemic symptoms, neurological deficits
  • Medical history: diabetes, cardiovascular disease, malignancy
  • Medications: especially statins or anticoagulants

Physical Examination

  • Inspection: asymmetry, swelling, muscle wasting
  • Palpation: tenderness over AC joint, greater tuberosity
  • Range of motion: active, passive, resisted movements
  • Special tests: painful arc, cross-arm test, external rotation
  • Neurological assessment if indicated
Urgent Referral Indicators: Traumatic rotator cuff tear Suspected septic arthritis Unreduced dislocation Severe restriction of movement

Diagnosis

Common Diagnoses

  • Rotator Cuff Disorders: Pain on abduction, painful arc (70-120°), weakness against resistance
  • Frozen Shoulder: Global restriction of active/passive movement, severe loss of external rotation
  • Instability Disorders: History of dislocation, feeling of "slipping out", apprehension signs
  • AC Joint Disorders: Localized tenderness, pain with cross-body adduction
  • Glenohumeral OA: Deep joint pain, crepitus, X-ray confirmation needed

Differential Diagnosis

  • Referred pain from cervical spine
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica
  • Malignancy (metastases)
  • Inflammatory arthritis
  • Early herpes zoster
Diagnostic Uncertainty: Unclear diagnosis after assessment Atypical presentation Consider ultrasound if available

Treatment

Initial Management

  • Analgesia: Paracetamol first-line, then NSAIDs or codeine
  • Activity modification: Avoid aggravating activities
  • Education: Explain condition and expected recovery timeline
  • Physiotherapy referral for exercise programs

Condition-Specific Management

  • Rotator Cuff: Subacromial corticosteroid injection (max 2 injections)
  • Frozen Shoulder: Intra-articular steroid injection in painful phase
  • Instability: Physiotherapy for muscle patterning, surgical referral for recurrent dislocations
  • AC Joint: Activity modification, steroid injection for osteoarthritis
  • Glenohumeral OA: Core treatments per NICE OA guidelines
Referral Criteria: Acute traumatic rotator cuff tear No improvement after 6-12 weeks conservative management Recurrent instability Consider MSK service for injections

Follow-up

Monitoring & Review

  • Review at 2 weeks if severe symptoms
  • Assess response to analgesia and activity modification
  • Evaluate functional improvement
  • Monitor for development of red flags
  • Adjust management plan based on progress

Long-term Management

  • Gradual return to normal activities
  • Continue exercises as advised by physiotherapist
  • Manage expectations: full recovery may take 6-24 months
  • Address comorbidities (e.g., diabetes control)
  • Consider psychological impact of chronic pain
When to Refer: Worsening symptoms despite treatment Persistent functional limitation at 3 months Patient request for specialist opinion

Based on NICE Clinical Knowledge Summaries (CKS) - Last revised November 2022

This algorithm is for healthcare professionals working within the NHS in the UK
