Osgood-Schlatter Disease

Age 6+ years
URGENT REFERRAL NEEDED IF: Trauma, systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss), night pain, or pain at rest
Key Diagnostic Features
Typical Presentation
  • Pain localized to tibial tuberosity
  • Gradual onset, initially mild/intermittent
  • Usually unilateral (both knees in 20-30%)
  • Exacerbated by running, jumping, kneeling
  • Relieved by rest
Examination Findings
  • Tenderness over tibial tuberosity
  • Pain with resisted knee extension
  • Tight quadriceps/hamstrings
  • Swelling or bony enlargement of tibial tuberosity
  • Normal passive range of movement
  • No effusion
Management Approach
1
Reassure & Educate
Benign, self-limiting condition that typically resolves with growth completion
2
Activity Modification
Continue exercise with minimal pain; reduce intensity if symptoms worsen
3
Pain Relief
Ice packs (10-15 min, 3x/day), protective knee pads, paracetamol/NSAIDs if needed
4
Preventative Strategies
Regular stretching of quadriceps/hamstrings, cross-training to reduce repetitive stress
Red Flags & Differential Diagnosis
Urgent Referral Indicators
  • Trauma with abnormal physical signs
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, sweats)
  • Bone or joint pain elsewhere
  • Night pain or pain after rest
  • Referred pain from hip
Differential Dx
Risk Factors
Condition Key Features
Traumatic knee injury Sudden onset after trauma
Juvenile idiopathic arthritis Multiple joints, morning stiffness
Slipped upper femoral epiphysis Hip pain, limp, overweight adolescent
Septic arthritis/osteomyelitis Acute pain, swelling, warmth, fever
Bone tumours Severe night pain, weight loss
  • Age: 12-15 years (boys), 8-13 years (girls)
  • Participation in high-impact sports
  • Quadriceps muscle tightness
  • Reduced hamstring flexibility
When to Refer
Physiotherapist
If symptoms worsen or don't improve with initial management
Paediatrician/Orthopaedic Surgeon
If diagnostic uncertainty or severe symptoms
Orthopaedic Surgeon
If symptoms persist into adulthood and significantly affect functioning
Key Clinical Points
Remember
  • Most cases resolve with growth completion (14-18 years)
  • Only 10% of cases persist into adulthood
  • Knee X-ray is not routinely recommended
  • Imaging only if loose ossicles or alternative diagnoses suspected
