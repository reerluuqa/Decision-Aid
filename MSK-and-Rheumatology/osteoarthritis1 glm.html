Osteoarthritis Quick Reference

Clinical guidance for assessment and management of osteoarthritis

Quick Diagnosis

Typical Clinical Features

  • Age 45+ years
  • Activity-related joint pain
  • Morning stiffness lasting ≤30 minutes
  • Limited joint movement
  • Crepitus (grating sound/sensation)

Common Joint Patterns

Hand: DIP, PIP, CMC joints
Knee: Medial compartment
Hip: Groin pain
No routine imaging needed

Red Flags

Refer urgently if: Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss), prolonged morning stiffness (>30 min), severe night pain, history of cancer, or sudden worsening of symptoms.
Consider alternative diagnosis if: Joint hot and swollen, multiple joints affected, or associated with psoriasis, inflammatory bowel disease, or uveitis.

Management Approach

Non-Pharmacological
Pharmacological
Referral Criteria
Core Treatments: Therapeutic exercise, weight loss if overweight/obese, and information/advice about condition and self-management.

Exercise Recommendations

  • Local muscle strengthening
  • Aerobic exercise
  • Range of motion activities
  • Consider referral to physiotherapy

Weight Management

  • Offer support to lose weight if BMI ≥25
  • Target 10% weight loss for symptom improvement
  • Combination of diet and exercise most effective
Treatment When to Consider Notes
Topical NSAIDs First-line for knee/hand OA Consider before oral NSAIDs
Paracetamol For pain relief Monitor effectiveness
Oral NSAIDs If inadequate response Lowest effective dose, shortest time, consider PPI
Opioids Severe pain not controlled by other measures Only after trial of other options
Intra-articular corticosteroids Severe pain not responding to other treatments Consider if joint not severely damaged

Consider referral to multidisciplinary team if:

  • Symptoms not adequately controlled with core treatments
  • Functional limitation significantly impacts quality of life

Consider referral for surgery if:

  • Persistent symptoms despite optimized non-surgical management for ≥3 months
  • Joint pain significantly impacting quality of life
  • Structural damage consistent with symptoms

Do NOT use scoring tools to determine surgical eligibility

Key Facts

Prevalence

  • ~10 million people in the UK have OA
  • 5.4 million with knee OA
  • 3.2 million with hip OA
  • Median age of symptom onset: 55 years

Prognosis

  • Variable progression
  • Hand OA generally good prognosis
  • Hip OA poorer prognosis (93% of hip replacements for OA)
  • Knee OA variable prognosis (98% of knee replacements for OA)
