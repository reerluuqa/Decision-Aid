JIA Quick Reference

Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis Clinical Tool

Last updated: Jul 12, 2024
10-20% of JIA patients risk anterior uveitis - Regular ophthalmological exams required

Epidemiology

  • Affects 1 in 1000 children
  • Most common chronic rheumatic disorder of childhood
  • 3-6.6× more common in females

Definition

Chronic pediatric inflammatory arthritides with:

  • Onset before 16 years
  • Objective arthritis in ≥1 joint
  • Duration ≥6 weeks

Treatment Goals

  • Relieve immediate pain
  • Prevent joint damage
  • Prevent disability

JIA Subtypes (ILAR Classification)

Systemic

10-20% of cases

  • • Daily spiking fevers
  • • Evanescent rash
  • • Lymphadenopathy

Oligoarticular

50-60% of cases

  • • ≤4 joints affected
  • • Large joints (knee, ankle)
  • • High uveitis risk

Polyarticular (RF-)

30-35% of cases

  • • ≥5 joints affected
  • • Young girls
  • • Small joints involved

Polyarticular (RF+)

Part of polyarticular cases

  • • ≥5 joints affected
  • • Teenage girls
  • • Aggressive disease

Psoriatic

Variable

  • • Arthritis + psoriasis
  • • Dactylitis
  • • Nail changes

Enthesitis-related

Variable

  • • Boys >6 years
  • • Asymmetrical arthritis
  • • HLA-B27 positive

Undifferentiated

Variable

  • • Doesn't fit criteria
  • • Multiple subtype features

Diagnostic Approach

1

Clinical Diagnosis

Based on history and physical examination

2

Laboratory Tests

Support subtype classification, guide treatment

3

Imaging

Aid diagnosis, assess joint damage

4

Specialist Referral

Prompt referral to pediatric rheumatologist

Key Diagnostic Factors

Duration >6 weeks

Objective arthritis in joints for at least 6 weeks

Joint pain & swelling

Commonly at knees in oligoarticular JIA

Morning stiffness

Improves as day progresses

Limp

More pronounced in mornings

Fever

High-spiking in systemic-onset JIA only

Laboratory Tests

Test Typical Findings Clinical Significance
FBC Normal in oligoarticular; anemia, thrombocytosis in systemic Baseline for all patients; helps exclude infection/malignancy
ESR/CRP Variable elevation by subtype Non-specific inflammatory marker; part of disease activity score
ANA Positive in 30-60% (oligoarticular > polyarticular) Risk factor for uveitis; routine testing recommended
RF Positive in 2-7% (RF-positive polyarticular JIA) Associated with aggressive disease; requires 2 positive tests
Ferritin May be abnormal Test if systemic-onset JIA suspected

Treatment Goals

Clinically inactive disease
Excellent symptom control
Minimize joint damage
Minimize side effects
Promote physical health

Oligoarticular JIA

First-line

Intra-articular corticosteroids

If progressive

Methotrexate

Biologics

TNF-alpha inhibitors if inadequate response

Supportive care

Physiotherapy, regular ophthalmology exams

Polyarticular JIA

First-line

Methotrexate (most common DMARD)

Second-line

Leflunomide or sulfasalazine

Biologics

TNF-alpha inhibitors (etanercept, adalimumab)

Refractory cases

Tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor) or abatacept

Systemic JIA

First-line

NSAIDs + corticosteroids

Biologics

IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors (anakinra, tocilizumab)

Refractory cases

TNF-alpha inhibitors

Supportive care

Monitor for MAS (macrophage activation syndrome)

Enthesitis-related JIA

First-line

NSAIDs + physiotherapy

If progressive

Sulfasalazine

Biologics

TNF-alpha inhibitors (adalimumab effective)

Supportive care

Monitor for acute anterior uveitis

Monitoring Requirements

Routine Monitoring

  • Disease activity assessment (every 3-6 months)
  • Growth and development monitoring
  • Physiotherapy and occupational therapy review

Medication Monitoring

  • Methotrexate: FBC, LFTs, renal function (every 3-4 months)
  • Tocilizumab: LFTs at initiation, 4-8 weeks, then every 3-4 months
  • Biologics: Screen for TB, hepatitis before initiation

Emergency Actions Required

Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)

  • • Persistent fever >38°C
  • • Falling platelet count
  • • Elevated ferritin (>684 ng/mL)
  • • Triglycerides >156 mg/dL
  • • Fibrinogen ≤2.5 g/L
Immediate pediatric rheumatology consult

Systemic JIA with Cardiac Involvement

  • • Chest pain or dyspnea
  • • Pericardial effusion
  • • Tachycardia out of proportion to fever
  • • Hypotension
Urgent cardiology consultation

High Risk Indicators

Poor Prognosis Factors

  • Anti-CCP positive
  • Rheumatoid factor positive
  • Joint damage at presentation
  • High-risk joint involvement (cervical spine, wrist, hip)
  • High disease activity

Medication Toxicity Concerns

  • Tocilizumab: Elevated LFTs (>5× ULN)
  • Methotrexate: LFTs >2× ULN
  • Biologics: Signs of infection
  • Corticosteroids: Hypertension, hyperglycemia

Ophthalmology Monitoring Requirements

Risk Category Screening Frequency Key Features
High Risk Every 3 months Oligoarticular JIA, ANA positive, disease duration <4 years
Moderate Risk Every 6 months Polyarticular JIA, ANA negative, disease duration 4-7 years
Low Risk Every 12 months Systemic JIA, RF-positive polyarticular JIA, disease duration >7 years
Note: Uveitis in JIA is often asymptomatic until advanced stages. Regular screening is essential regardless of symptoms.
