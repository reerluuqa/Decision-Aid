Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)

Quick Reference Clinical Tool

EMERGENCY: Unstable SCFE

Patient unable to bear weight requires urgent surgical intervention within 24 hours to prevent avascular necrosis

Red Flags

Inability to bear weight

Indicates unstable SCFE requiring urgent surgical intervention

Acute severe pain after minor trauma

May indicate unstable slip with vascular compromise

Weight < 50th percentile

May indicate underlying endocrine disorder requiring investigation

Key Diagnostic Features

Obligatory external rotation on hip flexion

Key examination finding for SCFE

Knee, groin, thigh, or hip pain

Pain may be referred to knee region

Abnormal Klein line on radiograph

Does not intersect the femoral head on AP or frog-leg lateral view

Overview

Definition:

Displacement of the capital femoral epiphysis through weakness in the proximal femoral growth plate

Age Group:

Adolescents (11-13 years for boys, 11-12 years for girls)

Incidence:

10.8 cases per 100,000 children per year in the US

Bilateral Occurrence:

Up to 60% of cases

Risk Factors

Obesity

Strongest risk factor (RR: 3.45)

Puberty

Peak age: 11-13 years

Male sex

Higher incidence than females

Endocrine disorders

Hypothyroidism, panhypopituitarism, renal osteodystrophy

Treatment Algorithm

Stable SCFE

1

In situ screw fixation

First-line treatment with single screw in center of epiphysis

2

Consider prophylactic fixation

For contralateral hip, especially in patients with endocrine disorders

Unstable SCFE

1

Urgent surgical repair

Within 24 hours to reduce risk of AVN

2

Joint decompression

Percutaneous decompression with gentle repositioning

Diagnostic Imaging

View Key Findings
AP Radiograph Klein line doesn't intersect femoral head
Frog-leg Lateral More sensitive for early slip detection
Bilateral Films Recommended as SCFE often occurs in both hips
  • Widening of the physis on affected side
  • Loss of overlap of metaphysis (Capener's sign)
  • Increased sclerosis of proximal metaphysis (blanch sign of Steel)

Prognosis

Early diagnosis and treatment

Prevents slip progression and complications

Avascular necrosis (AVN)

More common in unstable SCFE (up to 47%)

Chondrolysis

Rare but serious complication causing joint space narrowing
