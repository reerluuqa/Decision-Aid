1. Assessment

Take a focused history and examine

  • Mechanism of injury (inversion/plantar flexion ‚Üí lateral ankle sprain; sudden acceleration ‚Üí hamstring strain)
  • Ability to bear weight immediately and now
  • ‚ÄúPop‚Äù sensation or sudden giving way ‚Üí possible ligament rupture or ACL
  • Location and severity of pain, swelling, bruising (may appear after 24h)
  • Previous injuries (‚Üë risk of chronic instability)

Examination

  • Look: swelling, bruising, deformity
  • Palpate: specific tenderness (e.g. ATF ligament, distal fibula, base of 5th MT)
  • Active/passive ROM, strength, stability (anterior drawer, talar tilt)
  • Neurovascular status (distal pulses, sensation)

Red flags ‚Üí Urgent ED referral (same day)
URGENT REFERRAL if suspected:

  • Fracture/dislocation
  • Neurovascular compromise
  • Penetrating wound or open fracture
  • Tendon rupture (palpable gap, inability to actively move)
  • Complete ligament rupture with gross instability
  • Large haematoma / haemarthrosis

If no red flags and Ottawa negative ‚Üí diagnose soft-tissue sprain/strain

4. Follow-up & Prevention

Review if:

  • No improvement after 5‚Äì7 days of self-care
  • Worsening pain/swelling
  • Inability to progress weight-bearing

‚Üí Consider routine physiotherapy referral for persistent symptoms.

Prevention of re-injury (advise all patients)

  • Warm-up and cool-down properly
  • Balanced training (strength + proprioception + flexibility)
  • Appropriate footwear and replace when worn
  • Ankle bracing or taping for high-risk sports (previous sprain)
  • Address risk factors: obesity, poor balance, previous injury
