Assessment & History

Demographics & History

  • Age: 40-70 years most common Risk Factor
  • Sex: Slightly more common in women
  • Hand dominance & occupation: Important for treatment planning
  • Onset: Insidious, progressive over weeks to months

Key Symptoms

  • Shoulder pain, especially at night and with extremes of motion
  • Difficulty sleeping on affected side
  • Progressive stiffness limiting ADLs (combing hair, dressing)
  • Four classic stages:
    • Stage 1: Pain with minimal motion limitation
    • Stage 2: Increasing pain and decreasing ROM ("freezing")
    • Stage 3: Severe motion limitation with pain only at extremes ("frozen")
    • Stage 4: Minimal pain, profound stiffness ("thawing")

Physical Examination

  • Critical Finding: Limited BOTH active AND passive ROM Key Sign
  • Examine supine to isolate glenohumeral motion
  • Typical pattern: Forward flexion ~75째, Abduction ~75째, External rotation ~15째, Internal rotation to iliac crest
  • Coracoid Pain Test: 96% sensitive, 87-89% specific Diagnostic
  • Shoulder Shrug Test: 95% sensitive, 50% specific Diagnostic

Strong Risk Factors

Diabetes Mellitus 5x increased risk

11-25% of adhesive capsulitis patients have diabetes

Prior History 20-30% bilateral

High risk of contralateral shoulder involvement

Shoulder Immobilization Post-traumatic

Recent trauma, surgery, or prolonged sling use

Thyroid Disease 5-10% associated

Both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism

Exclusion Tests (Negative in Adhesive Capsulitis)

These tests should be negative to rule out other pathologies:

  • Spurling Manoeuvre: Rules out cervical radiculopathy
  • Hawkins Test: Rules out impingement
  • Neer Test: Rules out impingement
  • Speed/O'Brien Tests: Rule out SLAP lesions
  • Yergason Test: Rules out biceps pathology
  • Apprehension Test: Rules out instability
Diagnosis & Investigations

Diagnostic Approach

Adhesive capsulitis is a diagnosis of exclusion based on:

  • Characteristic history and exam findings
  • Limited active AND passive ROM
  • Absence of other shoulder pathology on examination
  • Normal or inconclusive imaging studies

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Shoulder stiffness and pain Required
  • Decreased active ROM Common
  • Decreased passive ROM Critical
  • Positive coracoid pain test 96% sensitive
  • Positive shoulder shrug test 95% sensitive
  • Presence of risk factors Supports diagnosis

Investigations

Treatment Algorithm

Treatment Principles

Multi-faceted, individualized approach based on disease stage and severity:

  • Start with non-invasive treatments
  • Progress to invasive interventions if refractory
  • Physiotherapy is cornerstone throughout
  • Pain control enables effective rehabilitation
1

First-line: Conservative Management

Activity modification - avoid exacerbating activities

Physiotherapy - start as early as possible:

  • Home exercise program (active/passive ROM)
  • Supervised outpatient therapy
  • High-grade mobilization techniques (Maitland technique)
  • Sleeper stretch for internal rotation

NSAIDs - scheduled regimen for inflammatory phase:

Drug Dosage Max Daily Dose
Ibuprofen 200-400mg q4-6h PRN 2400mg
Meloxicam 7.5-15mg once daily 15mg
Naproxen 500mg then 250mg q6-8h PRN 1250mg
First-line
2

Second-line: Corticosteroid Injection

Indication: Persistent pain despite first-line therapy

Benefits: Local anti-inflammatory effect, enables physiotherapy

Technique: Intra-articular glenohumeral injection

  • Triamcinolone acetonide + lidocaine (no epinephrine)
  • Can consider subacromial injection (fewer systemic effects)
  • Multiple injections beneficial for up to 16 weeks (max 3 injections)
Consider early
3

Third-line: Invasive Options (Refractory Cases)

Choose based on patient factors and resources:

A. Manipulation Under Anesthesia

  • Effective early in disease course
  • Post-op interscalene block for analgesia
  • Caution: previous surgery, poor bone quality
Medium-term benefit

B. Hydrodilation (Arthrographic Distension)

  • Saline + corticosteroid injection
  • Rapid improvement at 1-3 months
  • Similar outcomes to other injections at 6 months
Short-term benefit

C. Arthroscopic Capsular Release

  • Anterior capsule, rotator interval, coracohumeral ligament release
  • Consider posterior release for internal rotation loss
  • Short-term and long-term benefits demonstrated
Definitive treatment

Emerging Treatments Experimental

  • Hyaluronic acid injection: Similar outcomes to corticosteroids
  • Extracorporeal shockwave therapy: Promising for pain relief and ROM
  • Pulsed radiofrequency: Suprascapular nerve stimulation + physiotherapy
  • Bee venom acupuncture: Limited evidence of benefit
Follow-up & Prognosis

Monitoring

  • Regular scheduled visits to assess progress
  • Measure shoulder ROM at each visit
  • Assess pain levels and functional improvement
  • Monitor adherence to physiotherapy regimen
  • Adjust treatment based on disease stage

Complications

Complication Timeframe Likelihood Prevention
Fracture or surgical repair disruption Short-term Medium Caution with MUA in prior surgery, poor bone quality
Axillary nerve injury Short-term Low Careful surgical technique
Persistent pain/stiffness Long-term Low Early intervention, adherence to therapy

Prognosis Generally Good

  • Self-limiting condition: Usually resolves within 18-24 months
  • Recovery rate: Vast majority make full recovery with appropriate treatment
  • Residual symptoms: Some reports of pain/stiffness up to 7 years
  • Early intervention: Can decrease duration of symptoms
  • Operative treatment: Improves refractory cases

Primary Prevention

  • Manage diabetes: Good glycemic control may prevent secondary adhesive capsulitis
  • Avoid prolonged immobilization: Early ROM after shoulder trauma/surgery
  • Patient education: Counsel on natural disease course and importance of adherence
  • Establish strong physiotherapy relationship: Active patient participation crucial

Recurrence

  • Contralateral shoulder: 20-30% risk in women with existing adhesive capsulitis
  • Ipsilateral shoulder: Recurrence rate not well-defined but appears low
  • Risk reduction: Continue home exercise program after recovery
