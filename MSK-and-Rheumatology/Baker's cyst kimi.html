Baker's Cyst Management Algorithm

Primary Care Management Pathway for Popliteal Cysts

Assessment

Initial evaluation and history taking

Clinical History

  • Ask about swelling and pain characteristics
  • Identify symptom onset and progression
  • Assess impact on range of movement
  • Screen for underlying knee pathology
Red Flag Screening
Sudden severe pain Neurovascular symptoms Rapid swelling change

Physical Examination

Inspect and palpate popliteal fossa with knee in full extension and flexion. Look for:

  • Visible bulge in medial popliteal fossa
  • Fluctuant, smooth, round mass
  • Foucher's sign (tense in extension, softens in flexion)
  • Restricted range of movement

Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis and rule out differentials

Diagnostic Approach

Children: Ultrasound scan recommended for all cases to confirm diagnosis and rule out serious conditions.
Adults: History and examination may be sufficient; imaging guided by clinical judgement.

Differential Diagnosis

Consider and exclude:

  • Deep vein thrombosis Urgent referral
  • Popliteal artery aneurysm Urgent referral
  • Soft tissue sarcoma High suspicion
  • Meniscal cyst, ganglion cyst, lipoma Routine

Imaging Strategy

  • Ultrasound: Confirms diagnosis, evaluates complications, excludes DVT
  • MRI: Identifies underlying conditions (meniscal tear, arthritis)
  • X-ray: Limited value; assess underlying joint disease only

Treatment

Management in Children

Primary Cysts: Reassure - usually resolve spontaneously without treatment.
Refer if: Symptomatic, very large, underlying condition suspected, or diagnosis unclear.
Paediatric orthopaedics Paediatric rheumatology

Management in Adults

Asymptomatic: No treatment required Monitor

Symptomatic Treatment:
  • Simple analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs)
  • Physiotherapy
  • Treat underlying condition (osteoarthritis, meniscal tear)
Aspiration not recommended in primary care

Secondary Care Referral

Same-day assessment if red flags or high suspicion of DVT
Routine referral if:
  • Diagnosis unclear
  • Symptoms persist despite conservative measures
  • Cyst very large or troublesome
  • Specialist treatment of underlying disease required

Follow-up

Monitoring and prognosis

Expected Course

Children: Many resolve spontaneously within a few years. Resolution occurs when underlying condition improves.
Adults: Spontaneous resolution is common. Treating underlying knee condition often leads to regression.

Prognostic Factors

  • Severity of underlying condition is key Severe OA may prevent resolution
  • Surgical excision has high recurrence rate Reserve for selected cases

Follow-up Plan

Arrange review if:

  • Symptoms worsen or persist beyond 3 months
  • New red flag symptoms develop Re-assess urgently
  • Functional limitation increases
No routine follow-up needed if asymptomatic
