Plantar Fasciitis Management

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm - Based on NICE CKS Guidelines (December 2024)

Assessment
Key Questions
  • Location and timing of heel pain Typical
  • Occupation and activity levels Risk factor
  • Systemic conditions (e.g., spondyloarthritis) Screen
  • Previous episodes and treatments
Clinical Examination
  • Palpate medial calcaneal tuberosity Key sign
  • Assess ankle dorsiflexion range Limited ROM
  • Perform Windlass test Reproduces pain
  • Evaluate foot alignment and footwear Important
  • Check gait pattern
When to Consider Imaging
  • Atypical features present Caution
  • Persistent symptoms despite management Re-evaluate
  • Alternative diagnosis suspected Urgent

Options: Weight-bearing X-ray (assess alignment, exclude bony lesions) or Ultrasound (show thickening, rule out fibroma)

Diagnosis
Diagnostic Criteria
  • Medial plantar heel pain Essential
  • Tenderness at medial calcaneal tuberosity Essential
  • First-step pain worst in morning Typical
  • Pain improves with activity but worsens with prolonged use
Differential Diagnosis

Musculoskeletal

  • Achilles tendinopathy
  • Fat pad atrophy
  • Calcaneal stress fracture
  • Plantar fascia rupture

Systemic/Other

  • Spondyloarthritis Screen
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Tarsal tunnel syndrome
  • Neoplasm (rare) Rule out
Key Point: Plantar fasciitis is a clinical diagnosis. Imaging is not routinely required unless red flags or atypical features are present.
Treatment
First-Line: Self-Care
  • Rest and activity modification Essential
  • Appropriate footwear with arch support Essential
  • Over-the-counter insoles/heel pads Recommended
  • Weight loss if overweight/obese If applicable
  • Ice packs 15-20 min every few hours Symptom relief
Pharmacological
  • Paracetamol for pain relief First choice
  • NSAIDs if no contraindications Check contraindications
  • Consider PPI if GI risk Caution
Exercise Programme
  • Plantar fascia stretching 4-6 weeks
  • Gastrocnemius muscle stretching
  • Progressive loading exercises
  • Continue for 4-6 weeks minimum Adherence key
Referral Options
  • Podiatrist/Orthotist: Orthoses, night splints Effective
  • Physiotherapist: Supervised exercises, taping Recommended
  • Consider if self-care ineffective after 4-6 weeks Timing
Second-Line: Specialist Interventions
  • Corticosteroid injection (ultrasound-guided) Painful, risks
  • Extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT) Limited availability
  • Surgery (endoscopic plantar fasciotomy) Last resort

Injection risks: Fat pad atrophy, fascia rupture (rare), post-injection pain

Follow-up & Prognosis
Expected Timeline
80%
Improve by 12 months
Non-surgical treatment
90%
Relief by 3-6 months
Conservative treatment
Review Points
  • 4-6 weeks: Assess exercise adherence and symptom response Key milestone
  • 3 months: Consider referral if no improvement Action point
  • 6 months: Specialist referral if persistent Escalation
  • Long-term: 54% asymptomatic at 10 years, 46% persistent symptoms
When to Escalate
  • Persistent severe pain >3 months Refer
  • Significant functional limitation Refer
  • Diagnostic uncertainty Refer
  • Bilateral symptoms (worse prognosis) Monitor closely
  • Systemic features (e.g., enthesitis elsewhere) Rheumatology
Prognostic Factors: Female sex, bilateral symptoms, and presence of systemic disease are associated with worse outcomes.
