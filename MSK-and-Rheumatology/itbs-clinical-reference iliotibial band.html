Rule Out These Conditions First

ITBS is a clinical diagnosis. If any red flags present, investigate alternative/additional diagnoses before proceeding with ITBS management.

Clinical Overview

Definition

Overuse injury from repetitive friction of the ITB sliding over the lateral femoral epicondyle. The most common cause of lateral knee pain in runners and cyclists (up to 22.2% of lower extremity injuries).

Key Characteristic

Impingement zone occurs at ~30째 of knee flexion during deceleration into stance-phase running.

Risk Factors

Extrinsic (Training) Factors
  • High weekly mileage - repetitive irritation at impingement zone
  • Sudden mileage increase - over-training phase
  • Downhill running - increased knee flexion forces
  • Cambered surfaces - lateral knee stress
  • Worn-out shoes - loss of proper foot support
Intrinsic (Anatomical) Factors
  • Weak hip abductors - primary causative factor
  • Genu varum (bow leg)
  • Pes cavus (high arch)
  • Prominent lateral femoral epicondyle
  • Leg length discrepancy

Diagnostic Tests

Noble's Compression Test

Patient supine/side-lying. Apply pressure over lateral femoral epicondyle while extending knee from 90째 flexion.

Positive: Pain at ~30째 knee flexion

Ober's Test

Patient side-lying. Stabilize pelvis, flex affected knee 90째, extend and abduct hip, then slowly lower leg to table.

Positive: Leg cannot adduct to horizontal (ITB tight)

Modified Thomas's Test

Patient supine holding unaffected knee to chest. Slowly lower affected limb toward floor.

Positive: Femur angle below horizontal

Rule Out Differentials

Condition Key Differentiators
Lateral Meniscal Tear Joint line tenderness, positive McMurray's test, MRI changes
LCL Sprain Pain over LCL, positive stress tests
Stress Fracture Bony tenderness, MRI/bone scan evidence
Degenerative Joint Disease Activity-related pain/stiffness, X-ray findings (osteophytes, joint space narrowing)
Popliteal Tendinopathy Tenderness of popliteal tendon, positive Figure-4 test
Biceps Femoris Tendinopathy Pain over biceps femoris, pain with resisted knee flexion

Imaging Indications

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Recurrent or refractory ITBS
  • Suspected alternative pathology

Treatment Phases

Phase 1: Acute (Inflammation)

Goals: Reduce inflammation, pain relief

  • NSAIDs (Diclofenac 50mg TID or Ibuprofen 400-600mg Q4-6H)
  • Activity modification - limit to symptom threshold
  • Ice therapy
  • Refractory cases: Corticosteroid injection (Methylprednisolone 40mg + Lidocaine 10mg)
Phase 2: Sub-Acute

Goals: Restore flexibility, address myofascial restrictions

  • ITB stretching exercises
  • Soft tissue therapy (foam rolling)
  • Trigger point release
Phase 3: Recovery & Strengthening

Goals: Improve hip abductor strength, correct movement patterns

  • Hip abductor strengthening (side-lying abduction)
  • Progressive resistance training (>60% MVIC)
  • Single-leg squat exercises
  • Motor control training (reduce excessive hip adduction)

Return to Sport Protocol

Entry Criteria

Must be able to perform open-chain AND closed-chain strengthening exercises pain-free before starting return to running.

Week 1-2: Gradual Reintroduction

Run on alternate days, easy sprints on level ground, avoid downhill running entirely

Week 3-4: Build Volume

Faster-paced running daily, begin gradual distance increases if asymptomatic

Week 5+: Sport-Specific Training

Further gradual increases in distance and frequency, reintroduce terrain variations slowly

Key Cues for Running Analysis

  • Trunk lean posture
  • Crossover foot strike pattern
  • Extended knee at heel strike
  • Low stride rate
  • Center of mass posterior to heel

Prevention Strategies

Avoid These Training Patterns

  • Always running same direction
  • Abrupt mileage/frequency increases
  • Running on cambered surfaces
  • Worn-out or incorrect shoes

Recommended Actions

  • Regular ITB stretching
  • Hip abductor strengthening
  • Gradual training progression
  • Appropriate footwear replacement

Prognosis & Follow-up

Expected Outcome

Most patients recover from ITBS with 6 weeks of conservative therapy. However, ITBS typically follows a fluctuating course with potential for relapse.

Patient Counseling Points

  • Expect chronic relapsing course
  • Adherence to rehab crucial
  • Some experience long remissions

When to Consider Surgery

  • Refractory to 6+ months conservative treatment
  • Persistent impingement symptoms
  • PLAR technique or ITB resection

ITBS Injury Grading

Grade Presentation
Grade 1 Pain only 1-3 hrs after training, resolves within 24 hrs
Grade 2 Minimal pain at end of training run, no performance impact
Grade 3 Earlier onset pain, interferes with speed/duration
Grade 4 Pain restricts training AND daily activities, cannot continue sport
Grade 5 Pain affects training AND ADLs significantly
