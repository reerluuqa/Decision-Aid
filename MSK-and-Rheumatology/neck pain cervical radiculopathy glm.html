Neck Pain - Cervical Radiculopathy

Last revised: November 2023

RED FLAGS - URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED

Immediate specialist advice or urgent referral if severe/progressive motor weakness or sensory loss

Definition

Radiculopathy is a neurological state where conduction is limited along a spinal nerve or its roots. Cervical radiculopathy describes pain in upper extremities corresponding to the dermatome of the involved cervical nerve root.

Prevalence

121-580 per 100,000 people

Most affected

People aged 50-54 years

Gender difference

Men: 107/100,000 | Women: 64/100,000

Causes

  • 22% Cervical disc herniation
  • Common Spondylosis (degenerative changes)
  • Less common Spinal instability and trauma

Risk Factors

  • Age Middle-aged people
  • Sex More prevalent in men
  • Race People of white ethnicity
  • Lifestyle Smoking, sports stressing cervical spine
  • Medical history Prior lumbar radiculopathy, neck trauma
  • Occupation Heavy lifting, operating vibrating equipment

Prognosis

Most people improve regardless of treatment:

  • 88% Improve within 4 weeks with non-operative management
  • Good Long-term prognosis

Assessment Approach

History Taking

  • Signs and symptoms
  • Occupational history
  • Medical history
  • Symptoms of anxiety or depression
  • Previous injury or infection
  • History of cancer
  • Presence of fever

Physical Examination

  • Neck appearance and range of motion
  • Palpation for tenderness
  • Skin inspection
  • Kernig's and Brudzinski's signs (if meningitis suspected)

Diagnostic Tests

Physical Tests

Spurling test: Flex neck laterally, rotate and press on head. Positive if causes radicular arm pain.
Arm squeeze test: Squeeze middle third of upper arm. Positive if pain score increases by ≥3 points.
Axial traction: Combination with positive Spurling test increases likelihood of diagnosis.
Upper limb neurodynamic tests: Can rule out cervical radiculopathy.

Imaging

Not routinely required for diagnosis of neck pain with radiculopathy.

MRI indicated for:
  • Suspicion of myelopathy or abscess
  • Persistent or progressive neurologic findings
  • Failure to improve after 4-6 weeks of conservative treatment

Neurological Features by Nerve Root

Nerve Root Muscle Weakness Reflex Changes Sensory Changes
C5 Shoulder abduction/flexion, Elbow flexion Biceps Lateral arm
C6 Elbow flexion, Wrist extension Biceps, Supinator Lateral forearm, Thumb, Index finger

T1 Finger abduction/adduction None Medial upper forearm, Lower arm

Initial Management (4-6 weeks)

Provide reassurance and information: Most cases improve without surgery.
Encourage activity: Home exercise and return to normal lifestyle/work as soon as possible.
Driving advice: Do not drive if neck range of motion is restricted.
Avoid cervical collars: They restrict mobility and may prolong symptoms.
Recommend firm pillow: Should provide lateral support and support the hollow of the neck.

Medication Management

NSAIDs Guidelines

  • Regularly review appropriateness, especially in older people
  • Consider lower-risk NSAIDs or stopping treatment
  • Consider alternatives before prescribing NSAIDs
  • Choose NSAIDs with lowest risk based on individual factors
  • Use lowest effective dose and shortest duration
  • Ibuprofen (≤1,200 mg/day) or naproxen (≤1,000 mg/day) preferred

PPI Co-prescribing

Co-prescribe a proton pump inhibitor with NSAIDs for:

  • People with osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis
  • Elderly patients
  • Those with lower back pain
  • Axial spondyloarthritis, psoriatic arthritis
  • People at moderate/high risk for GI adverse effects

Referral Criteria

Urgent Referral Required:
  • Severe or progressive motor weakness
  • Severe or progressive sensory loss
  • Any red flag symptoms (see Red Flags tab)
Consider Referral:
  • Failure to improve after 4-6 weeks of conservative treatment
  • Persistent or progressive neurologic findings
  • Suspicion of myelopathy or abscess

IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

Refer urgently or arrange immediate assessment if any red flags are present

Malignancy, Infection, Inflammation

Fever, night sweats, or unexplained weight loss
Excruciating pain, cervical lymphadenopathy
Intractable night pain, increasing pain
Exquisite tenderness over vertebral body
Generalised neck stiffness
Nausea or vomiting
New or severe headache
Photophobia or phonophobia
Visual loss
Skin erythema, wounds, or exudate

Cervical Myelopathy

Paresis
Sensory changes or loss of sensation
Altered muscle tone
Clumsy or weak hands
Gait disturbance
Babinski's sign (up-going plantar reflex)
Hyper-reflexia, clonus, spasticity
Hoffman's sign
Lhermitte's sign (electric shock-type sensation with neck flexion)
Profound weakness of hands, bowel/bladder dysfunction

Other Red Flags

Neurological:
  • Altered cognitive state
  • Weakness involving >1 myotome
  • Sensory loss involving >1 dermatome
  • Headaches, facial pain, ataxia, vertigo
Age-related:
  • New symptoms before 20 years
  • New symptoms after 55 years
Medical History:
  • Inflammatory arthritis
  • Cancer
  • Tuberculosis
  • Immunosuppression
  • Drug abuse, AIDS
Trauma:
  • Violent trauma (RTA)
  • Fall from height
  • Minor trauma in osteoporosis risk
