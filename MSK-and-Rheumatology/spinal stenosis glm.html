Spinal Stenosis Quick Reference

Point of Care Clinical Tool

Last Updated: Dec 14, 2021

Red Flags & Emergency Actions

Emergency Cauda Equina Syndrome
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction
  • Saddle paraesthesiae
  • Severe lower extremity weakness
Action: Immediate surgical referral
Urgent Progressive Neurological Deficit
  • Sensory or motor loss occurs in up to 30% of patients
  • Severe morbidity from reduction in walking ability
Action: Surgical decompression within 24-48 hours

Overview

Definition

Degenerative conditions of the lumbar spine that narrow the spinal canal, lateral recesses, and neural foramina.

Symptoms result from neural compression of the cauda equina, exiting nerve roots, or both.

Epidemiology
  • Symptomatic in patients aged 50-69 years
  • 80% of women and 95% of men over 65 have radiographic evidence
  • Most common at L4-L5, then L3-L4, L2-L3, and L5-S1

Key Symptoms

Neurogenic Claudication
Symptom Description
Leg pain when walking Pain and weakness in thighs and calves, sensation of numbness in lower extremities
Relief with flexion Forced to lean forwards while walking, eventually sit down to gain relief
"Shopping cart sign" Leaning forwards onto a shopping trolley to flex the spine and increase canal size
Back pain Related to activity, may result from degenerative changes or spondylolisthesis

Diagnostic Approach

Key Diagnostic Factors
  • Age >40 years
  • Previous back injury/surgery
  • Manual labour
  • Insidious onset
  • Duration: months to years
  • Stooped posture when walking
  • Leg numbness/paraesthesiae
  • Absence of examination findings
Imaging
Preferred

MRI (T2-weighted) - Best technique to show neural element compression

Supportive

X-ray - Shows degenerative changes, spondylolisthesis

Alternative

CT myelography - For patients unable to undergo MRI

Treatment Options

Non-Surgical Management
First-line

NSAIDs - Several weeks course

Adjunct

Physiotherapy - Individualized exercise regimen

Second-line

Epidural corticosteroid injections - For radicular pain when oral medicine/physiotherapy inadequate

Surgical Management
Indication

Symptoms persisting 3-6 months despite medical management

Procedure

Decompressive spinal surgery - Bilateral laminectomy of involved segments

With fusion

For degenerative spondylolisthesis or degenerative scoliosis

Key Differentials

Common Peripheral Vascular Disease
  • Cramping pain that begins distally and progresses proximally
  • No improvement with lumbar flexion
  • Difficulty walking uphill
  • Decreased pedal pulses, trophic skin changes
Common Lumbosacral Disc Herniation
  • Unilateral radiculopathy rather than neurogenic claudication
  • Acute onset
  • Straight leg raise often positive
  • More common in younger patients (30-60 years)
Less Common Metastatic Disease
  • History of malignancy and/or unexplained weight loss
  • Pain worse at night, unable to find position of comfort
  • Short duration of symptoms (days to months)
Less Common Spinal Infection
  • History of tuberculosis, HIV, or IV drug use
  • Fever, malaise, back pain
  • WBC count, CRP, ESR elevated
