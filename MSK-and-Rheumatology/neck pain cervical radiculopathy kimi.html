Initial Assessment
Red Flag Screening
Always screen for serious pathology first
Red Flag Symptoms
URGENT REFERRAL
  • Fever, night sweats, unexplained weight loss
  • Excruciating pain or intractable night pain
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy
  • History of cancer, immunosuppression, TB
  • Recent infection or IV drug use
  • Significant trauma (or minor trauma with osteoporosis)
  • New severe headache, photophobia, meningism
  • Myelopathy signs: gait disturbance, hand clumsiness, weakness
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction
  • Age <20 or >55 with new symptoms
Key History Points
  • Occupational history (heavy lifting, vibrating equipment)
  • Prior neck/nerve injuries or lumbar radiculopathy
  • Lifestyle factors (smoking, contact sports)
  • Symptoms of anxiety/depression
  • Exact pain distribution (dermatomal pattern)
  • Duration and progression of symptoms
Physical Examination

Inspect:

  • Postural asymmetry (head tilt)
  • Muscle wasting if chronic
  • Skin changes (rash, infection)
  • Range of motion restrictions

Special Tests:

  • Spurling Test: Flex, rotate, press on head (+ = radicular pain)
  • Arm Squeeze: Compress middle upper arm (+ = pain ≥3/10 worse than other areas)
  • Axial Traction: Relieves symptoms
  • Neurological Exam: Myotomes, dermatomes, reflexes
Red Flags → Immediate Referral No Red Flags → Continue Assessment
Clinical Diagnosis
Typical Symptoms
Classic Presentation
  • Unilateral neck, shoulder, and arm pain following dermatomal pattern
  • Sensory symptoms: numbness, tingling, shooting pains (more common than motor)
  • Motor symptoms: weakness, muscle spasm
  • Pain may be severe enough to wake patient at night
  • Gradual onset (may be abrupt)
  • Most common: C7 nerve root, then C6
Neurological Features
Nerve Root Muscle Weakness Reflex Changes Sensory Changes
C5 Shoulder abduction/flexion, Elbow flexion Biceps Lateral arm
C6 Elbow flexion, Wrist extension Biceps, Supinator Lateral forearm, Thumb, Index
C7 Elbow extension, Wrist flexion, Finger extension Triceps Middle finger
C8 Finger flexion None Medial forearm, Ring/Little fingers
T1 Finger abduction/adduction None Medial upper forearm, Lower arm

Multi-level involvement suggests widespread neurological disorder

Differential Diagnosis
  • Acute torticollis, whiplash injury
  • Rotator cuff syndrome, biceps tendonitis
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome
  • Complex regional pain syndrome
  • Herpes zoster (before rash)
  • Parsonage-Turner syndrome (brachial plexopathy)
  • Migraine, cervicogenic headache
  • Malignancy (thyroid, esophageal, lung)
MRI Indicated if: >4-6 weeks, progressive neuro signs, or high suspicion <4 weeks + no deficits → No imaging needed
Management Strategy
Key Principle
88% improve within 4 weeks with conservative management
Present <4-6 Weeks, No Objective Signs
Conservative Management
  • Reassurance: Excellent prognosis, most cases improve without surgery
  • Activity: Encourage normal activities and early return to work
  • Driving: Advise against if neck ROM restricted
  • Pillows: Firm pillow with lateral support, avoid two pillows
  • Cervical Collars: Discourage (restrict mobility, prolong symptoms)
Pharmacological Management

Analgesics:

  • NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen 1200mg/day, naproxen 1000mg/day) - preferred
  • Paracetamol
  • Codeine (if severe)

Neuropathic Agents:

  • Amitriptyline
  • Duloxetine
  • Pregabalin or Gabapentin
NSAIDs: Use lowest effective dose, shortest duration, co-prescribe PPI if high GI risk
Non-Pharmacological Management
  • Physiotherapy: Strengthening, stretching exercises, manual therapy
  • Home Exercise Program: Posture correction, neck mobility
  • Activity Modification: Ergonomic assessment
  • Pain Education: Address concerns, set realistic expectations
Referral Criteria
≥4-6 Weeks Duration Objective Neurological Signs Progressive Motor Weakness

Refer for:

  • MRI confirmation of diagnosis
  • Specialist assessment for invasive procedures

Invasive Options:

  • Interlaminar cervical epidural injections
  • Transforaminal injections
  • Surgical decompression
Surgery Indicated: Unremitting pain >6-12 weeks despite conservative Rx + MRI compression
Follow-up & Monitoring
Review Schedule
  • Initial Review: 2-3 weeks after starting treatment
  • Subsequent Reviews: Every 4-6 weeks as needed
  • Reassess: If symptoms worsen or new deficits appear
Red Flags for Reassessment
URGENT REVIEW
  • Progressive motor weakness
  • New sensory loss
  • Worsening pain despite treatment
  • Development of myelopathy signs
  • Bowel or bladder dysfunction
  • Signs of systemic illness (fever, weight loss)
Outcome Measures

Monitor for Improvement:

  • Pain reduction (VAS score)
  • Return of neurological function
  • Improvement in daily activities
  • Patient-reported outcome measures

Expected Course:

  • Most improvement within 4 weeks
  • 85-90% resolution by 6-12 weeks
  • Some may have residual mild symptoms
Complications & Comorbidities
  • Chronic radiculopathy: May lead to persistent deficits
  • Functional impairment: Difficulty with ADLs, work limitations
  • Psychosocial: Depression, anxiety common - screen and manage
  • Recurrence: Education on prevention strategies
Discharge when: Stable/improving, no red flags Persistent >12 weeks → Consider specialist referral
