Popliteal Cyst (Baker's Cyst)

Quick Clinical Reference Tool
EMERGENCY ALERT: Ruptured cyst can mimic DVT (pseudothrombophlebitis). Misdiagnosis with anticoagulation can lead to compartment syndrome. Always confirm with imaging before treatment.

Red Flags & Emergencies

  • Acute cyst rupture - Presents as sudden calf pain, swelling, and ecchymosis mimicking DVT
    IMMEDIATE IMAGING REQUIRED
  • Compartment syndrome - Sensory or motor deficits secondary to elevated intra-compartmental pressures
    SURGICAL EMERGENCY
  • Septic arthritis - Particularly in patients with osteoarthritis
    URGENT ORTHOPEDIC EVALUATION
  • Popliteal artery compression - May cause intermittent claudication
    VASCULAR CONSULTATION

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Popliteal bulge - Visible as a bulge behind the knee, particularly noticeable when standing
  • Knee pain - From the cyst itself or underlying cause (arthritis, meniscal tear)
  • Leg swelling - Especially with cyst rupture/dissection
  • Calf tenderness - Particularly with large or ruptured cysts

Safe Actions & Treatment

NO TREATMENT REQUIRED

Asymptomatic patients require no specific treatment, but evaluate for underlying knee pathology.

CONSERVATIVE TREATMENT
  • Physiotherapy
  • Simple analgesics (aspirin or paracetamol)
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400 mg orally every 4-6 hours)
TREAT UNDERLYING PATHOLOGY
  • Knee joint aspiration if effusion present
  • Intra-articular corticosteroid injection
  • Ultrasound-guided fenestration with injection
  • Direct aspiration of large cysts (may recur)

Investigations

Test Indication Key Finding
1st Line Duplex ultrasound Clinical suspicion Cystic mass in posterior-medial popliteal fossa
2nd Line MRI If ultrasound negative Identifies underlying knee pathology
Adjunct X-ray Initial evaluation Identifies loose bodies, osteoarthritis

Definition & Pathophysiology

Definition: Popliteal cyst (Baker's cyst) is an accumulation of joint synovial fluid outside the knee joint, forming behind the knee in the interval between the semimembranosus and the medial gastrocnemius.

Pathophysiology

  • Increased intrasynovial pressure causes synovial capsule to bulge at area lacking external anatomical support
  • Weak area between semimembranosus muscle and medial gastrocnemius
  • Posterior horn of medial meniscus is only reinforcing structure at this location

Risk Factors

Knee joint trauma - Medial meniscus injury, chondral lesions, ACL tears
Underlying knee arthritis - Rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis
Joint infection - TB, coccidioidomycosis, candidiasis
Increasing age - 26% in 31-50 years, 53% in 51-90 years

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Differentiating Features Diagnostic Test
Critical Deep vein thrombosis Risk factors for DVT, acute calf pain/swelling without knee pathology Venous ultrasound shows thrombus
Popliteal haematoma Recent trauma or anticoagulant use Ultrasound shows haematoma
Popliteal muscle tear Pain out of proportion localized at tear site Ultrasound/MRI shows muscle tear
Knee effusion Excessive intra-articular fluid in areas other than cyst location Ultrasound/MRI shows effusion
Popliteal artery aneurysm Pulsatile mass in popliteal fossa Arterial ultrasound shows aneurysm
