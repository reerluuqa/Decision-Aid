Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis - Interactive Clinical Pathway

Unstable = Urgent Stable = Timely Prophylactic = Consider
Assessment & History

Key Clinical Pearl

With unexplained unilateral knee pain in an adolescent, always consider the hip as the source. SCFE commonly presents with referred pain to the knee.

Critical History Questions

  • Onset of pain (acute vs. insidious)?
  • Location: hip, groin, thigh, and/or medial knee?
  • Ability to bear weight? Critical for stability classification
  • Recent trauma or sports injury?
  • Recent growth spurt?
  • Weight percentile?
  • Symptoms of endocrine disorders?

Physical Examination Findings

Obligatory External Rotation Pathognomonic

Hip flexion causes automatic external rotation due to deformity. Test with passive/active flexion.

Gait Analysis

Externally rotated leg, limp, positive Trendelenburg test (trunk leans toward affected side)

Range of Motion

Restricted ROM, especially internal rotation

Weight Assessment Risk Stratification

>90th percentile
Major risk factor for idiopathic SCFE (63.2% of cases)
<50th percentile
Consider endocrine disorder as contributory factor

Classification Systems

Stability Classification (Most Widely Accepted):

STABLE

Can bear weight with or without support. Accounts for ~90% of cases.

UNSTABLE

Unable to bear weight. Higher risk of AVN. Requires urgent surgical intervention within 24 hours.

Based on Loder classification - determines prognosis for avascular necrosis and urgency of intervention.

Duration Classification

  • Acute: Prodromal symptoms <3 weeks prior to acute fracture-like event
  • Chronic: Symptoms >3 weeks
  • Acute-on-chronic: Symptoms >3 weeks with sudden onset of increased pain

Critical Decision Point

Weight-bearing ability is the single most important factor to determine in initial assessment. It directly influences urgency of surgical intervention and risk of complications.

Diagnosis & Investigation

Immediate Action

ADVISED NOT TO BEAR WEIGHT → Immediate referral to orthopaedic surgeon

First-line Investigations

Bilateral AP and frog-leg lateral radiographs Essential

[Klein Line X-ray Assessment]
Normal: Klein line intersects epiphysis
SCFE: Klein line does NOT intersect epiphysis

AP View Findings

  • Klein line fails to intersect femoral head
  • Widened physis
  • Loss of Capener's sign (metaphysis overlap)
  • Blanch sign of Steel (double shadow)

Frog-leg Lateral View More Sensitive

  • Posteriorly displaced capital femoral epiphysis
  • Bloomberg's sign (blurred/widened physis)
  • Better for early slip detection

Important: Valgus SCFE (5% of cases) is difficult to recognize on AP films. Lateral and contralateral comparison views are essential.

Laboratory Tests (Consider if indicated)

Metabolic Panel

If renal osteodystrophy suspected

Thyroid Function

If hypothyroidism suspected (elevated TSH)

Growth Hormone

If growth hormone deficiency suspected

Vitamin D

Associated with moderate risk increase (RR 1.42)

Differential Diagnoses

Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease Age <10 years

Differentiating: Younger age, sclerosis/cysts/collapse on x-ray

Septic Arthritis Emergency

Differentiating: Severe pain, fever, elevated inflammatory markers

Avascular Necrosis

Differentiating: Older age (30-50), can result from SCFE itself

Hip Dysplasia

Differentiating: Shallow acetabulum, may have leg shortening

Stress Fracture

Differentiating: Overuse history, endurance athlete

Osteomyelitis

Differentiating: Constitutional symptoms, positive cultures

Timing Priority

Unstable SCFE should be treated within 24 hours of instability onset to minimize avascular necrosis risk.

Treatment Algorithm

Overall Treatment Goal

Prevent progression of slip through in situ fixation of the epiphysis. Prophylactic fixation of contralateral hip may be necessary in selected cases.

Treatment Decision Tree

SCFE Diagnosis

Assess Stability

Unstable vs. Stable

Determine Urgency

Unstable: Within 24h
Stable: Timely scheduling

Unstable SCFE URGENT

Timing: Within 24 hours of instability onset

Meta-analysis shows treatment within 24h is associated with lower risk of AVN compared to delayed treatment.

1st Line: Urgent Surgical Repair

  • Percutaneous decompression of hip joint
  • Gentle repositioning of the slip (avoid forcible reduction)
  • Stabilization with 1-2 screws or wires

Alternative: Open Reduction + Internal Fixation Specialized Centers

Modified Dunn procedure (surgical hip dislocation + subcapital osteotomy)

  • Restores proximal femoral anatomy
  • May decrease AVN risk in experienced hands
  • Technically demanding, AVN rates variable (0-29%)
  • Restrict to specialized centers until long-term outcomes established

Evidence Summary

Meta-analysis of 4 studies found no significant difference in AVN risk between reduced vs. unreduced unstable SCFE. However, timing matters - 24-hour window is critical.

Adjunct: Prophylactic Contralateral Fixation Consider

Low threshold in pathological SCFE (endocrine/metabolic). No consensus for idiopathic cases.

Stable SCFE TIMELY

1st Line: In Situ Single-Screw Fixation

Widely accepted first-line treatment

  • Screw placed in center of epiphysis on both AP and lateral views
  • Easy technique with low subsequent slip rate
  • Post-op: Toe-touch weight bearing for 2 weeks → then weight bearing as tolerated

2nd Line: Open Reduction Severe Slips

Used most frequently for severe slips. More controversial for moderate slips.

Consider for:

  • Severe deformity causing impingement
  • Cosmetic concerns
  • Functional limitations

3rd Line: Bone Graft Epiphysiodesis Historical

Antiquated technique with disadvantages:

  • Wide exposure, blood loss, longer hospital stay
  • Risk of further slippage until physis closes

Prophylactic Fixation of Contralateral Hip

Pathological SCFE

Low threshold for prophylactic fixation when underlying metabolic/endocrine disorder present

Idiopathic SCFE

No consensus. Consider case-by-case based on risk factors.

Risk Factors for Contralateral Slip:

Posterior Sloping Angle (PSA)

Most predictive factor. PSA >13° in girls suggests high risk. Younger patients with high PSA most likely to benefit.

Other Variables

Sex, symptom duration, obesity, trauma, severity of index slip, laterality, age, bone age

Late Deformity & Corrective Surgery

Indicated for disabling external rotation deformity causing gait disturbance and femoroacetabular impingement.

Cuneiform Osteotomy Technically Demanding

Through femoral neck - achieves better correction but higher osteonecrosis risk

Inter-trochanteric Osteotomy Less Demanding

Southwick's or Imhauser's procedures - lower osteonecrosis risk

Emerging Options

Free-Gliding Screw: Theoretical benefit of allowing ongoing longitudinal growth and remodeling. Requires further long-term studies to identify appropriate candidates.

Follow-up & Complications

Monitoring Protocol

  • 2 weeks post-op: Wound check, assess weight-bearing ability
  • Periodic x-rays: Ensure no further slippage and monitor physeal closure
  • Instructions: Emphasize compliance as pain subsides (common issue)

Chondrolysis Rare with Current Methods

Definition: Acute dissolution of articular cartilage with rapid progressive joint stiffness and pain

Risk Factors: Persistent pin penetration, manipulative reduction, prolonged spica immobilization

Diagnosis: X-ray shows <50% joint space reduction or <3mm with bilateral involvement

Management: Protected weight-bearing, physiotherapy, anti-inflammatories

Prognosis: Better prognosis than osteonecrosis

Osteonecrosis (Avascular Necrosis) Most Serious

Prevalence: Unstable SCFE has 9.4-fold increased risk vs stable (pooled prevalence ~20%)

Stable SCFE: Low prevalence

Risk Factors: Unstable SCFE, forcible reduction, pin penetration of posterosuperior quadrant, multiple pins

Diagnosis: Hip/thigh/knee pain, restricted ROM. X-ray shows collapse, cysts, sclerosis. MRI confirms.

Management: Protected weight-bearing, physiotherapy, bisphosphonates, surgical options (decompression, grafting)

Contralateral SCFE High Risk

Incidence: ~11% in US retrospective review

Prevention: Prophylactic fixation in selected high-risk cases (pathological SCFE, PSA >13°)

Late Deformity & Impingement

Mechanism: Persistent retroverted deformity causing gait disturbance and femoroacetabular impingement

Management: Corrective osteotomy (cuneiform vs inter-trochanteric)

Prognosis Factors

Good Outcomes

  • Mild slip severity
  • Stable SCFE
  • Successful surgery
  • No serious complications

Poorer Outcomes

  • ⚠ Severe slip
  • ⚠ Unstable SCFE
  • ⚠ AVN complication
  • ⚠ Underlying endocrine disorder

Long-term Outcomes

  • 33% of patients report persistent mild pain after 16-year follow-up
  • ~5% require total hip arthroplasty at 20-year follow-up (most commonly due to AVN)
  • Obesity contributes to long-term disability risk
  • 85-87% have some degree of cartilage/labral damage at surgery

Patient Discussions

  • Crutches for few weeks post-surgery
  • Weight monitoring and healthy lifestyle counseling
  • Importance of follow-up compliance
  • Long-term activity modifications if needed

Algorithm based on BMJ Best Practice (May 2025). Individual clinical judgment should be applied to each case.
