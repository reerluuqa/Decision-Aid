Assessment

Initial evaluation of the patient

‚ñº
1

Patient History

  • Duration of symptoms and pain location
  • Impact on walking and daily activities
  • Footwear difficulties and preferences
  • Family history of bunions
  • Previous treatments attempted
  • Comorbidities (vascular disease, neuropathy)
2

Physical Examination

  • Observe gait and lower limb alignment
  • Examine foot in standing and seated positions
  • Assess hallux deformity and pes planus
  • Check skin quality and complications
  • Palpate for pain and tenderness
  • Assess neurovascular status
  • Evaluate range of motion
  • Examine footwear and fit
Radiology: Do not routinely arrange foot X-ray in primary care. Reserve imaging for pre-surgical planning or suspected complications.

Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis and rule out alternatives

‚ñº
1

Suspect Bunion if Key Features

  • Progressive pain at medial first MTP joint
  • Difficulty wearing shoes
  • Lateral deviation of hallux at first MTP joint
  • Medial prominence of first metatarsal head
2

Differential Diagnosis Consider

  • Hallux rigidus - OA with stiffness and rigidity
  • Hallux interphalangeus - lateral deviation at tip only
  • Gout - acute inflammatory features
  • Chronic sesamoiditis - activity-related pain
  • Fracture - trauma history, point tenderness
  • Inflammatory arthritis - multiple joint involvement
  • Peripheral neuropathy - sensory changes
  • Osteomyelitis - systemic features, severe pain
Urgent Red Flags:
  • Non-healing foot ulcer
  • Suspected peripheral limb ischaemia
  • Systemic signs of infection

Treatment

Management options based on severity

‚ñº
1

Conservative Management First Line

  • Footwear advice: Low-heeled, wide-fitting shoes
  • Orthotic devices: Insoles, toe spacers, bunion pads
  • Pain relief: Paracetamol and/or NSAIDs
  • Physical measures: Ice for pain/inflammation
  • Podiatry referral: Footwear assessment, night splints
  • MSK referral: Balance training, calf stretches
2

Surgical Indications Referral

  • Progressive symptoms/deformity after 3 months conservative tx
  • Significant impact on daily functioning
  • Unable to wear suitable footwear
  • Second toe involvement
  • Persistent pain affecting quality of life
Note: Not for cosmetic/prophylactic reasons alone
3

Surgical Options Specialist

  • Osteotomy: First metatarsal or proximal phalanx
  • Soft tissue procedures: Ligament/tendon balancing
  • Arthrodesis: MTP or TMT joint fusion (for OA/neuropathy)
  • Minimally invasive: Still under evaluation
Complications: Infection, stiffness, transfer metatarsalgia, recurrence, nerve damage

Follow-up

Monitoring and ongoing care

‚ñº
1

Conservative Treatment Follow-up

  • 6-8 weeks: Assess response to conservative measures
  • 3 months: Evaluate treatment success/failure
  • Ongoing: Monitor for progression
  • Annual: Review footwear and symptom control
2

Post-Surgical Follow-up Specialist

  • 2 weeks: Wound check, suture removal
  • 6 weeks: X-ray assessment of bone healing
  • 3 months: Assess functional recovery
  • 6-12 months: Final outcome assessment
  • Long-term: Monitor for recurrence
3

Warning Signs Action

  • Worsening pain or deformity
  • New ulcer formation
  • Signs of infection
  • Stiffness or loss of function
  • Transfer metatarsalgia
  • Recurrence after surgery
