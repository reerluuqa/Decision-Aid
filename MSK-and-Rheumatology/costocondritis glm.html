Costochondritis Clinical Reference

Quick-scan guide for diagnosis and management
RED FLAGS
Always rule out cardiac emergencies before diagnosing costochondritis
Key Diagnostic Factors
Chest pain
Typically insidious onset, worse on movement of the chest wall especially with inspiration; usually unilateral
Chest-wall tenderness
Elicited by pressing slightly over the costochondral joints. May also affect sternoclavicular joint, rib, and adjacent muscle
Chest-wall swelling
If present in addition to costochondritis, this is known as Tietze's syndrome
Management Approach
Inflammatory
Infective
1
NSAIDs (10-14 days)
Ibuprofen 400-800mg every 6-8 hours (max 2400mg/day) OR Naproxen 250-500mg twice daily

2
Corticosteroid injection (if NSAIDs ineffective)
Methylprednisolone acetate 10-20mg intra-articularly with lidocaine
Usually performed by specialist (rheumatologist)
3
Re-evaluate if no response after 3-4 weeks
Consider referral to rheumatologist to rule out underlying rheumatic disease
1
Surgical drainage and/or debridement
Antibiotics alone are usually ineffective due to poor blood supply to cartilage

Appropriate antibiotic coverage
Based on culture results and local sensitivities

Risk Factors
Strong risk factors
• Recent prolonged coughing
• Repetitive upper-limb movement
• Sternotomy wound infection
• Intravenous drug use
Weak risk factors
• Female sex
• Age >40 years
Follow-up & Prognosis
Prognosis
Excellent for idiopathic costochondritis. Most patients respond to treatment. May relapse and last from weeks to a year, but eventually resolves spontaneously.
Monitoring
Follow up if no response after 3-4 weeks for reconsideration of diagnosis and further work-up.
Complications
• NSAID-related GI bleeding (short term, low risk)
• Post-surgical degenerative joint changes (long term, low risk)

Quick Reference

Red Flags
• Fever, weight loss, malaise
• Localized swelling and erythema
• History of IV drug use
• No response to NSAIDs
First-line Treatment
NSAIDs for 10-14 days
Consider PPI in high-risk patients
Key Diagnostic Sign
Pain on palpation of costochondral joints (particularly 2nd to 5th)
