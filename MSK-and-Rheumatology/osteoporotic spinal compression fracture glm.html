Osteoporotic Spinal Compression Fractures
Clinical Quick Reference Tool
URGENT: Neurological signs, severe unrelenting pain, or significant deformity require immediate CT/MRI and spine surgeon consultation.
Key Points
Most are isolated fractures of the anterior spinal column
Postmenopausal women and long-term corticosteroid users are most susceptible
Caused by flexion and axial compression loading
Most patients are successfully treated with conservative methods
Red Flags & Emergency Actions
Neurological deficits - Urgent CT/MRI and spine surgeon consultation
Severe unrelenting pain - Consider surgical intervention
Significant deformity - Surgical evaluation required
Loss of vertebral height >50% - May require surgical treatment
Segmental kyphosis >20Â° - Suggests posterior ligamentous injury
Multiple adjacent compression fractures - May require surgical treatment
Diagnostic Approach
Presentation
Examination
Imaging
Key Presentations:
  • Often incidental finding on chest/abdominal x-rays
  • Acute onset back pain from minimal trauma (standing, bending, coughing)
  • Pain aggravated by movement, may disturb sleep
  • May radiate bilaterally to abdomen but rarely to legs
Kyphosis - Local at fracture site, may cause neck pain
Loss of height - Particularly with multiple fractures
Local tenderness - At fracture site if recent
Neurological exam - Essential to identify deficits
1
Plain X-rays (AP and lateral)
Initial investigation for all patients. Shows wedge fracture with loss of anterior vertebral height.
2
MRI/CT Spine
If neurological signs, concerns over stability, or suspicion of pathological fracture. MRI better for soft tissue and neural structures.
3
Bone Scan (Tc-99m)
Helpful to determine painful vertebrae and fracture acuity, particularly if MRI unavailable.
Conservative Management
Analgesia - To reduce pain and encourage mobilization
Short bed rest - Followed by early mobilization
Orthosis - Temporary use for select patients
Osteoporosis treatment - To prevent future fractures
Surgical Options
Vertebroplasty - For severe unrelenting pain
Kyphoplasty - For significant deformity
Open surgical stabilization - For potentially unstable acute fractures
Risk Factors
Older age
Previous fracture
Low body weight
Family history
Smoking
Postmenopausal status
Corticosteroid use
Alcohol (>2 units/day)
Follow-up & Monitoring
Radiographic and clinical follow-up - Every 6 weeks for 3 months post-injury
Monitor for complications - Progressive kyphosis, non-union, neurological deterioration
Bone density assessment - To guide osteoporosis management
Denis Classification
Column Components Stability
Anterior Anterior two-thirds of annulus and vertebral body, anterior longitudinal ligament Stable
Middle Posterior one-third of annulus and vertebral body, posterior longitudinal ligament Potentially unstable
Posterior All bony and tissue elements posterior to anterior and middle columns Unstable
