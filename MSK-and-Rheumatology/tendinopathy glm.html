Tendinopathy Quick Reference

Clinical decision support tool | Last updated: Mar 09, 2023
RED FLAGS - Immediate Referral Required
Complete tendon rupture - Sudden pain, loss of function, visible gap
Inability to bear weight - Especially Achilles or patellar tendon
Significant weakness - Rotator cuff with weakness persisting after local anesthesia
Systemic symptoms - Fever, night sweats, weight loss
Definition & Pathophysiology

Tendinopathy describes tendon degeneration characterized by pain, swelling, and impaired performance.

Key pathophysiological features:

  • Collagen fragmentation and loss of orientation
  • Increased type III collagen (thinner, less capable of forming bundles)
  • Increased proteoglycans
  • Neovascularization (potential pain source)
  • Failed healing process
Important: Tendinopathy is primarily degenerative, not inflammatory!
Common Sites
Rotator cuff (supraspinatus tendon) - Common with overhead activities
Lateral epicondyle (tennis elbow) - Extensor carpi radialis brevis origin
Medial epicondyle (golfer's elbow) - Wrist flexors origin
Patellar tendon (jumper's knee) - Inferior pole of patella
Quadriceps tendon - Superior pole of patella
Achilles tendon - 2-6 cm above calcaneal insertion

Diagnostic Approach
History

Key diagnostic factors:

  • Presence of risk factors
  • Insidious onset
  • Well-localized tenderness
  • Pain during activity
Physical Examination

Site-specific examination techniques:

  • Focal tenderness to palpation
  • Specific provocative tests
  • Assessment of strength and range of motion
Imaging (if needed)

Not required for diagnosis but may assist in recalcitrant cases:

  • Ultrasound - quick, inexpensive, operator-dependent
  • MRI - best for evaluating tendon injury and surrounding structures
  • X-ray - only for calcific tendinopathy or bony abnormalities

First-Line Management
Relative Rest & Activity Modification

Decrease mechanical overload to promote tendon healing and pain relief. Evaluate and correct athletic over-training errors.

Site-specific approaches:
  • Epicondylitis: Counterforce brace over proximal lower arm
  • Achilles: Heel lifts, immobilization for 7-10 days if acute
  • Patellar: Patellar tendon strap
Ice & NSAIDs

Ice provides acute pain relief. NSAIDs provide good short-term pain relief but have no effect on long-term outcomes.

Caution: NSAIDs may be more harmful than beneficial in chronic settings due to adverse effects.
Physiotherapy

Main component of conservative treatment. Strengthening and stretching exercises, 2-3 sessions per week for 6 weeks.

Eccentric exercises are particularly effective when done under guidance of experienced physiotherapist.

Strong Evidence
Adjunctive Treatments
Corticosteroid Injections

Effectively relieves pain and improves short-term function (<12 weeks) but symptoms may recur.

Effective for lateral epicondylitis
No benefit for patellar tendinopathy
Moderate Evidence
Extracorporeal Shockwave Therapy (ESWT)

Non-invasive treatment that passes acoustic shockwaves to the painful area.

Effective for calcific rotator cuff
No benefit for non-calcific tendinosis
Moderate Evidence
Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) Injection

Prepared from autologous blood, injected at site of injury. Evidence is inconsistent.

May benefit patients with chronic or recalcitrant patellar tendinopathy
Weak Evidence
When to Refer for Surgery
Refer for surgical evaluation if:
  • Persistent pain after 6-12 months of appropriate conservative treatment
  • Evidence of complete tendon tears
  • Significant functional limitation affecting quality of life
Evidence suggests patients should pursue conservative treatment for at least 6-12 months before considering surgery.
Follow-up & Monitoring
2-4 weeks

Assess response to initial treatment, adjust activity modification, ensure proper physiotherapy technique.

6-8 weeks

Evaluate need for adjunctive treatments if minimal improvement with first-line therapy.

3-6 months

Consider more invasive options (PRP, ESWT) if still not improving. Discuss surgical options if appropriate.

Activity modification with physiotherapy (stretching and strengthening)

Adjunct: Ice + NSAIDs

Second Line

If unable to tolerate physiotherapy due to severe pain:

Subacromial corticosteroid injection

If no improvement with physiotherapy and corticosteroids:

Glyceryl trinitrate patch

Percutaneous ultrasound-guided tenotomy
