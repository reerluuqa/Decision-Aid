Osteoarthritis Clinical Algorithm

Fast Reference Guide for Primary Care Professionals

Diagnosis

When to Suspect OA

  • Age ≥45 years
  • Activity-related joint pain
  • Morning joint stiffness lasting ≤30 minutes
  • Functional limitation

Clinical Assessment

  • History: Location, onset, duration, progression of symptoms
  • Pattern: Flares typically last 3-8 days
  • Risk factors: Obesity, previous joint injury, occupational stresses
  • Impact: Function, sleep, work, quality of life
  • Previous treatments: Self-help strategies and OTC medications

Investigations

Do NOT routinely X-ray to diagnose OA

Consider X-ray only if:

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Atypical features
  • Sudden worsening of symptoms

Hand OA Features

  • CMC, DIP, PIP joints
  • Heberden's/Bouchard's nodes
  • Squaring at joint
  • Ulnar/radial deviation

Hip OA Features

  • Groin pain
  • Limited internal rotation
  • Trendelenburg gait
  • Referred pain to thigh/knee

Knee OA Features

  • Compartmental pain
  • Crepitus
  • Varus/valgus deformity
  • Giving way/locking

Management

Core Principles

  1. Non-pharmacological first
  2. Tailored therapeutic exercise
  3. Weight management if overweight/obese
  4. Drug treatment at lowest effective dose for shortest time
  5. Non-surgical treatments for ≥3 months before surgery referral

Key Quality Standards (NICE)

  • Diagnose clinically without imaging if typical features
  • Provide information about condition and management
  • Advise tailored therapeutic exercise
  • Offer weight management support if needed
  • Agree follow-up plan with patient
  • Support non-surgical treatments for ≥3 months before surgery referral
  • Do not use scoring tools to determine surgical eligibility

Differential Diagnosis

Inflammatory Arthropathy

  • Prolonged morning stiffness >30 minutes
  • Night pain
  • Stiffness and pain relieved by activity
  • Multiple joints affected
  • Systemic symptoms

Other Conditions

  • Widespread pain disorders (e.g., fibromyalgia)
  • Septic joint: Red, hot, swollen joint + systemic symptoms
  • Fracture: History of trauma
  • Malignancy: Progressive pain unrelated to activity, night pain, unexplained mass

Red Flags for Referral

Refer When:

  • Atypical features suggesting alternative diagnosis
  • Rapid worsening of symptoms
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
  • Joint instability affecting function
  • Inadequate response to conservative management
