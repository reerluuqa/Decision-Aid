Sciatica Management Algorithm

Evidence-based clinical decision support for lumbar radiculopathy management

Based on NICE CKS Guidelines (January 2025)

Initial Assessment

Key Questions

  • Pain onset, duration, radiation below knee
  • Sensory changes (numbness, tingling)
  • Weakness or reflex changes
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction
  • History of cancer, trauma, infection
  • Occupational and psychosocial factors
Red Flags: Check for cauda equina syndrome, cancer, infection, fracture

Physical Examination

  • Observe gait and spine deformity
  • Assess range of motion (flexion/extension)
  • Neurological exam: strength, reflexes, sensation
  • Straight leg raise test (positive if <60°)
  • Hip examination to rule out hip pathology

Red Flag Symptoms

Cauda Equina Syndrome Spinal Fracture Infection Cancer/Metastasis
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction (urinary retention)
  • Saddle anesthesia
  • Progressive neurological weakness
  • Sudden bilateral radicular pain
  • Unrelenting night pain, fever, unexplained weight loss

Decision Point

Are red flags present?

YES → Emergency Referral NO → Continue to Diagnosis

Diagnosis Confirmation

Suspect Sciatica If:

  • Unilateral leg pain radiating below knee to foot/toes
  • Low back pain (if present) less severe than leg pain
  • Dermatomal numbness/tingling (L4-S1 distribution)
  • Myotomal weakness or reflex changes
  • Positive straight leg raise test
Typical Presentation

Investigations

Do NOT routinely X-ray to confirm diagnosis

  • Bloods if infection/inflammation suspected (FBC, ESR, CRP)
  • MRI only if red flags present or for surgical planning
  • Consider STarT Back tool for psychosocial risk

Differential Diagnosis

Peripheral Neuropathy Piriformis Syndrome Spinal Stenosis Hip Pathology Vascular Claudication

Examine hips, consider vascular assessment if claudication symptoms

Pathway Decision

Bilateral Sciatica: Urgent referral to musculoskeletal triage within 2 weeks
Unilateral Sciatica: Continue to management

Management & Treatment

Self-Management Advice

Prognosis: 50% recover within 6 weeks, most improve over time
  • Stay active and continue normal activities
  • Avoid prolonged bed rest
  • Local heat application for pain relief
  • Work adjustments for early return
  • Regular exercise to reduce recurrence risk
  • Reassure: pain increase with activity ≠ damage
Activity is KEY

Pharmacological Treatment

Analgesia for pain relief:

  • NSAIDs (lowest effective dose, shortest duration)
  • Consider GI/cardio-renal risks
  • Paracetamol may be combined with NSAIDs
DO NOT OFFER:
Gabapentinoids Antiepileptics Oral Corticosteroids Benzodiazepines Opioids for chronic sciatica

Non-Pharmacological

Exercise & Physical Therapy:

  • Group exercise programs (biomechanical, aerobic, mind-body)
  • Physiotherapy for manual therapy (as part of package)
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy for high-risk patients
If already on harmful meds: Discuss risks and withdrawal with patient

Ineffective Interventions

Foot Orthotics Belts/Corsets Rocker Sole Shoes Traction Acupuncture Ultrasound/TENS

Follow-up & Referral

When to Reassess

Seek follow-up if:
  • Symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks
  • Severe pain not subsiding within 1 week
  • Symptoms worsen or new symptoms develop
  • Recurrence of symptoms
  • Red flag symptoms emerge

Referral Criteria

Consider specialist referral for:

  • Severe radicular pain at 2-6 weeks
  • Non-tolerable pain at 6 weeks
  • Significant functional impairment
  • Failure of conservative treatment
Injection Therapy: Epidural corticosteroid for acute severe sciatica
Surgical Referral: Spinal decompression if non-surgical treatment fails

Urgent Referral Indicators

CES Symptoms Suspected Fracture Suspected Cancer Suspected Infection Bilateral Sciatica

Prognosis Monitoring

Favorable factors: Younger age, better health, low pain intensity, negative SLR, low physical workload
Poor prognostic factors: Time off work, psychological distress, heavy work, social withdrawal

Recurrence is common (54% at 6 months) - provide ongoing support

This algorithm is based on NICE CKS Guidelines (January 2025). Always use clinical judgment and consider individual patient circumstances.
