Gout — One‑Page Quick Reference Algorithm

Step 1

Diagnosis / Rule‑out

  • Sudden severe monoarthritis (often 1st MTP) with erythema, warmth, swelling. Consider other joints (midfoot, ankle, knee, hand/wrist/elbow).
  • Septic arthritis? If systemically unwell or hot swollen joint → urgent aspiration/culture via local pathway.
  • Measure serum urate: ≥ 360 µmol/L (≥6 mg/dL) supports diagnosis. If <360 but high suspicion, repeat 2–4 weeks post‑flare.
  • Crystals (if available) confirm; imaging (US/DECT/X‑ray) if uncertainty.

Gout may occur with normal urate; hyperuricaemia may exist without gout.

Step 2

Acute Flare — Treat ASAP

  • NSAID (e.g., naproxen, full dose) + consider PPI.
  • Colchicine.
  • Prednisolone 30–35 mg OD × 3–5 days.
  • Intra‑articular / IM steroid if NSAID/colchicine not tolerated or ineffective.
  • Combine therapies if suboptimal response; add paracetamol as adjunct.
Tips
Supportive:
rest, elevate, cool environment, ice packs. Continue ULT if already on it. Avoid aspirin. IL‑1 inhibitors only if NSAIDs/colchicine/steroids are contraindicated, not tolerated, or ineffective (specialist advice).
Step 3

4–6 Weeks Post‑Flare

  • Check serum urate.
  • Educate: nature of gout, progression without ULT, options for flares.
  • Assess lifestyle + comorbidities (CVD risk, CKD, obesity, alcohol). Offer CKD testing (eGFRcreatinine, ACR).
  • Review meds; discuss long‑term ULT risks/benefits.
Step 4

When to Start ULT & Targets

Offer ULT (treat‑to‑target) if
≥2 flares/yr; CKD G3–G5; tophi; chronic gouty arthritis; diuretic therapy.
Discuss ULT with others
After first or subsequent flare.
Serum urate targets
<360 µmol/L (<6 mg/dL); consider <300 µmol/L (<5 mg/dL) if tophi/chronic arthritis or frequent flares.
Step 5

ULT Practicalities

  • Timing: start ≥2–4 weeks after flare settles (can start during frequent flares).
  • Agent: Allopurinol first‑line (esp. with major CVD). Febuxostat if needed/contraindication to allopurinol.
  • Titration: check urate monthly and up‑titrate to target as tolerated.
  • Screening: LFTs before febuxostat; consider HLA‑B*58:01 testing in high‑prevalence groups before allopurinol.
Step 6

Flare Prophylaxis During ULT Start/Titration

  • Colchicine preferred.
  • If contraindicated/not tolerated/ineffective → NSAID ± PPI or short corticosteroid.
  • Do not use IL‑1 inhibitor for prophylaxis unless other options unsuitable (specialist advice).
Step 7

Monitoring

  • Urate monthly until target, then annually.
  • Check renal function regularly; screen CVD risks & comorbidities at least annually; offer CKD testing (eGFR, ACR).
  • Watch for allopurinol hypersensitivity (esp. on initiation).
  • Febuxostat: monitor LFTs periodically by clinical judgement.
Step 8

Referral Triggers

  • Suspected septic arthritis → immediate referral
  • Uncertain diagnosis; poor response/intolerance/contraindications.
  • CKD G3b–G5 or transplant.
  • Need for joint injection where expertise/facilities lacking.
  • Pregnancy; age <30; suspicion of systemic rheumatic disease; complications; high risk of drug AEs.
Step 9

Key Patient Messages

  • Gout is lifelong; untreated hyperuricaemia forms crystals and drives progression.
  • ULT prevents flares, shrinks tophi, and averts joint damage when sustained to target.
  • Healthy balanced diet; manage weight and alcohol. Continue ULT during flares and treat flares promptly.
Prescribing Quick Notes

Selected Dosing & Safety Reminders

  • Colchicine (acute): 500 µg 2–4× daily until pain relief (max 6 mg/course; don’t repeat within 3 days). Reduce dose or extend interval if eGFR 10–50; contraindicated if eGFR <10. Narrow therapeutic index; toxic in overdose.
  • Allopurinol (ULT): start ≤100 mg daily (after food); titrate by 100 mg every 4 weeks to urate target (typical 100–600 mg/day; up to 700–900 mg/day severe—divide doses >300 mg). Use lower doses in elderly, hepatic/renal impairment; consider HLA‑B*58:01 in high‑risk ancestry.
  • Febuxostat (ULT): consider if allopurinol contraindicated/not tolerated; check LFTs before start and monitor as needed; use caution in major cardiovascular disease—prefer allopurinol first‑line in CVD.
  • NSAIDs: use full anti‑inflammatory dose; consider PPI for gastric protection; avoid aspirin for gout.
  • Corticosteroids: short oral course for flares; intra‑articular helpful for monoarthritis when feasible.

Always check BNF/eMC for full contraindications, interactions, and adverse effects.

At‑a‑Glance Thresholds

Numbers to Remember

  • Diagnosis: urate ≥ 360 µmol/L (≥6 mg/dL)
  • Targets: < 360 µmol/L; consider < 300 µmol/L if tophi/chronic or frequent flares
  • Follow‑up: 4–6 weeks post‑flare
  • Pred: 30–35 mg OD × 3–5 days
