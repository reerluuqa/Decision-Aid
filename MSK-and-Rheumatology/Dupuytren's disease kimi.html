Clinical Assessment
▼
Key Features to Assess
  • Skin thickening, puckering, or pitting on the palm
  • Firm nodules fixed to skin and palmar fascia
  • Longitudinal cords extending towards fingers
  • Flexion contracture at MCP or PIP joints
  • Functional limitations (hand function, daily activities)
Risk Factors
Age > 40 years Male sex (7x risk) Northern European descent Family history Diabetes mellitus Smoking High alcohol intake
Tabletop Test

Procedure: Ask patient to place hand flat on a table

Positive result: Inability to flatten palm and fingers indicates contracture

Interpretation: Positive test = referral indicated

Examination Checklist
  • Examine BOTH hands (usually bilateral)
  • Check all digits (4th & 5th most common)
  • Assess for ectopic lesions (knuckles, feet, penis)
  • Evaluate pain/discomfort level
  • Document functional impairment
Diagnosis & Differential
▼
Diagnostic Criteria

Clinical diagnosis - No investigations usually required

  • Palpable nodules and/or cords in palm/fingers
  • Progressive flexion contracture of digits
  • Characteristic disease pattern and risk factors
Differential Diagnosis
Callus: Thickened skin from friction/pressure (common)
Epithelioid sarcoma: Malignant, enlarging indurated mass in adolescents/young adults (rare)
Ganglion: Fluid-filled mass attached to tendon/joint (most common benign hand tumor)
Trigger finger: Tender nodule with clicking/locking of finger
Ulnar nerve palsy: Numbness, tingling, weakness (not seen in Dupuytren's)
Severity Indicators
No contracture - Monitor
Mild contracture (≤30°) - Consider referral
Moderate-severe contracture (>30°) - Urgent referral
Functional impairment - Urgent referral
Painful nodules - Consider referral
Management Pathway
▼
Primary Care Management
No Contracture / Mild Disease
  • Reassure: Benign, non-cancerous condition
  • Explain progressive nature and possible future need for surgery
  • Simple analgesia for painful nodules if needed
  • Advise return if contracture develops
Referral Indicators
  • Contracture present (especially >30°)
  • Significant functional loss
  • Rapid disease progression
  • Painful nodules (consider based on clinical judgement)
Secondary Care Treatment Options
Needle Fasciotomy Minimally Invasive

• Outpatient procedure under local anaesthetic
• Small cuts in contracted cord using needle
• Short recovery time, high recurrence rate (50% at 3-5 years)

Limited Fasciectomy Standard Surgery

• Surgical removal of affected fascia
• More invasive, longer recovery
• Lower recurrence rate (~20% at 10 years)

Dermofasciectomy Complex Surgery

• Removal of skin and fascia with skin graft
• For aggressive/recurrent disease
• Lowest recurrence rate (~8% at 10 years)

Prognosis & Follow-up
▼
Disease Progression
  • Progressive condition - 75% develop advanced features
  • 35% progress from nodules/cords to contracture over 18 years
  • Rate varies: faster in men, age <50, strong family history
  • ~10% may spontaneously regress
  • Early onset (<40 years) indicates aggressive disease (Dupuytren's diathesis)
Recurrence Rates After Surgery
Needle Fasciotomy
50% at 3-5 years 85% at 10 years
Limited Fasciectomy 20% at 10 years
Dermofasciectomy 8% at 10 years
When to Re-refer
Recurrence Indicators:
  • New or worsening contracture
  • Return of functional impairment
  • Post-surgical complications (infection, nerve injury)
  • Complex regional pain syndrome symptoms
Key Prognostic Factors
Younger age at onset Bilateral disease Radial digit involvement Ectopic disease PIP joint contractures Little finger involvement
Complications to Monitor
  • Tendon rupture, nerve/artery injury
  • Infection, ischaemia, haematoma
  • Flare reaction (2-3 weeks post-op)
  • Complex regional pain syndrome
  • Amputation (rare, for severe recurrent cases)
