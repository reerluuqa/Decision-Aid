Legg-Calvé-Perthes' Disease

Clinical Reference Tool | Last Updated: March 3, 2022

Quick Overview
Typically affects children 4-8 years old (mean 7 years)
4-5 times more common in males than females
Self-limiting disease with necrosis, collapse, repair, and remodeling
Typically unilateral, but bilateral in 10-12% of cases
Can lead to painful, poorly functioning hip in later life
Prognosis depends on age at onset and extent of femoral head involvement
Red Flags & Emergency Actions
Acute onset with fever and systemic symptoms - Consider septic arthritis
Marked restriction of movement - Requires urgent evaluation
Onset after age 8 - Higher risk of poor prognosis
Females with disease - Typically more severe involvement
Emergency Actions:
Joint Aspiration
Immediate Referral
Hospital Admission
Key Diagnostic Factors
Painless limp (most common presentation)
Limited range of motion at hip joint
Short stature compared to peers
Hyperactivity in affected children
Positive Actions:
Bilateral Hip X-rays
Range of Motion Assessment
Growth History Review
Disease Stages
1
Ischaemia (6-12 months)
Femoral epiphysis stops growing, Waldenstrom's sign may appear
2
Resorption/Fragmentation (12-18 months)
Subchondral fracture, Gage's sign, metaphyseal cysts
3
Re-ossification (6-24 months)
Starts at epiphyseal margins, potential for bone bridges
4
Re-modelling
Femoral head collapse, potential for flattening and distortion
Management by Age
Under 5 Years
1
Monitor & Maintain Motion
Very good healing potential at this age
2
Consider Non-Surgical Containment
If radiographs suggest lateral extrusion
5-7 Years
1
Assess Epiphyseal Involvement
Key factor in treatment decision
2
<50% Involvement
Mobilization and maintaining hip movement
3
≥50% Involvement
Surgical containment is treatment of choice
Over 7 Years
1
Stages I & II
Surgical containment as primary treatment
2
Stages III & IV
Containment contraindicated, consider salvage procedure
3
Over 12 Years
Limited remodeling potential, salvage procedures only
Classification Systems
Catterall Classification
Group I
<25% epiphysis involved, no metaphyseal changes
Group II
<50% epiphyseal involvement, limited metaphyseal reaction
Group III
<100% head involvement, extensive fragmentation
Group IV
Total head involvement, severe collapse
Herring Lateral Pillar Classification
Group A
No involvement of lateral pillar, retains original height
Group B
Lateral pillar shows lucency, loss of height <50%
Group C
Lateral pillar collapse >50% of original height
Prognostic Indicators
Stulberg Classification
Class I: Normal hip - Excellent prognosis
Class II: Spherical head, congruent joint - Good prognosis
Class III: Aspherical head, congruent joint - Moderate prognosis
Class IV: Aspherical head, incongruent joint - Poor prognosis
Class V: Flat head, incongruent joint - Poor prognosis
Key Prognostic Factors
Age at onset
Extent of epiphyseal involvement
Lateral pillar height
Head-at-risk signs
Range of motion
Differential Diagnosis
Condition Key Differentiating Features Diagnostic Tests
Septic Arthritis Acute onset, systemic symptoms, marked restriction Elevated WBC, positive blood cultures, joint aspiration
Transient Synovitis Prior viral illness, spontaneous improvement in 24-48h Normal WBC, negative blood cultures, sterile joint aspirate
Sickle Cell Disease Acute abdominal pain, systemic symptoms Positive sickling test
Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis Single or multiple joint involvement, systemic symptoms Rheumatoid factor or ANA positive, joint aspirate shows WBC
Multiple Epiphyseal Dysplasia Bilateral epiphyseal dysplasia at same stage Sequential films show continued disease equality
