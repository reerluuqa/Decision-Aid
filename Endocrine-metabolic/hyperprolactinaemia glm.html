Definition

Hyperprolactinemia is a condition of elevated serum prolactin (PRL) levels above the normal reference range.

Normal PRL Levels
  • Women: <1087 picomol/L (25 μg/L or 500 mU/L)
  • Men: <870 picomol/L (20 μg/L or 400 mU/L)
Reference: [1] [2] [3]
Epidemiology
  • Most common endocrine disorder of the hypothalamus-pituitary axis
  • Prevalence: 0.4% in unselected normal adult population
  • 5% in family planning clinic population
  • 9% in women with amenorrhea
  • 25% in those with galactorrhea
  • 70% in women with both amenorrhea and galactorrhea
  • 5% of men with impotence or infertility
Reference: [2] [3] [4]
Pathophysiology
Prolactin Production & Regulation

Prolactin is a 199-amino acid peptide synthesized and secreted by lactotroph cells in the anterior pituitary gland:

  • 80% of circulating PRL exists as a 23 kD monomer
  • 10-15% is dimeric PRL (big PRL, 50-60 kD)
  • Remainder is macroprolactin (big-big PRL, >150 kD complex)

PRL secretion is pulsatile and controlled predominantly by the inhibitory effect of dopamine from the hypothalamus.

Reference: [5] [6]
Physiological Effects
  • Stimulates breast epithelial cell proliferation and milk production
  • Promotes formation and action of corpus luteum
  • Suppresses pulsatile secretion of GnRH
  • Results in decreased FSH and LH levels
  • Direct inhibitory role in spermatogenesis and steroidogenesis
Reference: [7] [8]

Signs of Hypopituitarism

May occur as a result of compression of the hypothalamic-pituitary stalk, or other pituitary cells:

  • Growth hormone (GH) deficiency
  • Secondary hypogonadism
  • Secondary hypothyroidism
  • Secondary adrenal failure
  • Diabetes insipidus
Reference: [51]
Diagnostic Approach
Step 1: Initial Assessment
  • Detailed history and physical examination
  • Exclude physiological causes (pregnancy, lactation, stress)
  • Review medications known to cause hyperprolactinemia
Step 2: Laboratory Evaluation
  • Serum prolactin measurement
  • Renal, liver, and thyroid function tests
  • Pregnancy test (when applicable)
  • Macroprolactin levels (in asymptomatic or atypical presentations)
Step 3: Imaging Studies
  • MRI of the pituitary gland (after excluding other causes)
  • CT scan for calcified sellar/suprasellar tumors
  • Further evaluation for hypopituitarism if macroadenoma detected
Prolactin Testing
Testing Considerations
  • Single measurement generally adequate for consistent clinical picture
  • Repeat testing recommended for mild elevation (<5x upper limit)
  • Avoid stress-related false positives
  • Consider resting sample if random PRL <4087 pmol/L
Reference: [47] [48] [58]
Imaging Indications
When to Order MRI
  • After excluding physiological, pharmacological, and secondary causes
  • PRL levels >4348 pmol/L (100 μg/L)
  • Persistently elevated PRL without identifiable secondary cause
  • Symptoms suggesting mass effect

Pituitary Apoplexy
Definition

A clinical syndrome caused by infarction or hemorrhage of a pituitary adenoma, resulting in rapid expansion and compression of neighboring structures.

Reference: [42] [43]
Predisposing Factors
  • Reduced blood flow in the pituitary gland
  • Acute increase of pituitary blood flow
  • Stimulation of the pituitary gland
  • Anticoagulated state

Management
Immediate Management
  • Supportive therapy with IV fluids
  • Corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone)
  • Urgent endocrinology consultation
Surgical Intervention
  • Transsphenoidal surgical decompression
  • Ideally within 24-48 hours of onset
  • Indicated for progressive vision loss and/or cranial neuropathy
Long-term Follow-up
  • Treat residual tumor
  • Manage pituitary dysfunction
  • Regular monitoring

Etiology of Hyperprolactinemia
Distribution of Causes

Based on a retrospective multicentre study of 1234 patients:

  • 56.2% had prolactinomas
  • 14.5% had drug-induced hyperprolactinemia
  • 9.3% had macroprolactinemia
  • 6.6% had non-functioning pituitary adenomas
  • 6.3% had primary hypothyroidism
  • 3.6% had idiopathic hyperprolactinemia
  • 3.2% had acromegaly
Reference: [12]

Idiopathic Hyperprolactinemia

In some cases, the underlying cause of hyperprolactinemia cannot be determined. This condition is termed idiopathic hyperprolactinemia and is usually associated with PRL levels of approximately 4348 pmol/L (100 μg/L).

Such patients may harbor microadenomas that are not detectable with imaging studies. Anti-PRL auto-antibodies have been detected in approximately 16% of cases.

Reference: [1] [2] [3] [40] [41]
Special Consideration: Macroprolactinemia
Key Points
  • High levels of macroprolactin lead to reduced clearance rates of the PRL-IgG complex
  • Prevalence in hyperprolactinemic sera ranges from 15% to 46%
  • Overall prevalence in general population is estimated at 3.7%
  • PRL levels are usually <4348 pmol/L (100 μg/L)
  • Suspected when patient lacks typical symptoms and/or radiographic evidence of pituitary tumor
