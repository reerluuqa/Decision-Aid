History Taking Critical
Symptoms & Onset: Onset and progression of symptoms; be alert for features unusual for age
Medication Review: All corticosteroids (oral, intra-articular, inhaled, topical) to exclude exogenous cause
Hormonal Therapy: Oral contraceptives or HRT (can increase cortisol-binding globulin)
Associated Conditions: Pregnancy, obesity, alcohol use disorder, PCOS, psychiatric conditions, chronic disease

When to Suspect Clinical Judgement
üí°
Suspect Cushing's syndrome in people presenting with:
  • Clinical features unusual for age (e.g., HTN <40y, osteoporosis in premenopausal women/men <65y)
  • Multiple and progressive clinical features
  • Adrenal incidentaloma on imaging
Screening Investigations Primary Care

üß™ First-Line Screening Tests

Perform ‚â•1 test to confirm hypercortisolism:

1. Overnight Dexamethasone Suppression Test

  • Method: 1mg dexamethasone at 11pm-midnight, cortisol at 8-9am
  • Best for: Night-shift workers/disrupted circadian rhythm
  • False positives: Malabsorption, CYP3A4 inducers, oral estrogens, pseudo-Cushing's
  • False negatives: CYP3A4 inhibitors, nephrotic syndrome, slow metabolizers

2. 24-Hour Urinary Free Cortisol

  • Method: Complete urine collection over 24 hours
  • Requirements: High compliance, 2-3 samples recommended
  • False positives: Large urine volumes (>3-4L/day)
  • False negatives: Incomplete collection, renal impairment

3. Late-Night Salivary Cortisol

  • Method: 2-3 samples at 11pm-midnight
  • Preparation: No eating/drinking/brushing teeth 2h prior
  • Contraindicated: Night-shift workers
  • False positives: Smoking, older age, HTN, diabetes, variable sleep patterns

Important: Random cortisol levels are rarely useful unless grossly elevated due to circadian fluctuations

Emergency Management Urgent
üö®
Life-Threatening Complications ‚Üí Urgent Admission: Sepsis, pulmonary embolism, MI, stroke
Exogenous Cushing's Syndrome Primary Care
‚ö†Ô∏è
Gradual Steroid Reduction: Abrupt discontinuation can cause life-threatening adrenal insufficiency
Management: Gradual reduction and withdrawal of corticosteroid treatment
Monitoring: Individualize based on underlying condition and response
Adrenal Insufficiency: Advise urgent medical help if symptoms develop
Steroid Withdrawal: May cause fever, myalgia, arthralgia, skin nodules, weight loss
If withdrawal symptoms: Resume higher dose and reduce more slowly
Condition Worsening: Dose reduction may worsen underlying condition - discuss alternatives
Endogenous Cushing's Syndrome Referral
üéØ
Suspected Cancer Pathway: Refer within 2 weeks if malignant cause suspected
Children & Pregnancy: Urgent referral to paediatric endocrinology or obstetrics
Adults: Refer to endocrinology for confirmation and treatment
Goal: Normalization of cortisol levels and treatment of comorbidities
Secondary Care Management Specialist

üè• Pituitary Tumour (Cushing's Disease)

  • First-line: Transsphenoidal surgery
  • Recurrence common (9.6-18%)
  • Options: Pituitary-directed meds or bilateral adrenalectomy
  • Lifelong monitoring required

ü©∫ Adrenal Adenoma

  • First-line: Unilateral adrenalectomy
  • Usually curative
  • Recurrence in contralateral gland is rare

üéóÔ∏è Adrenal Carcinoma

  • First-line: Surgical resection
  • Often aggressive and rapidly growing
  • Prognosis varies by stage

ü´Å Ectopic ACTH Secretion

  • First-line: Tumour resection
  • Often metastatic at presentation
  • Medical therapy to stabilize cortisol while awaiting oncology treatment
Medical Therapy Adjunctive
üíä
Indications: Mild hypercortisolism, adjunct for severe disease, persistent/recurrent disease post-surgery, or when surgery declined/not feasible

Primary Care Responsibilities Ongoing
üè•
All patients with Cushing's syndrome require long-term follow-up to monitor and treat complications
Screen for Complications:
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hypertension
  • Osteoporosis
  • Increased cardiovascular risk
Individualized Care: Assessment and monitoring based on complications present, following local/national guidelines
Care Coordination: Coordinate between secondary and primary care as appropriate
Vaccinations Prevention
üíâ
Offer age-appropriate vaccinations: Influenza, herpes zoster, pneumococcus (increased infection risk)
Persistent Morbidity Important

üìä Resolution Rates After Treatment

  • Diabetes: 56% resolve
  • Depression: 52% resolve
  • Hypertension: 36% resolve
  • Hyperlipidaemia: 23% resolve
  • Weight: 75% still overweight/obese at 5 years

‚ö†Ô∏è Long-term Risks

  • Increased cardiovascular risk may persist
  • Decreased quality of life
  • Recurrence common in pituitary disease
  • Lifelong monitoring required
Prognosis & Mortality Critical
üìà
Mortality: 3x higher than age/sex-matched population. Most common causes: cardiovascular disease, infection, thromboembolism, malignancy
Predictors of Higher Mortality: Age, male sex, diabetes, ectopic ACTH secretion
Improving Prognosis: Early diagnosis, prompt treatment, strict cardiovascular/metabolic risk control
