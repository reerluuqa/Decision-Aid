Gout: Quick-Scan Reference

Last revised June 2023 • NICE CKS Based
Priority Actions & Red Flags

Suspect Septic Arthritis?

If the person is systemically unwell with an acutely painful, hot, swollen joint.

ACTION: Refer immediately according to local care pathway for emergency joint aspiration and culture.

When to Refer

  • Specialist Advice/Referral needed if:
  • Diagnosis uncertain
  • Inadequate response to treatment or intolerance
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) stages 3b to 5
  • Organ transplant recipient
  • Suspected underlying systemic illness (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis)
Diagnosis & Assessment

Key Clinical Features

  • Rapid Onset: Often overnight, peaks < 24 hours.
  • Location: 1st MTP joint (Podagra) in 50% of cases (big toe). Also: midfoot, ankle, knee.
  • Symptoms: Severe pain, redness, swelling, warmth.
  • Tophi: Hard, white nodules (extensor surfaces, ears, Achilles) suggest chronic untreated gout.

Investigations

Serum Urate Level:
≥ 360 µmol/L (6 mg/dL) confirms diagnosis in context of symptoms.

Note: Do not diagnose on hyperuricaemia alone. If SUA < 360 but gout strongly suspected, repeat 2-4 weeks after flare settles.

Scenario: Acute Gout Attack

1st Line NSAIDs

Drug: Naproxen (or other NSAID at max dose).

Action: Continue until 1-2 days after attack resolves.

Caution: Consider PPI for gastric protection. Aspirin not indicated.

1st Line Colchicine

Dose: 500mcg 2-4 times daily.

Or: 1mg start, then 500mcg after 1hr. Wait 12hrs, then 500mcg every 8hrs. Max 6mg per course.

Adjust dose in renal impairment/elderly.

1st Line Corticosteroids

Oral: Prednisolone 30-35mg once daily for 3-5 days.

Injection: Intra-articular or IM if NSAIDs/Colchicine not tolerated.

Off-label use

Adjuncts

  • Apply Ice packs to affected joint.
  • Rest and elevate the limb.
  • Keep joint exposed and cool.
  • DO NOT STOP Urate-Lowering Therapy (Allopurinol/Febuxostat) if already taking it.

Follow Up

Arrange follow-up 4-6 weeks after flare settles to:

  • Measure serum urate level.
  • Discuss lifestyle changes.
  • Review need for long-term Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT).
Scenario: Preventing Recurrent Gout

Treat-to-Target Strategy

Offer ULT to people with:

  • Multiple/Troublesome flares
  • CKD stages 3-5
  • Tophi
  • Diuretic therapy
  • Chronic gouty arthritis
Target Serum Urate: < 360 µmol/L
(Consider < 300 µmol/L for severe disease/tophi)

Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)

Start: 2-4 weeks after flare settles (or during flare if frequent).
Drug: Allopurinol first-line (start 100mg).
Consider Febuxostat if Allopurinol unsuitable.
Titration: Check SUA monthly, increase dose until target reached.
Prophylaxis: Co-prescribe NSAID or Colchicine for up to 6 months when starting ULT.
Prescribing Safety & Cautions

Critical Safety Checks

Drug Critical Warning / Action Key Interaction / Caution
Allopurinol RASH: Stop immediately & seek medical advice (Risk of SJS/TEN). Screen for HLA-B*5801 in high-risk populations (Chinese, Thai, Korean).
Azathioprine: Reduce azathioprine dose by 75%.
Febuxostat CV Risk: Caution in major CVD (MI, Stroke). Monitor LFTs. Theophylline: Monitor levels.
Azathioprine/Mercaptopurine: Avoid or reduce dose drastically.
Colchicine Toxic in overdose. Narrow therapeutic index. Contraindicated if eGFR < 10.
Macrolides/Ritonavir: Contraindicated (risk of toxicity).
NSAIDs Renal Risk: Monitor function closely, especially in Heart Failure/CKD. Anticoagulants: Increased bleeding risk.
ACE Inhibitors: May reduce anti-hypertensive effect.

Lifestyle Advice

  • Diet: Healthy, balanced diet. No specific diet proven to cure, but limit excess alcohol, sugary drinks, meat, and seafood.
  • Weight: Address excess body weight/obesity.
  • Hydration: Maintain good fluid intake.
