Red Flags - Emergency Actions Required

Symptomatic Hyperglycaemia

  • Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, fatigue
  • Blurred vision, slow wound healing
  • Recurrent infections (UTI, candidiasis)
  • ACTION: Consider rescue therapy with insulin or sulphonylurea
  • Review and adjust regular therapy once controlled

Severe Hypoglycaemia

  • Requires assistance from another person
  • Impaired consciousness or seizure
  • Double long-term CVD risk (BMJ 2013)
  • AVOID: Sulphonylureas in drivers, elderly, frail
  • Use CGM in high-risk patients

DKA Risk with SGLT2i

  • Euglycaemic DKA possible - low glucose but high ketones
  • Risk during acute illness, surgery, fasting
  • Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • ACTION: Stop SGLT2i during acute illness - see sick day rules
  • Measure ketones if suspected

Cardiovascular Emergency

  • Offer gliflozin to ALL with established CVD
  • Consider gliflozin for those with CV risk factors
  • GLP-1s reserved for obesity per NICE
  • Benefits seen across all outcomes (mortality, HF, renal)
  • Early intensive control reduces lifetime complications

NICE 2022 Algorithm - First-Line Therapy

Major Change: Gliflozins Now First-Line (with Metformin)

Metformin + Gliflozin (SGLT2i) → New First-Line for High CV Risk

Who should get gliflozin as first-line?

  • ✓ Established CVD (MI, stroke, PAD, HF) - OFFER
  • ✓ CV risk factors (QRISK >10%) - CONSIDER
  • • Others may still benefit - use clinical judgment

When to Intensify

  • HbA1c ≥ 58mmol/mol (7.5%) on dual therapy
  • HbA1c ≥ 53mmol/mol (7%) on single drug
  • Personalise targets - not everyone needs tight control
  • De-intensify if HbA1c <48mmol/mol (6.5%)
  • Consider frailty - avoid hypos in elderly

Key Benefits Evidence

  • Gliflozins: 17 fewer deaths/1000 over 5 years
  • GLP-1s: Same mortality benefit, weight loss
  • Heart Failure: 33 fewer admissions/1000 (gliflozins)
  • Renal: 34 fewer ESKD/1000 over 5 years (gliflozins)
  • Meta-analysis BMJ 2023: 500,000 patients

Monitoring Requirements

Metformin & B12 Monitoring

⚠️ Metformin reduces B12 - affects ~1 in 10 patients

  • Check B12 if symptomatic (fatigue, neuropathy, glossitis)
  • Risk increases with dose and duration
  • Add to annual diabetes bloods for pragmatic monitoring
  • Higher risk: Vegans, elderly, IBD, PPI users
  • Continue metformin if B12 deficiency confirmed (treat deficiency)

Blood Glucose Monitoring

  • NOT routine for type 2 diabetes on oral meds
  • Offer if: On insulin, hypoglycaemia risk, driving
  • Confirm suspected hypoglycaemia
  • Starting steroids - short term monitoring
  • Pregnancy planning or pregnant
  • CGM for type 2 on multiple daily insulin with recurrent hypos

Renal Function & Dosing

  • Check eGFR before starting, then annually
  • Metformin: Stop if eGFR <30; halve dose if 30-45
  • SGLT2i: Can use down to eGFR 20 (dapagliflozin HF)
  • Gliptins: Dose adjustment needed (except linagliptin)
  • Audit: Review dosing for all eGFR <45

Sick Day Rules - SGLT2i (Gliflozin) Patients

CRITICAL: Stop SGLT2i During Acute Illness

Why? Risk of euglycaemic DKA - glucose may be normal but ketones high. Patients may not recognise classic DKA symptoms.

SADMANS Mnemonic - Drugs to Pause:

S SGLT2 inhibitors
A ACE inhibitors
D Diuretics
M Metformin
A ARBs
N NSAIDs
S Sulfonylureas

Patient Advice:

  • Keep taking insulin (if applicable)
  • Stay hydrated with sugar-containing fluids
  • Check blood glucose more frequently
  • Seek medical help if: persistent vomiting, unable to eat, confusion, or DKA symptoms
  • Restart SGLT2i when eating normally and recovered (24-48h)
