Type 1 Diabetes Management

Interactive clinical algorithm based on BMJ Best Practice guidelines (July 2021). This tool provides step-by-step guidance for assessment, diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up of patients with Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus.

Assessment

Initial evaluation and clinical presentation

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Rapid weight loss with polyuria/polydipsia
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (possible DKA)
  • Deep rapid respirations (Kussmaul breathing)
  • Altered mental status or lethargy

Risk Factors

  • Genetic Predisposition: HLA-DR/DQ polymorphisms, family history (5% risk vs 0.3% general population) Strong
  • Geographical Region: More common in Europeans (Finland 40.9/100,000) vs Asians (China 0.1/100,000) Weak
  • Infectious Agents: Human enteroviruses show strongest association Possible
  • Dietary Factors: Vitamin D may be protective, cow's milk/cereals uncertain Uncertain
  • Autoimmune Diseases: Coeliac disease more common (shared HLA-DQ2) Association

Diagnosis

Diagnostic criteria and differential diagnosis

WHO Diagnostic Criteria

  • Random plasma glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L (≥200 mg/dL) with symptoms
  • Fasting plasma glucose ≥7.0 mmol/L (≥126 mg/dL)
  • Plasma glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L 2 hours after 75g oral glucose
  • HbA1c ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol)

Repeat confirmatory test required in most cases (except classic DKA presentation)

Adult Presentation Features

  • Ketosis: Presence indicates absolute insulin deficiency Strong indicator
  • Rapid weight loss: Suggests insulin deficiency rather than resistance Type 1 feature
  • Age <50 years: More likely type 1, but DO NOT discount if >50 years Guide only
  • BMI <25 kg/m²: Lean individuals more likely type 1, but obesity doesn't exclude it Guide only
  • Autoimmune disease: Personal/family history supports type 1 Supporting feature

Initial Assumption

Assume type 1 unless strong indications otherwise

Default

Consider Type 2 if: Obese, strong family history, black/Asian origin, acanthosis nigricans

Consider Monogenic if: Diabetes in first year, syndromic features, neonatal diabetes

Differential Diagnosis

  • Monogenic Diabetes (MODY): Non-ketotic, responds to oral agents, C-peptide present, autoantibodies absent Genetic test
  • Neonatal Diabetes: Diagnosed <6 months, genetic testing required KCNJ11/ABCC8
  • LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults): Age >30, initially non-obese, slow progression to insulin requirement 2+ of 4 criteria
  • Type 2 Diabetes: Insulin resistance signs (acanthosis nigricas), older age, obesity, slow onset Differentiate

LADA Diagnostic Criteria (2+ needed)

  • Age of onset <50 years
  • Acute symptoms (polydipsia, polyuria, weight loss)
  • BMI <25 kg/m²
  • Personal/family history of autoimmune disease

Not Recommended

Routine screening for type 1 diabetes: Low population prevalence

Antibody screening: No proven preventive treatment available

C-peptide at diagnosis: Only if difficulty distinguishing types

Adjunct Therapies

  • Metformin: Consider if BMI ≥25 kg/m² (≥23 in South Asian) to improve control and reduce insulin dose Off-label in UK
  • Dapagliflozin: No longer licensed for type 1 diabetes (withdrawn Oct 2021) due to DKA risk Contraindicated

Surgical/Procedure Considerations

Before procedures: Discuss insulin requirements with diabetes team

During illness: "Sick-day rules" - never stop insulin, monitor ketones, stay hydrated

Foot care: Annual examination, podiatry referral if at risk

Dental: Regular examinations, good oral hygiene

Prognosis

  • Untreated type 1 diabetes is fatal (DKA)
  • Intensive control reduces microvascular and macrovascular complications
  • DCCT/EDIC 30-year follow-up: benefits persist
  • Leading cause of death <30 years: acute complications
  • Leading cause of death >30 years: cardiovascular disease
  • Successful pregnancy achievable with optimal control

Key Message: Intensive glucose control for a few years translates to reduced complications 10 years later. Early optimal management has lifelong benefits.
