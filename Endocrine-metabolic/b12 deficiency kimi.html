Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference Tool

EMERGENCY: Severe neurological symptoms or pancytopenia require immediate parenteral B12 and hospital admission
Critical Red Flags
Subacute combined spinal degeneration
Ataxia / Balance issues
Peripheral neuropathy
Severe anaemia (Hb <8 g/dL)
Cognitive impairment/dementia
Pregnancy with deficiency
Safe Actions

High-dose oral B12 as effective as IM for most patients

High-Risk Groups
Gastric surgery

Roux-en-Y, gastrectomy

Vegan/vegetarian
Metformin >4 years
Atrophic gastritis
H. pylori infection
Age >65 years
Diagnostic Thresholds
Serum B12 <148 pmol/L
Probable deficiency (<200 pg/mL)
Possible deficiency 148-258 pmol/L
MMA (confirmatory) >0.4 μmol/L
Homocysteine >15 μmol/L
Treatment Protocol
Severity Initial Treatment Maintenance Monitoring
Severe Emergency Cyanocobalamin 1000mcg IM daily
×1-2 weeks, then weekly ×1 month 		1000mcg IM monthly

Moderate 1000mcg oral daily
Switch to IM if poor response 		Recheck B12 in 8 weeks
Asymptomatic Oral B12 50-1000mcg daily
Dietary counseling + supplements 		Annual screening if high risk
Key Clinical Pearls
  • Neurological symptoms may precede anemia
  • Normal MCV doesn't exclude B12 deficiency
  • Pernicious anemia: test anti-IF antibody BEFORE treatment
  • High serum B12 doesn't exclude deficiency in pernicious anemia
Common Pitfalls
  • Relying only on anemia/macrocytosis
  • Delaying treatment for test results in severe cases
  • Not investigating cause after diagnosis
  • Stopping treatment when cause is irreversible
