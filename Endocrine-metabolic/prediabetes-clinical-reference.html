Positive Messages for Patients

This condition is reversible through lifestyle modification

Support packages available to help with lifestyle changes

Wide range of benefits: Reduces risk of diabetes, CVD, cancer

Even temporary reversal delays associated risks

Understanding Prediabetes Subtypes

Impaired Fasting Glucose

  • Inappropriately high glucose production
  • Hepatic insulin resistance
  • Reduced hepatic glucose clearance
  • Auto-regulation of glucose impaired

Impaired Glucose Tolerance

  • Skeletal muscle resistance to insulin
  • Delaying glucose uptake
  • Beta-cell dysfunction

Varying degrees of both may be present in prediabetes and type 2 diabetes

Major Risk Factors for Prediabetes

These factors significantly increase patient risk - consider screening in individuals with any of these characteristics

Major Risk Factors

Being overweight/obese - strongest modifiable risk factor

Older age - risk increases with age

Physical inactivity - sedentary lifestyle

Unhealthy diet - high processed foods, sugars

Genetic predisposition - family history

Spontaneous Remission

Remission Is Possible

Spontaneous remission does occur, but chance reduces the longer prediabetes has been present.

Annual monitoring recommended to detect changes

Critical: Complications Occur Even Without Progression to Diabetes

Patients with prediabetes have increased risk of micro- and macrovascular complications even if they never develop type 2 diabetes

Macrovascular Complications

Cardiovascular Events

25% higher CV risk in those with prediabetes compared to normoglycaemia

BMJ 2016;355:i5953 - massive cohort study

9 extra cases

Of major CV events per 10,000 person-years

Manage CV risk aggressively: BP and lipids are crucial even without diabetes

Microvascular Complications

Retinopathy

Up to 8% in US DPP trial had retinopathy changes

Peripheral Neuropathy

Studies suggest 8-16% prevalence

CKD

May be present in 10% of those with prediabetes

Cognitive Impairment

More common than in normoglycaemia

Note: Screening for microvascular complications not recommended due to lack of evidence on clinical/cost-effectiveness

Lifestyle Interventions Are Central to Management

Lifestyle changes have persistent benefits after intervention ends - unlike drug therapy which stops working when discontinued

Why Lifestyle Over Drugs?

Persistent benefits: Effects continue after intervention ends

More effective than metformin: Meta-analysis shows lifestyle has greater impact on progression to diabetes

Benefits metabolic syndrome: Reduces hypertension, hyperlipidaemia

No injections required - better adherence potential

NICE Lifestyle Recommendations

Aim for 150+ minutes moderate activity weekly or 75 minutes vigorous activity

5-10% body weight loss can significantly improve insulin sensitivity

  • Increase dietary fibre intake
  • Reduce dietary fat intake
  • Limit processed carbohydrates

Pharmacological Options - NICE Guidance

Drug therapy is considered when lifestyle interventions are insufficient or patient unable to participate

Metformin

Metformin First-line drug

Start: 500mg once daily

Target: 1500-2000mg/day if tolerated

Consider: Modified-release if GI side effects

Plan to prescribe for 6-12 months initially. Check HbA1c 3-monthly; stop if no benefit seen.

Consider metformin when:

  • HbA1c ≥42mmol/mol despite lifestyle programme
  • Unable to participate in lifestyle programme
  • BMI >35
  • BMI ≥35 (may be as effective as lifestyle)
  • Women with gestational diabetes history
  • Younger patients (≤60y) - much less effect in >60y
  • HbA1c closer to diabetic threshold

Orlistat

Orlistat Weight management

Consider: HbA1c ≥42mmol/mol, BMI ≥28 after 3 months lifestyle intervention

Review: After 12 weeks - stop if < 5% weight loss

Caution: Slower weight loss in type 2 diabetes or high diabetes risk

Treatment Considerations

Trialed for diabetes prevention but NICE does not advocate. ADA says can be considered for stroke/MI risk reduction but weigh against risks of weight gain, oedema, fractures (Clin Diabetes 2023;41(1):4)

US Endocrine Society 2025 recommends vitamin D for high-risk prediabetes (JAMA 2025;333:1824). NOT currently recommended in UK. Treatment did NOT reduce progression in BMJ 2022 trial but hints justify further research.

Starting metformin delays reaching HbA1c 48mmol/mol threshold - delaying retinal screening and free prescriptions. However, also delays DVLA/car insurance notification requirements. Discuss openly with patients.

Patients who achieve normoglycaemia are at high risk of relapse. Practice approach: Annual HbA1c monitoring. Discuss stopping metformin but check HbA1c 3-6 months later.

Managing Prediabetes - Summary

1. Refer to Diabetes Prevention Programme

Offer intensive lifestyle change programme focusing on diet, physical activity, and weight management

2. Assess Cardiovascular Risk

Calculate QRISK3, manage BP, consider statin therapy

3. Consider Metformin

If HbA1c rising despite lifestyle programme, or unable to participate, especially if BMI >35

4. Consider Orlistat

If BMI ≥28 after 3 months lifestyle intervention

5. Annual Monitoring

HbA1c, BP, cholesterol - even if normoglycaemia achieved
