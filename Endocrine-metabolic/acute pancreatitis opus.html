WHEN TO SUSPECT

  • Acute sudden-onset upper abdominal pain
  • Epigastric, RUQ, or LUQ location
  • Radiates to back or flanks
  • Worse with movement, better leaning forward
  • Associated nausea/vomiting
  • History of gallstones or alcohol misuse
ü©∫

CLINICAL ASSESSMENT

  • Pain character: Sudden, constant, knife-like (gallstones) or poorly localized (alcohol)
  • Duration & onset: May worsen after food
  • Risk factors: Alcohol, gallstones, drugs, hypertriglyceridaemia
  • Previous episodes
  • Family history: Hereditary pancreatitis, familial cancer
  • Shock signs: Tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension
  • Fever: May indicate necrosis/sepsis
  • Abdominal tenderness: ¬± guarding, rebound
  • Distension: Retroperitoneal fluid leak
  • Ecchymoses (rare, severe): Cullen's (umbilical), Grey-Turner's (flanks), Fox's (inguinal)
