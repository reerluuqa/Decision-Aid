Hyperthyroidism Clinical Reference

Fast decision-making algorithm for primary care management

Definition & Classification

  • Hyperthyroidism: Pathologically increased thyroid hormone production
  • Thyrotoxicosis: Clinical manifestation of excess thyroid hormones
  • Overt: TSH suppressed + FT4 and/or FT3 elevated
  • Subclinical: TSH suppressed + normal FT4 and FT3

Risk Factors

  • Female sex (10× more common than men)
  • Family history (especially Graves' disease)
  • Smoking (risk factor for Graves' and orbitopathy)
  • Low iodine intake
  • Autoimmune conditions

Common Causes

  • Graves' disease (80% of cases)
  • Toxic multinodular goitre (common in older adults)
  • Toxic thyroid nodule (adenoma)
  • Thyroiditis: Postpartum, Subacute, Drug-induced
  • Exogenous thyroid hormone (iatrogenic or factitious)

Diagnosis

When to Suspect

  • Weight loss, heat intolerance
  • Palpitations, tremor, anxiety
  • Fatigue, sleep disturbances
  • Tachycardia, AF, tremor
  • Goitre, eye signs (proptosis, lid lag)
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • New atrial fibrillation

Investigations

  • First-line: TSH, FT4, FT3
  • If Graves' suspected: TRAb
  • Other: Thyroid ultrasound if structural abnormality suspected

Management

Initial Actions

  • Refer all patients to endocrinology (urgency based on clinical judgement)
  • If Graves' orbitopathy suspected: Refer to thyroid eye specialist
  • Provide information and advice about condition

Treatment Options (Specialist-Directed)

  • Antithyroid drugs: Carbimazole first-line (avoid in pregnancy), propylthiouracil alternative
  • Radioactive iodine: Contraindicated in pregnancy and breastfeeding
  • Surgery: For large goitres, compressive symptoms, or suspected malignancy

Monitoring

  • Frequency based on specialist advice
  • Monitor thyroid function tests
  • Watch for medication side effects (agranulocytosis, hepatotoxicity)

Complications to Monitor

  • Graves' orbitopathy: Eye changes, visual disturbances
  • Thyroid storm: Fever, tachycardia, agitation (medical emergency)
  • Cardiac: Atrial fibrillation, heart failure
  • Bone: Osteoporosis (increased fracture risk)
  • Pregnancy: Miscarriage, preterm delivery, fetal thyrotoxicosis

Special Considerations

  • Pregnancy: Requires urgent referral; propylthiouracil preferred in first trimester
  • Subclinical hyperthyroidism: May progress to overt (8% at 1 year, 26% at 5 years)
  • Amiodarone-induced: Can occur up to 1 year after discontinuation

Red Flags (Urgent Referral)

  • Severe symptoms or complications
  • Pregnancy or planning pregnancy
  • Thyroid storm suspected
  • Sight-threatening orbitopathy
