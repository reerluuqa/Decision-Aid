Insulin Therapy Quick Reference

For Type 2 Diabetes Management

1. When to Consider Insulin

  • Symptomatic Hyperglycemia: Marked polyuria, polydipsia, or unintended weight loss.
  • Inadequate Control: HbA1c ≥ 75 mmol/mol (9.0%) despite max tolerated oral meds.
  • High Risk of Complications: Especially in younger patients with suboptimal control.
  • Contraindications/Intolerance: Oral medications are not suitable for the patient.

2. Insulin Regimen Selection

Initial Choice (First-Line)

Offer NPH insulin (isophane), injected once or twice daily.

If HbA1c ≥ 75 mmol/mol (9.0%)

Consider a biphasic regimen: NPH insulin + short-acting insulin (as separate injections or a pre-mixed human insulin).

If NPH Causes Problems

Switch to long-acting analogues (insulin detemir or glargine) if the patient has:

  • Recurrent, symptomatic hypoglycemia.
  • A need for a carer to inject (to reduce injection frequency).

If Post-Meal Glycemia is an Issue

Consider pre-mixed analogue insulins if the patient:

  • Prefers injecting immediately before meals.
  • Experiences problematic hypoglycemia or post-meal spikes.

3. Troubleshooting

Persistent Poor Control

Before adjusting dose, check adherence, injection technique, BG monitoring skills, and lifestyle.

Hypoglycemia (BG < 3.5)

Conscious: 15-20g fast-acting carbs. Re-check in 15 mins. Follow with long-acting carbs.
Unconscious: IM glucagon (1mg) and call 999.

Injection Site Problems

Assess sites at every review. Emphasize site rotation. Never inject into lumps (lipohypertrophy).

4. Patient Safety Alerts

Driving (DVLA): Must inform DVLA. Test BG ≤2 hrs before driving & every 2 hrs during. If hypo, stop, treat, and wait 45 mins after BG is normal before driving.
Sick Days: NEVER stop taking insulin. Monitor BG frequently and stay hydrated.
Alcohol: Risk of delayed hypoglycemia. Eat carbs before and after drinking.
