Addison's Disease

NICE CKS Summary • Quick Reference
Target: Primary Care

Adrenal Crisis: DO NOT DELAY

LIFE THREATENING

Consider in critically unwell people with circulatory shock, collapse, or failure to respond to initial treatment.

Clinical Features

  • Circulatory shock / collapse
  • Confusion / altered mental state
  • Severe weakness / lethargy
  • Hypoglycaemia (esp. children)
  • Hyponatraemia / Hyperkalaemia
  • Severe abdominal pain (Acute abdomen mimic)

Common Precipitants

  • Gastrointestinal illness (23%)
  • Infection (25%)
  • Peri-operative stress (10%)
  • Physical stress / pain
  • Missed medication doses
TREAT IMMEDIATELY • DO NOT WAIT FOR INVESTIGATIONS

Suspecting Addison's

Consider if symptoms persist with no other explanation. Diagnosis is often delayed due to non-specific symptoms.

Classic Presentation

Hyperpigmentation Salt craving Dizziness Weight loss Anorexia

Associated Conditions (Autoimmune)

Thyroid disease Type 1 Diabetes Pernicious Anaemia Vitiligo
Note: Hyperpigmentation may not be visible on black/brown skin. Check buccal mucosa or scars. Ask patient about colour changes.

Investigation Logic

If adrenal insufficiency is suspected but urgent treatment is NOT indicated:

Important:
  • Stop oral oestrogen for 6 weeks before test (falsely elevates cortisol).
  • Do not test if taking oral glucocorticoids at physiological doses.
  • Wait 4 weeks after IM/IA steroid injection.

Clinical Reference

  • Primary Adrenal Insufficiency: Caused by destruction of the adrenal cortex.
  • Result: Reduced production of glucocorticoids (cortisol), mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), and adrenal androgens.
  • Mechanism: Loss of cortisol → Loss of negative feedback → Increased ACTH → Hyperpigmentation.
  • Not Addison's: Secondary (pituitary issue) or Tertiary (hypothalamic/long-term steroid use) AI are separate entities, though similar symptoms exist.

Common

  • Autoimmunity (~90% in Western countries)
  • Autoimmune Polyendocrine Syndrome (APS)

Other Causes

  • Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia
  • Infections (TB, HIV, Meningococcus)
  • Adrenal metastases / Haemorrhage
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy
  • Acute Abdomen: Adrenal crisis causes pain/vomiting similar to surgical emergencies.
  • Gastroenteritis: Nausea and vomiting are key features of adrenal failure.
  • Depression: Fatigue, malaise, and anorexia mimic depression.
  • Type 1 Diabetes: Fatigue and weight loss are shared, but glucose is usually normal/low in adrenal failure.
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: Fatigue is predominant in both.
  • Hyperemesis Gravidarum: Vomiting of pregnancy can mask Addison's.
  • Suspect Addison's disease in people with suggestive symptoms.
  • Appropriately manage a person with suspected Addison's disease.
  • Support secondary care plan and liaise with specialists.
  • Educate on sick-day rules and emergency management of crisis.
  • Ensure appropriate drugs are available for emergency treatment.
