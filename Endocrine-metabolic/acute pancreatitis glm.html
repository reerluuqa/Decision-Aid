Clinical Reference

Acute Pancreatitis

Last Revised: May 2021 (NICE CKS)

SUSPECTED ACUTE PANCREATITIS?

Arrange emergency hospital admission immediately. Do not delay for blood tests or imaging in primary care. All people with suspected acute pancreatitis require urgent specialist assessment.

Clinical Presentation

Key Symptoms
  • •
    Severe acute upper abdominal pain
    Sudden onset, constant. Epigastric/RUQ/LUQ.
  • •
    Radiation
    Often radiates to the back or flanks.
  • •
    Associated Symptoms
    Nausea, vomiting, anorexia.

Common Causes

Etiology Breakdown
Gallstones ~50%
Alcohol Misuse ~25%
Other / Idiopathic ~25%
Other Risk Factors: ERCP, Trauma, Hypertriglyceridaemia, Hypercalcaemia, Drugs (e.g. Azathioprine, Steroids, DPP-4 inhibitors).

Examination

Red Flag Signs
  • !
    Signs of Shock
    Tachycardia, Hypotension, Tachypnoea.
  • !
    Peritonism
    Rebound tenderness, guarding (Severe).
  • !
    Fever
    May indicate necrosis or sepsis.
  • !
    Rare Signs
    Cullen's (umbilicus) or Grey-Turner's (flank) ecchymoses.

Severity Classification

It is not possible to classify severity based on clinical findings at presentation alone. However, prognosis varies significantly.

Type Characteristics Prognosis
Mild
80-85% of cases		 No organ failure. No local/systemic complications. Self-limiting. Uncomplicated recovery. Resolves <1 week.
Severe
~20% of cases		 Persistent organ failure (>48 hrs). Local complications (necrosis). Mortality 13-35%. Risk of infected necrosis.
Complications to Monitor
Systemic: Sepsis, ARDS, AKI, DIC.
Local: Pancreatic necrosis, Pseudocyst, Abscess.

Specialist Management

In-Hospital Care

Once admitted, management focuses on supportive care and treating the cause.

  • ✓
    Resuscitation
    IV Fluids (Aggressive hydration).
  • ✓
    Analgesia
    IV pain relief.
  • ✓
    Oxygen
    Supplemental if hypoxic.
  • ✓
    Nutrition
    Early oral/enteral feeding encouraged (unless vomiting severe).
  • ✓
    ERCP
    For gallstone pancreatitis with cholangitis/obstruction.

Differential Diagnosis

What else could it be?
• Perforated Peptic Ulcer
• Bowel Obstruction
• Myocardial Infarction
• Acute Cholecystitis
• Ruptured AAA
• Appendicitis
Post-Discharge Follow-up
  • Manage alcohol misuse.
  • Refer for cholecystectomy (gallstone cases).
  • Screen for hypertriglyceridaemia/hypercalcaemia.
