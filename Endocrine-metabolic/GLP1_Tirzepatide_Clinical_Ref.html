Medications Overview

Liraglutide (Victoza/Saxenda) Semaglutide (Rybelsus/Ozempic/Wegovy) Dulaglutide Exenatide Lixisenatide Tirzepatide (Mounjaro)

Tirzepatide is a dual GLP-1/GIP agonist with superior efficacy for both glycemic control and weight loss compared to GLP-1 monotherapy.

Mechanism of Action

  • Insulin: Increased secretion and improved tissue sensitivity
  • Glucagon: Suppressed secretion (post-prandial hyperglycemia target)
  • Gastric emptying: Slowed, promoting satiety
  • Appetite: Increased satiety, reduced hunger

üí° Key Points

  • Long half-life (5 days) - weekly dosing
  • Steady state reached after 4 weeks
  • Slow up-titration minimizes GI side effects
‚ö†Ô∏è

Cautions & Contraindications

  • History of pancreatitis or severe GI disease - use with caution
  • Delayed gastric emptying - especially after first dose
  • Pregnancy: Avoid - toxic in animal studies. Stop 1 month before trying to conceive.
  • Breastfeeding: Avoid (limited data, but large molecular weight reduces milk secretion)

Drug Interactions

‚ö†Ô∏è Oral Drugs with Narrow Therapeutic Index

Monitor patients closely when starting/increasing tirzepatide dose due to delayed gastric emptying affecting absorption.

üí° Oral Contraceptives

SPC advises: For 4 weeks after initiation or dose increase, use non-oral or barrier methods (tirzepatide reaches steady state after 4 weeks).

‚ö†Ô∏è GI Side Effects - Most Common

These affect more than 1 in 10 patients. Usually non-serious but can lead to severe complications including hospitalization due to dehydration.

  • Always warn patients at initiation and each dose increase
  • Remind patients to keep well hydrated
  • Slow dose titration helps minimize symptoms

üö® Emergency Reference Guide

Critical situations requiring immediate action

ü§ï

Pancreatitis

Symptoms

  • Severe persistent abdominal pain (may radiate to back)
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Fever, tachycardia

Action

STOP medication immediately. Refer for urgent assessment. Admit if severe.

ü©∏

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Risk Factors & Triggers

  • Concomitant insulin use
  • Rapid insulin dose reduction
  • Illness, surgery, trauma

Action

URGENT medical attention required. Check blood ketones if suspected. DKA can occur with normal or mildly elevated glucose.

üíß

Severe Dehydration

  • Signs: Dry mouth, reduced urine output, dizziness, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion
  • Cause: Persistent vomiting/diarrhea from GI side effects
  • Can lead to: Acute kidney injury, hospitalization

Action

Assess hydration status. Consider IV fluids if severe. Advise on oral rehydration. Consider dose reduction or temporary discontinuation.

ü§∞

Pregnancy Detection

Immediate Action Required

  • STOP medication immediately if pregnancy confirmed or suspected
  • Toxic effects in animal studies
  • No adequate human data

Pre-conception

Ensure adequate contraception. Stop tirzepatide 1 month, semaglutide 2 months before trying to conceive.
