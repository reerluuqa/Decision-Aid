Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) Deficiency

Quick Clinical Reference Tool

EMERGENCY ACTION REQUIRED

Presumptive treatment with thiamine replacement therapy should be commenced immediately if vitamin B1 deficiency is suspected. Do not delay treatment while awaiting test results.

Key Risk Factors

Chronic alcohol excess - Primary risk factor in developed countries
Malnutrition - Poor dietary intake of thiamine
Refeeding syndrome - Increased demand during nutritional reintroduction
Gastrointestinal surgery - Especially bariatric procedures
Hyperemesis gravidarum - During pregnancy
Renal replacement therapy - Loss of water-soluble vitamins
Infants (<1 year) in endemic regions - Especially exclusively breastfed

Diagnostic Approach

No rapid diagnostic test

Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Do not delay treatment while awaiting test results.

Laboratory Confirmation

Measurement of thiamine pyrophosphate levels in whole blood or erythrocytes can confirm diagnosis retrospectively.

MRI Findings

For Wernicke's encephalopathy: bilateral and symmetrical lesions in the thalamus, mammillary bodies, tectal plate, and periaqueductal region.

Emergency Treatment Protocol

Immediate Action

Start thiamine replacement immediately if deficiency is suspected, regardless of test results.

1

Wernicke's Encephalopathy

200-500 mg IV three times daily for 2-7 days

2

Beriberi

100-200 mg IV three times daily for 2-3 days

3

Refeeding Syndrome

300 mg IV before initiating nutrition therapy

Critical Warning

Administer thiamine BEFORE glucose infusion. Glucose without thiamine can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy.

Maintenance Therapy

After initial treatment: 250 mg once daily for 3-5 days, followed by 50-100 mg orally once daily

Infants & Children

Slow IV infusion followed by 7 days of IM thiamine, then 3-6 weeks of oral therapy. Mothers of breastfed infants should also receive treatment.
