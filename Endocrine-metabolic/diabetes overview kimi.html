Initial Assessment

Comprehensive evaluation for suspected diabetes mellitus

Clinical History

Key symptoms and risk factors

Classic Symptoms High Yield

  • Polyuria (excessive urination)
  • Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Blurred vision
  • Recurrent infections (skin, urinary, thrush)

Risk Factors Screen

Non-modifiable

  • • Age > 40 years
  • • Family history of diabetes
  • • Ethnicity (South Asian, African-Caribbean)
  • • History of gestational diabetes

Modifiable

  • • Overweight/obesity (BMI ≥ 25 kg/m²)
  • • Physical inactivity
  • • Hypertension
  • • Dyslipidaemia
  • • Smoking

Physical Examination

Focused examination for diabetes assessment

  • BMI and waist circumference - central obesity assessment
  • Blood pressure - hypertension screening
  • Skin examination - acanthosis nigricans, skin infections
  • Foot examination - ulcers, sensation, pulses
  • Eye examination - visual acuity, cataract signs
  • Neurological - peripheral neuropathy signs

Red Flags for Immediate Referral

  • Signs of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • Severe dehydration
  • Altered consciousness/confusion
  • Rapid deep breathing (Kussmaul breathing)
  • Severe hyperglycaemia with vomiting

Initial Investigations

Diagnostic and baseline tests

Diagnostic Tests Essential

HbA1c

Primary diagnostic test (no fasting required)

  • ≥ 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) = Diabetes
  • 42-47 mmol/mol = Prediabetes

Fasting Plasma Glucose

Alternative diagnostic test

  • ≥ 7.0 mmol/L (126 mg/dL) = Diabetes
  • 6.1-6.9 mmol/L = Impaired fasting glucose

OGTT (2-hour)

Used when results equivocal

  • ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL) = Diabetes
  • 7.8-11.0 mmol/L = Impaired glucose tolerance

Baseline Investigations

  • Full blood count
  • Urea and electrolytes
  • Liver function tests
  • Fasting lipid profile
  • Urine albumin:creatinine ratio
  • TFTs (if indicated)

Screen for Complications

Early detection of diabetes-related complications

Retinopathy

Annual eye screening from diagnosis (type 2) or 5 years post-diagnosis (type 1)

Nephropathy

Urine ACR and eGFR annually

Neuropathy

Annual foot examination with monofilament testing

Cardiovascular

BP, lipids, smoking status at each visit

Type Differentiation

Consider antibody testing (GAD, IA-2) and C-peptide if type 1 vs type 2 diabetes unclear, especially in younger patients or rapid onset.

Diagnosis & Classification

Confirm diagnosis and determine diabetes type

Diagnostic Criteria

Blood glucose thresholds

Symptomatic + Random Glucose

Classic symptoms + random venous plasma glucose ≥ 11.1 mmol/L

Fasting Plasma Glucose

After ≥ 8 hours fasting: ≥ 7.0 mmol/L

2-hour OGTT

75g glucose load: ≥ 11.1 mmol/L at 2 hours

HbA1c

≥ 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) - IFCC standard

Important Notes

  • In asymptomatic patients, test must be repeated on separate day
  • Use glucose-specific test (not urine glucose)
  • Consider HbA1c limitations (anaemia, haemoglobinopathies, CKD)

Type Classification

Differentiate type 1 vs type 2 diabetes

Type 1 Diabetes Autoimmune

  • Usually younger onset (< 40 years)
  • Rapid symptom onset (weeks)
  • Significant weight loss
  • Ketosis prone
  • Positive autoantibodies (GAD, IA-2, ZnT8)
  • Low C-peptide

Type 2 Diabetes Insulin Resistance

  • Usually older onset (> 40 years)
  • Gradual onset (months-years)
  • Overweight/obese
  • Metabolic syndrome features
  • Negative autoantibodies
  • Normal/high C-peptide

Diagnostic Clues

Consider LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults) if patient is 30-50 years, lean, with gradual onset but positive antibodies.

Special Situations

Gestational diabetes & other types

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

Screening Timing

24-28 weeks gestation (earlier if high risk)

Diagnosis Criteria (any one)

  • Fasting ≥ 5.1 mmol/L
  • 1-hour ≥ 10.0 mmol/L
  • 2-hour ≥ 8.5 mmol/L

Other Diabetes Types

  • MODY - Maturity Onset Diabetes of Young (genetic)
  • Secondary diabetes - Pancreatic disease, endocrine disorders, drugs
  • Steroid-induced - High-dose glucocorticoids

High-Risk Group

Individuals with prediabetes have 5-10% annual risk of progressing to type 2 diabetes. Intensive lifestyle intervention can reduce risk by 58%.

Management Strategy

  • Lifestyle modification (diet, exercise, weight loss)
  • Annual HbA1c monitoring
  • Consider metformin if high-risk
  • Cardiovascular risk assessment

Treatment Strategy

Evidence-based management approach

Lifestyle Management

Foundation of all diabetes treatment

Medical Nutrition Therapy

  • Individualized meal planning
  • Carbohydrate counting (especially for type 1)
  • Mediterranean or DASH diet patterns
  • Weight loss if BMI ≥ 25 (target 5-10%)
  • Limit saturated fats (< 10% total calories)

Physical Activity

Aerobic Exercise

150 minutes/week moderate intensity (brisk walking, cycling)

Resistance Training

2-3 sessions/week (weights, resistance bands)

Sedentary Behavior

Break up sitting time every 30 minutes

Other Lifestyle Measures

  • Smoking cessation
  • Alcohol moderation
  • Sleep optimization (7-9 hours)
  • Stress management

Cardiovascular & Renal Protection

Prioritize SGLT2i or GLP-1 RA if established ASCVD, heart failure, or CKD

Contraindications

  • SGLT2i: eGFR thresholds apply
  • GLP-1 RA: History of pancreatitis, MEN2
  • Insulin: Hypoglycaemia risk

Type 1 Diabetes Management

Insulin-based therapy

Insulin Regimens Essential

Basal-Bolus Regimen

Multiple daily injections (MDI)

  • Long-acting insulin (Glargine, Detemir, Degludec)
  • Rapid-acting insulin with meals (Aspart, Lispro, Glulisine)

Insulin Pump Therapy (CSII)

Continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion

Consider if MDI not achieving targets, pregnancy, or patient preference

Key Principles

  • Carbohydrate counting for meal boluses
  • Basal rate optimization
  • Correction factor for hyperglycaemia
  • Sick day rules education
  • Hypoglycaemia management

Education & Support

Structured education programs (DAFNE, BERTIE) essential for all type 1 patients

Monitoring Schedule

Regular assessment timeline

Annual Reviews Mandatory

  • HbA1c - Every 3-6 months
  • Eye screening - Digital retinal photography
  • Foot examination - Monofilament, pulses
  • Kidney function - eGFR, urine ACR
  • BP and weight - At every visit
  • Lipids - Annual or as indicated
  • Smoking status - Annual review

Every 3-6 Months

  • Glycaemic control - HbA1c or fructosamine
  • Medication review - Adherence, side effects
  • Hypoglycaemia - Frequency and severity
  • Lifestyle - Diet, activity, weight

Self-Monitoring of Blood Glucose (SMBG)

Type 1 Diabetes

Multiple daily checks (pre-meals, bedtime, driving, exercise). Use CGM if available.

Type 2 Diabetes

Individualized frequency based on medication regimen and glycaemic stability.

Complication Surveillance

Early detection and intervention

Retinopathy

Annual screening: Digital retinal photography

Refer if: Pre-proliferative changes, maculopathy, unexplained visual loss

Nephropathy

Urine ACR: Annual screening

Refer if: ACR ≥ 30 mg/mmol, eGFR < 30 mL/min

Neuropathy

Foot exam: Annual monofilament, vibration

Refer if: Absent pulses, ulceration, deformity

Cardiovascular

Annual: BP, lipids, smoking status

ECG: If symptomatic or high risk

Urgent Referral Criteria

  • Sight-threatening retinopathy
  • Proteinuria with declining eGFR
  • Foot ulceration or infection
  • Symptomatic CVD or abnormal ECG

Patient Education

Empowering self-management

Essential Education Topics Core Curriculum

  • What is diabetes? - Pathophysiology basics
  • Blood glucose monitoring - Technique and interpretation
  • Hypoglycaemia recognition - Symptoms and treatment (15g fast carbs)
  • Hyperglycaemia management - When to seek help
  • Sick day rules - During illness
  • Foot care - Daily inspection and protection
  • Medication adherence - Importance and timing
  • Lifestyle optimization - Diet, activity, smoking

Structured Education Programs

Type 2 Diabetes

DESMOND (Diabetes Education and Self-Management for Ongoing and Newly Diagnosed) - 6-hour structured group education

Type 1 Diabetes

DAFNE (Dose Adjustment For Normal Eating) - 5-day intensive insulin management course

Psychosocial Support

  • Screen for depression and anxiety
  • Address diabetes distress
  • Family involvement in care
  • Peer support groups
  • Consider psychology referral if needed
