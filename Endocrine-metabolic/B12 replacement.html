Common Symptoms and Signs

‚ö†Ô∏è Blood Abnormalities

  • Anaemia or macrocytosis (occurs in <20%)
  • Important: Do NOT rule out B12 deficiency if Hb and MCV are normal

üß† Neurological/Brain

  • Balance problems, impaired gait, falls
  • Sensory ataxia or paraesthesia
  • Poor concentration, short-term memory loss, "brain fog"
  • Optic nerve dysfunction (blurred vision, optic atrophy, scotoma)

üåø Other Signs

  • Unexplained fatigue
  • Glossitis (tongue inflammation)
  • Poor response to iron replacement in pregnancy/breastfeeding

Note: NICE identified an association between mental health issues and B12 deficiency, but there was insufficient evidence to recommend routine testing.

Common Risk Factors

üçΩÔ∏è Dietary Factors

  • No/infrequent animal-source foods
  • No B12-fortified foods/drinks
  • Allergy to eggs, milk, or fish
  • Poor food access/preparation ability (dementia, mental health, low income)
  • Restricted diet (e.g., eating disorder)

üè• Disease/Intervention

  • Any autoimmune condition (coeliac, thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes)
  • Atrophic gastritis of gastric body
  • GI surgery (gastrectomy, terminal ileum resection, bariatric surgery)
  • Abdominal or pelvic radiotherapy

üíä Medications

  • Metformin, colchicine, PPIs, H2-receptor antagonists

  • Recreational nitrous oxide use

üë• Family History

  • Family history of B12 deficiency

Which Test to Order?

üß™ Primary Tests

  • Total B12 (serum cobalamin): Most common test
  • Active B12 (serum holotranscobalamin): More specific

‚ö° Special Situations

  • Pregnant: Always measure serum holotranscobalamin
  • Nitrous oxide use: Measure plasma homocysteine or serum MMA

Special Considerations: OTC B12 supplements can mask deficiency while COCP can lower total B12 without causing true deficiency.

Suspected megaloblastic anaemia AND neurological symptoms (especially subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord)

Further Testing

üîç Anti-Intrinsic Factor Antibody

  • Positive result suggests autoimmune gastritis
  • Negative result doesn't rule it out (sensitivity 40-60%)
  • If negative but still suspected: consider anti-gastric parietal cell antibody, gastrin level, CobaSorb test, or OGD with biopsy

üìä Confirmatory Tests

  • Plasma homocysteine: Rises in B12 deficiency (also raised if low folate)
  • Serum methylmalonic acid (MMA): Rises in B12 deficiency (more specific)

Key Management Principles

‚è±Ô∏è Response Timeline

  • Symptoms may initially worsen
  • Should start improving within 2 weeks
  • May take up to 3 months
  • Neurological symptoms: months to years for complete resolution
  • Anaemia usually resolves within 6-8 weeks

Dosing Regimens

üíâ Intramuscular Hydroxocobalamin

With neurological involvement:

  • Initially: 1 mg IM on alternate days until symptoms stop improving
  • Maintenance: 1 mg every 2 months

Without neurological involvement:

  • Initially: 1 mg IM 3 times weekly for 2 weeks
  • Maintenance: 1 mg IM every 2-3 months

Prophylaxis: 1 mg IM every 2-3 months

üíä Oral Cyanocobalamin

  • Malabsorption (when IM not required): ‚â•1 mg/day (off-label)
  • Dietary deficiency: 50-150 mcg/day (1 mg/day if pregnant/breastfeeding)
  • Other causes: 1-2 mg/day (off-label)

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule: B before F

If deficient in both folate and B12, start B12 replacement at least 24 hours before starting folate to avoid precipitating subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.

ü§∞ Pregnant/Breastfeeding

üë• All Other Patients

  • Initial follow-up at 3 months after starting treatment

When to Stop Treatment?

‚úÖ Criteria for Stopping

ALL of the following must be met:

  • All symptoms have resolved
  • Underlying cause has been identified AND resolved
  • Examples: Stopped being vegetarian; coeliac disease stable on strict gluten-free diet

Special case: If appropriate to stop but pregnant/breastfeeding, continue until delivery/stops breastfeeding (benefits outweigh risks)

‚ö†Ô∏è Reality Check

For many patients, treatment is lifelong because the underlying cause is irreversible (e.g., autoimmune gastritis, surgical causes).

Troubleshooting: Symptoms Not Improving

If on IM Treatment:

üòî Symptoms Not Better or Worsening

  • Increase frequency of injections per SPC
  • Consider alternative diagnosis
  • Arrange follow-up

üòä Symptoms Improved

  • Continue IM treatment
  • Review if cause resolved and addressed

If on Oral Treatment:

üìâ Symptoms Not Improved

  • Increase oral B12 to maximum licensed dose
  • If already at maximum, switch to IM

üö® Symptoms Worsening/New Symptoms

  • Check if alternative test (MMA/homocysteine) has been done
  • If test confirms deficiency: increase dose or switch to IM
  • If test negative: explore alternative diagnosis

Monitoring Tests

ü©∏ B12 Levels on IM Treatment

No, do not recheck B12 levels for patients on IM therapy. Treatment impacts test results, making them unhelpful for monitoring. Instead, be guided by presence or resolution of symptoms.

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Netting

Advise all patients to return if they develop new or worsening symptoms of B12 deficiency, regardless of current treatment.
