Non-Diabetic Hypoglycaemia

Interactive Clinical Decision Support Algorithm - BMJ Best Practice

Last updated: September 2024 | For adult patients without diabetes mellitus

Assessment

Comprehensive evaluation of symptoms, history, and risk factors

Whipple's Triad - Diagnostic Criteria

True hypoglycaemia requires ALL three:

  1. Hypoglycaemic symptoms present
  2. Documented low serum glucose (<3.3 mmol/L or <60 mg/dL)
  3. Symptoms resolve after raising glucose to normal

Clinical Presentation

Sympathoadrenal Symptoms

  • Sweating (diaphoresis)
  • Anxiety and tremor
  • Nausea and palpitations
  • Hunger sensation
  • Generalised tingling
Typically begins <3.0 mmol/L

Neuroglycopenic Symptoms

  • Blurred vision & dizziness
  • Confusion & irritability
  • Dysarthria & somnolence
  • Seizures (severe)
  • Focal neurological deficits
Typically occurs <2.8 mmol/L

Key Diagnostic Factors

Important Clinical Clues
  • Unexplained weight gain may suggest insulinoma
  • Weight loss may suggest adrenal insufficiency
  • Hyperpigmentation suggests adrenal insufficiency
  • Short stature suggests growth hormone deficiency
  • Hypotension suggests adrenal insufficiency

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

  • Middle age (median 47 years)
  • Insulinoma (0.7-4 cases/million/year)
  • Exogenous insulin exposure
  • Sulfonylurea use
  • Bariatric surgery history
  • Critical illness/multi-organ failure

Weak Risk Factors

  • Female sex (59% predominance)
  • Heavy alcohol consumption
  • Liver or renal failure
  • Intense exercise
  • Mesenchymal tumors (sarcomas, fibromas)
  • Malnutrition/anorexia nervosa
  • Ackee fruit ingestion
  • Various medications (quinine, tramadol, PPIs, etc.)

Temporal Relationships

Timing of Symptoms

  • Fasting state: Suggests insulinoma, IGF-II hypersecretion, or endocrine dysfunction
  • Postprandial (1-2 hours): Suggests islet cell hypertrophy or nesidioblastosis
  • Late reactive (3-5 hours): May occur in prediabetes or pregnancy

Diagnosis

Investigation strategy and diagnostic confirmation

Immediate Investigations (When Symptoms Present)

Serum Glucose

  • Laboratory measurement (NOT glucometer)
  • Confirm <3.3 mmol/L (<60 mg/dL)
  • If <2.8 mmol/L, collect additional tests immediately
Critical

Serum Insulin & C-Peptide

  • Insulin >21 pmol/L (>3 μU/mL)
  • C-Peptide >200 pmol/L
  • Inappropriate elevation suggests insulinoma or sulfonylurea use
Important

Beta-Hydroxybutyrate

  • Excessive insulin inhibits ketogenesis
  • Low levels (<2.7 mmol/L) suggest insulinoma or IGF-II secretion
Important

Additional Tests

  • Sulfonylurea screen (urine or serum)
  • Liver & renal function tests
  • Cortisol, TSH (if endocrine suspected)
  • Ethanol level (if indicated)
Standard

48-72 Hour Fasting Test

1
Indication & Preparation
Inpatient Only

Indicated when: Symptoms present but glucose >2.8 mmol/L

Duration: Full 72-hour fast recommended (48-hour may miss insulinoma)

Preparation: Non-caloric, caffeine-free beverages allowed; encourage activity

2
Monitoring Protocol
  • Check glucose every 6 hours initially
  • When fingerstick <3.3 mmol/L: check hourly
  • Collect serum: glucose, insulin, C-peptide, proinsulin at each check
  • Test ends at: symptoms, glucose <2.8 mmol/L, or 72 hours complete
3
End-of-Test Actions
  • Collect: sulfonylurea, beta-hydroxybutyrate, cortisol, HGH, ACTH
  • Administer glucagon 1 mg IM
  • Check glucose at 30 minutes
  • Allow patient to eat
  • Positive test: Glucose increase >1.4 mmol/L suggests insulin excess

Imaging Studies

Imaging Sequence for Insulinoma
  1. CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast
  2. Transabdominal ultrasound (if CT negative)
  3. Endoscopic ultrasound (if both negative)
  4. Octreotide nuclear scan (last resort)
IGF-II Secreting Tumors

Large mesenchymal tumors are usually detectable on physical exam when they cause clinically significant hypoglycaemia.

CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast: Primary imaging modality

Tumor types: Sarcomas, fibromas, fibrosarcomas, renal cell carcinoma

Differential Diagnoses

Idiopathic Postprandial Syndrome

  • Symptoms after carbohydrate-rich meal
  • Glucose >3.3 mmol/L during symptoms
  • 5-hour mixed meal test has poor sensitivity
Benign

Insulin Autoimmune Hypoglycaemia

  • Extremely rare condition
  • Usually spontaneously resolves
  • Test: Insulin antibodies & insulin receptor antibodies
Rare

Diabetic Hypoglycaemia

  • History of diabetes mellitus
  • OGTT: 2-hour glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L
  • Clinical presentation similar to non-diabetic
Exclude first

Alternative Tests

Insulin Suppression Test

  • Administer insulin 0.4-0.6 units/kg IV after overnight fast
  • Glucose should decline to <3.3 mmol/L within 15-25 min
  • Draw serum at intervals for glucose, C-peptide, cortisol, HGH
  • Give 50 mL 50% dextrose IV at completion

Treatment

Management approach based on underlying aetiology

Universal Supportive Care

All Patients Require

Glucose administration: D10 or D50 infusion, or glucagon injection

Monitoring: Frequent blood glucose checks until stable

Goal: Maintain glucose in normal range and prevent recurrence

Treatment Algorithm by Aetiology

Reactive Hypoglycaemia

1st Line
Dietary Changes
  • High protein, low carbohydrate diet
  • Frequent, smaller meals
  • High fiber diet or supplementation
Lifestyle Intervention

Medication/Toxin/Ethanol Exposure

1st Line
Supportive Care ± Psychiatric Evaluation
  • Inpatient monitoring until substance cleared
  • Variable timeframe (prolonged with organ dysfunction)
  • Screen for intentional overdose
  • Psychiatric referral if suicidality present
May Require Commitment

Bariatric Surgery / Malnutrition

1st Line
Supportive Care + Specialist Referral
  • Correct glucose deficiency
  • Endocrinology consultation
  • Gastrointestinal/dietary specialist
  • Eating disorder specialist if anorexia
Multidisciplinary

Insulinoma

1st Line
Surgical Excision + Supportive Care
  • Definitive treatment: surgical excision
  • Pre-op: Self-administered glucagon education
  • Post-op: Re-explore if events persist (missed lesion or metastases)
Urgent Surgical Referral
2nd Line
Medical Therapy (if surgery fails/contraindicated)
  • Diazoxide: 3-8 mg/kg/day PO in 2-3 doses (inhibits insulin secretion)
  • Octreotide: 50 mcg SC 2-3 times daily (inhibits insulin secretion)
  • Streptozocin: Consult for dosing (may destroy tumor)
Significant Side Effects
Metastatic
Focal Embolisation or Chemotherapy

For rare malignant insulinoma with metastases

IGF-II Secreting Tumor

1st Line
Surgical Excision ± Chemotherapy/Radiotherapy
  • Refer to surgeon for tumor excision
  • Pre-op: Glucose infusion or glucagon as needed
  • Consider tumor burden reduction therapies
Urgent Oncology Referral

Organ Failure / Sepsis / Endocrinopathy

1st Line
Manage Underlying Condition + Supportive Care
  • Focus on treating primary condition
  • Glucose infusion until condition resolves
  • Monitor organ function closely
ICU May Be Required

Important Considerations

Medication Side Effects

  • Diazoxide: Oedema, hirsutism, hypotension with CCBs
  • Octreotide: Inhibits TSH, GH, glucagon
  • Streptozocin: May cause diabetes mellitus

Emerging Therapies

  • Verapamil: May inhibit insulin secretion (limited evidence)
  • Phenytoin: May inhibit insulin secretion (limited evidence)
  • Corticosteroids: Successful in some IGF-II tumor cases
Limited Evidence

Patient Education

Key Messages

  • Recognize early symptoms of hypoglycaemia
  • Always carry fast-acting carbohydrates
  • Seek prompt medical assessment if symptoms recur
  • Wear medical alert identification

Follow-up

Monitoring, complications, and long-term prognosis

Monitoring After Treatment

Post-Surgical Follow-up

  • Wound healing assessment
  • Monitor for postoperative complications
  • Frequency per surgeon discretion
  • Glucose monitoring until stable
Routine

Medically Managed Cases

  • Frequent follow-up for medication titration
  • Monitor for side effects
  • Assess symptom control
  • Continue until hypoglycaemia resolves
Close Monitoring

Potential Complications

Seizure

Central nervous system manifestation due to severe hypoglycaemia

Resolution: Correct glucose levels

Likelihood: Low

Timeframe: Short-term (during acute episode)

Coma

Extreme manifestation from insufficient brain glucose supply

Resolution: Requires immediate glucose correction

Likelihood: Low

Risk: Permanent neurological sequelae possible

Diabetes Mellitus

Reported after repeat surgery for insulinoma

Mechanism: Destruction of normal islet cells

Likelihood: Low

Timeframe: Long-term complication

Prognosis

Post-Surgical Prognosis

  • Cure rate: Most patients cured if all tumors excised
  • Recurrence: Small portion may need repeat surgery for incomplete excision or metastases
  • Long-term: Excellent prognosis with complete resection
Excellent Prognosis

Medically Managed Insulinoma

  • Effectiveness: Limited and not as effective as surgery
  • Best agent: Diazoxide most effective
  • Complications: Common with medical therapy
Guarded Prognosis

Inoperable IGF-II Secreting Tumors

  • Glucagon refractoriness: Strong possibility of becoming refractory
  • Alternative: Oral glucocorticoids may help (case studies)
  • Prognosis: Poor with limited treatment options
Poor Prognosis

Exposure-Related Hypoglycaemia

  • Outlook: Favorable
  • Recurrence: Should not recur if exposure avoided
  • Resolution: As toxin/medication is metabolized
Excellent Prognosis

Follow-up Schedule Recommendations

1
Immediate Post-Treatment (1-4 weeks)
  • Weekly glucose monitoring
  • Symptom assessment
  • Medication side effect monitoring
  • Surgical wound check (if applicable)
2
Short-term (1-6 months)
  • Monthly visits until stable
  • Gradually extend interval if asymptomatic
  • Annual glucose tolerance test (post-insulinoma surgery)
  • Screen for diabetes development
3
Long-term (6+ months)
  • Every 6-12 months for insulinoma patients
  • Annual screening for recurrence
  • Patient education reinforcement
  • Consider genetic counseling if hereditary syndrome suspected
