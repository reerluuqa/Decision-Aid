Subclinical Hypothyroidism – Interactive Decision Aid

Offline • Open index.html
This tool summarises key points and provides an interactive recommendation engine for adults with suspected subclinical hypothyroidism (non‑pregnant). Always apply clinical judgment.

Guideline summary

Reference ranges used in logic (updated FT4 10/05/2024): TSH 0.38–5.33 mU/L • Free T4 8.0–18.0 pmol/L • Free T3 3.8–6.0 pmol/L.

Definition & first steps

  • Subclinical hypothyroidism (SCH) = elevated TSH (>5.33 mU/L) with normal free T4 (8.0–18.0 pmol/L).
  • Repeat TSH in ~3 months to confirm persistent elevation before acting.
  • Check thyroid peroxidase antibodies (TPOAb); positivity ↑ risk of progression.

When to consider treatment

  • TSH ≥ 10 mU/L on two tests 3 months apart: consider levothyroxine.
  • TSH 5.34–9.99 mU/L (i.e., above lab upper limit but <10):
    • If < 65 years and symptomatic: consider a 6‑month trial of levothyroxine. Stop if symptoms persist once TSH normalises.
    • Otherwise: weigh features suggesting thyroid disease (TPOAb+, prior thyroid surgery or radioiodine, clear hypothyroid symptoms) vs observation.
  • ≥ 65 years: be more hesitant; over‑suppression risks (e.g., AF) may outweigh benefits.

If treating – how to start

  • Take on an empty stomach, ≥30 minutes before breakfast/caffeine/other meds.
  • Start low if ≥65y or cardiovascular disease: 25–50 mcg/day, titrate to TSH.
  • Otherwise: estimate dose ≈ 1.6 mcg/kg/day, round to nearest 25 mcg (e.g., 60 kg → 100 mcg; 80 kg → 125 mcg; 100 kg → 150 mcg).

If not treating – monitoring

  • With features of thyroid disease (e.g., TPOAb+, prior thyroid surgery): check TSH/FT4 annually.
  • Without such features: check every 2–3 years.
Evidence & notes
  • Progression to overt hypothyroidism occurs in ~2–6% per year; risk ↑ with female sex, age, TPOAb+.
  • TSH >10 mU/L is associated with higher coronary heart disease risk and mortality in some cohorts.
  • Randomised trials show no clear improvement in symptoms/quality of life from routine treatment overall.
Not applicable to pregnancy or those trying to conceive; seek specialist guidance.
This summary is provided for rapid reference. Always individualise decisions and consult local policies.
