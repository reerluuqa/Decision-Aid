Hypercalcaemia Management

Primary Care Algorithm | NICE CKS September 2024 | Adult patients (18+ years)

Assessment

When to Suspect Hypercalcaemia

Incidental Finding Malignancy Deterioration
  • Incidental finding of corrected serum calcium ≥2.6 mmol/L on blood testing
  • Sudden deterioration in patient with advanced cancer
  • Suggestive clinical features (often non-specific):

Clinical Features by System

Severity correlates with: Calcium level, rate of onset, and underlying cause
  • Skeletal: Bone pain, skeletal deformities, osteoporosis, fragility fractures
  • Neuromuscular: Drowsiness, delirium, coma, fatigue, muscle weakness, insomnia, impaired concentration
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, constipation, abdominal pain
  • Renal: Renal colic, polyuria, polydipsia, nocturia, renal impairment
  • Cardiovascular: Hypertension, shortened QT interval, arrhythmias (rare)

Assessment Checklist

  • Symptoms: Duration, severity, onset pattern
  • Malignancy symptoms: Fever, weight loss, night sweats, decreased appetite, cough
  • Medical history: Osteoporosis, fractures, renal stones, malignancy
  • Medications: Thiazides, lithium, vitamin D/A, calcium supplements
  • Family history: Hypercalcaemia, genetic endocrinopathies
  • Examination: Hydration status, cognitive function, head/neck/abdominal exam
  • Review previous calcium levels: Assess chronicity and progression
Pattern Recognition: Primary hyperparathyroidism = mild, stable, slowly progressive; Malignancy = rapid-onset, severe, with systemic symptoms
Diagnosis

Confirmation

Initial Test: Serum calcium (adjust for albumin) - avoid prolonged tourniquet
  • Repeat raised calcium test to confirm diagnosis
  • Frequency depends on severity and clinical presentation
  • Mild/asymptomatic: Repeat after 1 week
  • Severe/symptomatic: Confirm in secondary care setting

Severity Classification

Mild: 2.6–2.99 mmol/L Moderate: 3.0–3.5 mmol/L Severe: >3.5 mmol/L
  • Mild: Often asymptomatic, may be stable over years (primary hyperparathyroidism)
  • Moderate: Usually symptomatic, requires prompt treatment
  • Severe: Emergency requiring immediate hospital admission

Key Investigations

Core Tests:

  • PTH: Raised in primary hyperparathyroidism; suppressed/undetectable in malignancy
  • FBC: Exclude anaemia of chronic disease/haematologic malignancy
  • ESR/CRP: Elevated in malignancy/inflammatory conditions
  • eGFR/Creatinine: Assess hydration, AKI, CKD
  • Serum/urine protein electrophoresis: Exclude myeloma

Targeted Tests:

  • Thyroid function: If thyrotoxicosis suspected
  • Vitamin D: If toxicity suspected
  • Cortisol (8-9am): If Addison's disease suspected
  • Chest X-ray: Exclude lung cancer, lymphoma, sarcoidosis, TB
Note: If PTH testing not available in primary care, refer to endocrinologist unless clear non-endocrine cause
Treatment

Emergency Management

Severe: >3.5 mmol/L Severe Symptoms
Immediate Action: Arrange emergency hospital admission for IV fluids and IV bisphosphonate therapy
  • Severe hypercalcaemia (>3.5 mmol/L) = Emergency admission
  • Severe symptoms (delirium, coma, severe dehydration) = Emergency admission
  • Treatment: IV rehydration, IV bisphosphonates/denosumab, calcitonin, steroids (if indicated)

Urgent Management

Moderate: 3.0–3.5 mmol/L Symptomatic
Same-day referral: Prompt treatment usually required in hospital/ambulatory care
  • Moderate hypercalcaemia (3.0-3.5 mmol/L) + symptoms = Urgent referral
  • Known malignancy + moderate hypercalcaemia = Urgent assessment
  • Liaise with on-call endocrinologist, ambulatory care, or medical team

Primary Care Management

Mild: 2.6–2.99 mmol/L Asymptomatic
Investigate & Refer: Full clinical assessment and initial investigations before specialist referral

Reversible Causes:

  • Thiazide diuretics: Stop if possible, repeat calcium in 3 weeks
  • Lithium: Liaise with mental health specialist
  • Excess intake: Stop calcium/vitamin D/vitamin A supplements, repeat in 3 weeks

Specialist Liaison:

  • Raised PTH: Refer to endocrinology (primary hyperparathyroidism)
  • Malignancy suspected: Refer via local cancer pathway
  • Other conditions: Refer to relevant specialty (renal, thyroid, etc.)
  • No clear cause: Refer to endocrinology for further investigation
Always advise: Maintain adequate oral fluid intake

Known Malignancy

Key Decision: Discuss with patient/carers whether treatment is appropriate
  • Palliative care: May not be appropriate if approaching end of life
  • Severe/Moderate: Emergency/urgent admission as above
  • Mild/Asymptomatic: Liaise with oncologist/palliative care specialist
  • Review medications: Stop thiazides, calcium/vitamin D supplements if appropriate
Follow-up

Primary Hyperparathyroidism (Conservative)

Monitoring: Patients not having surgery or unsuccessful surgery
  • Annual: Serum calcium, vitamin D, eGFR/creatinine
  • Every 2 years: Bone mineral density (DXA scan)
  • If indicated: Renal imaging (ultrasound/CT) for stones
  • If indicated: X-ray/vertebral fracture assessment for height loss/back pain
  • Cardiovascular: Risk assessment
Re-refer if: Symptoms develop, calcium increases ≥0.25 mmol/L above normal, eGFR <60, renal stones, osteoporosis/fragility fractures

Post-Parathyroidectomy

  • Annual: Check calcium levels
  • If complications: Seek specialist advice about monitoring (osteoporosis, renal stones)
  • Cure rate: >95% for solitary adenoma, recurrence <2%

Malignancy-Associated

Recurrence: Usually returns 1–4 weeks after calcium-lowering treatment if cancer not actively treated
  • Monitor symptoms: Of hypercalcaemia and malignancy
  • Biochemistry: Serum calcium, eGFR, creatinine (frequency per specialist advice)
  • Liaison: With oncologist or palliative care specialist
  • Support: Cancer Research UK patient information resources

Lifestyle Advice for All Patients

Hydration Mobilization Vitamin D
  • Hydration: Maintain adequate oral fluid intake (avoid dehydration)
  • Vitamin D: Ensure repletion (cautious supplementation arranged by specialist)
  • Dietary calcium: Sufficient but not excessive intake
  • Mobilization: Encourage activity where possible (avoid immobility)
  • Avoid exacerbating meds: Thiazides, calcium-containing antacids, excessive vitamin A/D, NSAIDs
