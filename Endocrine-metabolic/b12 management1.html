B12 Deficiency in Adults

NICE NG239 (2024) — Clinical Decision Support

! Affects 5% of those aged 65–74y and >10% of those ≥75y. Symptoms are numerous, varied, and not specific to B12 deficiency.

Testing Decision

Who to test depends on:

  • Presence of symptoms/signs compatible with B12 deficiency AND
  • Presence of any underlying risk factors for B12 deficiency

Result Categories

NICE defines three result categories:

  • Normal: total B12 >350 ng/L
  • Low: total B12 <180 ng/L
  • Indeterminate: 180–350 ng/L

Immediate Treatment

In some cases, immediate B12 replacement is required rather than waiting for B12 test results.

Lifelong IM Required

If cause is irreversible, lifelong IM B12 replacement needed:

  • Autoimmune gastritis
  • Total gastrectomy
  • Complete terminal ileum resection

Lab Risk Warning

Labs may report values as 'normal' that NICE would classify as indeterminate. Always check against NICE thresholds.

Co-existing Deficiencies

B12 deficiency often co-exists with folate deficiency. If deficient in both, start B12 replacement at least 24h before starting folate ('B before F').

Clinical Impact

Symptoms can affect many aspects of a person's life, including family, social life, work and education. B12 replacement is an effective treatment for most people. Early identification and treatment can avoid permanent neurological disability.

Physiology Reminder

What B12 Does

B12 (cobalamin) acts as a co-factor for two enzymes. Necessary for:

  • Normal bone marrow function and erythropoiesis
  • Development and myelination of the nervous system and its maintenance

Dietary Sources

B12 is found in:

  • Animal products: meat, fish, eggs, milk
  • Fortified foods: breakfast cereals, fortified plant milks

CLINICAL JUDGEMENT

Symptoms but NO Risk Factors

If symptoms/signs present BUT no common risk factors:

Use clinical judgement considering:

  • Likelihood of undiagnosed risk factor (e.g. undiagnosed atrophic gastritis)
  • Are clinical features such that missing B12 deficiency could be devastating (e.g. ataxia)
NOTE

Mental Health

NICE identified an association between mental health issues and B12 deficiency, BUT there was a lack of evidence to recommend testing based on mental health alone.

Evidence Base

NICE decided who to test based on expert consensus, rather than because there is strong evidence for this approach.

Symptoms & Signs

Numerous, varied, and not specific to B12 deficiency

⚠ Do NOT rule out B12 deficiency if Hb and/or MCV are normal — macrocytic anaemia occurs in <20% of cases.

Blood

  • Anaemia
  • Macrocytosis

N.B. Normal Hb/MCV does NOT exclude B12 deficiency

Balance / Neurological

  • Balance problems
  • Impaired gait or falls
  • Sensory ataxia
  • Paraesthesia

Brain / Cognitive

  • Poor concentration
  • Short-term memory loss
  • Brain fog

Tiredness

  • Unexplained fatigue

Tongue

  • Glossitis

Optic Nerve Dysfunction

  • Blurred vision
  • Optic atrophy
  • Visual field loss (scotoma)

Iron Response

  • Poor response to iron replacement in pregnancy/breastfeeding

Risk Factors

Underlying conditions, medications, and dietary factors

Diet Low in B12

  • No/infrequent animal-source foods
  • No food/drinks fortified with B12
  • Allergy to eggs, milk or fish
  • Poor access to/inability to prepare B12 foods (dementia, mental health, low income)
  • Restricted diet (e.g. eating disorder)
  • Not taking OTC B12 supplements

Disease / Intervention

  • Any autoimmune condition (coeliac, thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes)
  • Atrophic gastritis of the gastric body
  • Gastrointestinal surgery (gastrectomy, terminal ileum resection, bariatric surgery — Roux-en-Y bypass or sleeve gastrectomy)
  • Abdominal or pelvic radiotherapy

Medications

  • Metformin
  • Colchicine
  • PPIs
  • H2-receptor antagonists
  • Phenobarbital
  • Pregabalin
  • Primidone
  • Topiramate
  • Recreational nitrous oxide use

Family History

  • Of B12 deficiency
  • Of any autoimmune condition

Which Test?

No gold-standard test exists — selection depends on clinical context

Available Tests

  • Total B12 (serum cobalamin) — most common, most cost-effective
  • Active B12 (serum holotranscobalamin) — thought to be more accurate
  • Plasma homocysteine — needs secondary care sample, direct to lab on ice
  • Serum MMA (methylmalonic acid)

NICE Recommendation

Measure total B12 or active B12.

Exceptions:

  • If pregnant: always measure holotranscobalamin (active B12)
  • If nitrous oxide use: measure homocysteine or MMA

Test Limitations

OTC B12 Interference

Taking OTC B12 can cause rises in total/active B12 but mask a deficiency that has not been properly addressed.

Example: Patient develops glossitis, self-treats with OTC B12, bloods show normal B12 — but the cause IS a partially treated deficiency.

COCP Interference

COCP can lower total B12 levels without causing a deficiency. Difficult to know if this is a true deficiency or false positive.

MMA Limitations

  • Recent antibiotic use can cause a false normal result
  • Can be elevated in renal impairment

Managing the Indeterminate Range

TREAT

Pregnant/Breastfeeding

Consider treating due to potential risks of not treating to foetus/newborn.

TREAT

Neurological/Haematological Risk

Underlying condition that could deteriorate rapidly (e.g. anaemia or ataxia). MMA may help confirm, but treat while awaiting result.

CONSIDER TREAT

Irreversible Cause Known

Autoimmune gastritis, gastrectomy, terminal ileum resection, certain bariatric surgery. High risk of developing deficiency — all should be on prophylaxis.

BETTER TEST

Symptoms but No High-Risk Features

Consider MMA to get more accurate idea of B12 status.

WATCH & WAIT

No Symptoms

No symptoms/signs and none of the above conditions. Review if symptoms develop.

Immediate Treatment

When NOT to wait for test results

START B12 REPLACEMENT IMMEDIATELY

If suspected megaloblastic anaemia AND neurological symptoms (especially any symptoms of subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord)

  • What is a 'suspected megaloblastic anaemia'? Symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency with or without an abnormal FBC
  • Symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency + FBC shows raised MCV/anaemia without blood film to confirm megaloblasts
  • Symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency and clinician suspects (without any tests) that the cause may be B12 deficiency

CONSIDER STARTING WHILE AWAITING RESULTS

If suspected B12 deficiency and cause is likely to be due to a drug

Test B12, but do not necessarily wait for test results before starting treatment.

Subacute Combined Degeneration of the Cord

Neurological Complication of B12 Deficiency

  • Potentially reversible if caught early
  • B12 deficiency results in demyelination of the spinal cord
  • Classically affects posterior (dorsal) columns
  • Symptoms: impaired proprioception, loss of vibration sense, sensory changes, hyperreflexia, muscle weakness
  • Symmetrical distal limb paraesthesia common initially
  • If left untreated, irreversible damage can occur

Megaloblastic Anaemia

What Is It?

A type of macrocytic anaemia. B12 deficiency causes ineffective erythropoiesis:

  • Erythroblasts become large and abnormal (megaloblastic)
  • Identifiable on blood film
  • May show poikilocytosis, anisocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils
  • Can occur with B12 and/or folate deficiency

IM vs Oral Route

Selection depends on underlying cause

LIFELONG IM REQUIRED

Intramuscular B12 must be used if:

  • Autoimmune gastritis
  • Total gastrectomy
  • Complete terminal ileum resection
  • Pregnant/breastfeeding and autoimmune gastritis suspected

Why? Inability to absorb B12 through the GI tract — oral replacement may be ineffective.

Route by Cause

Cause Route Duration & Notes
Autoimmune gastritis, total gastrectomy, complete terminal ileum resection IM Lifelong. IM must be used.
Other malabsorption (coeliac, partial gastrectomy, other bariatric surgery) Consider IM Depends on whether cause is treatable. IM more cost-effective if ≥6m. Oral dose ≥1mg/d cyanocobalamin if used.
Medication-induced Oral or IM While using causative drug and until symptoms resolved. Review medication — can it be stopped/changed?
Recreational nitrous oxide Oral or IM While using and until symptoms resolved. Advise stopping nitrous oxide.
Diet-related Consider oral (OTC) While diet remains unchanged and until symptoms resolved. Consider IM if: rapid deterioration risk, concerns about adherence.
Unknown cause (not likely malabsorption) Consider oral Likely will need to continue long-term.
Neurological Symptoms at Presentation

The BNF outlines an increased frequency of IM replacement for both loading AND maintenance if neurological symptoms present. The safest option would be IM replacement.

IM vs Oral: Evidence Summary

IM B12

  • Standard: 1mg hydroxocobalamin IM
  • More cost-effective if used ≥6 months
  • Absorption not dependent on GI function
  • Current UK practice uses hydroxocobalamin

Oral B12

  • At 2000mcg/d, higher B12 levels than IM (Cochrane)
  • Can use cyanocobalamin, methylcobalamin, or adenosylcobalamin
  • Absorption difficult to judge in malabsorption
  • Self-administration easier for patients

Dosing Protocols

Based on BNF guidelines

Key Rule

If deficient in both folate and B12, start B12 replacement at least 24 hours before starting folate ('B before F').

Intramuscular Hydroxocobalamin

Indication Initial (Loading) Maintenance
B12 deficiency WITH neurological involvement 1mg IM on alternate days until symptoms stop improving 1mg IM every 2 months
B12 deficiency WITHOUT neurological involvement 1mg IM 3 times per week for 2 weeks 1mg IM every 2–3 months
Prophylaxis (e.g. post-gastrectomy, ileal resection) — 1mg IM every 2–3 months (BNF)
Obesity & Metabolic Surgery Society: 1mg IM every 3 months for sleeve/Roux-en-Y

Oral Cyanocobalamin

Indication Dose Notes
Malabsorption (when IM not required) ≥1 mg/day Off-licence dose per NICE
Dietary deficiency — Pregnancy/breastfeeding 1 mg/day Higher than standard dose
Dietary deficiency — Everyone else 50–150 mcg/day Licensed dose
Other causes (when IM not required) 1–2 mg/day Off-licence (BJH suggestion)
⚠ When using oral B12 for malabsorption: if already on maximum dose and symptoms not improving, switch to IM.

Follow-Up & Monitoring

Response to treatment and when to review

Response Timeline

  • Symptoms may initially worsen
  • Should start to improve within 2 weeks
  • May take up to 3 months
  • Complete resolution: potentially months to years for neurological symptoms
  • Anaemia usually resolves within 6–8 weeks

Review If

  • Symptoms have not improved
  • Symptoms have worsened
  • Symptoms return
  • New symptoms appear after starting treatment

Follow-Up Schedule

Pregnancy/Breastfeeding

Initial follow-up at 1 month

Everyone Else

Initial follow-up at 3 months after starting treatment, or earlier depending on symptom severity.

! DO NOT recheck B12 levels when on IM therapy. Treatment will impact test results. Blood tests should not be used to monitor clinical improvement or decide injection frequency. Be guided by presence or resolution of symptoms.

IM Treatment Pathway

Irreversible Cause

Lifelong IM treatment even if symptom-free. Review if new/worsening symptoms.

Symptoms Not Better/Worsening

  • Increase frequency per SPC
  • Consider alternative diagnosis
  • Arrange follow-up

Symptoms Improved/Resolved

AND cause identified and addressed:

  • Consider stopping/decreasing frequency
  • Safety-net: return if symptoms recur

AND cause unknown/ongoing:

  • Continue treatment
  • Stopping risks return of deficiency

Oral Treatment Pathway

Symptoms Not Improved

  • Increase oral B12 to maximum licensed dose
  • If already at max → switch to IM

Symptoms Worsening/New

Consider alternative diagnosis. Check: has alternative test been done (MMA/homocysteine)?

If alternative test confirms deficiency: increase dose or switch to IM.

If no alternative test done: do MMA/homocysteine (continue treatment while awaiting results).

Symptoms Improved/Resolved

AND cause addressed (e.g. diet changed, drug stopped): consider stopping. Safety-net for return of symptoms.

AND cause unknown/ongoing: continue treatment.

When to Stop Treatment

Can Stop If

  • All symptoms resolve AND
  • Underlying cause identified AND resolved
  • E.g.: stopped being vegetarian; coeliac disease stable on strict gluten-free diet

Do NOT Stop If

  • Pregnant/breastfeeding (continue until delivery/weaning)
  • Underlying cause is irreversible
  • Benefits of treating outweigh risk of stopping

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

Special considerations and management

! B12 replacement is safe in pregnancy and breastfeeding. There is no known harm of treatment, and stopping treatment risks harm.

NICE Recommendations

Scenario Management
B12 deficiency + autoimmune gastritis suspected Offer IM replacement
Dietary deficiency Offer 1mg/day oral cyanocobalamin (rather than licensed 50–150mcg/day)
Follow-up Initial follow-up at 1 month (rather than 3 months)
During pregnancy/breastfeeding Do NOT stop B12 replacement
Testing in pregnancy Always measure holotranscobalamin (active B12)

Risks of Untreated B12 Deficiency in Pregnancy

Adverse Pregnancy Outcomes

  • Neural tube defects
  • Early pregnancy loss
  • Pre-eclampsia
  • Preterm birth
  • Low birth weight

Neonatal Complications

  • Hypotonia
  • Failure to thrive
  • Apnoeas
  • Seizures
Iron Response

Poor response to iron replacement in pregnancy/breastfeeding may be a sign of B12 deficiency. Consider testing.

Metformin & B12

MHRA guidance (June 2022)

⚠ 1 in 10 people on metformin are affected — much more common than previous estimates.

Mechanism

Thought to be multifactorial:

  • Altered intestinal motility
  • Reduced absorption of B12 in ileum
  • Bacterial overgrowth

Risk Factors (on Metformin)

  • B12 at lower end of normal range
  • No food of animal origin
  • Older people (reduced absorption)
  • Malabsorption conditions (IBD, previous surgery)
  • Other drugs impairing B12 absorption (PPI, colchicine)
  • Genetic conditions (Imerslund-Gräsbeck syndrome, transcobalamin II deficiency)

What To Do

If Symptoms Present

If one or more symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency: check B12

Periodic Monitoring

Consider 'periodic' monitoring in those with risk factors. No one defines 'periodic'.

Pragmatically, most clinicians will probably add B12 to annual diabetes bloods.

If Deficiency Found

  • Treat in the usual way
  • Importantly: try to continue metformin (unless not tolerated or contraindicated)
Timing Consideration

The body holds B12 in storage in the liver. Deficiency occurs slowly over time, not suddenly. Checking a few weeks after starting metformin will NOT tell you if this person has developed metformin-induced B12 deficiency.

Autoimmune Gastritis

Previously termed 'pernicious anaemia' — NICE uses 'autoimmune gastritis'

Pathophysiology

Auto-antibodies against parietal cells and intrinsic factor cause stomach inflammation:

  • Reduced gastric acid secretion: prevents B12 release from food, impairs iron absorption
  • Reduced intrinsic factor: impairs B12 absorption

Terminology Note

NICE defines pernicious anaemia as "life-threatening anaemia as a consequence of chronic, severe B12 deficiency" — such severe presentations are rarely seen now.

Gastric Cancer Monitoring

Increased Cancer Risk

People with autoimmune gastritis:

  • Are at increased risk of gastric neuroendocrine tumours
  • May be at increased risk of gastric adenocarcinoma

New/Worsening Upper GI Symptoms

If dyspepsia, nausea, or vomiting develop:

  • Follow NICE guidance on suspected cancer
  • If not meeting cancer criteria, consider referral for OGD

Baseline OGD

British Society of Gastroenterology: consider baseline OGD for ALL patients ≥50 years with pernicious anaemia/autoimmune gastritis.

Anti-Intrinsic Factor Antibody Testing

Scenario Action
Pregnant/breastfeeding Test for anti-IF antibody
Previous positive anti-IF OR previous operation causing malabsorption Testing won't change management — NOT needed
Everyone else with unknown cause CONSIDER testing for anti-IF antibody AND offer coeliac serology
Interpreting Anti-IF Results

Positive: autoimmune gastritis is likely.
Negative: does NOT rule out autoimmune gastritis (sensitivity only 40–60%).
If anti-IF negative and autoimmune gastritis still suspected, consider: anti-gastric parietal cell antibody, gastrin level, cobaSorb test, or OGD with biopsy.

Copper Deficiency

Less common but important differential

⚠ Copper deficiency can present with similar symptoms to B12 deficiency and is often delayed in diagnosis. Can co-exist with B12 deficiency.

Key Points

  • Copper is absorbed in the stomach and duodenum
  • Diagnosis is often delayed
  • Likely to become more common with increasing bariatric surgery
  • Can co-exist with B12 deficiency

When to Test Copper

Consider testing copper levels in individuals with:

  • Persistent or unexplained cytopenias
  • Peripheral neuropathy syndromes
  • PLUS history of upper GI surgery (gastric bypass) or malabsorption syndromes

Incidental Raised B12

Approach to elevated B12 levels

Limited Evidence

There is very little published material about an approach to high B12 in primary care. There is no agreed consensus about a diagnostic pathway.

Possible Causes of Raised B12

Common

  • Supplementation (most common cause)
  • Liver disease
  • Chronic renal failure (CKD)

Less Common

  • Autoimmune disorders
  • Inflammatory diseases
  • Myeloid blood malignancies
  • Solid tumours

Pragmatic Approach

Suggested Steps

  1. Check for supplementation — most common cause
  2. Repeat sample — acute conditions may transiently raise B12
  3. If persistently elevated: consider FBC, LFT, U&E
  4. If still unexplained: consider more invasive investigation on case-by-case basis

The absolute risk of solid cancer based on raised B12 seems insufficient to systematically perform extensive tests following incidental raised B12.
