Regimen

First‑line: Multiple Daily Injection (MDI) Basal–Bolus

  • Adults Offer MDI basal–bolus to all. Basal: insulin detemir preferred (twice daily). If not acceptable/tolerated → insulin glargine once daily. Bolus: rapid‑acting analogue before meals.
  • Do not recommend routine post‑meal rapid‑acting insulin.
  • Do not start non‑basal–bolus regimens (mixed/basal‑only/bolus‑only) in newly diagnosed adults.
Rationale. Basal–bolus best mirrors physiologic insulin; enables flexible dose matching to meals, activity and glucose pattern.

Evidence summary: NICE CKS recommendations for adults; detemir first‑choice basal, rapid‑acting analogues for meals.

Fallback Options

If MDI is not feasible

  • Adults Consider twice‑daily mixed human insulin (biphasic). If hypoglycaemia affecting QoL → trial analogue mixed.
  • Children If MDI unsuitable → consider insulin pump (CSII) via specialist team.
Note. Choice should reflect patient preference, lifestyle and acquisition cost when alternatives are needed.
Technology

When to use Pump (CSII) & Hybrid Closed‑Loop

Pump therapy (CSII)

  • Consider if disabling hypoglycaemia on optimized MDI, or HbA1c ≥ 69 mmol/mol (≥ 8.5%) despite high‑level support.
  • Initiate and follow‑up within a trained specialist team; provide structured education.

Hybrid closed‑loop (Adults)

  • Offer if HbA1c ≥ 58 mmol/mol (≥ 7.5%), or disabling hypoglycaemia persists despite best use of CSII and/or CGM (rtCGM/isCGM).
Paediatrics

Children & Young People (≥12 months)

  • Start MDI at diagnosis.
  • Adjust doses to SMBG patterns; use rapid‑acting analogue before meals.
  • If MDI unsuitable → pump therapy; ensure specific training + ongoing specialist support.
  • If suboptimal control → intensify team contact; consider regimen change.
Monitoring

Self‑Monitoring Targets & Frequency

Adults

  • Test ≥ 4×/day (pre‑meals + bedtime); more often if driving, ill, exercising or targets unmet.
  • Waking: 5–7 mmol/L
  • Pre‑meal (day): 4–7 mmol/L
  • Post‑meal (≥ 90 min, if testing): 5–9 mmol/L
  • Bedtime: agree individually; align with waking target.
  • CGM (rtCGM or isCGM) should be offered; still keep capillary checks for calibration/back‑up.

Children & Young People

  • Test ≥ 5×/day; more with activity or intercurrent illness.
  • Waking: 4–7 mmol/L
  • Pre‑meal: 4–7 mmol/L
  • Post‑meal: 5–9 mmol/L
  • Driving age: ≥ 5 mmol/L before driving.
Complications

Hypoglycaemia: Prevention & Treatment

Immediate treatment (able to swallow)

  • Give 10–20 g fast‑acting carbohydrate (e.g., 3–6 glucose tablets; 90–180 mL sugary drink; 1–2 tubes glucose gel).
  • Children: ≈ 0.3 g/kg fast‑acting carb.
  • Recheck in 10–15 min; repeat if needed.
  • When improved: if meal due → increase its carbs; if not → take long‑acting starchy snack (not required for pump users).

Severe (unconscious/unable to swallow)

  • IM glucagon immediately: < 8 years or < 25 kg → 500 micrograms; otherwise 1 mg.
  • If no response within 10 min → urgent transfer for IV glucose.
  • Once awake → give oral carbohydrate; monitor closely (risk of vomiting/recurrence).

If recurrent / nocturnal

  • Review regimen, injection technique, sites, lifestyle (meals/alcohol/activity), drug interactions and co‑morbidities.
  • Consider nocturnal checks (2–3 am); bedtime snack; involve specialist team.

Impaired awareness

  • Assess annually (Gold/Clarke). Reinforce education; prioritise strategies to avoid hypos.
  • Consider CSII and/or CGM via specialist team if persisting or severe.
Administration

Injection Technique, Sites & Storage

  • Use insulin at room temperature; check expiry; read the PIL.
  • Cloudy insulin: invert/roll ≥ 10× (do not shake). Prime pen before each dose. New needle each time.
  • Inject at 90°; insert fully; leave needle in for 5–10 s (longer for big doses). Split doses > 50 U.
  • Rotate sites; do not inject into lipohypertrophy lumps; examine skin regularly.
  • Site speed: abdomen (fastest) → arm (med‑fast) → thigh (slower) → buttocks (slowest).
  • Storage: spares in fridge 2–8 °C (do not freeze). In‑use: room temp (usually up to 28 days); protect from heat/sunlight.
Equipment

Essential Accessories

  • Injection devices: syringes, disposable or reusable pens, CSII (pump), or needle‑free (rare; severe needle phobia).
  • Pen needles in varied lengths (4–12.7 mm); match to body fat; adjust if site problems.
  • SMBG kit: meter (choice per team/patient), test strips, lancets.
  • Ketone testing: blood/urine strips (blood ketone meters often not NHS‑prescribable).
  • Hypo rescue: glucose 40% oral gel; glucagon kit (1 mg; 500 mcg for small children).
  • Sharps: needle‑clipping devices; 1 L or 5 L sharps bins.
  • Insulin Passport + patient information booklet; carry medical ID (e.g., MedicAlert).
Medicines

Drug Interactions (selected)

Increase hypoglycaemia risk / lower insulin need

  • Alcohol (also masks symptoms; advise snack before/after and regular testing).
  • ACE inhibitors, beta‑blockers (mask tremor), fibrates, MAOIs, salicylates, sulphonamides, anabolic steroids, lanreotide/octreotide (may also ↑ requirements).

Reduce insulin effect / raise insulin need

  • Corticosteroids, danazol, diuretics (loop/thiazide), glucagon, growth hormone, levothyroxine, oral contraceptives, sympathomimetics (adrenaline/salbutamol/terbutaline).
Safety

Safety, Driving & Lifestyle

  • Use only prescribed/recommended devices. Do not withdraw insulin from pens with syringes.
  • Education: hypo awareness/management; sick‑day rules; technology use (CGM/pump).
  • Driving (DVLA): test just before travel and then every 2 h; carry fast‑acting carbs; if low, stop safely, treat, and wait ≈ 45 min after normalisation before resuming.
  • Travel: carry meds & sharps in hand luggage; obtain clinician letter; check travel insurance covers T1D.
  • Car insurance: notify insurer if on insulin therapy.

Always individualise goals and regimens to the person’s preferences, tolerance and life context.
