PHPT Reference
Updated: Nov 2025
RED FLAG: Severe Hypercalcaemia (>14 mg/dL or >3.5 mmol/L)
Admit for IV Fluids + Loop Diuretics. Consider Parathyroid Carcinoma if palpable neck mass.
Definition

Autonomous overproduction of PTH causing hypercalcaemia.

Confirmed by: Elevated Serum Calcium + Inappropriately Normal/Elevated PTH.
Aetiology:
85% Single Adenoma 15% Multi-gland <1% Carcinoma
Epidemiology
  • Incidence: 1 in 500 women (Postmenopausal peak)
  • Incidence: 1 in 2000 men (>40 years)
  • Asymptomatic: 80% in Western countries (Incidental finding)
  • Symptomatic: 80% in resource-poor nations
Clinical Presentation

Symptomatic "Classic" Signs

Nephrolithiasis (Stones) Osteoporosis/Fx Proximal Muscle Weakness

Common "Non-Specific" Signs

Fatigue Depression Cognitive Fog Poor Sleep Myalgia
Physical Exam

Usually Normal

CRITICAL: Palpable hard, dense neck mass suggests Parathyroid Carcinoma.

Diagnostic Algorithm (First-Line)
Investigation Expected Result in PHPT Notes
Serum Calcium Elevated Adjust for albumin. Stop thiazides 2 weeks prior.
Intact PTH High or "Inappropriately Normal" High Ca + Low PTH = Search for other causes.
25-OH Vitamin D Low or Normal Replete if low to distinguish from secondary cause.
24h Urine Calcium High or Normal >250mg (F) / >300mg (M) = Surgical indication.
Serum Phosphate Low / Low-Normal Concomitant electrolyte shift.
Imaging (Not for Dx!)

Used for surgical localisation only after biochemical confirmation.

  • Neck US: Preferred initial. Checks thyroid too.
  • Sestamibi (MIBI) Scan: Standard functional test.
  • SPECT/CT: High sensitivity (88%).
  • 4D CT: Good for re-operations or distorted anatomy.
  • DXA Scan: Baseline BMD at Spine, Hip, Forearm.
The Variant

Normocalcaemic PHPT

Normal Serum & Ionised Calcium + Elevated PTH.

  • Exclude secondary causes (Vit D deficiency, renal failure).
  • Can progress to hypercalcaemic PHPT.
  • Monitor: Bone density and kidney stones.
Definitive Treatment: Parathyroidectomy

The only cure. Indicated for all symptomatic patients and asymptomatic patients meeting specific criteria.

Indications for Surgery (Asymptomatic)

Click items to check off (Interactive)

Age < 50 years
Serum Calcium > 1 mg/dL (0.25 mmol/L) above normal
Creatinine Clearance < 60 mL/min
DXA T-score < -2.5 (Spine, Hip, or 1/3 Radius)
Vertebral Fracture (radiographic evidence)
Nephrolithiasis or Nephrocalcinosis
24h Urine Calcium > 300mg (M) / >250mg (F)
Medical Management / Monitoring

Option for: Patients who decline surgery, are not surgical candidates, or have mild disease without indications.

  • Hydration: Maintain high fluid intake.
  • Monitoring: Serum Calcium & Creatinine every 6 months. DXA scan every 1-2 years.
  • Bisphosphonates: For osteoporosis if surgery not done.
  • Cinacalcet: Lowers serum calcium (if contraindicated to surgery).
Key Differentials
Condition Key Differentiator (Clue) Labs
Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcaemia (FHH) Asymptomatic family history. No bone/kidney issues. Low Urine Calcium (<100 mg/day). Ratio Ca/Cr clearance < 0.01.
Malignancy (Humoral) History of solid tumor (lung, breast, kidney). Weight loss. PTH is Low. PTHrP is High.
Multiple Myeloma Back pain, anemia. Serum Protein Electrophoresis (M-spike). Bence-Jones protein.
Milk-Alkali Syndrome History of excessive antacid intake. PTH is Low.
Thiazide Diuretics Patient on HCTZ for hypertension. Discontinue drug; Calcium normalizes. PTH low/suppressed.
