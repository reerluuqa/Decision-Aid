Hypothyroidism Clinical Reference

Evidence-Based Management Algorithm
NICE 2023 Guideline

When to Suspect Hypothyroidism

Consider diagnosis if patient presents with ≥1 of these symptoms:

Fatigue/Lethargy
Cold Intolerance
Weight Gain
Constipation
Weakness/Arthralgia
Menstrual Irregularities
Infertility
Depression/"Brain Fog"
Dry Skin/Hair Loss
Eyelid Swelling
Hoarse Voice
Bradycardia

Initial Assessment

History

  • Pregnancy status (current/recent)
  • Non-thyroidal illness
  • Medications: amiodarone, lithium, biotin
  • Family history of thyroid/autoimmune disease
  • Personal history of autoimmune disorders
  • Previous thyroid/neck surgery or radiotherapy

Examination

  • Thyroid enlargement/nodules
  • Signs of complications (cardiovascular, neurological)
  • Signs of other autoimmune diseases
  • Goitre assessment
  • Skin and hair changes
  • Reflex testing

Investigations

  • Check serum TSH
  • If TSH elevated, check FT4 in same sample
  • Consider TPO antibodies if subclinical
  • Lipid profile (cardiovascular risk)
  • Consider CBC for anemia

Interpretation of Thyroid Function Tests

Check TSH
TSH NORMAL

Consider: non-thyroidal illness, early disease, mild symptoms

TSH ELEVATED
FT4 NORMAL

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

FT4 LOW

Overt Hypothyroidism

Classification & Management

Overt Hypothyroidism

TSH >10 mU/L, FT4 low

  • Start levothyroxine replacement
  • Monitor TFTs every 6-8 weeks until stable
  • Long-term follow-up required
  • Dose: 1.6 μg/kg/day

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

TSH elevated, FT4 normal

  • TSH 4.5-10 mU/L: Consider treatment if symptomatic, TPO+, young, or pregnant
  • TSH >10 mU/L: Treat with levothyroxine
  • Monitor TSH every 6 months if not treated

Secondary Hypothyroidism

Low/normal TSH, low FT4

  • URGENT REFERRAL to endocrinology
  • Consider pituitary/hypothalamic disease
  • Do not treat without specialist input

When to Refer

Urgent Referral (within 2 weeks)

  • Suspected secondary hypothyroidism
  • Goitre with suspicious features (hard, irregular, rapid growth)
  • Thyroid pain (possible subacute thyroiditis)
  • Myxoedema coma

Routine Referral

  • Pregnancy or planning pregnancy
  • Persistent symptoms despite adequate levothyroxine
  • Difficulty stabilizing thyroid function
  • Comorbidities complicating management (cardiac disease)

Complications to Monitor

Cardiovascular

Increased CHD, heart failure risk

Reproductive

Infertility, pregnancy complications

Neurological

Cognitive impairment, depression

Myxoedema Coma

Rare, life-threatening emergency

Prognosis

Good with adequate levothyroxine replacement

Most patients recover full physical and psychological wellbeing

Persistent symptoms

5-10% have symptoms despite normalized TFTs

Subclinical progression

5% annual progression to overt disease
