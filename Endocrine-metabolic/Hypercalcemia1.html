🦴 Hypercalcaemia in Adults

NICE NG132 | GEMS Quick Reference for Primary Care
⚠ All calcium values = CORRECTED calcium (adjusted for albumin)
⚠ 90% of hypercalcaemia is due to PRIMARY HYPERPARATHYROIDISM or CANCER

🚨 Red Flags – Act Same Day

▼

Corrected Calcium ≥3.00 mmol/L → Discuss with secondary care THAT DAY

  • May need admission for IV rehydration/treatment
  • May need urgent referral

Ca 2.60–2.99 mmol/L WITH symptoms → Discuss with secondary care that day

▼

✅ CHECK Corrected Calcium if ANY of:

▼

Symptoms of Hypercalcaemia:

  • Thirst (polydipsia)
  • Frequency / excessive urination (polyuria)
  • Constipation
  • Nausea / vomiting
  • Drowsiness (LATE sign)

Features suggesting Primary Hyperparathyroidism:

  • Bone pathology – osteoporosis, previous fragility fracture
  • Renal pathology – renal stones

Known/suspected cancer associated with hypercalcaemia:

  • Lung cancer, myeloma

🤔 CONSIDER Checking if Chronic Undifferentiated Symptoms

▼
  • Bone, muscle, or joint pain
  • Anxiety, depression, low mood, irritability, apathy, insomnia
  • Digestive problems, fatigue, mild confusion

Think: "Bones, Stones, Groans & Psychic Moans"

🟢 Ca 2.50–2.59 mmol/L (Normal Range)

Only investigate further if features of primary hyperparathyroidism (bone/renal pathology as above).

If features present → repeat corrected calcium on a separate occasion, then arrange PTH.

🟠 Ca 2.60–2.99 mmol/L

If symptomatic → Discuss with secondary care THAT DAY

If asymptomatic → Repeat corrected calcium on a separate occasion

If confirmed ≥2.60 on 2 occasions → Arrange PTH + repeat corrected calcium

🔴 Ca ≥3.00 mmol/L

⚠ DISCUSS WITH SECONDARY CARE THAT DAY

  • May need admission for IV rehydration
  • May need urgent referral

🔁 When to Repeat & Arrange PTH

▼

Repeat corrected calcium. If on 2 separate occasions:

  • Corrected Ca ≥2.60 mmol/L
  • OR corrected Ca 2.50–2.59 mmol/L AND features of 1° HPT

→ Arrange PTH + repeat corrected calcium again

🧪 Interpreting PTH Results

▼

🔵 PTH Low
(below lower limit of ref range)

= Non PTH-dependent hypercalcaemia

Causes include:

  • Cancer (lung, myeloma, bony mets)
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Vitamin D toxicity
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Thiazide diuretics
  • Acromegaly
  • Phaeochromocytoma

🟢 PTH Below Midpoint
(within ref range) AND Ca <2.60

Primary hyperparathyroidism UNLIKELY

🟠 PTH Below Midpoint
(within ref range) AND Ca ≥2.60

Cannot rule out primary hyperparathyroidism

→ Seek endocrine advice

🔴 PTH Raised / Above Midpoint
AND 1° HPT suspected

LIKELY Primary Hyperparathyroidism

→ Seek endocrine advice (referral/phone call)

Other causes:

  • Tertiary HPT (renal disease)
  • MEN 1 / Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcaemia
  • Lithium treatment

🔬 What Other Tests?

▼

Vitamin D: NICE says do NOT check vitamin D or any other tests when assessing for primary HPT.

Local guidance may differ – may suggest checking Vit D to assess underlying cause.

Useful tests to assess underlying cause:

  • Rest of bone profile
  • U&Es
  • TFTs
  • Myeloma screen (if appropriate)

To assess for familial causes:

  • Prolactin if age <45
  • Calcium excretion index (urine sample)

💊 Immediate Actions – Don't Forget

▼
If on THIAZIDE diuretic → STOP IT
If excess dietary calcium → REDUCE IT
If Vitamin D low → DO NOT DELAY REFERRAL

📞 When to Refer / Discuss

▼
Situation Action
Ca ≥3.00 mmol/L Same-day discussion with secondary care
Ca 2.60–2.99 + symptoms Same-day discussion with secondary care
PTH raised / above midpoint + suspected 1° HPT Endocrine referral / phone advice
PTH below midpoint but Ca ≥2.60 Seek endocrine advice (can't rule out 1° HPT)
PTH low (non PTH-dependent) Investigate for cancer/other causes
