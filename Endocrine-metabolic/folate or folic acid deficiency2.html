High-risk prescribing rule

Folate deficiency management with a single dominant safety rule: B before F.

This tool is designed for fast clinical retrieval. Isolated folate deficiency is rare in the Western world. Always assess for co-existing B12 deficiency, identify the underlying cause, treat confirmed deficiency correctly, and review whether long-term folic acid prescribing is still clinically appropriate.

!

Critical safety alert

Check B12 levels in ALL before starting folic acid. If B12 and folate are both deficient, start B12 replacement at least 24 hours before folate. Folate alone can mask B12 deficiency and risks neurological complications, including subacute combined degeneration of the cord.

Fast pathway

Decision algorithm

1. Suspect folate deficiency?

Consider symptoms, blood count abnormalities, diet, alcohol use, gastrointestinal disease, pregnancy/lactation, increased requirements, medications, and genetic causes.

↓

2. Check serum folate first-line + check B12 in ALL

Serum folate is the recommended first-line test. Always assess for co-existing B12 deficiency before folic acid is started.

↓

3. If both B12 and folate are deficient

B before F: start B12 replacement first, and begin folate at least 24 hours later.

↓

4. Treat confirmed folate-deficient megaloblastic anaemia

Folic acid 5 mg orally once daily for 4 months. Up to 15 mg daily may be needed in malabsorptive states.

↓

5. Add context-specific actions

If treating deficiency in pregnancy, continue until term. Give dietary advice. Identify and address the underlying cause.

↓

6. Review prescribing duration

Folate replacement for deficiency is generally a 4-month treatment course, not indefinite therapy. Consider recall/annotation and audit ongoing prescriptions.

Clinical essentials

At-a-glance facts

Definition
Folate
Biologically active forms of the vitamin.
Synthetic form
Folic acid
Used in supplements, fortified food, and treatment.
Function
DNA synthesis
Deficiency can produce megaloblastic change.
Absorption
Proximal small intestine
About half of body folate is stored in the liver.
Cooking reduces folate content in food. There is more folate in raw spinach than cooked spinach. The body can absorb roughly twice as much folate from folic acid as from a food with the same folate concentration.
In the Western world, true isolated folate deficiency is very rare. If folate deficiency is found, ask why. Folate deficiency often co-exists with B12 deficiency, and symptoms can overlap.
Why ask why?

Causes of folate deficiency

Dietary deficiency

Low folate intake

  • Low intake of folate-rich or folic acid-fortified foods.
  • Relevant foods: legumes, green leafy vegetables, fortified grains.
  • Goats’ milk (unless fortified) has much lower folate content than cows’ milk.
Gastrointestinal disorders

Impaired absorption

  • Coeliac disease.
  • Crohn’s disease.
  • Tropical sprue.
  • Inflammation of the small intestine can impair folate absorption.
Alcoholism

Alcohol-related deficiency risk

  • Risk increases if alcohol intake is >10 units/day.
  • Liver disease, poor nutrition, and increased urinary excretion contribute.
Pregnancy / lactation

High physiological demand

  • Maternal deficiency can occur due to high foetal and infant requirements.
  • Risk is increased with multiparity and hyperemesis.
  • Supplementation is advised preconceptually and in early pregnancy.
  • No need to monitor folate in pregnancy unless deficiency is clinically suspected or hyperemesis is present.
Increased requirements

Accelerated folate use

  • Sickle cell disease.
  • Haemolytic anaemias.
Medications

Drug-related causes

  • Anticonvulsants.
  • Methotrexate.
  • Trimethoprim.
Genetic causes

Inherited disorders

  • Mutations in the SLC46A1 gene.
Interpretive point

Do not stop at “low folate”

  • Ask what is driving the deficiency.
  • Assess for co-existing B12 deficiency.
  • Look for poor diet as part of poor general health.
Clinical presentation

Signs and symptoms

Neurocognitive impairment
Especially in the elderly
Megaloblastic anaemia
Pancytopenia

May reflect poor diet / poor general health
Interpret carefully

Testing strategy

Recommended first-line

Serum folate

  • Reflects recent dietary intake.
  • Can drop several weeks after reduced folate intake.
  • Recent folate consumption can make the result appear normal despite deficiency.
  • Use local laboratory reference ranges and units.
Pitfalls

False low serum folate may occur in

  • Anorexia
  • Acute alcohol consumption
  • Pregnancy
  • Anticonvulsant use
Usually not needed

Red cell folate

  • Reflects tissue folate status over about 3 months.
  • No benefit in the vast majority.
  • May be considered only if strong suspicion persists despite normal serum folate, after B12 deficiency has been ruled out.
  • Can be low in B12 deficiency.
Special circumstances

Plasma homocysteine

  • Can help confirm folate deficiency in selected cases.
Most important section

Treatment of folate deficiency

Safety rule

Check B12 levels in ALL before starting folic acid

  • If both folate and B12 are deficient, start B12 replacement at least 24 hours before folate.
  • Remember: B before F.
  • Folate alone may mask B12 deficiency and risk neurological complications, including subacute combined degeneration of the cord.
Standard treatment

Confirmed folate-deficient megaloblastic anaemia

  • Folic acid 5 mg orally once daily for 4 months.
  • Capture the 4-month duration with a recall or prescription annotation if possible.
Dose adjustment

Malabsorptive states

  • Up to 15 mg daily may be needed.
Pregnancy

If treating deficiency during pregnancy

  • Continue treatment until term.
Dietary advice

Advise folate-rich foods

  • Spinach
  • Kale
  • Broccoli
  • Cabbage
  • Beans
  • Legumes
  • Nuts and seeds
Do not omit

Find and address the cause

  • Investigate why deficiency developed.
  • Do not assume indefinite folic acid is needed.
Do not confuse with treatment

Prophylactic folic acid

Feature Treatment of deficiency Prophylaxis
Purpose Treat folate-deficient megaloblastic anaemia / confirmed deficiency Prevent deficiency or reduce neural tube defect risk
Typical dose Folic acid 5 mg once daily 400 micrograms daily for most women preconceptually until 12 weeks of pregnancy
Duration 4 months usually; until term if treating deficiency in pregnancy Context dependent; pregnancy prophylaxis usually until 12 weeks
Higher dose situations Up to 15 mg daily in malabsorptive states 5 mg for selected higher-risk women
Chronic haemolysis / dialysis

Secondary-care guided prophylaxis

  • In chronic haemolytic states and renal dialysis, prophylactic folic acid 5 mg daily to weekly may be recommended.
  • Depends on diet and rate of haemolysis.
  • Be guided by secondary care.
Standard pregnancy prophylaxis

Most women

  • 400 micrograms daily.
  • Start preconceptually and continue until 12 weeks of pregnancy.
Who needs 5 mg instead?

Higher-risk pregnancy groups

  • Medication affecting folic acid metabolism, e.g. antiepileptics or HIV medicines.
  • Previous pregnancy affected by neural tube defect or other congenital malformation.
  • Woman or partner with personal/family history of neural tube defects or other congenital malformations.
  • Type 1 or type 2 diabetes.
  • Sickle cell disease or thalassaemia.
Special notes

Obesity and bariatric surgery

  • NICE found no evidence supporting high-dose folic acid solely for overweight/obesity.
  • Use the standard 400 micrograms daily unless other risk factors are present.
  • Those with previous bariatric surgery may need specialist micronutrient advice; consult the bariatric surgery unit.
