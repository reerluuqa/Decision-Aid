Neck Lump Assessment & Management

A quick reference algorithm for clinical decision making

Initial Assessment

History

  • Onset & Duration: Acute (<2 weeks) vs. Chronic (>6 weeks)
  • Changes: Size progression, pain development
  • Associated Symptoms:
    • Red flags: Dysphagia, odynophagia, hoarseness, otalgia, weight loss, night sweats
    • Infective: Fever, sore throat, recent URTI
    • Thyroid: Heat/cold intolerance, mood changes
    • Salivary: Pain with eating, dry mouth

Examination

  • Location:
    • Anterior triangle (thyroid, submandibular, lymph nodes)
    • Posterior triangle (lymph nodes, vascular)
    • Midline (thyroglossal, dermoid)
  • Characteristics: Size, consistency, mobility, tenderness, pulsatility
  • Associated findings: Skin changes, lymphadenopathy elsewhere

Diagnostic Pathways

Finding Likely Cause Action

Anterior triangle, tender Reactive lymphadenopathy, salivary gland Treat infection, review 2-3 weeks
Posterior triangle, rubbery Lymphoma Urgent referral (2-week wait)
Fluctuant, painful Abscess, infected cyst Antibiotics, consider drainage
Pulsatile with bruit Vascular (aneurysm, carotid body tumor) Urgent vascular referral
Associated with eating Salivary gland stone/infection USS, specialist referral
Present since childhood Congenital (branchial/thyroglossal cyst) Specialist referral

Management Scenarios

Skin Lump

  • Abscess: Antibiotics (flucloxacillin), drainage if large
  • Epidermoid cyst: Excision if recurrent or symptomatic

Lymphadenopathy

  • Acute (<2 weeks): Likely reactive, monitor 2-3 weeks
  • Persistent (>3 weeks):
    • With infective symptoms: Consider antibiotics
    • Without infective symptoms: FBC, USS, specialist referral

Thyroid Lump

  • TFTs, USS
  • Solitary nodule >1cm or suspicious features: Urgent referral

Salivary Gland Lump

  • Recurrent swelling with eating: USS for stones
  • Persistent firm mass: Specialist referral

Congenital Lump

  • Specialist referral for consideration of excision

Unknown Cause

  • <40 years, no red flags, <2cm: Monitor 4-6 weeks
  • Any red flags or persistent: USS, specialist referral

Investigations in Primary Care

Blood Tests

FBC, TFTs, inflammatory markers

Imaging

Ultrasound (first-line investigation)

Biopsy

Only if expertise available, otherwise refer

Note: When in doubt, refer for specialist assessment.
