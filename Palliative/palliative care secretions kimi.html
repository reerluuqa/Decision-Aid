Management of Respiratory Secretions in End-of-Life Care

Assessment

‚ñº
1
Initial Evaluation
  • Listen for gurgling/rattling sounds from airway
  • Assess patient's level of consciousness
  • Determine if patient can swallow/cough effectively
  • Evaluate hydration status
  • Check positioning (supine/semi-recumbent)
2
Identify Contributing Factors
  • Decreased consciousness (illness, sedatives, opioids)
  • Dehydration causing sticky mucus
  • Ciliary dysfunction (smoking history)
  • Excessive secretions from chest infection
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Bronchorrhea (rare, >100mL/day watery mucus)
  • Gastric reflux/aspiration
3
Assess Impact & Prognosis
  • Determine if noise is distressing to patient or family
  • Reassure that unconscious patients are not distressed
  • Estimate prognosis (typically 17-57 hours before death)
  • Presence of 'death rattle' is strong predictor of death
  • 50% die within 24 hours, 75% within 48 hours
Urgent Referral
If patient appears distressed or prognosis uncertain

ü§î Key Consideration

Is the patient in the terminal phase? Look for:

  • Rapid deterioration day by day
  • Patient expressing they are dying
  • Reduced cognition, drowsy/comatose
  • Bed-bound, minimal food/fluid intake
  • Peripheral cyanosis, cold extremities
  • Altered breathing pattern

üî¨
Diagnosis

‚ñº
4
Confirm Secretions
  • Persistent gurgling/rattling/bubbling noises from airway
  • Secretions pooling in hypopharynx
  • Typically occurs 17-57 hours before death (range: 23-92% prevalence)
  • Distinguish from other respiratory symptoms
5
Assess Secretion Type
  • Salivary secretions: Most common, from salivary glands
  • Bronchial secretions: From bronchial mucosa
  • Infected secretions: Purulent sputum, chest infection signs
  • Bronchorrhea: >100mL/day watery mucus (rare)
  • Gastric contents: Reflux/aspiration risk

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Exclusions

This algorithm does NOT cover:

  • Management of hemoptysis
  • Active chest infection treatment (see separate guideline)
  • Non-palliative care settings

üíä
Treatment

‚ñº
6
Non-Pharmacological Measures
  • Repositioning: Place patient on side with upper body elevated
  • Postural drainage: Encourage gravity-assisted drainage
  • Suction: If available and appropriate
  • Reassurance: Explain to family that patient is not distressed
First-line approach
7
Pharmacological Treatment

Consider drug treatment if:

  • Positioning/suction fails to provide adequate benefit
  • Symptoms causing distress to patient or family
  • Family distress persists despite explanation
Antimuscarinic Drug Options (Click to expand) ‚ñº
Drug Initial Bolus Assessment Time 24-hour Infusion Key Considerations
Glycopyrronium bromide 200-400 micrograms SC 30-60 minutes 600-1200 micrograms Preferred - minimal CNS effects
Hyoscine butylbromide 20 mg SC 20-30 minutes 60-180 mg No CNS penetration
Hyoscine hydrobromide 200-400 micrograms SC 20-30 minutes 1200-2400 micrograms May cause sedation/confusion - crosses BBB

Note: Hyoscine hydrobromide 1 mg patch available but slow onset, poor absorption, and often insufficient dose.

Administration Guidelines (Click to expand) ‚ñº
  • Initial bolus: Give SC injection first
  • Positive response: Continue with SC infusion via syringe driver
  • Monitoring: Check every 4 hours (minimum every 12 hours)
  • Duration: Drugs may take up to 12 hours to become effective
  • Review: Change/stop if no benefit after 12 hours or side effects persist

üí° Pro Tip

Regular SC injections are generally not practical or optimal. Syringe drivers provide better symptom control and patient comfort.

Side Effects & Management (Click to expand) ‚ñº
  • Dry mouth: Mouth care, sips of water if appropriate
  • Constipation: Prophylactic laxatives
  • Urinary retention: Monitor urine output
  • CNS effects: More common with hyoscine hydrobromide
  • Delirium/agitation: Consider reducing dose or switching drug
  • Blurred vision: Usually temporary
8
Special Considerations
Chest Infection with Purulent Sputum (Click to expand) ‚ñº
  • Decision: Antibiotics unlikely to alter course in terminal phase
  • Consider if: Profuse purulent sputum causing distress
  • Approach: Single dose broad-spectrum antibiotic
  • Review: Second dose only if convincing response
  • Communication: Sensitive discussion with family about goals of care
Use with caution

Follow-up & Monitoring

‚ñº
9
Initial Response Assessment
  • Timeframe: Assess response 20-60 minutes after administration
  • Effectiveness: Reduction in noisy secretions
  • Patient comfort: Improved patient/family distress
  • Side effects: Monitor for antimuscarinic effects
  • Decision: Continue, adjust, or stop treatment
Critical assessment period
10
Ongoing Monitoring
  • Frequency: Check every 4 hours (minimum every 12 hours)
  • Parameters:
    • Noise level and character
    • Patient comfort/distress
    • Family concerns
    • Side effects (dry mouth, sedation, confusion)
    • Respiratory pattern changes
  • Documentation: Record all assessments and interventions
11
Review & Adjust Treatment
When to Change/Stop Treatment (Click to expand) ‚ñº
  • No benefit after 12 hours: Medicines may take up to 12h to work
  • Continued distress: If secretions still problematic after 12h
  • Unacceptable side effects:
    • Persistent dry mouth
    • Urinary retention
    • Delirium or agitation
    • Excessive sedation
  • Patient death: Discontinue all treatments
Alternative Strategies (Click to expand) ‚ñº
  • Switch antimuscarinic: If side effects with one drug
  • Increase dose: Within recommended range if partial response
  • Add second drug: Only under specialist advice
  • Non-drug measures: Re-emphasize positioning
  • Family support: Ongoing reassurance and education
12
Family & Carer Support
  • Education: Explain that secretions are normal at end of life
  • Reassurance: Emphasize patient is not choking or suffocating
  • Expectations: Set realistic goals for treatment
  • Communication: Regular updates on management plan
  • Emotional support: Acknowledge distress and provide support
  • Practical help: Teach positioning techniques
Essential component of care

üéØ Goals of Care

Remember the primary goals:

  1. Alleviate distress to the person
  2. Alleviate distress to family/carers
  3. Maintain patient comfort and dignity
  4. Support family through the dying process
