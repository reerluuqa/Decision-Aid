SIBO Clinical Reference

Severe symptoms require immediate attention: Significant weight loss, Malabsorption, Dehydration

What is SIBO?

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) is a condition characterized by excessive bacteria in the small intestine, leading to gastrointestinal symptoms.

Key Fact: SIBO affects up to 1 in 7 people and is often confused with IBS. Up to 80% of IBS patients may also have SIBO.
Definition
Pathophysiology

Normal small intestine has <104 CFU/mL of bacteria. SIBO is diagnosed when levels exceed >105 CFU/mL.

Excessive bacteria break down carbohydrates producing excess gas, leading to symptoms. The bacteria (coliforms) are typically found in the colon.

New Technology

Hydrogen Breath Test (First-line)

  • Non-invasive, safe, and accessible
  • Measures hydrogen/methane production
  • Substrates: glucose or lactulose
Positive Test Criteria: Rise of ≥20 ppm from baseline in hydrogen by 90 min or methane level ≥10 ppm

Sensitivity: Glucose 44%, Lactulose 31%Compared with small bowel aspirate via endoscopy

Specificity: Glucose 80%, Lactulose 86%Compared with small bowel aspirate via endoscopy

Small Bowel Aspirate (Gold Standard)

  • Invasive procedure via endoscopy
  • Bacterial concentration >103 CFUs/mL is diagnostic
  • Common species: Bacteroides, Enterococcus, Lactobacillus
Limitations: Invasive, time-consuming, risk of contamination, low yield of bacterial cultures

Capsule-based Technology (Emerging)

  • Real-time measurements of intraluminal gases
  • Provides information on site of intestinal gas production
  • Safe and reliable in early trials
Potential: May provide more accurate diagnosis with less invasive testing

Treatment

Medication Dosage Duration Effectiveness
Rifaximin (First-line) 550 mg Twice daily 7-14 days
Ciprofloxacin 500 mg Twice daily 7-14 days
Metronidazole 250 mg Three times daily 7-14 days
Trimethoprim 160/800 mg Twice daily 7-14 days
Success Rate: Antibiotics normalize SIBO in 51.1% of patients vs 9.8% with placebo
Recurrence: Common after treatment. Consider prophylactic antibiotics for patients with 4+ episodes per year

Management Approach

  1. Identify Red Flags: Screen for severe symptoms requiring immediate intervention
  2. Confirm Diagnosis: Use breath testing as first-line investigation
  3. Address Predisposing Factors: Review medications (PPIs, opioids), manage underlying conditions
  4. Initiate Antibiotic Therapy: Rifaximin as first-line when available
  5. Dietary Modifications: Consider low FODMAP diet with dietitian support
  6. Monitor Response: Reassess symptoms and consider repeat testing if needed
  7. Manage Recurrence: Consider rotating antibiotics for prophylaxis

Emergency Actions

Severe Malabsorption:
  • IV fluids for dehydration
  • Nutritional support
  • Vitamin/mineral replacement
  • Specialist referral
Significant Weight Loss:
  • Nutritional assessment
  • Dietitian referral
  • Consider elemental diet
  • Monitor for refeeding syndrome
Severe Dehydration:
  • IV fluid resuscitation
  • Electrolyte monitoring
  • Renal function assessment
  • Consider hospital admission
Vitamin B12 Deficiency:
  • IM B12 injections
  • Monitor neurological symptoms
  • Repeat blood tests
  • Long-term replacement plan
