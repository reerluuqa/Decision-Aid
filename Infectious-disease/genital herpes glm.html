Genital Herpes: Clinical Reference

Quick-scan tool based on NICE CKS (Last revised May 2024)

Target Audience: NHS Primary Care
Age: 13+ years
URGENT: Red Flags & Complications

Be aware of these serious complications requiring immediate action or specialist referral.

  • Systemic Infection: Aseptic meningitis, encephalitis, fulminant hepatitis, pneumonitis, disseminated infection.
  • Urinary Retention: Secondary to severe pain or autonomic neuropathy.
  • Immunocompromise: Progressive, multifocal, coalescing lesions (common in untreated HIV).
  • Herpes Proctitis: Common in MSM (Men who have sex with men).
  • Secondary Infection: Lesions infected with Candida or Strep.
  • NEONATAL RISK: Highest risk if primary infection acquired in 3rd trimester (esp. <6 weeks of delivery).

Key Clinical Features

Confirmation ideally via specialist sexual health service.

First Episode Presentation:
  • Bilateral lesions, redness, vesicles, blisters, ulcers.
  • Tender bilateral inguinal lymphadenopathy.
  • Systemic symptoms: Headache, malaise, fever.
  • Dysuria, vaginal/urethral discharge.
Recurrent Episode Presentation:
  • Usually unilateral, localized to same area.
  • Milder severity, heals 6-12 days.
  • Prodromal tingling/burning 48hrs prior.
Investigations (If specialist referral not possible):
  • Viral swab from base of lesion (PCR if available).
  • Rectal swab if suspected proctitis.
  • Screen for other STIs (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, HIV, Syphilis).

Transmission & Risks

Transmission

Direct contact with infected secretions at mucosal surfaces or skin breaks.
Note: Often transmitted via asymptomatic shedding.

Virus Type

HSV-1Most common genital cause in UK. Linked to oral sex.
HSV-2More likely to cause recurrence. Linked to vaginal/anal sex.

Risk Factors

Age 15-24 Female sex MSM Multiple partners HIV/Immunocompromise Trauma/Stress

Management: First Episode

PRIMARY ACTION: Refer all suspected first episodes to Specialist Sexual Health Service for diagnosis confirmation, treatment, and partner notification.

If managing in Primary Care:

  • Self-Care: Saline bathing (1 tsp salt in 560ml warm water) to ease pain. Keep lesions clean/dry.
  • Analgesia: Simple analgesics for pain (paracetamol/ibuprofen).
  • Topical Anaesthetic: Lidocaine 5% ointment may help with urinary pain (apply 5-10 mins before).
  • Antivirals: Follow local formulary (usually Aciclovir/Valaciclovir/Famciclovir). See Prescribing Info.

Patient Advice & Support

  • Avoid sexual contact until lesions fully healed.
  • Condoms reduce risk but do not eliminate it entirely (virus sheds from areas not covered by condom).
  • Psychological impact is common; offer reassurance.
  • Resources: FPA.org.uk, Herpes.org.uk.
