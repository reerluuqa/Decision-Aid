CKS: Bacterial Meningitis & Meningococcal Disease

Target: Primary Care (Birth onwards)

Last Revised: August 2025

⚠️ IMMEDIATE SUSPICION

Do not delay transfer. These are rapidly evolving conditions.

Suspect Bacterial Meningitis

Red Flag Combination (All 4):

Fever Headache Neck Stiffness Altered Consciousness

*Note: Can be strongly suspected on clinical assessment even without all 4, especially in babies/older adults.

Suspect Meningococcal Disease

Red Flag:

Haemorrhagic Rash (Non-blanching)

Any rash that does not fade under pressure.

HOVER TO SIMULATE GLASS TEST

If this rash does not fade (blanch), it is a medical emergency.

Special Considerations

  • Babies & Children: Fever/neck stiffness less common. Look for bulging fontanelle, lethargy, high-pitched cry, poor feeding.
  • Older Adults: Fever less common. Altered consciousness often mistaken for delirium/dementia.
  • Young Adults: Altered behavior often mistaken for alcohol/substance misuse.

Diagnostic Assessment

Risk Stratification: Use Sepsis risk tables for people with suspected sepsis (Feverish children).

History: Incubation usually 3-5 days (Meningococcal) or 1-3 days (Pneumococcal). Ask about immunization status.

Vital Signs & Check

Check for signs of shock:

  • Tachycardia / Tachypnoea
  • Pale, mottled skin / Cyanosis
  • Reduced urine output
  • Delayed capillary refill

Comparison: Symptoms by Age Group

Symptom Babies / Children Adults
Classic Triad + Fever Headache/Neck stiffness hard to identify. Fever may be masked by antipyretics. Red Flag: Fever + Headache + Neck Stiffness + Altered Cognition.

Primary Care Action

1. Immediate Transfer: Admit to secondary care immediately.

2. Do Not Delay: Do not perform lumbar puncture in primary care.

3. Pre-hospital Antibiotics:

  • Give if transfer to hospital is likely to be delayed (e.g., >30 mins depending on local protocol).
  • Drug: Ceftriaxone (IM/IV). Dosage updated Aug 2025 to align with BNFC.
  • Alternative: Benzylpenicillin (IV/IM) if Ceftriaxone unavailable.

Follow-up & Contacts

Public Health: Meningococcal disease is notifiable. Close contacts may need prophylaxis.

Post-Discharge (NICE Quality Standards)

  • ✔ 4 Weeks: Audiological assessment (once well enough).
  • ✔ 6 Weeks: Follow-up appointment in secondary care.

Safety Netting

If sent home after assessment (low risk), provide safety netting advice:

"Watch the child/person closely. If they get worse, develop a non-blanching rash, or you are worried, seek help immediately."

Causes

  • Neisseria meningitidis (MenB most common now).
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • Group B Streptococcus (Babies <3 months).
  • Hib (Rare due to vaccination).

Transmission

Aerosol, droplets, direct contact.

Requires frequent/prolonged close contact.

Incubation: Usually 3-5 days.

Prevalence (UK)

Very rare. Incidence <1 per 100,000 (2023/24).

Highest risk: < 2 years and adolescence.

Complications

Neurological (30%): Hearing loss, seizures, cognitive impairment, motor deficits.

Physical: Amputations (up to 8% children), skin scars.

Other: Hydrocephalus, renal failure.

Risk Factors

  • Winter season
  • Young/Old age extremes
  • Asplenia/Sickle cell
  • Immunocompromised
  • Smoking/Student halls

Prognosis

Excellent with prompt treatment.

IMD Fatality: ~2.3% (England 2023/24).

Worse prognosis if coma, shock, or seizures present.

Exclusions & Scope

This CKS topic covers: Primary care management of suspected acute bacterial meningitis and meningococcal disease.

It does NOT cover: Diagnosis and management in secondary care, or viral meningitis.
