Oral Candidiasis Quick-Scan

NICE CKS Topic (Last Revised March 2025)

Target: Primary Healthcare (UK NHS)

Scope: Assessment & Management

Immediate Action Required (Admit/Refer)

Admit if: Evidence of systemic illness (candidaemia) or widespread infection (e.g., oesophageal candidiasis: dysphagia, odynophagia, retrosternal pain).
Refer/Specialist Advice if: Child has extensive/severe oral candidiasis or HIV indicator condition is suspected (unexplained oral candidiasis).

Instant Management Pathway

Select patient characteristics to reveal the correct management protocol.

Scenario: Child (Immunocompetent)

1. Prescribe Topical Antifungal:

  • Miconazole oral gel (First-line for age ≥ 4 months). Unlicensed < 4 months
  • If unsuitable: Nystatin suspension (Unlicensed for neonates).

2. Duration: Continue for at least 7 days after lesions have healed.

3. Advice: Hygiene, treat inhaled corticosteroid use (rinse mouth), check dentures.

Scenario: Adult (Immunocompetent)

1. Prescribe Topical Antifungal:

  • Miconazole oral gel or Nystatin suspension.
  • Denture Stomatitis: Treat dentures with fungicidal agent alongside oral gel.

2. Duration: Continue for at least 7 days after lesions have healed.

3. Advice: Identify risk factors (e.g., diabetes, inhaled steroids), smoking cessation, hygiene.

Scenario: Adult (Immunosuppressed)

1. Prescribe Systemic Antifungal:

  • Fluconazole (Dose aligned to BNF: usually 50-100mg daily).
  • Consider specialist advice if severe or recurrent.

2. Note: If treatment fails, consider resistant species (e.g., C. glabrata/krusei) and seek specialist advice.

Scenario: Adult (HIV Positive)

1. Prescribe Systemic Antifungal:

  • Fluconazole is generally preferred.

2. Considerations:

  • Protease inhibitors in HAART can attenuate Candida virulence.
  • Check for interactions between Fluconazole and HIV medications.

Differential Diagnosis Matrix

Type Visual Signs Common In
Pseudomembranous (Thrush) Curd-like white/yellow plaques. Wipes off leaving red base. Neonates, Elderly, HIV, Antibiotic users.
Erythematous (Atrophic) Red, smooth areas. Loss of papillae on tongue. Soreness. Antibiotic use, Denture wearers.
Denture Stomatitis Redness under denture. Rarely sore. 50-70% of denture wearers.
Angular Cheilitis Redness, fissuring, soreness at corners of mouth. Elderly, reduced facial height, Anaemia.
Chronic Hyperplastic White plaques that cannot be rubbed off. Smokers >30yrs. Risk of malignancy.
Note: Swabs are not usually helpful (Candida is a normal commensal in 30-45% of adults). Diagnosis is clinical.

Critical Drug Safety Alerts

High Risk Interaction

Warfarin + Miconazole Oral Gel

Miconazole significantly enhances the anticoagulant effect of warfarin, leading to bleeding risk.

Action: Avoid combination. Use alternative (e.g., Nystatin or Fluconazole) or monitor INR closely if unavoidable.

Usage & Caution

  • Choking Hazard: Miconazole gel in infants/young children. Apply carefully.
  • Pregnancy: Fluconazole manufacturers advise washout period of ~1 week (5-6 half-lives) before becoming pregnant.
  • Ketoconazole: Oral ketoconazole suspended by EMA. Do not prescribe.

Risk Factors & Prognosis

Key Risk Factors

  • Extremes of age (Immature/weakened immunity).
  • Dry mouth (Xerostomia).
  • Diabetes mellitus (Hyperglycemia).
  • Inhaled/Oral Corticosteroids.
  • Antibiotics (Broad spectrum).
  • Iron or Vitamin deficiencies.

Prognosis

  • Untreated: Persists months/years unless risk factors removed.
  • Neonates: Spontaneous cure usually after 3–8 weeks.
  • Systemic: Invasive candidiasis has high mortality (up to 79%).
