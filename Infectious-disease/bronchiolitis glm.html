What is Bronchiolitis?

Bronchiolitis is an acute viral infection of the lower respiratory tract that occurs primarily in infants under 2 years of age. It is characterized by inflammation of the bronchioles, leading to airway obstruction, wheezing, and difficulty breathing.

Key Points:

  • Most common lower respiratory infection in the first year of life
  • Peak incidence between 3-6 months of age
  • Leading cause of hospitalization in infants under 1 year
  • Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is responsible for up to 80% of cases
  • Most cases are mild and self-limiting, requiring only supportive care
Red Flags
Immediate Referral Required:
  • Apnoea (observed or reported)
  • Respiratory rate >70 breaths/minute
  • Central cyanosis
  • Oxygen saturation <92%
  • Severe chest recession or grunting
Consider Referral:
  • Respiratory rate >60 breaths/minute
  • Inadequate fluid intake
  • Signs of dehydration
  • Age <3 months or premature birth
  • Significant comorbidities
Aetiology
Common Causes:
  • Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) - 80% of cases
  • Human metapneumovirus (hMPV)
  • Rhinovirus
  • Adenovirus
Less Common Causes:
  • Bocavirus
  • Parainfluenza virus
  • Influenza virus
  • Coronavirus
Epidemiology
  • ~33 million RSV ALRI episodes globally in children <5 years (2019)
  • ~3.6 million hospitalizations worldwide
  • Peak incidence: Winter months (Nov-Apr in Northern Hemisphere)
  • 2-3% of infants with bronchiolitis require hospitalization
  • ~70 deaths per year in England
Typical Course
Days 1-3

Upper respiratory tract symptoms (coryza, cough)

Days 3-5

Peak symptoms: wheeze, increased work of breathing

Days 7-10

Gradual improvement in respiratory symptoms

Weeks 2-3

Cough may persist for up to 3 weeks

Clinical Diagnosis

Bronchiolitis is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on typical symptoms and signs. Diagnostic tests are generally not required for uncomplicated cases.

Diagnostic Criteria:
Children under 2 years with 1-3 day history of coryzal symptoms, followed by:
  • Persistent cough
  • Either tachypnoea or chest recession (or both)
  • Either wheeze or crackles on chest auscultation (or both)
Typical Findings:
  • Tachypnoea (respiratory rate >60 breaths/min)
  • Chest recessions, subcostal/intercostal retractions
  • Bilateral wheezes and crackles
  • Fever (usually <39°C)
  • Poor feeding, irritability
Investigations

Most cases do not require routine investigations. Consider only if diagnosis uncertain or deterioration.

Recommended for any infant with more than mild symptoms being considered for hospitalization.

Oxygen Saturation Targets:
  • ≥90% for children aged 6 weeks and over
  • ≥92% for babies under 6 weeks or children with underlying health conditions

Not routinely required for diagnosis but may be useful for:

  • Infection control in hospital settings
  • Infants receiving immunoprophylaxis (palivizumab/nirsevimab)
  • Epidemiological surveillance

RT-PCR is more sensitive than ELISA for detecting RSV and other respiratory viruses.

Not routinely required. Consider only if:

  • Strong suspicion of bacterial pneumonia (fever >40°C and focal crackles)
  • Severe or atypical presentation
  • Poor response to treatment
  • Concern for complications (e.g., pneumothorax)

Typical findings may include hyperinflation, interstitial inflammation, and atelectasis.

Differential Diagnosis
  • Viral-induced wheeze: Consider if wheeze without crackles, episodic symptoms, family history of atopy
  • Pneumonia: Consider if temperature >39°C and persistent focal crackles
  • Asthma: Consider if recurrent episodes of wheezing, atopic dermatitis, history of food allergies
  • Croup: Characterized by inspiratory stridor, hoarse cry, and barking cough
  • Cystic fibrosis: Usually associated with symptoms of pancreatic insufficiency

Age <3 months

Nursery attendance
Management Approach

The main goal of treatment is to correct abnormalities in oxygenation and hydration. Treatment is primarily supportive.

1
Supportive Care

Most infants can be managed at home with supportive measures including adequate fluid intake and monitoring.

2
Oxygen Therapy

Administered for infants with persistent hypoxemia to maintain SpO₂ ≥90% (≥92% in infants under 6 weeks or with underlying conditions).

3
Fluid Management

Consider intravenous or nasogastric fluids for infants with inadequate oral intake (50-75% of usual volume).

4
Respiratory Support

Consider CPAP or high-flow nasal cannula for infants with impending respiratory failure.

Hospital Admission Criteria
Immediate Admission:
  • Apnoea (observed or reported)
  • Oxygen saturation <90% (≥6 weeks) or <92% (<6 weeks or with comorbidities)
  • Severe respiratory distress (grunting, marked chest recession)
  • Respiratory rate >70 breaths/minute
  • Inability to maintain adequate hydration
Consider Admission:
  • Persistent oxygen saturation 90-92%
  • Respiratory rate 60-70 breaths/minute
  • Moderate respiratory distress
  • Reduced fluid intake (50-75% of normal)
  • Age <3 months or significant comorbidities
  • Social concerns or inability to monitor at home
Treatments NOT Recommended
No Evidence of Benefit:
  • Antibiotics: Not routinely indicated unless bacterial co-infection suspected
  • Bronchodilators: No effect on oxygen saturation, hospital admission rate, or duration of illness
  • Corticosteroids: No clinically relevant effect on hospital admissions or length of stay
  • Chest physiotherapy: Conventional techniques do not provide benefit
Limited Evidence:
  • Hypertonic saline: May slightly reduce length of hospital stay but evidence is mixed
  • Nebulised adrenaline: May improve short-term outcomes for outpatients but no benefit for inpatients
  • Ribavirin: May be considered for immunocompromised infants but not for otherwise healthy infants
Home Management Advice
For Parents:
  • Continue feeding as normal; offer smaller feeds more frequently if needed
  • Monitor for signs of deterioration (see red flags)
  • Use antipyretics only if fever causing distress
  • Avoid exposure to tobacco smoke
  • Keep the child comfortable but not overheated
When to seek urgent medical help:
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Blue lips or tongue
  • Pauses in breathing
  • Severe drowsiness
  • Fewer wet nappies than usual
Discharge Criteria

Infants can be discharged from hospital when:

  • Clinically stable
  • Taking adequate oral fluids
  • Maintained adequate oxygen saturations for 4 hours (including during sleep)
  • SpO₂ ≥90% (≥6 weeks) or ≥92% (<6 weeks or with comorbidities)
  • Parents understand red flag symptoms and when to seek help
Immunoprophylaxis

Since September 2024, RSV vaccine has been offered to adults aged 75-79 years and pregnant women in the UK to reduce serious disease in infants.

For Infants at High Risk:

High-risk infants and young children are recommended to receive RSV monoclonal antibody immunisation seasonally (around week 40/start of October).

Recommended Options:
  • Nirsevimab (Beyfortus®): First-line immunisation if available
  • Palivizumab (Synagis®): Recommended if nirsevimab is not available
High-Risk Groups:
  • Bronchopulmonary dysplasia/chronic lung disease
  • Congenital heart disease
  • Severe combined immunodeficiency
  • Premature infants (<32 weeks gestation)
Infection Control

Disease transmission may be limited by:

  • Hand washing: Regular and thorough hand washing with soap and water
  • PPE: Use of gloves and aprons or gowns when in direct contact with infected patients
  • Isolation: Isolating infected patients in cubicles when possible
  • Environmental cleaning: Regular cleaning of surfaces and toys
  • Staff cohorting: Assigning dedicated staff to care for infected infants when possible
General Prevention Strategies
During Pregnancy:
  • Receive RSV vaccine if offered (from week 28 onwards)
  • Avoid smoking during pregnancy
  • Attend regular antenatal appointments
After Birth:
  • Breastfeeding if possible (considered protective)
  • Avoid exposure to tobacco smoke
  • Limit contact with people with respiratory infections
  • Consider avoiding crowded places during peak RSV season
Prognosis
  • Most children make a full recovery
  • Illness typically lasts 3-7 days
  • Cough may persist for up to 3 weeks
  • 2-3% of infants require hospitalization
  • Death is uncommon (~70 deaths per year in England)
  • Most deaths occur in infants <6 months or with underlying conditions
Long-term Outlook:

There is an association between bronchiolitis and later development of asthma, but causality is not established.

Follow-up

Routine follow-up is not required for uncomplicated cases. Consider follow-up for:

  • Infants with severe disease
  • Those with underlying conditions
  • Persistent symptoms beyond 3 weeks
  • Recurrent wheezing episodes
