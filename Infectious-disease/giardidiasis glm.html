Giardiasis Clinical Reference

Quick reference guide for diagnosis and management
Severe Dehydration Alert
Infants and pregnant women with giardiasis are at high risk for life-threatening dehydration. Monitor closely and rehydrate aggressively.

Red Flags

  • Profuse watery diarrhea
  • Signs of severe dehydration
  • Weight loss >10% body weight
  • Persistent symptoms >2 weeks
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Failure to thrive in children

Emergency Actions

  • Aggressive rehydration (IV if needed)
  • Immediate antiprotozoal therapy
  • Stool testing for confirmation
  • Monitor electrolytes
  • Consider specialist referral

Key Facts

  • Caused by Giardia duodenalis protozoa
  • Fecal-oral transmission
  • Common in children <4 years
  • Travel-related risk
  • Incubation: 1-25 days (avg 7 days)
  • No fever typically

Diagnostic Approach

History
Travel, water exposure, contact with children
Symptoms
Greasy, foul-smelling stools, bloating, belching
Testing
Stool DFA (gold standard), antigen detection, PCR
Differential
Celiac disease, IBS, viral gastroenteritis

Treatment Failure

Approach

  • Confirm persistent infection
  • Assess for reinfection
  • Check for immunocompromise
  • Use different drug class

Combination Therapy

  • Metronidazole + Albendazole
  • Metronidazole + Quinacrine
  • Minimum 2 weeks duration
  • Consider specialist referral

Prevention Strategies

Personal Hygiene

  • Strict hand washing
  • Careful diaper disposal
  • Hand washing after toilet use
  • Hand washing before food prep

Water Safety

  • Boil water (70Â°C for 10 min)
  • Use high-quality water filters
  • Iodine disinfection (8+ hours)
  • Avoid untreated water

Special Populations

Children

  • Screen in daycare settings
  • Breastfeeding protection
  • Monitor growth

Travelers

  • High-risk destinations
  • Avoid uncooked food
  • Boil/filter water

Complications

Complication Signs/Symptoms Management
Dehydration Dry mouth, decreased urination, tachycardia Aggressive rehydration, electrolyte monitoring
Malabsorption Weight loss, steatorrhea, vitamin deficiencies Nutritional support, vitamin supplementation
Lactose intolerance Bloating, diarrhea after dairy Lactose-free diet (usually temporary)
Failure to thrive Growth delay in children Nutritional support, treat infection
Post-infectious IBS Chronic abdominal pain, altered bowel habits Dietary modification, symptom management

Follow-up Schedule

3-5 Days
Parasites typically cleared from stool
5-7 Days
Symptoms should resolve
If Persistent Symptoms
Assess for treatment failure, lactose intolerance, or IBS
Reinfection
Consider if symptoms return after resolution

Patient Education

During Treatment

  • Complete full course of medication
  • Avoid alcohol with metronidazole
  • Maintain hydration

After Treatment

  • Continue hand hygiene
  • Ensure water safety
  • Consider temporary lactose-free diet
