Assessment

Clinical evaluation and initial suspicion of UTI in men

üéØWhen to Suspect UTI

Suspect a lower urinary tract infection if a man presents with:

  • Dysuria - pain or discomfort on passing urine
  • Frequency - increased need to urinate
  • Urgency - desire to pass urine immediately
  • Nocturia - having to urinate during the night more frequently
  • Suprapubic pain - pain above the pubic bone
  • Haematuria - blood in urine
Special consideration for men ‚â•65 years: UTI is likely if dysuria alone is present, OR two or more of:
  • Temperature 1.5¬∞C above normal twice in 12 hours
  • New frequency or urgency
  • New incontinence
  • New or worsening delirium/debility
  • New suprapubic pain
  • Visible haematuria

üß™Urine Sample Collection

Confirm diagnosis by urine culture and sensitivity:

  • Mid-stream urine (MSU) - routine recommended method. Discard first part, collect 10mL without interrupting flow
  • Catheter specimen (CSU) - drain and discard a few mL first, then collect using aseptic technique
  • Transport: within 4 hours OR refrigerate at 4¬∞C (suitable for 48 hours)
  • Boric acid preservative: can store at room temperature up to 96 hours
Important: Do NOT take specimens from catheter collection bags - more likely to be contaminated

In frail elderly and/or catheterized men in institutional care, do NOT rely on classical symptoms. UTI may present with atypical symptoms.

üö´Dipstick Testing

Do NOT use urine dipstick tests or microscopy to diagnose UTI in men:

  • Aged over 65 - dipsticks become more unreliable with increasing age
  • Aged under 65 - unreliable at ruling out infection but may help decide if UTI is likely when positive for nitrites
  • With indwelling catheter - make working diagnosis based on clinical judgement

üîÑScenario: Recurrent UTI in Men

Defined as 3+ UTIs in 12 months or 2+ episodes in 6 months

Management Strategy:

  • Treat each episode as acute UTI with urine culture
  • Advise behavioral measures (fluid intake, post-coital urination)
  • Refer for specialist investigation to identify underlying cause
Antibiotic Prophylaxis Options:
  • Trimethoprim 200mg single dose when exposed to trigger, OR 100mg at night
  • Nitrofurantoin 100mg single dose when exposed to trigger, OR 50-100mg at night (if eGFR ‚â•45)
  • Amoxicillin 500mg single dose, OR 250mg at night (off-label)
  • Cefalexin 500mg single dose, OR 125mg at night

üí¨Patient Information & Advice

Self-care Advice:
  • Use paracetamol for pain (or ibuprofen if suitable)
  • Drink sufficient fluids to avoid dehydration
  • Seek medical help if symptoms worsen or don't improve within 48 hours
  • No evidence for cranberry products or urine alkalinizing agents

Follow-up & Referral

Monitoring response and referral criteria

üîçReview and Reassessment

  • Arrange follow-up after 48 hours (or per clinical judgement)
  • Check patient's response to treatment
  • Review urine culture results when available
  • Change antibiotic according to susceptibility if resistant and symptoms not improving
  • Reassess if symptoms worsen rapidly or fail to improve within 48 hours

üìûWhen to Seek Medical Help

Advise patients to seek urgent help if:
  • Symptoms worsen rapidly or significantly at any time
  • Symptoms do not start to improve within 48 hours of antibiotics
  • They become systemically very unwell (fever, confusion, vomiting)
  • Visible blood in urine persists after treatment
  • Severe pain not controlled with simple analgesia
