Encephalitis Quick Reference

Clinical decision support tool - Last updated: Oct 23, 2025

EMERGENCY ACTION

Administer IV aciclovir immediately in ALL suspected viral encephalitis cases - DO NOT DELAY

Definition & Key Features

Encephalitis: Inflammation of brain parenchyma with neurological dysfunction

Red Flag: Altered mental status + fever = Medical emergency

Fever
Altered consciousness
Headache
Seizures
Focal neurological signs
Behavioral changes

Typical Presentation: Acute to subacute onset of altered mental status with fever, headache, seizures, and focal neurological signs

Red Flag Symptoms

IMMEDIATE CONCERN: Rapidly progressive altered mental status

Cranial nerve palsies
Papilledema
Respiratory distress
Autonomic instability
Status epilepticus
High fever (>40°C)

WARNING: Immunocompromised patients may present without fever

Initial Assessment

ABCs, vital signs, neurological exam, GCS score

Urgent Actions

Start IV aciclovir immediately if suspected viral encephalitis

Initial Investigations

Blood cultures, neuroimaging (MRI preferred), CSF analysis (if no contraindications)

Further Testing

Targeted testing based on clinical presentation and risk factors

Key Differentiators

Condition Distinguishing Features
Encephalitis Brain parenchymal inflammation, altered mental status
Meningitis Meningeal inflammation, headache, neck stiffness
Encephalopathy Metabolic/toxic, generalized symptoms, no focal findings

Clinical Clues by Etiology

HSV: Temporal lobe involvement on MRI, focal seizures
Arboviruses: Seasonal (summer/early autumn), geographical variation
Autoimmune: Psychiatric symptoms, movement disorders, female predominance

Essential for All Patients

CSF Analysis: Opening pressure, cell count, protein, glucose, Gram stain, bacterial culture, HSV-1/2 PCR, Enterovirus PCR
Blood: FBC, electrolytes, LFT, blood cultures (2 sets)
Imaging: MRI with contrast (preferred) or CT, chest X-ray
EEG: If seizures, status epilepticus, or altered behavior/consciousness

Additional Tests by Patient Group

Adults: VZV PCR, cryptococcal antigen, VDRL, West Nile testing, HIV serology

Children: EBV serology, Mycoplasma IgM/IgG, respiratory viral PCR

Immunocompromised: CMV PCR, EBV PCR, HHV-6/7 PCR, HIV PCR, JC virus PCR, TB testing

Consider: Autoimmune antibody testing, prion disease testing, brain biopsy if diagnosis uncertain

Initial Management

DO NOT DELAY: Start IV aciclovir (10 mg/kg q8h) immediately in suspected cases

Supportive Care: Airway protection, hemodynamic support, seizure control, ICP monitoring if needed

Empiric Antibiotics: Consider until bacterial meningitis ruled out

Specific Treatments

Etiology Treatment
HSV IV aciclovir 10-15 mg/kg q8h (14-21 days)
VZV IV aciclovir 10 mg/kg q8h (14-21 days)
Autoimmune First-line: steroids + IVIG or plasma exchange

Monitoring & Follow-up

Neurological Status: Regular GCS assessments, focal deficits
Repeat Imaging: If clinical deterioration or to assess response
Therapeutic Drug Monitoring: For aciclovir (renal function)

Complications

Seizures
Hydrocephalus
Neurological sequelae
Cognitive deficits

Strong Risk Factors

Age: <1 year or >65 years
Immunodeficiency: HIV, chemotherapy, immunosuppressants
Location: Endemic areas for specific pathogens
Vector Exposure: Mosquito/tick bites, animal exposure
Season: Summer/early autumn for arboviruses

Weaker Risk Factors

Vaccination Status: Unvaccinated against measles/mumps
Occupation: Forestry, farming, laboratory work
Activities: Hunting, trekking, spelunking
Cancer: Paraneoplastic encephalitis
Organ Transplant: Donor-derived infections

Common Etiologies

Category Common Pathogens
Viral HSV (most common), VZV, enteroviruses, arboviruses
Bacterial Neisseria meningitidis, Mycoplasma, Listeria
Autoimmune Anti-NMDAR, anti-LGI1, ADEM
Other Fungal, parasitic, prion diseases

Note: An etiological agent is identified in only ~50% of cases

Prognosis

Poor Prognostic Factors: Delayed treatment, extremes of age, immunocompromised status, HSV encephalitis

Better Outcomes: Early treatment, young age, mild initial presentation, autoimmune etiology (with treatment)

Mortality: 5-20% (varies by etiology)
30-50% develop sequelae
Cognitive deficits common
Seizure disorder in 10-25%
