Quick-Scan: Mono

Clinical Reference Tool

Immediate Action / Red Flags
Admit to Hospital

Arrange emergency admission immediately if:

  • Upper airway obstruction: Stridor, respiratory distress, severe tonsillar hypertrophy.
  • Severe dehydration: Cannot be managed in primary care.
  • Sepsis: Signs of systemic infection.
  • Splenic Rupture: Sudden acute upper abdominal pain (often left shoulder tip pain), hemodynamic instability.
  • Peritonsillar abscess: (Quinsy) Severe throat pain, trismus, "hot potato" voice.
Seek Urgent Review

Advise patient to return urgently if they develop:

  • Difficulty swallowing fluids.
  • Severe systemic illness (high fever persisting > usual course).
  • Acute upper abdominal pain (Hepatitis or Splenic rupture concern).
  • Signs of neurological complication (seizures, severe headache).
Overview
Infectious Mononucleosis (Glandular Fever) EBV Virus

Definition: Infection characterized by sore throat, fever, and lymphadenopathy, primarily caused by Epstein-Barr virus (EBV).


Age Group: Most common in 15–24 years. Rare in first year of life.


Transmission: Saliva (kissing, shared utensils). Incubation ~4–7 weeks. Contagious during incubation and while symptoms present.

Prognosis

Self-limiting (2–4 weeks).

Fatigue may persist for months (10% of cases). 20% have persistent sore throat at 1 month.

Diagnosis & Assessment
Clinical Features
Sore ThroatUsually severe. Tonsillar enlargement + exudate ('whitewash'). Palatal petechiae common.
LymphadenopathyBilateral posterior cervical (typical). May persist for 3+ weeks.
FeverUsually low-grade.
SplenomegalyMax size at week 2, regresses 7-10 days later.
RashMaculopapular (often if given Amoxicillin).
Investigation Algorithm Age > 4y
Step 1: 2nd Week of Illness

Arrange FBC + Differential AND Heterophile antibody (Monospot) test.

Step 2: Interpretation
  • Positive Monospot: Likely Mono.
  • FBC Criteria (Hoagland): >20% atypical lymphocytes OR >10% atypical lymphs + Lymphs >50% of WBC.
Negative / Uncertain?

Repeat Monospot in 5-7 days OR arrange specific EBV serology (VCA IgM/IgG).

Children < 4 Years

Infection is often asymptomatic or non-specific.

Action: Arrange EBV Serology after being unwell for at least 7 days.

Management Plan
Self-Care & Advice
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol and/or Ibuprofen for pain/fever.
  • Hydration: Maintain adequate fluids.
  • Rest: As needed. Do NOT enforce bed rest (risk of deconditioning).
  • Social: No exclusion from work/school/nursery.
  • Hygiene: Avoid kissing, sharing utensils.
Restrictions & Cautions
NO Contact Sports

Avoid heavy lifting and contact/collision sports for 3 weeks after symptom onset to reduce risk of splenic rupture.

*Can resume if asymptomatic and no signs of acute infection after this period.

Medication Notes:
  • Avoid Amoxicillin/Ampicillin: High risk of characteristic rash if EBV positive.
  • Avoid Alcohol: During acute illness (due to transient hepatitis risk).
Differential Diagnosis
Viral:
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV) - Splenomegaly, -ve Monospot.
  • Acute Toxoplasmosis.
  • Primary HIV - "Mono-like illness" is an indicator.
  • Viral Hepatitis.
  • Influenza/Adenovirus.
Bacterial/Other:
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis (Anterior nodes).
  • Leukaemia / Lymphoma.
  • Diphtheria (rare).
  • Head & Neck cancer.
Potential Complications
Splenic Rupture

Rare (<1%), usually first 3 weeks. Can be spontaneous.

Airway Obstruction

Severe tonsillar hypertrophy.

Hepatitis

Common (75%), usually subclinical. Rarely fulminant.

Neurological

Meningoencephalitis, Guillain-Barré, Bell's palsy.

Haematological

Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia.
