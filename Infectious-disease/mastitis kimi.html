Mastitis & Breast Abscess

Quick Reference Guide | Primary Care | Last revised March 2025
IMMEDIATE ADMISSION/REFERRAL REQUIRED
Any of these signs = Emergency action
Hospital Admission
  • Signs of sepsis (tachycardia, fever, chills)
  • Rapidly progressing infection
  • Hemodynamically unstable
  • Immunocompromised patient
Urgent Referral
  • Suspected breast abscess ‚Üí General surgeon
  • Suspected malignancy ‚Üí 2-week wait
  • Underlying mass present
When to Suspect Abscess
  • History of recent mastitis or prior abscess
  • Painful, swollen lump with redness & heat
  • Fluctuant mass on examination
  • Note: Fever may have subsided if antibiotics started
Mastitis Features
  • Painful breast
  • Fever and/or malaise
  • Tender, red, swollen, hard area
  • Wedge-shaped distribution
Infection Clues
  • No improvement after 24h self-care
  • Infected-appearing nipple fissure
  • Purulent discharge
  • Spreading cellulitis features
  • Lactating: No improvement after 12-24h despite milk removal
Investigations
Breast Milk Culture (Lactating):
  • Severe or recurrent cases
  • Hospital-acquired infection likely
  • Deep 'burning' breast pain
Abscess:
  • All cases ‚Üí Urgent US in secondary care
  • Fluid for culture & sensitivity
KEY PRINCIPLE
Continue breastfeeding from affected breast ‚Üí Improves milk removal & recovery
First-Line Management
  • Continue breastfeeding (affected breast)
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol or Ibuprofen
  • Cold compresses: 10 min/hour if retroareolar inflammation
  • Hand express if too painful to feed
  • Rest & supportive bra
  • Address predisposing factors
Antibiotic Criteria
  • Infected nipple fissure
  • No improvement after 12-24h despite milk removal
  • Positive breast milk culture
Preventing Recurrence
  • Correct infant attachment
  • Feed on demand
  • Avoid missed feeds
  • Finish first breast before offering second
  • Avoid dummies
  • Hand hygiene
  • Clean breast pump thoroughly
  • Check breasts regularly
First-Line Management
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol or Ibuprofen
  • Cold compresses or warm shower
  • Address predisposing factors
  • Stop smoking (major risk factor)
  • Good hygiene (hand washing, remove nipple rings)
All Patients Require Antibiotics
  • First-line: Co-amoxiclav 500/125mg TDS
  • Duration: 10-14 days
  • If penicillin allergic: Erythromycin + Metronidazole
Treatment Failure / Recurrence
  • Check adherence & consider alternative diagnosis (cancer/abscess)
  • Consider MRSA if risk factors
  • Seek microbiology advice after multiple failures
  • Refer if no obvious cause for recurrence
URGENT SURGICAL REFERRAL REQUIRED
All suspected abscesses need ultrasound confirmation & drainage
Secondary Care Management
  • Ultrasound confirmation
  • Drainage: US-guided needle aspiration or surgical
  • Fluid culture for sensitivity
Lactating Women
  • Continue breastfeeding if possible
  • Express milk if too painful
  • Infant can accompany if mother admitted
Abscess Risk Factors
  • Previous mastitis
  • Delayed/inadequate antibiotic treatment
  • Sudden cessation of breastfeeding
  • Immunosuppression
  • S. aureus carriage
  • Low socioeconomic status
Lactating Mastitis - Empirical Treatment
Flucloxacillin 500mg
4 times daily for 10-14 days
If true penicillin allergy ‚Üí Erythromycin 250-500mg QDS or Clarithromycin 500mg BD
Non-Lactating Mastitis
Co-amoxiclav 500/125mg
3 times daily for 10-14 days
If penicillin allergic ‚Üí Erythromycin 250-500mg QDS + Metronidazole 400mg TDS
Second-Line (Treatment Failure)
Co-amoxiclav 500/125mg
3 times daily for 10-14 days
Review choice when culture results available
Critical Drug Interactions
Drug Interaction Action
Macrolides QT prolonging drugs, Statins (Simvastatin, Atorvastatin) Avoid combination
Metronidazole Warfarin, Alcohol Monitor INR, Avoid alcohol
Flucloxacillin Methotrexate, Warfarin Monitor levels/INR
