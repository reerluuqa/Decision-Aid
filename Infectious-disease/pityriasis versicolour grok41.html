Pityriasis Versicolor – Rapid Clinical Tool (NICE CKS March 2025)

New Diagnosis – First Presentation

Key messages to give every patient:
• Not contagious – Malassezia is normal skin flora
• Not due to poor hygiene
• Treatment works well but pigmentation can take 3–6 months to normalise
• Recurrence very common (60–80% in 2 years) especially in summer

What to Ask

  • Recent travel to hot/humid climate?
  • Worse in summer or with sweating?
  • Previous episodes?
  • Immunosuppression / corticosteroids / diabetes?
  • Mild itch or asymptomatic?

What to Examine

  • Multiple hypo- or hyper-pigmented macules/patches with fine scale (trunk, neck, upper arms)
  • Wood's light → yellow-green fluorescence (if available)
  • Scale more visible after gentle scraping
Decision: Limited (few patches) → go to Limited Disease tab
Extensive / cosmetically bothersome → go to Extensive tab

Limited / Small Areas Involved

First-line: Topical antifungal cream BD for 2–3 weeks
Preferred options (all equally effective):
  • Clotrimazole 1% cream (safe in pregnancy)
  • Econazole 1% cream
  • Ketoconazole 2% cream
  • Terbinafine 1% cream

When to Review

  • No routine follow-up needed if improving
  • Review only if no improvement after 4 weeks

When to Refer

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • No response to two different topicals
  • Child <12 years or pregnant (limited options)

Extensive or Cosmetically Bothersome

Gold standard: Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (Nizoral) as body wash
• Apply once daily for 5 days
• Leave on skin 5–15 minutes before rinsing
• Licensed ≥12 years (off-label <12 but widely used)
Alternative regimen (equally effective):
Selenium sulfide shampoo (Selsun) – same method but less well tolerated
Or combine shampoo + topical cream to resistant areas

Recurrent Disease – Prophylaxis

Ketoconazole 2% shampoo prophylaxis:
• Apply to whole body once monthly (or once every 2 weeks in summer)
• Leave on 10 min then rinse
• Reduces recurrence from ~80% to ~20% at 2 years
Before holidays to hot climates:
Ketoconazole shampoo daily for 3 days before departure

Not Responding to Topical Therapy

First ask: Did patient actually apply treatment correctly for full duration?

Step-up options (adults, non-pregnant)

Preferred oral regimen:
Itraconazole 200 mg OD for 7 days (best evidence)
or
Fluconazole 300–400 mg once weekly for 1–3 weeks
Consider skin scrapings if not done (to confirm Malassezia)

When to Refer to Dermatology

  • Diagnostic doubt or scrapings negative
  • No response to adequate topical + oral therapy
  • Child <12 years needing systemic treatment
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding
  • Extensive disease in immunocompromised patient
  • Frequent severe recurrences needing long-term prophylaxis
