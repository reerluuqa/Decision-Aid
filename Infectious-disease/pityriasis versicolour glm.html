Pityriasis Versicolor (Tinea Versicolor)

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference (NICE CKS) | Updated March 2025
Ages: 10+ years
Diagnosis

Clinical features. Microscopy rarely needed initially.

  • Appearance: Discrete/confluent macules with fine powdery scale.
  • Color: Pink, fawn, brown, black, or white (hypopigmented).
  • Location: Sebum-rich areas (Trunk, Neck, Upper Arms).
  • Wood's Light: May fluoresce gold-yellow/coppery-orange.
Differential Includes: Vitiligo, Psoriasis, Tinea corporis, Seborrhoeic dermatitis, Erythrasma.
First-Line Management
EXTENSIVE Areas? Preferred
Ketoconazole 2% Shampoo

Use as body wash. Leave on 3-15 mins. Rinse.

Once daily for up to 5 days.

Note: Unlicensed < 12 yrs.

SMALL Areas? Alternative
Antifungal Cream

Clotrimazole, Econazole, Ketoconazole, or Terbinafine.

Apply twice daily for 2–3 weeks.

Patient Advice:
• Not contagious (normal skin flora).
• Pigmentation may take weeks/months to resolve after cure.
• Recurrence is common.
Refer / Red Flags

Refer to Dermatologist if:

  • Diagnosis uncertain (or negative microscopy).
  • Inadequate response to optimal primary care treatment.
  • Severe or very extensive infection.
  • Patient is immunocompromised.
Specialist Management Required:
  • Children < 12 years
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women (if oral antifungals needed)
Recurrence & Prevention

Relapse is common (up to 80% in 2 years).

  • Prophylaxis: Ketoconazole shampoo once every 1–4 weeks.
  • Holiday/Sun: Apply once daily for max 3 days prior to exposure.
  • Outcome: Warn patient that pigment changes persist for months even after mycological cure.
Treatment Failure

If initial topical treatment fails:

  1. Confirm adherence to regimen.
  2. Check if pigmentary change is the only remaining sign (may not need treatment).
  3. Switch to alternative topical.
  4. Consider Oral Antifungals (Adults only).
Oral Options (Adults):
• Itraconazole 200 mg once daily for 7 days.
• Fluconazole 50 mg daily for 2-4 weeks OR 300-400 mg weekly for 1-3 weeks.
Prescribing & Safety Details

Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

  • Clotrimazole: Preferred. Safe.
  • Econazole: Manufacturers recommend avoidance in pregnancy. Care with breastfeeding (avoid nipple).
  • Terbinafine: Avoid unless clearly necessary (Pregnancy). Do not use breastfeeding (infant contact risk).
  • Miconazole: Caution in pregnancy/breastfeeding.

Adverse Effects

Local irritation, burning, erythema, hypopigmentation.

Key Interactions

Miconazole/Econazole: May enhance Warfarin (Monitor INR).

Contraindications

  • Acute Porphyria.
  • Concurrent use with QT-prolonging drugs metabolised by CYP3A4 (e.g., cisapride, pimozide, quinidine, erythromycin).

Caution

  • Hepatic impairment (discontinue if symptoms develop).
  • Renal impairment (halve dose if CrCl < 50 mL/min after initial dose).
  • QT prolongation susceptibility.

Pregnancy

Washout Period: Manufacturers advise approx. 1 week (5-6 half-lives) before becoming pregnant.

Major Interactions

  • Warfarin: Increased INR.
  • Sulfonylureas: Hypoglycaemia risk.
  • Statins: Increased myopathy risk.
  • Cisapride/Astemizole: CONTRAINDICATED.

Always check BNF/eMC for current interaction profiles as Itraconazole has extensive CYP3A4 interactions.

Note: Consult specialist advice before prescribing for children or pregnant/breastfeeding women.
  • Monitor liver function if prolonged treatment.
  • Negative inotropic effects (caution in heart failure).
