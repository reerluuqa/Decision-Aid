Assessment

Clinical evaluation and initial presentation

‚ñº

üåü Key Point

Diagnosis is clinical based on visual inspection of lesions. Biopsy is generally not required unless atypical features are present.

üîç Clinical Presentation

Typical lesions:

  • 1-3mm discrete, sessile, smooth-surfaced exophytic papillomas
  • May coalesce into larger plaques
  • Color varies: flesh-colored, whitish, or hyperpigmented
  • Located on genital, perianal, or adjacent areas (mons pubis)
  • Tend to occur in areas of high friction

Symptoms:

  • Generally asymptomatic (most common)
  • May be painful, friable, or pruritic
  • Bleeding due to local trauma or maceration
  • Urinary symptoms if urethral involvement

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Diagnostic Factors

Risk Exposure Immunocompromise Typical Lesions

Risk Factors:

  • Early age of sexual intercourse
  • Increasing number of lifetime sexual partners (individual & partner)
  • Young sexually active adults (females 19-22, males 22-26)
  • Immunocompromised status (cell-mediated deficiency)

üìñ Case History Pattern

A 21-year-old woman presents with new bumps on labia/perineum developing gradually over 1 month. Lesions are asymptomatic. She is sexually active with multiple partners and inconsistent condom use. Most recent sexual encounter was with a new partner several weeks prior.

Diagnosis

Clinical confirmation and differential diagnosis

‚ñº

‚úÖ Primary Method

Diagnosis is made by direct visual inspection aided by bright light and magnification. No initial test required.

üî¨ Investigations to Consider

Biopsy Indicated If:
  • Lesions are fixed to underlying structures
  • Refractory to standard therapy
  • Ulcerated or pigmented
  • Individual wart >1cm
  • Worsen despite treatment

Histological features (if biopsied):

  • Epidermal hyperplasia, parakeratosis, koilocytosis, papillomatosis

üîç Special Examinations

  • Anoscopy: For recurrent perianal warts (evaluate intra-anal warts)
  • Urethroscopy: If urinary symptoms (terminal hematuria, abnormal stream)

Treatment

Management strategies by patient type

‚ñº

üí° Important Principle

Treatment is not always indicated due to benign course and potential for spontaneous resolution (10-30% resolve within 3 months without treatment).

üéØ Treatment Goals

  • Destroy or remove visible lesions
  • Does not eradicate HPV infection
  • No evidence of reduced sexual transmission
  • Choice depends on: location, extent, morphology, patient preference, cost, side effects

üë§ Immunocompetent Patients - External Warts

Patient-Applied Therapy Self-Administered

  • Podophyllotoxin 0.5%: Apply twice daily for 3 days, then 4 days off. Repeat up to 4 cycles. Side effects: Local inflammation, burning, itching, pain
  • Imiquimod 5%: Apply 3√ó/week at bedtime for up to 16 weeks (leave 6-10 hours). Advantages: Low recurrence, non-scarring. Side effects: Pruritus, erythema, erosion
  • Sinecatechins 15%: Apply 3√ó/daily for up to 16 weeks. Clearance rate: Up to 57.2%

Provider-Administered Therapy Clinic-Based

  • Cryotherapy: Liquid nitrogen. Effective, few adverse effects. Requires multiple treatments. Can be painful.
  • TCA/BCA 80-90%: Chemical coagulation for small, moist warts. High recurrence rates. Must be applied by healthcare provider.
  • Surgical removal: Single-visit elimination. Methods: tangential scissor excision, shave excision, curettage, electrosurgery.
  • Laser/CO‚ÇÇ ablation: For large area involvement. Single-visit clearance.

‚ö†Ô∏è Special Populations

Immunocompromised Patients High Risk

  • More resistant to standard treatment, higher recurrence rates
  • Increased risk of transformation to squamous cell carcinoma
  • Require longer duration or combination therapy
  • Surgical excision followed by non-invasive therapies may be indicated
  • Improvement of underlying HIV infection reduces relapse rates

Pregnant Women Caution

  • Warts may proliferate and become easily irritated
  • Avoid: Podophyllotoxin (teratogenic)
  • Use: Cryotherapy, surgery, laser, or TCA/BCA
  • Imiquimod and sinecatechins: insufficient data, avoid use
  • Delivery: Not an indication for caesarean section (unless obstruction)

üîÅ Treatment Failure Criteria

Switch If:
  • No significant improvement after 3 provider-administered treatments
  • No complete clearance after 6 treatments

Follow-up

Monitoring and prognosis

‚ñº

‚ö†Ô∏è No Cure Available

There is no cure for genital warts. Treatment eliminates visible lesions but does not eradicate HPV infection. Recurrence is common.

üëÅÔ∏è Monitoring Schedule

  • Post-treatment: Schedule follow-up to document wart-free period
  • Recurrence check: Evaluate at 3 months after successful treatment (peak recurrence time)
  • Immunocompromised: More frequent monitoring due to higher recurrence risk

üìà Prognosis

  • Spontaneous resolution: 10-30% resolve within 3 months without treatment
  • Recurrence: Common, especially in first 3 months post-treatment
  • Transmission: Treatment does not affect HPV infectivity or transmission rate
  • Long-term: Generally benign course; oncogenic potential linked to high-risk HPV types (not typical in genital warts)

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention & Counselling

HPV Vaccination Barrier Methods
  • Primary prevention: HPV vaccination (quadrivalent or 9-valent) for ages 9-45
  • Barrier methods: Condoms reduce but do not eliminate transmission risk
  • Partner screening: Screen for other STIs (chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis, hepatitis B, HIV)
  • Cervical screening: Women with genital warts should have annual cervical cytology
  • Patient education: Treatment does not reduce transmission; abstinence during treatment may be advised
