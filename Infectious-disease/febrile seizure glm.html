Febrile Seizure

NICE CKS Reference Last Revised: Jan 2024
Target: Primary Care
Call 999/Ambulance immediately if seizure lasts > 5 minutes or if signs of meningitis are present.

During Seizure (Active)

  • Protect Airway: Place in recovery position once safe.
  • Do NOT: Put anything in the mouth or restrain movements.
  • Monitor: Time the seizure accurately.
  • Medication: Usually none needed if < 5 mins and self-limiting.
Criteria for Status Epilepticus: Seizure > 5 minutes (NICE/Resus Council) or > 30 mins (Traditional definition). Treat immediately if > 5 mins.

Post-Seizure Management

  • Check Temperature: Confirm > 38°C.
  • Identify Cause: Focus on treating the fever source (viral, UTI, etc.).
  • Reassure Parents: Explain the benign nature of simple febrile seizures.
  • Observation: Monitor until fully recovered (usually < 1 hour).
Safety Note: Antipyretics do not prevent febrile seizures but improve comfort.

Admission Decision Tree

ADMIT
  • Complex features (focal, prolonged, recurrent).
  • Child < 18 months with uncertain diagnosis.
  • Suspected CNS infection (Meningitis/Encephalitis).
  • Incomplete recovery after 1 hour.
  • No clear focus of infection or antibiotic usage masking signs.
DISCHARGE
  • Simple febrile seizure fully recovered.
  • Clear focus of infection identified (e.g., URTI).
  • No red flags present.
  • Parents/carers understand fever management and when to seek help.

Assessment & Red Flags

Red Flags (Meningitis/Sepsis)

Immediate admission required if present:

  • Bulging fontanelle (infants).
  • Neck stiffness / Photophobia.
  • Petechial or purpuric rash (non-blanching).
  • Decreased level of consciousness.
  • Focal neurological deficit lasting > 1 hour.
  • Prolonged post-ictal period (drowsiness).

Key History to Take

  • Age: Typically 6 months to 6 years.
  • Fever History: Peak temp, duration, onset relative to seizure.
  • Seizure Description: Duration (crucial), type (generalized vs focal), recurrence in 24h.
  • Context: Recent vaccinations (MMR/DTP), immunization status, antibiotic use.
  • Family History: Epilepsy or febrile seizures in 1st degree relatives.
  • Development: Any neurodevelopmental delay?

Examination Checklist

Neurology
  • Consciousness level.
  • Focal deficits (e.g., Todd's palsy).
  • Neurocutaneous stigmata (Sturge-Weber, NF, Tuberous Sclerosis).
Systemic
  • Temperature (>38°C).
  • Hydration status.
  • Respiratory effort.
  • Cardiovascular status.
Source of Fever
  • Otoscopic exam (Otitis Media).
  • Throat/Chest (RTI).
  • Skin (Rashes).
  • Abdomen/GU (consider UTI if no focus).

Simple vs. Complex

Differentiating the type of seizure guides management and prognosis.

Feature Simple Febrile Seizure Complex Febrile Seizure
Duration < 15 minutes > 15 minutes
Type Generalized Tonic-Clonic Focal features / One-sided
Recurrence Once in 24 hours Recurrent in 24 hours
Recovery Full recovery within 1 hour Incomplete recovery / Todd's palsy
Management Often manage at home/outpatient Consider admission/Urgent investigation

Differential Diagnosis

Conditions to rule out:

  • Meningitis/Encephalitis: The most critical differential.
  • Hypoglycemia: Check glucose if drowsy.
  • Electrolyte Imbalance / Metabolic.
  • Head Injury.
  • Breath-holding spells / Syncope.
  • Epilepsy Syndromes: Dravet Syndrome (prolonged seizures triggered by fever) or GEFS+.

Typical Features

What usually happens:

  • Onset: Usually within first 24 hours of fever.
  • Seizure: Loss of consciousness, muscle stiffening (tonic), then jerking (clonic).
  • Duration: Typically < 5 minutes.
  • Appearance: Rolling eyes, foaming mouth, cyanosis (blue lips).
  • Post-Ictal: Brief drowsiness, confusion, then full recovery.

Prognosis & Prevention

General Outlook

Benign

Most children have normal growth and development.

Cognitive outcome is generally normal.

Recurrence Risk

~32%

Chance of having another febrile seizure.

  • 75% occur within 1 year.
  • 90% occur within 2 years.

Epilepsy Risk

Slightly increased risk vs general population.

  • Simple: 1 - 2%
  • Complex: 6 - 8%

Risk higher if neurodevelopmental abnormality or family history of epilepsy exists.

Risk Factors for Recurrence

Age < 18 months Family history (1st degree relative) Low grade fever (<39°C) at onset Short duration of fever before seizure Daycare attendance

Note: The greater the number of risk factors, the higher the recurrence rate (up to 80% if all present).

Advice to Parents

What to do if it happens again:
  • Note time (start/stop).
  • Place in recovery position.
  • Do not put anything in mouth.
  • Call ambulance if > 5 mins.
Reassurance:

Emphasize that it does not cause brain damage.

Treat the fever (paracetamol/ibuprofen) to make the child comfortable, but understand this does not necessarily prevent seizures.
