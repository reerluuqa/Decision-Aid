Assessment

Nappy rash is an irritant contact dermatitis confined to the nappy area. Colonization with Candida albicans is usual.

Typical Presentation

The rash typically presents as:

  • Well-defined areas of confluent erythema and scattered papules over convex surfaces in contact with the nappy (the buttocks, genitalia, suprapubic area, and upper thighs)
  • Sparing of the inguinal skin creases and gluteal cleft
  • The child may appear distressed, agitated, or uncomfortable, as the rash may be itchy and painful
  • Skin erosions, edema, and ulceration may occur in severe cases

Consider These Alternative Diagnoses

Candidal Infection

Satellite lesions, erythematous plaques, often in skin creases

Bacterial Infection

Pustules, crusting, possible fever, rapidly spreading

Atopic Eczema

Often affects other areas, family history of atopy, chronic course

Seborrhoeic Dermatitis

Greasy yellow scales, may affect scalp, face, and other flexural areas

Management

1

Initial Management

Advise parents/carers on self-management strategies:

  • Consider using nappies with high absorbency (e.g., disposable gel matrix nappies)
  • Ensure the nappy fits properly
  • Leave nappies off for as long as possible
  • Change nappies every 2–3 hours, or as soon as possible after wetting or soiling
  • Clean the skin with water, or fragrance and alcohol-free baby wipes
  • Dry the skin gently after cleaning — avoid vigorous rubbing
  • Bath the child daily — avoid excessive bathing
  • Avoid using soap, bubble baths, lotions, talcum powder, or topical antibiotics
  • Avoid the use of plastic underpants over the nappy
2

Assess Severity and Determine Treatment

Select the most appropriate treatment based on severity:

Mild

Redness only, child asymptomatic

Moderate

Redness with inflammation, causing discomfort

Severe

With possible secondary infection

Treatment Failure

If the nappy rash is not improving following initial management:

  • Consider and, if possible, manage any underlying cause of treatment failure:
    • Non-adherence to self-care advice or the treatment regimen
    • Candidal or bacterial secondary infection — take a skin swab for culture and sensitivity
    • An alternative cause for the rash
    • An immunocompromised host
  • If the rash persists following treatment for secondary infection:
    • Adjust the choice of topical imidazole or oral antibiotic if indicated, depending on swab results
    • Seek specialist advice from a medical microbiologist if the rash has not responded to appropriate treatment

Barrier Preparations

Indications

Mild erythema and the child is asymptomatic

Options

  • Zinc and Castor Oil ointment
  • White soft paraffin BP ointment

Application

Apply thinly at each nappy change

Cautions

Advise against use if candidal infection is suspected or confirmed

Hydrocortisone 1% Cream

Indications

If the rash appears inflamed and is causing discomfort in a child aged 1 month or over

Application

Apply a thin layer once daily until symptoms settle or for a maximum of 7 days

With Barrier Cream

The barrier preparation should be applied a few minutes after applying the topical corticosteroid

Cautions

Not recommended for children under 1 month of age

Topical Imidazoles

Indications

If the rash persists and candidal infection is suspected or confirmed on swab

Options

  • Clotrimazole 1% (licensed for candidal nappy rash)
  • Miconazole 2% (licensed for fungal skin infections)
  • Econazole 1% (licensed for fungal skin infections)

Application

Apply thinly and evenly 2-3 times daily. Continue for at least 1 week after symptoms disappear.

Cautions

Advise against the use of a barrier preparation until the candidal infection has settled

Flucloxacillin

Indications

If bacterial infection is suspected or confirmed by swab

Dosage

  • Age 1 month to 1 year: 62.5 mg to 125 mg four times daily for 7 days
  • Age 2–9 years: 125 mg to 250 mg four times daily for 7 days

Contraindications

True penicillin hypersensitivity, history of urticaria or rash immediately after penicillin administration, history of flucloxacillin-associated jaundice/hepatic dysfunction

Adverse Effects

Gastrointestinal adverse effects, skin reactions, thrombocytopenia, hypersensitivity reactions, hepatitis and cholestatic jaundice

Clarithromycin

Indications

If bacterial infection is suspected or confirmed by swab and the child is allergic to penicillin

Dosage

  • Body weight up to 8 kg: 7.5 mg/kg twice daily for 7 days
  • Body weight 8–11 kg: 62.5 mg twice daily for 7 days
  • Body weight 12–19 kg: 125 mg twice daily for 7 days
  • Body weight 20–29 kg: 187.5 mg twice daily for 7 days
  • Body weight 30–40 kg: 250 mg twice daily for 7 days

Contraindications

Electrolyte disturbances, history of QT prolongation or ventricular cardiac arrhythmia, severe hepatic impairment in combination with renal impairment

Adverse Effects

Gastrointestinal adverse effects, hearing impairment, hepatic dysfunction, QT interval prolongation

Red Flag Symptoms

Systemic Symptoms

Fever, irritability, poor feeding, lethargy
