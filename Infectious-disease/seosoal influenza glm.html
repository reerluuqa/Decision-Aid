Assessment

Initial Assessment

  • Check if influenza is circulating in the community (national surveillance scheme)
  • Identify symptoms of influenza-like illness
  • Determine if patient is in an 'at risk' group

Common Symptoms of Influenza

  • Abrupt onset of symptoms (around 2 days after exposure)
  • Fever (typically 38-40°C)
  • Cough
  • Headache, malaise, myalgia, and arthralgia
  • Sore throat
  • Nasal discharge and coryza
  • Ocular symptoms (photophobia, conjunctivitis)

Note: The Department of Health and Social Care notifies GPs directly if influenza is circulating in the community.

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

  • Diagnosis is generally made using clinical features when influenza is known to be circulating
  • Consider testing for influenza when COVID-19 is also circulating
  • For people with complicated influenza, rapid testing should be undertaken

Differential Diagnosis

  • COVID-19 infection Consider co-infection
  • Common cold (coryza)
  • Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) infection
  • Parainfluenza virus infection
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis
  • Meningitis Urgent referral
  • Bacterial pneumonia or acute bronchitis
  • Infectious mononucleosis
  • Pertussis (whooping cough)

No
Signs of severe/complicated influenza include:
  • Signs requiring hospital admission
  • Lower respiratory tract infection (hypoxaemia, dyspnoea, lung infiltrate)
  • Central nervous system involvement
  • Significant exacerbation of underlying medical condition
Information: Initiation of antiviral treatment should not be delayed while awaiting laboratory confirmation.

Treatment

When to Prescribe Antivirals

Prescribe an antiviral drug (oral oseltamivir or inhaled zanamivir) for people with influenza if:

  • The national surveillance scheme indicates that influenza is circulating
  • The person is in an 'at risk' group
  • The person can start treatment within 48 hours of symptom onset (36 hours for zanamivir in children)

Which Antiviral to Prescribe

For 'at risk' groups:

  • Oral oseltamivir (first-line)
  • Inhaled zanamivir (alternative)

For pregnant women:

  • Oseltamivir is recommended over zanamivir (except in cases of known/suspected oseltamivir resistance)

Self-Care Advice

  • Rest and increase fluid intake
  • Use paracetamol or ibuprofen to reduce fever and pain
  • Seek medical help if symptoms worsen or don't improve after 7 days
  • Practice good hygiene to prevent spread
Note: Starting treatment after 48 hours of symptom onset is an off-label use and clinical judgement should be exercised.

Follow-up

When to Admit to Hospital

  • Signs of severe/complicated influenza
  • Deteriorating symptoms despite antiviral treatment
  • Persistent high fever despite antipyretics
  • Signs of dehydration
  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
  • Chest pain
  • Confusion or disorientation
  • Seizures

Complications to Monitor For

  • Acute bronchitis
  • Exacerbations of asthma and COPD
  • Otitis media
  • Pneumonia Urgent referral
  • Sinusitis
  • Cardiac complications (myocarditis, pericarditis)
  • Febrile convulsions
  • Myalgia, myositis and rhabdomyolysis
  • Neurological complications (encephalitis, Guillain-Barré syndrome)
Special consideration: Pregnant women are at increased risk of morbidity and mortality from influenza infection, particularly in the third trimester.
