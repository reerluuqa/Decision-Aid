Herpes Simplex - Ocular

Age: 1m+ Topic: CKS May 2024 Status: Urgent Referral

CRITICAL: URGENT REFERRAL REQUIRED

Refer ALL cases of suspected ocular herpes simplex to eye casualty or emergency eye service for same-day assessment.

DO NOT initiate drug treatment in primary care
Seek specialist advice if same-day not possible
Advice to Give
  • Stop contact lenses (until 24h after resolved).
  • Avoid touching lesions; wash hands frequently.
  • Warn that virus is easily transmitted.
  • Provide patient information leaflets.
Scope & Exclusions
  • Includes: Age 1 month onwards. Suspected HSV.
  • Excludes: Neonates (separate pathway).
  • Excludes: Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (Shingles).
  • Note: Some optometrists can initiate treatment for specific unilateral epithelial cases.

Diagnosis: Clinical Suspicion

Symptoms (Subjective)
  • Eye pain (may be mild if sensation reduced).
  • Eye irritation / photophobia.
  • Eye watering (suggests keratitis).
  • Blurred vision.
  • Malaise / fever (common in primary infection).
Signs (Objective)
  • Acute red eye.
  • Vesicles/Ulcers on lids/periocular skin.
  • Hazy cornea (edema/scarring).
  • Fixed irregular pupil (iritis).
  • Reduced corneal sensation.
  • Fluorescein stain: Dendritic ulcer (linear branching).
Reminder: Diagnosis is usually unilateral (88%), but may be bilateral in children or immunosuppressed.

Infection Types

Epithelial Keratitis
Common

Most common manifestation (50-80% of cases). Inflammation of superficial layer.

Dendritic Ulcer Punctate lesions

What else could it be?

Condition Key Distinguishing Features
Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus Vesicular rash in ophthalmic division (V1) of trigeminal nerve; usually older people; rarely recurrent.
Acute Glaucoma Painful red eye, blurred vision, headache; usually >50 years; semi-dilated fixed pupil.
Bacterial Conjunctivitis Bilateral red eye with purulent discharge.
Corneal Abrasion History of trauma; foreign body sensation.
Orbital Cellulitis Eye pain, double vision, limited painful eye movements, fever, malaise.

Background & Prognosis

Transmission & Recurrence
  • Usually HSV-1 (acquired in childhood).
  • Latent in trigeminal ganglion; reactivates with UV light, stress, fever, trauma.
  • Recurrence: Very common. 40% by 5 years; 67% by 7 years.
Prognosis
  • Blepharoconjunctivitis: Resolves ~2 weeks.
  • Epithelial Keratitis: Resolves 1-2 weeks (can be spontaneous).
  • Complications: Corneal scarring, perforation (necrotizing), secondary glaucoma.
  • 90% maintain driving level vision; 3% develop severe vision loss.
