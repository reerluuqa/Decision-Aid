Initial Assessment

Clinical evaluation and risk stratification

‚ñº
  1. Clinical Presentation
    • Fever (80% of patients) Common
    • New or changed heart murmur (65% of patients)
    • Signs of embolism (25% of patients)
    • Heart failure symptoms
  2. Risk Factor Assessment
    • Previous IE history
    • Prosthetic valve or cardiac device
    • Congenital heart disease
    • IV drug use
    • Recent dental work or vascular access
  3. Physical Examination
    • Complete cardiac examination
    • Look for peripheral stigmata:
      • Janeway lesions
      • Osler nodes
      • Roth spots
      • Splinter hemorrhages
    • Neurological assessment

  • Severe sepsis or septic shock
  • Acute heart failure
  • Valve dehiscence
  • Large vegetation (>10mm) with emboli

Diagnostic Workup

Laboratory investigations and imaging

‚ñº
  1. Blood Cultures CRITICAL
    • 3 sets from different sites at 30-minute intervals
    • 10mL per bottle (aerobic & anaerobic)
    • Before antibiotic administration if possible
    • Repeat after 48-72 hours to assess treatment
  2. Initial Laboratory Tests
    • FBC, CRP, ESR
    • Urea & electrolytes, liver function
    • Urinalysis (microscopic hematuria)
    • Troponin (myocardial involvement)
  3. Echocardiography
    • TTE: First-line for native valves
    • TOE: If TTE negative or prosthetic valve
    • Look for vegetations, abscess, dehiscence
    • Repeat in 5-7 days if high suspicion
  4. Additional Imaging (if needed)
    • Cardiac CT for paravalvular complications
    • MRI for neurological complications
    • PET/CT for prosthetic valve IE

Treatment Strategy

Antimicrobial therapy and surgical considerations

‚ñº
  1. Initial Management URGENT
    • ABC approach - stabilize patient
    • Start empirical antibiotics after cultures
    • Multidisciplinary team involvement
    • Consider ICU admission if severe
  2. Empirical Antibiotic Therapy
    • Native valve: Amoxicillin + Gentamicin
    • Prosthetic valve: Vancomycin + Gentamicin + Rifampicin
    • Penicillin allergy: Vancomycin-based regimen
    • Adjust based on culture results
  3. Targeted Therapy (by organism)
    • Streptococci: Penicillin ¬± Gentamicin (4-6 weeks)
    • Staphylococci: Flucloxacillin/Vancomycin (4-6 weeks)
    • Enterococci: Amoxicillin + Gentamicin (6 weeks)
    • HACEK: Ceftriaxone (4-6 weeks)
  4. Surgical Indications CONSIDER
    • Heart failure with hemodynamic compromise
    • Uncontrolled infection (persistent fever/cultures)
    • Large vegetation (>10mm) with emboli
    • Paravalvular complications (abscess, fistula)
    • Prosthetic valve dehiscence

Follow-up & Monitoring

Post-treatment surveillance and complications

‚ñº
  1. During Treatment
    • Weekly review by IE team
    • Monitor for persistent infection (>7 days fever/cultures)
    • Repeat blood cultures at 1 and 2 weeks post-treatment
    • Baseline echocardiogram before discharge
  2. Common Complications WATCH
    • Heart failure - Most common, requires surgery
    • Systemic emboli - 22-50% incidence, CNS most common
    • Renal impairment - Up to 30%, multifactorial
    • Valve dehiscence/rupture - Requires emergency surgery
  3. Long-term Follow-up
    • Cardiology follow-up at 1, 3, 6, 12 months
    • Repeat echocardiogram at 3 months
    • Patient education on IE prevention
    • Screen for occult malignancy (S. gallolyticus)
  4. Prophylaxis Education
    • Maintain excellent dental hygiene
    • Prompt treatment of infections
    • Avoid tattoos and piercings
    • Know symptoms requiring immediate review

‚úÖ Prognosis Indicators OUTCOME

Poor prognostic factors:

  • Heart failure
  • Cerebral complications
  • Vegetation >10mm
  • S. aureus infection
  • Delay in surgery when indicated

Good outcomes with: Early diagnosis, appropriate antibiotics, timely surgery when indicated
