RED FLAGS

Hepatitis B

Clinical Knowledge Summary (Sept 2024)

Revised Sept 2024
Notifiable Disease: Acute Hepatitis B is notifiable. Labs are statutorily required to report all diagnoses (acute & chronic) to UKHSA.
Primary Goals
  • Vaccinate at-risk populations.
  • Offer advice to reduce transmission risk.
  • Appropriately refer all positives.
  • Admit if necessary (fulminant failure).
Key Facts
  • Incubation: Avg 12 weeks (40-160 days).
  • Survival: Survives on surfaces >7 days.
  • Chronicity: 90% infants, 5% adults.
  • Transmission: Blood/body fluids.
Transmission Routes
  • Injecting Drug Use
  • Sexual Contact
  • Perinatal (Mother-to-child)
  • Non-sterile equipment (Tattoos)
Prevention
  • Vaccination (High risk)
  • Post-exposure Prophylaxis
  • Barrier protection
Prevalence (UK)
  • Overall: Low prevalence (~0.45%)
  • Acute Cases: ~350/year (Eng)
  • Chronic: ~206,000 people
  • Urban Areas: Higher rates (up to 1%)

When to Test: Asymptomatic
  • Born/brought up in intermediate/high prevalence country.
  • Current/historic injecting drug users.
  • Frequent sexual partners / Sex workers.
  • Close contacts of HBV positive person.
  • Haemodialysis patients.
  • People with chronic liver disease.
  • Needlestick injury or bite.
When to Test: Symptomatic
  • Prodrome: Fever, arthralgia, rash (before jaundice).
  • General: Malaise, fatigue, nausea, anorexia.
  • Specific: RUQ pain, Jaundice, dark urine.
  • Extrahepatic: Glomerulonephritis, vasculitis.

Management Goals

Referral Criteria

ALL people who test positive for Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) should be referred to specialist care for further assessment.

Primary Care Actions
  • Refer: To specialist hepatology/GUM/ID.
  • Document: Notify UKHSA (Acute cases).
  • Advise: Avoid alcohol, safe sex practices.
  • Contact Tracing: Screen sexual/household contacts.
Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

For significant exposure (e.g. needlestick):

  • If unvaccinated: Give HBV vaccine immediately. Consider HBIG depending on status of source.
  • If previously vaccinated: Check anti-HBs levels. If adequate (>100mIU/mL), no action. If low, give booster.
  • Source known HBsAg positive: HBIG + Vaccine (rapid course).
PEP should be given ASAP, ideally within 48 hours.

Emergency Recognition

Fulminant Hepatic Necrosis (Acute Liver Failure)

Rare (<1%) but often fatal. Immediate admission required.

Clinical Signs:
  • Coagulopathy (INR prolonged)
  • Encephalopathy (Confusion, coma)
  • Cerebral edema
  • Hypoglycemia
Action:
ADMIT TO HOSPITAL IMMEDIATELY
Other Serious Complications
  • Serum sickness-like syndrome: Fever, rash, glomerulonephritis, polyarteritis.
  • Extra-hepatic malignancy: Increased risk of cervical, gastric, non-Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • Cirrhosis: Occurs in 20-25% of chronic carriers.
  • Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC): Surveillance needed if cirrhosis present.

Chronic Prognosis

Progression

20-25% of chronic infections develop progressive liver disease.

Cirrhosis Risk

5-year cumulative risk of 8-20% for untreated chronic HBV.

Survival

5-year survival for decompensated cirrhosis is as low as 15%.

Phases of Chronic HBV
  • Immune Tolerant: HBeAg+, High DNA, Normal ALT. Highly infectious.
  • Immune Active: HBeAg+, High DNA, High ALT. Liver inflammation/fibrosis.
  • Inactive Carrier: HBeAg-, Low DNA, Normal ALT. Low risk.
  • Immune Escape: HBeAg-, Fluctuating DNA, High ALT. High risk of HCC/Cirrhosis.
