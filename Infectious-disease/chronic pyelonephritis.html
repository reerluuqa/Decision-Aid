Assessment

Key History Factors

  • Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) - most common
  • Recurrent acute pyelonephritis
  • Renal obstruction (stones, BPH, congenital)
  • Prior renal surgery
  • Frequent UTIs

Demographics & Symptoms

  • Female predominance
  • Children: VUR risk
  • Adults: XGP & EPN risk
  • Hematuria
  • Cloudy urine
  • Weight loss (XGP)

Strong Risk Factors

  • Acute pyelonephritis history
  • Vesicoureteral reflux
  • Urinary obstruction
  • Renal calculi (staghorn)
  • Diabetes mellitus

Moderate Risk Factors

  • Neurogenic bladder
  • Inadequate bladder emptying
  • Indwelling catheter
Critical Assessment Points: Chronic pyelonephritis often has no specific physical findings until late disease. Signs of hypertension and renal insufficiency appear in advanced stages. Always evaluate for underlying anatomical abnormalities.
Diagnosis
Diagnostic Approach: Histopathology is definitive but biopsies are NOT routine. Diagnosis is clinical + imaging. Imaging is essential to exclude other causes of renal impairment.

Laboratory Evaluation

  • Urinalysis: Leukocytes, hematuria, proteinuria
  • Serum creatinine: Reflects severity
  • eGFR calculation: Assess dysfunction
  • Urinary sediment: May show leukocytes/casts
  • Urine culture: Exclude active infection
  • FBC: Anemia, leukocytosis
  • Electrolytes: Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, acidosis
  • CRP: Marker of severe disease

Imaging Studies

  • Renal ultrasound: Initial test - scarring, stones, obstruction
  • KUB x-ray: Baseline, calcifications
  • CT abdomen: Most informative - scars, obstruction, stones
  • MRI: Alternative to CT, better for GFR
  • VCUG: Diagnose VUR in children

Xanthogranulomatous Pyelonephritis (XGP)

  • Symptoms: Fever, malaise, weight loss, flank pain
  • Lab: Persistent anemia, leukocytosis
  • Culture: Proteus (60%), E. coli, Klebsiella
  • Imaging: Enlarged kidney with calculi, mass-like
  • Risk: Often misdiagnosed as renal cancer

Emphysematous Pyelonephritis (EPN)

  • Presentation: Acutely ill, sepsis signs
  • Lab: Leukocytosis, abnormal urinalysis
  • Glucose: Elevated (diabetics)
  • Cultures: E. coli (66%), Klebsiella (26%)
  • Imaging: Gas in collecting system/parenchyma
Differential Diagnoses: Acute pyelonephritis Renal calculi Renal cancer (vs XGP)
Treatment
Core Principle: NO specific treatment for chronic pyelonephritis - damage is irreversible. Focus on eliminating recurrent UTIs and correcting anatomical abnormalities to prevent further damage.

General Management

  • Identify and treat underlying causes
  • Eradicate recurrent infections
  • Correct obstruction/urolithiasis
  • Antibiotics only if ongoing infection documented
  • Refer to nephrology/urology

XGP Management Surgical

  • Nephrectomy: Treatment of choice
  • Partial nephrectomy: Focal disease only
  • Antibiotics: Before and after surgery
  • Cover gram-negative organisms
  • Duration: 10-14 days

EPN Management Emergency

  • Stabilization: Fluid resuscitation, oxygenation
  • Tight glucose control (diabetics)
  • Percutaneous drainage + antibiotics
  • Nephrectomy: If no improvement in 24-48h or severe sepsis
  • Duration: 10-14 days

Emerging Therapies

  • Cefepime/Enmetazobactam: Approved for complicated UTIs
  • Aztreonam/Avibactam: Approved in Europe
Follow-up

Monitoring Parameters

  • Blood pressure - regularly
  • Blood glucose (diabetics)
  • Lipid profiles - CKD risk
  • Renal function (creatinine, eGFR)
  • Frequency depends on severity

Potential Complications

  • Acute renal failure (EPN, sepsis)
  • Secondary hyperparathyroidism (late CKD)
  • Acute pyelonephritis (recurrent)
  • Obstruction (stones, strictures)
  • Chronic kidney disease (progression to ESRD)

Pediatric Considerations

  • DMSA imaging: Assess scarring at 4+ months post-infection
  • Continuous antibiotics: High-risk children with VUR
  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction: Treat concurrently
  • Surgical correction: Consider for breakthrough febrile UTIs

Prognosis Factors

  • Adults: Focal scarring, proteinuria = poor prognosis
  • Children: VUR + infection = significant damage
  • XGP: Unilateral, surgical excision curative
  • EPN: Mortality 40-90% depending on treatment
  • Progression: Can lead to ESRD if untreated
Primary Prevention: Treat acute infections promptly, consider surgical correction of anatomical abnormalities (VUR, obstruction)
Secondary Prevention: High index of suspicion for recurrent infections, rapid treatment, glucose control in diabetics, continuous prophylaxis for high-risk children with VUR
