ü©∫ Clinical Diagnosis
  • Rash: Macules ‚Üí Papules ‚Üí Vesicles (intensely itchy) ‚Üí Pustules ‚Üí Crusts.
  • Pattern: Crops appear over several days; "stars and galaxies" appearance.
  • Distribution: Scalp, face, trunk, proximal limbs. Mucous membranes may be affected.
  • Prodrome: Fever, malaise, anorexia, headache (common in adults/adolescents).
ü¶† Transmission & Control
  • Incubation: 1‚Äì3 weeks (longer if immunocompromised/VZIG given).
  • Infectious Period: From 24 hours before rash onset until all vesicles have crusted over (usually 5 days).
  • Exclusion: Keep off school/nursery until all lesions crusted.
üö® Key Complications
  • Secondary Bacterial Infection: GAS (Cellulitis, Necrotizing fasciitis) or S. aureus. Look for spreading redness, pain, fever after initial improvement.
  • Neurological: Encephalitis, Cerebellar ataxia.
  • Pneumonia: Especially in adults/smokers.
üíä Management: Healthy Child

Self-care is primary. Antivirals are not routinely recommended.

  • Analgesia/Antipyretic: Paracetamol. Avoid NSAIDs (Ibuprofen) due to risk of necrotizing soft tissue infections.
  • Pruritus: Calamine lotion, Chlorphenamine (1y+). Keep nails short.
  • Hydration: Encourage fluids.
üö´ Do NOT Prescribe
  • Aciclovir: Not recommended for uncomplicated cases in healthy children.
  • NSAIDs: Associated with severe skin complications.
üìÖ Advice
  • Stay away from school until crusted.
  • Avoid immunocompromised contacts, pregnant women, and neonates.
  • Seek help if high fever returns or surrounding skin becomes red/hot.

üö≠ Risk Factors
  • Adults are more likely to be hospitalized than children.
  • Smokers are at high risk of fulminating varicella pneumonia.
  • Monitor closely for respiratory symptoms.

  • Paracetamol for pain/fever.
  • Calamine / Chlorphenamine for itch.
  • Avoid NSAIDs.
üö® IMMEDIATE ACTION
  • Seek immediate specialist advice.
  • Consider admission if respiratory symptoms or severe disease.
  • Risk of pneumonia (approx 10%) increases with gestation.

  • 20‚Äì28 weeks: Small risk (lower than first 20 weeks).

üíä Treatment
  • Specialist-led Oral Aciclovir (usually within 24h of rash onset). Discuss risk/benefit.
  • Severe disease: IV Aciclovir.
üè• ADMIT IMMEDIATELY

Indication: Any neonate or immunocompromised person with suspected chickenpox.

  • Administer IV Aciclovir.
  • These groups are at risk of disseminated/haemorrhagic disease.
üë∂ Neonate Specifics
  • Risk is highest if mother develops rash 7 days before to 7 days after delivery.
  • Severe infection may be fatal.
  • Seek specialist advice immediately if exposed (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis may be needed).
ü¶† Immunocompromised
  • Includes those on high-dose steroids, chemotherapy, HIV.
  • Rash may be atypical (haemorrhagic, more extensive).
  • Infectious period may be prolonged.
üìû Exposure Management

Relevant for pregnant women, neonates, and immunocompromised people without a history of chickenpox/shingles.

  • Significant Exposure: Face-to-face contact (15+ mins), same room (60+ mins), or direct contact with lesions.
  • Check antibody status (IgG) if uncertain.
  • If Non-immune: Seek immediate specialist advice for Varicella-Zoster Immunoglobulin (VZIG).
‚úÖ Significant Protection
  • History of chickenpox/shingles usually confers immunity (no action needed).
  • Reinfection is uncommon.
