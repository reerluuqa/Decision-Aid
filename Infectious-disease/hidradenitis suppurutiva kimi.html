Assessment & History

üí°
Key Principle: Diagnosis is clinical based on characteristic lesions in typical locations with chronicity and recurrences.

Patient History

Essential questions and risk factors

‚ñº
Essential
  • Typical Locations: Axillae, inguinal regions, perineum, infra-mammary
  • Lesion Characteristics: Painful nodules, abscesses, sinus tracts, scarring
  • Chronicity: Recurrent flares over ‚â•6 months
  • Previous Treatments: Response to antibiotics, need for incision & drainage
  • Premenstrual Flares: Common in women (hormonal influence)
  • Family History: ~33% have familial predisposition
  • Smoking Status: Strong association with disease severity
  • Weight Assessment: Obesity as risk factor & severity modifier
  • Comorbidities: Screen for metabolic syndrome, diabetes, depression
üîç

Physical Examination

Key diagnostic findings

‚ñº
Standard
  • Inflammatory Nodules: Firm, tender lesions in intertriginous areas
  • Abscesses: Fluctuant lesions with purulent drainage
  • Open Comedones: Multiple "double-headed" comedones - pathognomonic
  • Sinus Tracts: Linear cord-like scars with purulent drainage
  • Scarring: Fibrosis, dermal contractures, disfiguring scars
  • Distribution: Usually bilateral and symmetrical
  • Malodorous Discharge: Characteristic foul-smelling purulent material
  • Tunneling: Subcutaneous sinus tracts connecting lesions

Red Flags

When to consider urgent referral

‚ñº
Urgent
  • Severe Pain: Uncontrolled pain requiring immediate intervention
  • Systemic Symptoms: Fever, malaise, sepsis indicators
  • Rapid Progression: Rapid worsening of lesions
  • Extensive Involvement: Multiple sites with severe inflammation
  • Suspicion of SCC: Ulcerated, non-healing lesions in chronic disease
  • Perianal Disease: Consider Crohn's disease differential
  • Immunosuppression: Increased risk of complications
  • Psychological Crisis: Severe impact on mental health

Required

4

Hurley Classification

Disease severity staging

‚ñº
Classification
Stage I - Mild:
  • Abscesses & nodules without sinus tracts/scarring
  • Single or isolated lesions
  • No significant scarring
Stage II - Moderate:
  • Recurrent abscesses with sinus tracts & scarring
  • Single or multiple lesions
  • Separated by normal skin areas
Stage III - Severe:
  • Diffuse involvement with multiple interconnected lesions
  • Extensive sinus tracts & scarring
  • Minimal normal skin between lesions

Investigations

Targeted tests when indicated

‚ñº
Selective
  • Bacterial Cultures: Rule out MRSA furunculosis, guide antibiotic therapy
  • Skin Biopsy: Exclude SCC in chronic lesions, differentiate from Crohn's
  • Zinc Levels: Consider in refractory cases (acrodermatitis enteropathica)
  • HbA1c: Screen for diabetes/metabolic syndrome
  • CRP/ESR: Assess inflammatory burden if systemic symptoms
  • Psychological Screening: PHQ-9 for depression assessment
  • Endoscopy: If perianal disease ¬± GI symptoms (Crohn's workup)
‚ùì

Differential Diagnosis

Key distinguishing features

‚ñº
Essential
  • Furunculosis: Single lesions, positive cultures, responds to antibiotics
  • Crohn's Disease: GI symptoms, perianal fistulas, granulomas on biopsy
  • Epidermal Inclusion Cysts: Solitary lesions with visible punctum
  • Acne Vulgaris: Facial/chest distribution, no sinus tracts
  • Inverse Psoriasis: Macerated red patches, no abscesses
  • SCC: Ulcerated, non-healing, biopsy diagnostic
  • Lymphogranuloma Venereum: STD risk, pronounced lymphadenopathy

Management & Treatment

üéØ
Treatment Goal: Reduce lesion formation, manage symptoms, prevent scarring, and improve quality of life through multidisciplinary approach.
üå±

Mild Disease (Hurley I)

Localized disease without scarring

‚ñº
First-Line

Topical Therapies

  • Clindamycin 1%: Apply twice daily for minimum 8 weeks
  • Metronidazole 0.75-1%: Alternative topical antibiotic
  • Antimicrobial Wash: Chlorhexidine, benzoyl peroxide, or zinc pyrithione
  • Topical Resorcinol 15%: Keratolytic agent for mild-moderate disease

Systemic Therapies (if needed)

  • Tetracyclines: Doxycycline 100mg BD or Minocycline 100mg BD for 12 weeks
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs (Ibuprofen 400mg QDS PRN)

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Smoking Cessation: Critical intervention
  • Weight Management: Reduce skin friction
  • Loose Clothing: Reduce occlusion & friction
  • Wound Care: Gentle cleansing, avoid manipulation
‚ö†Ô∏è

Moderate Disease (Hurley II)

Recurrent disease with scarring

‚ñº
Second-Line

Antibiotic Therapy

  • Clindamycin + Rifampicin: 300mg each BD for 10-12 weeks (first-line)
  • Tetracyclines: If combination not tolerated (12-week course)
  • Triple Therapy: Moxifloxacin + Metronidazole + Rifampicin (second-line)

Hormonal Therapy

  • Spironolactone: 100-150mg daily for premenstrual flares (women only)
  • Anti-androgens: Finasteride may be considered
  • Contraception: Required with anti-androgen therapy

Retinoids

  • Isotretinoin: 0.5-1mg/kg/day for 6+ months (if concomitant acne)
  • Acitretin: 25-50mg daily for 6+ months (teratogenic - avoid in pregnancy)

Surgical Options

  • Local Excision: For small, quiescent lesions
  • Deroofing: Selective sinus tract deroofing for recurrent lesions
üö®

Severe Disease (Hurley III)

Diffuse disease with extensive scarring

‚ñº
Advanced

Biologic Therapy

  • Adalimumab: First-line biologic - 160mg Day 1, 80mg Day 15, then 40mg weekly
  • Infliximab: Second-line (off-label) - 5mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, 6, then every 8 weeks
  • Ustekinumab: IL-12/23 inhibitor (off-label, moderate efficacy)
  • Secukinumab: IL-17 inhibitor (off-label, emerging evidence)
  • Anakinra: IL-1 receptor antagonist (small studies, promising)

Rescue Therapy

  • IV Ertapenem: 1g daily for 6 weeks (severe flares)
  • Consolidation: Moxifloxacin + Metronidazole + Rifampicin for 6 weeks

Surgical Management

  • Wide Excision: Gold standard - radical excision with reconstruction
  • Laser Therapy: CO2 laser or Nd:YAG laser options
  • Plastic Surgery Referral: Essential for extensive disease

Multidisciplinary Care

  • Dermatology: Primary management
  • Surgery: Excision & reconstruction
  • Pain Management: Chronic pain specialist input
  • Mental Health: Psychology/psychiatry support
  • Endocrinology: Metabolic syndrome management
‚ö°

Acute Flare Management

Immediate interventions for acute presentations

‚ñº
Acute

Immediate Actions

  • Oral Antibiotics: Start tetracycline immediately (Doxycycline 100mg BD)
  • Intralesional Steroids: Triamcinolone acetonide injection for painful nodules
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs ¬± opioids for severe pain
  • Incision & Drainage: Only if fluctuant abscess and patient systemically unwell

Supportive Measures

  • Warm Compresses: Promote drainage and comfort
  • Rest: Limit activities that cause friction
  • Hygiene: Gentle cleansing with antimicrobial washes
  • Pain Control: Adequate analgesia for quality of life
‚ö†Ô∏è
Important: Incision & drainage alone is NOT definitive treatment - recurrence is very common. Always plan ongoing management.

Monitoring Schedule

Follow-up frequency and assessments

‚ñº
Routine

Follow-up Intervals

  • Lesion Count: Nodules, abscesses, draining tracts
  • Photographs: Document disease extent

Complications

Potential long-term complications

‚ñº
Important

Local Complications

  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma: Risk in chronic lesions (especially perianal)
  • Lymphoedema: Chronic inflammation impairs lymphatic drainage
  • Contractures: Fibrosis limits mobility (especially axillary)
  • Anaemia: Chronic inflammation & blood loss
  • Local Infection: Including cellulitis & sepsis risk

Systemic Complications

  • Metabolic Syndrome: Obesity, diabetes, hypertension
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Increased MI & stroke risk
  • Inflammatory Arthritis: Spondyloarthropathy association
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: Crohn's disease association
  • Depression/Anxiety: High prevalence due to QoL impact

Screening Recommendations

  • Annual: Diabetes screening (HbA1c), Blood pressure
  • Symptomatic: Cardiovascular risk assessment
  • Regular: Mental health screening (PHQ-9)
  • Chronic Lesions: Monitor for malignant transformation

Treatment Response

Assessing efficacy and adjusting therapy

‚ñº
Key Metrics

HiSCR Criteria

  • Response: ‚â•50% reduction in total abscess & nodule count
  • No Increase: In abscess count or draining fistulas
  • Assessment: At Week 12 for biologics, Week 8-12 for antibiotics

Treatment Adjustment

  • Inadequate Response: Escalate to next treatment line
  • Partial Response: Consider combination therapy
  • Loss of Response: Re-evaluate adherence & triggers
  • Side Effects: Dose adjustment or switch therapy

Patient-Reported Outcomes

  • Pain Score: Should improve with effective treatment
  • DLQI: Quality of life improvement goal
  • Work Productivity: Assess impact on daily activities
  • Sleep Quality: Often impaired by pain & discharge

Patient Support

Resources and support strategies

‚ñº
Essential

Patient Education

  • Disease Nature: Chronic inflammatory condition, not infection
  • Expectations: Control rather than cure, realistic goals
  • Trigger Management: Weight, smoking, friction, stress
  • Medication Adherence: Importance of completing courses
  • Early Intervention: Seek care for flares promptly

Support Resources

  • Patient Support Groups: Hope for HS, HS Foundation
  • Online Resources: Verified medical information websites
  • Counseling: Psychology services for coping strategies
  • Occupational Therapy: Adaptations for work/daily activities
  • Pain Management: Chronic pain clinic referral

Lifestyle Counseling

  • Weight Management: Dietitian referral if needed
  • Smoking Cessation: Nicotine replacement & support
  • Clothing Advice: Loose, breathable fabrics
  • Skin Care: Gentle hygiene, avoid harsh products
  • Exercise: Low-impact activities to reduce friction
