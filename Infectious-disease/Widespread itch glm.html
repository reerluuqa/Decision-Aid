Overview

Scope

This CKS topic covers the diagnosis and management of chronic widespread itch which is not associated with an underlying skin disorder or rash, and chronic widespread itch of unknown cause.

Age Range: From 12 years onwards

Not Covered
  • Localized itch
  • Itch in pregnancy
  • Specific skin conditions (see separate CKS topics)
Related CKS Topics
Candida - female genital Chickenpox Eczema - atopic Psoriasis Scabies Urticaria Pruritus ani Pruritus vulvae

Background Information

Definition

Itch (pruritus) is a subjective symptom defined as a poorly localized, usually unpleasant sensation of the skin which elicits a desire to scratch. It may be:

  • Acute: Symptoms lasting less than 6 weeks
  • Chronic: Symptoms lasting more than 6 weeks
Causes
Common Causes of Widespread Itch
  • Dry skin (xerosis) - Common in elderly
  • Infection/infestation - Scabies, parasitic infections
  • Systemic disease (20-30% of cases):
    • Renal disease (ESRD)
    • Liver disease (cholestasis)
    • Haematological disorders
    • Endocrine disorders
    • Malignancy
  • Neurological - Spinal cord tumors, MS
  • Menopause
  • Iatrogenic - Opioids, statins, ACE inhibitors
  • Psychogenic - Functional itch disorder
  • Idiopathic - Unknown origin (8% of cases)
Prevalence
  • Point prevalence of chronic itch: 13.9-16.8%
  • Lifetime prevalence: 22.6-25.5%
  • Prevalence increases with age
Complications
  • Disturbed sleep
  • Anxiety and depression (up to one-third of patients)
  • Social isolation and embarrassment
  • Secondary scratch lesions (excoriations, lichenification)
  • Secondary bacterial infection

Diagnosis

Assessment
Key History Questions
  • Location, onset, timing, severity, duration
  • Nocturnal itch? (suggests uraemia, cholestasis, or psychogenic)
  • Sensory symptoms: prickling, crawling, burning
  • Impact on quality of life and psychological distress
  • Associated systemic symptoms (fatigue, fever, weight loss)
  • Medications (including OTC and herbal)
  • Alcohol intake and travel history
Physical Examination
  • Skin lesions, rash, signs of infestation
  • Secondary scratch marks and excoriations
  • Signs of dermographism
  • Skin color (jaundice, anemia)
  • Lymphadenopathy and/or hepatosplenomegaly
Initial Investigations
Recommended Blood Tests
  • Full blood count and blood film
  • Liver function tests
  • Renal function tests
  • Serum iron and ferritin levels
  • Inflammatory markers (ESR and/or CRP)

Consider additional tests based on clinical suspicion: Thyroid function, HbA1c, bone chemistry, HIV/hepatitis serology, chest X-ray

Management

Stepwise Management Approach
1
Identify and treat underlying cause

Manage known causes in primary care or refer to appropriate specialist

2
Self-care advice
  • Limit bathing (<20 min), use cool/lukewarm water
  • Avoid bubble baths, high pH soaps, perfumed products
  • Use emollient as soap substitute
  • Keep nails short, pat skin dry
  • Wear loose cotton clothing
  • Avoid spicy foods, alcohol, caffeine
3
Emollients

Use as soap substitute and moisturizer

4
Emollient with active ingredient

If inadequate: Try menthol 0.5-1% in aqueous cream

5
Non-sedating antihistamine (off-label)

If persistent: Cetirizine 10mg, loratadine 10mg, or fexofenadine 180mg for 2-3 weeks

6
Sedating antihistamine for nocturnal itch

Hydroxyzine 25mg at night (adults) or chlorphenamine 4mg at night (adults/children, off-label) for 2-3 weeks

7
Refer to dermatologist

If symptoms persist despite above measures

Not Routinely Recommended in Primary Care

Topical anaesthetics, antihistamines, crotamiton, calamine, capsaicin, corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, doxepin, and oral corticosteroids

When to Refer
  • Suspected serious underlying cause
  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Failure to respond to primary care management
  • Significant psychological distress

Prescribing Information

Cetirizine

Contraindications

  • End stage renal disease (eGFR <15 mL/min)

Cautions

  • Epilepsy
  • Renal impairment (adjust dose)

Dose Adjustment

  • eGFR 30-59: Half normal dose
  • eGFR 15-29: 5mg every 2 days
Loratadine

Cautions

  • Severe hepatic impairment (reduce dose frequency to alternate days)
Fexofenadine

Notes

  • No absolute contraindications or cautions
Hydroxyzine

Common Adverse Effects

  • Drowsiness
  • Dry mouth
  • Blurred vision
Chlorphenamine

Notes

  • Off-label use for itch
  • Caution with driving and alcohol
