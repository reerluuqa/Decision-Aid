Campylobacter Infection - Clinical Reference

Last updated: April 24, 2024
RED FLAGS: High fever, suspected bacteraemia, grossly bloody diarrhoea, symptoms >1 week, or immunocompromised status require immediate antimicrobial therapy.
POSITIVE OUTLOOK: Most cases are self-limited and resolve in 5-7 days with proper hydration.
KEY FACT: Campylobacter is the most commonly reported bacterial cause of acute gastroenteritis in developed countries.

Overview

Primary Cause
C. jejuni (80-90% of cases)
Secondary Cause
C. coli (less common)
Extraintestinal
C. fetus (immunocompromised)

One of the leading causes of acute diarrhoea worldwide and the most common bacterial cause of acute gastroenteritis in developed countries. Also a major cause of traveller's diarrhoea.

Key Symptoms & Red Flags

Symptom Frequency Notes
Diarrhoea Common Watery, 8-10 movements/day
Bloody diarrhoea 20-25% Inflammatory presentation
Abdominal pain Common More severe than other causes
Right lower quadrant pain Common May mimic appendicitis
Fever Common High fever = red flag
Prodrome 33% Headache, myalgias, chills

Risk Factors

Strong
Recent foreign travel, undercooked poultry, animal exposure, HIV infection
Weak
Untreated water, unpasteurised milk, PPI/H2 antagonist use

Diagnosis

Initial Tests
Other Tests
Stool Culture
Primary diagnostic method
Stool Microscopy
Shows RBCs and neutrophils
WBC Count
Often normal but may show left shift
Serology
For late complications
Blood Culture
For suspected bacteraemia
Intestinal Biopsy
Rarely needed

Treatment Algorithm

1
Rehydration
Essential for all patients. ORS for mild cases, IV fluids for severe dehydration.
2
Antibiotics (Only for indicated cases)
Azithromycin (Preferred)
Children: 10 mg/kg once daily (max 500mg)
Adults: 500 mg once daily
Duration: 3-5 days
Erythromycin (Alternative/Pregnancy)
Children: 30-50 mg/kg/day in 4 divided doses (max 2000mg)
Adults: 500 mg four times daily
Duration: 5 days
Fluoroquinolone Resistance Warning
28% of Campylobacter infections were resistant to ciprofloxacin in 2019. Use only when other antibiotics are inappropriate.
3
Adjunctive Therapy
Consider probiotics for immunocompetent patients and zinc supplementation for children in high-risk areas.

Complications

Post-infectious IBS: 9-13% of cases
Reactive Arthritis: 2-5% of cases
Guillain-Barre Syndrome: Rare but serious
Bacteraemia: Very rare, mainly in immunocompromised

Prevention

Cook meat to minimum internal temperature of 74°C (165°F)
Wash hands after handling raw meat
Avoid unpasteurised milk and untreated water
Maintain good kitchen hygiene standards
Hand washing for all people with diarrhoea, especially children
Keep infected infants away from nurseries until resolved
