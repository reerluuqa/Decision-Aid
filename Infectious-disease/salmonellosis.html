Salmonellosis

Non-typhoidal • Quick Scan Reference
Last Updated: Dec 05, 2023
BMJ Best Practice Source
Clinical Snapshot
Status: Usually Self-Limited
Primary Transmission: Ingestion (Poultry, Eggs, Raw Produce, Reptiles)
Incubation: 6–48 hours
Key Symptom: Gastroenteritis (Nausea, Vomiting, Diarrhoea, Cramps)
Diagnosis & Labs

Definitive: Stool Culture or Multiplex PCR (High sensitivity).

When to Test (Stool):

  • Symptoms > 7 days
  • Bloody/Mucoid stools OR Fever
  • Sepsis signs
  • Specific exposures (Raw milk, poultry, reptiles, travel)

Blood Cultures Required For:

  • Age < 3 months
  • Immunocompromised
  • Suspected Septicaemia/Enteric Fever
  • Haemolytic anaemia
High Risk Groups
Demographics:
  • < 12 months
  • > 50 years
Comorbidities:
  • HIV/AIDS
  • Immunosuppression
  • Sickle cell / Iron overload
  • Low gastric acidity

Note: These groups are at higher risk for invasive disease and severe complications.

Primary Management
Most cases are self-limited. Antibiotics are NOT routine.

1. Fluid & Electrolytes (Priority)

  • Mild/Moderate: Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS). Clear fluids.
  • Severe: IV Lactated Ringer's or Normal Saline.

2. Symptom Control

  • Anti-diarrheal: Loperamide ONLY for immunocompetent adults with watery diarrhoea. Avoid in bloody diarrhoea/fever.
  • Anti-emetic: Ondansetron (children >4 yrs) if vomiting interferes with ORS.
Antibiotic Criteria
!
DO NOT treat empirically with antibiotics in most cases. Prolongs carriage.

Indications for Treatment:

  • Infants < 3 months (suspected bacterial aetiology)
  • Adults > 50 years (prevent atherosclerosis seeding)
  • Immunocompromised (HIV, steroids, transplant)
  • Vascular/Prosthetic (Valves, grafts, joints)
  • Severe Sepsis or Systemic manifestations
View Regimens (Click)

Adults: Fluoroquinolone (e.g., Ciprofloxacin) OR Azithromycin. Check local resistance.

Infants <3mo: 3rd Gen Cephalosporin OR Azithromycin.

Note: Fluoroquinolones have black box warnings (tendon rupture, aortic dissection).

Differentials & Epidemiology
Condition Key Differentiator
Shigellosis Often bloody; faecal-oral contact.
Campylobacter Undercooked poultry; often bloody.
E. coli (EHEC) Bloody diarrhoea; DO NOT give antibiotics (risk of HUS).
Viral Gastro (Norovirus) Predominantly vomiting; short incubation.
Vibrio Raw shellfish; watery profuse diarrhoea.

Common Sources

  • Poultry (most common)
  • Raw / Undercooked Eggs
  • Unpasteurized Milk/Dairy
  • Raw Produce (Sprouts, Peanuts, Melons)
  • Contaminated Water

Animal Contact

  • Reptiles (Turtles, Lizards, Snakes) - High Risk
  • Live Poultry (Backyard flocks)
  • Pet food/treats

Epidemiology: Peak incidence in warmer months (May-Oct). Infants and elderly are most susceptible.

Definition

Positive stool or urine culture for Salmonella at 12 months or more post-acute illness.

Management

  • Prevalence: Rare (0.5%) compared to S. Typhi.
  • Risk Factors: Gallstones, Kidney stones, S. haematobium coinfection.
  • Treatment: Long-term antibiotics (Amoxicillin, TMP/SMX, Ciprofloxacin) based on sensitivity.
  • Surgery: Cholecystectomy if gallstones present and carriage persists.
