Assessment

Comprehensive evaluation of vaginal discharge

‚ñº

üìã Clinical History

Take a detailed history to differentiate physiological from pathological discharge:

  • Discharge characteristics: onset, duration, color, smell, consistency
  • Associated symptoms: itch, abdominal pain, dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding, fever
  • Exacerbating factors: post-intercourse, cyclical patterns
  • Previous treatments: prescription/OTC and their effects
  • Vaginal products: douches, deodorants, washes
  • Past medical & drug history: including contraceptive use

Women are at Increased Risk if they:

  • Have condomless sex with new/casual partners
  • Are younger than 25 years
  • Have had a new/multiple partners in last 12 months
  • Have had a previous STI
  • Are of Black ethnicity

Examination may be omitted and empirical treatment started if:

  • History indicates BV or candidiasis
  • STI risk is low
  • No symptoms of upper genital tract infection

If examination indicated:

  • Inspect vulva for discharge, lesions, inflammation
  • Speculum examination (avoid in pregnancy with low-lying placenta)
  • pH testing: normal < 4.5; BV/trichomoniasis ‚â• 4.5; candidiasis < 4.5
  • Bimanual examination if upper tract infection suspected

High vaginal swabs: Reserve for specific situations:

  • Symptoms/signs/pH inconsistent with diagnosis
  • Pregnancy, postpartum, post-abortion, post-surgery
  • Within 3 weeks of IUD insertion
  • Recurrent symptoms (‚â•4 episodes/year)
  • No/partial/poor response to treatment

STI screening: Offer if high-risk or symptoms suggestive:

  • Chlamydia, gonorrhoea, trichomoniasis, HIV, syphilis
  • Strongly recommend GUM clinic referral

Additional tests:

  • Urine pregnancy test
  • Urine dipstick for UTI

Diagnosis

Differentiate infective and non-infective causes

‚ñº

ü¶† Infective Causes

Bacterial Vaginosis Most Common
  • Offensive fishy-smelling discharge
  • Thin, grey/white homogenous discharge
  • 50% of women asymptomatic
  • No vulvovaginal inflammation
Vulvovaginal Candidiasis
  • Vulval itch predominant symptom
  • Non-offensive curdy discharge
  • Soreness, burning, superficial dyspareunia
  • Erythema, swelling, satellite lesions on examination
Chlamydia STI
  • Most common bacterial STI in UK
  • 70% of women asymptomatic
  • Increased discharge, post-coital/intermenstrual bleeding
  • Mucopurulent cervicitis on examination
Gonorrhoea STI
  • Up to 50% of women asymptomatic
  • Increased/altered vaginal discharge most common
  • Lower abdominal pain in 25%
  • Mucopurulent endocervical discharge
Trichomoniasis STI
  • Relatively rare in UK
  • 10-50% of women asymptomatic
  • Frothy yellow discharge (10-30% of cases)
  • Strawberry cervix in 2% of women
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease URGENT
  • Infection of upper genital tract
  • Bilateral lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia
  • Abnormal bleeding, purulent discharge
  • Fever, adnexal tenderness, cervical motion tenderness

üè• Non-Infective Causes

Inadequate Hygiene
  • Itching and malodour
  • Smegma in labial folds
  • History of frequent fungal infections
Retained Foreign Body
  • Foul-smelling or bloody discharge
  • Tampon, condom, or vaginal sponge
  • Longstanding cases may cause adhesions
Irritant/Allergic Vaginitis
  • Chemical irritants (soaps, tampons, condoms)
  • Latex, sperm, douching reactions
  • Symptoms resolve when irritant discontinued
Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause
  • Yellowish malodorous discharge
  • Itching, burning, dyspareunia
  • Pale, smooth, shiny vaginal epithelium
  • Patchy erythema, petechiae
Gynaecological Cancers URGENT
  • Abnormal discharge may be presenting symptom
  • Consider in appropriate clinical context
üö®
Red Flag Features Requiring Urgent Referral:
  • Suspected pelvic inflammatory disease with severe symptoms
  • Suspected gynaecological cancer
  • Continuous discharge suggesting fistula
  • Persistent ulceration or erosions

If no identifiable infective or non-infective cause:

  • Reassure likely physiological
  • Advise on basic hygiene and avoidance of feminine hygiene products
  • Recommend safe sexual practices
  • Provide patient information resources

Follow-up

Monitoring, referral, and ongoing care

‚ñº

üîÑ When to Seek Further Medical Help

Advise patients to return if:

  • Symptoms persist despite treatment
  • Symptoms recur after initial resolution
  • New symptoms develop (pain, bleeding, fever)
  • Symptoms worsen at any point

üè• Referral Criteria to GUM Clinic

Refer if:

  • Symptoms are persistent or recurrent
  • There is doubt about the cause of vaginal discharge
  • Microbiologically confirmed STI requiring partner notification
  • Complex or resistant cases
  • Need for specialist investigations

‚è∞ Review Timelines

  • PID: Review within 72 hours to ensure improvement
  • BV/Candidiasis: Review if symptoms persist after 7 days
  • STIs: Follow-up per GUM clinic protocol/test of cure
  • Physiological discharge: Reassess if symptoms change

üéì Patient Education & Prevention

  • Explain normal physiological discharge variations
  • Advise against douching and harsh feminine hygiene products
  • Promote safe sexual practices and condom use
  • Discuss STI prevention and regular screening
  • Provide information on recognizing abnormal symptoms
