Bronchiolitis Management Algorithm

Evidence-based clinical decision support for healthcare professionals

Initial Assessment
Clinical evaluation and risk stratification
Patient Profile
Age Range
• Typically under 2 years • Peak incidence: 3-6 months
Seasonal Pattern
• Winter months (Oct-Mar) • Annual 6-8 week epidemic
Clinical Onset
• 1-3 days coryzal symptoms • Progressive respiratory distress
Key Clinical Features
Diagnostic Criteria
Persistent cough AND (tachypnea OR chest recession) AND (wheeze OR crackles)
Required
Fever
Usually <39°C. Higher temperatures suggest alternative diagnosis
Caution
Red Flags
Apnea, marked recession, RR>70, central cyanosis, SpO2<92%
Urgent
Immediate Hospital Referral: Any red flag features require emergency assessment. Do not attempt home management.
Vital Signs & Observations
Respiratory Rate
• >60 breaths/min: Consider referral • >70 breaths/min: Urgent referral • Always consider age-appropriate norms
Oxygen Saturation
• <92%: Requires oxygen/urgent referral • 92-94%: Close monitoring • ≥95%: Generally acceptable
Hydration Status
• Monitor fluid intake (aim 50-75% normal) • Reduced urine output • Dry mucous membranes
Risk Factors for Severe Disease: Prematurity (<37 weeks), age <12 weeks, chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease, neurological disease, immunocompromise.
Diagnostic Process
Differential diagnosis and investigations
Diagnostic Confirmation
Clinical Diagnosis
• Based on history and examination • No specific tests required • Typical symptom constellation
Age Consideration
• Criteria apply to children <2 years • Rare <6 weeks (consider alternative)
Virology
• RSV in 60-80% of cases • Other viruses: hMPV, adenovirus, rhinovirus
Differential Diagnosis
Viral-Induced Wheeze
When to consider:
• Wheeze without crackles
• Episodic pattern
• Personal/family atopy
Pneumonia
When to consider:
• Temperature >39°C
• Focal chest signs
• Child appears toxic
Asthma
When to consider:
• Recurrent wheeze episodes
• Responsive to bronchodilators
• Typically >12 months
Foreign Body
When to consider:
• Sudden onset choking
• Unilateral findings
• Clear history
Investigations
Pulse Oximetry: Only investigation routinely recommended for bronchiolitis assessment.
Not Routine
• Chest X-ray
• Blood tests
• Blood gases
Selective Use
• Viral swabs (infection control)
• CXR (if pneumonia suspected)
• Tests if deterioration
Management & Treatment
Evidence-based management strategies
Primary Care Management
Most cases are mild and suitable for home management. Provide clear safety netting advice to parents.
Supportive Care
• Adequate fluid intake (>50% normal) • Antipyretics if fever causing distress • Nasal saline drops for congestion
Education
• Illness peaks at 3-5 days • Cough may persist 2-3 weeks • When to seek urgent help
Follow-up
• Review if not improving by day 5 • Daily weight check if possible • Clear escalation plan
Hospital Admission Criteria
Emergency
• Apneic episodes
• Severe respiratory distress
• Central cyanosis
• SpO2 <90% (≥6 weeks) or <92% (<6 weeks)
Immediate
Required
• Persistent SpO2 <92-94%
• RR >60 breaths/min
• Inadequate feeding (<50%)
• Clinical dehydration
Admission
Hospital Management
Oxygen Therapy
Target SpO2:
• ≥90% (≥6 weeks)
• ≥92% (<6 weeks or high risk)
Delivery: Nasal cannula, HFNC, or CPAP
Fluid Management
Options:
• NG feeding (preferred)
• IV fluids if NG not tolerated
• Monitor electrolytes
Airway Clearance
Upper Airway:
• Nasal suction before feeds
• Physiotherapy not routine
• Consider if secretions problematic
Respiratory Support
Escalation:
• HFNC commonly used
• CPAP if impending failure
• Intubation if refractory
Treatments NOT Recommended
Bronchodilators
Evidence: No benefit in oxygen saturations, time to resolution, or hospital stay
Corticosteroids
Evidence: Consistently failed to show benefit in clinical trials
Epinephrine
Evidence: No better than placebo for routine use
Antibiotics
Evidence: Minimal benefit except specific complications
Hypertonic Saline: Low-quality evidence suggests slight reduction in length of stay, but not routinely recommended.
Prognosis & Prevention
Long-term outcomes and preventive strategies
Prognosis
Typical Course
• Self-limiting illness (3-7 days) • Cough may persist up to 3 weeks • Full recovery expected
Severity Indicators
• More severe if <3 months or <32 weeks • Chronic lung/heart disease at risk • Mortality: ~70 deaths/year (England)
Long-term Risk
• Association with recurrent wheeze • Possible asthma link (causal uncertain) • Most have normal long-term outcomes
Prevention Strategies (Updated 2024)
RSV Vaccination
Target Groups:
• Pregnant women (week 28+)
• Adults aged 75-79 years
• Protects infants via transplacental immunity
Monoclonal Antibodies
High-Risk Infants:
• Nirsevimab (first-line)
• Palivizumab (alternative)
• Seasonal administration (from October)
High-Risk Groups for Immunoprophylaxis: Bronchopulmonary dysplasia, congenital heart disease, severe combined immunodeficiency.
Infection Control
Standard Precautions
• Hand washing before/after contact • Use gloves/aprons for direct contact • Patient isolation during epidemic periods
Transmission Reduction
• Minimize cross-contamination • Staff education on RSV transmission • Environmental cleaning protocols
Discharge Criteria: Clinically stable, adequate oral intake, maintaining target SpO2 for 4 hours (including sleep period).
Follow-up: Not routinely required if recovery is uncomplicated. Advise parents to seek review if symptoms worsen or fail to improve by day 5-7.
