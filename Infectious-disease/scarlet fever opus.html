üìñ What is Scarlet Fever?
  • Notifiable infectious disease caused by Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus)
  • Toxin-producing strains cause clinical manifestations
  • Typically causes sore throat, fever, and characteristic sandpaper rash
  • Highly contagious via aerosol transmission or direct contact
  • Usually mild and self-limiting - resolves within ~1 week
  • Recurrence unlikely after infection

  • Pregnant or postpartum (within 28 days)
  • Comorbidities: diabetes, CVD, malignancy, skin breakdown
  • Chickenpox with active lesions within 7 days
  • IV drug users / alcohol dependent

üî¥ Characteristic Rash
  • Onset: Within 48 hours of initial symptoms
  • Location: Starts chest/stomach ‚Üí spreads to body and flexures
  • Texture: Sandpaper-like (pathognomonic)
  • Appearance: Red, generalised, pinpoint (punctate)
  • Blanching: Yes - blanches on pressure
  • Spares: Palms and soles typically spared
  • Resolution: May peel (especially fingertips/toes)

üß™ When to Consider Throat Swab

Consider swab for GAS culture if:

  • Uncertainty about clinical diagnosis
  • Suspected outbreak case (HPT will advise)
  • True penicillin allergy (for susceptibility)
  • Regular contact with vulnerable people (e.g., healthcare workers)

Serology (ASO titres):

Not useful in acute infection

May help diagnose post-infection complications:

  • Acute rheumatic fever
  • Glomerulonephritis

‚ö° Invasive GAS (iGAS) - Life Threatening
  • Necrotizing fasciitis - rapidly spreading infection, severe pain out of proportion
  • Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome - hypotension, multi-organ failure
  • Sepsis - systemic inflammatory response
  • Meningitis - headache, neck stiffness, photophobia
  • Streptococcal pneumonia - respiratory distress
  • Cellulitis - spreading erythema, warmth, pain
Case Fatality Rate (UK 2021-22):

Overall: 10% | Age 75+: 18.1% | Age 15-44: 2.5%

‚è∞ Suppurative Complications (Early)
  • Acute sinusitis/mastoiditis
  • Otitis media
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy)
  • Retropharyngeal abscess

‚è≥ Non-Suppurative Complications (Later)

Occur 1-3+ weeks post-infection, especially if untreated

  • Acute post-strep glomerulonephritis
  • Acute rheumatic fever - carditis, valvular disease, arthritis

üè† Self-Care & Infection Control Advice

‚úÖ Advise To:

  • Use OTC paracetamol/ibuprofen for symptom relief
  • Drink adequate fluids
  • Clean and cover any skin breaks (wounds, bites, cuts)
  • Frequent effective handwashing
  • Avoid sharing utensils/towels

  • Avoid contact with high-risk individuals

üë• High-Risk Contacts

üí¨ Key Messages for Patients
  • Take full course of antibiotics even if feeling better
  • Contagious until 24h after starting antibiotics
  • Symptoms usually resolve within 1 week
  • Recurrence unlikely after having scarlet fever
  • Seek help if worsening or no improvement by day 7
üè† Self-Care Checklist

‚úÖ Do:

  • Stay home from school/work for at least 24 hours after starting antibiotics
  • Take paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain/fever (if no contraindications)
  • Drink plenty of fluids
  • Eat soft foods if swallowing is painful
  • Clean and cover any skin breaks, cuts, or wounds
  • Wash hands frequently (before eating, after toilet, after sneezing)
  • Dispose of tissues promptly

‚ùå Don't:

  • Share cups, utensils, or towels
  • Have close contact with vulnerable people (elderly, pregnant, newborns, immunocompromised)
  • Stop antibiotics early even if feeling better
üö®

Safety Netting - Seek Immediate Medical Advice If:

  • Symptoms getting worse
  • No improvement after 7 days
  • New symptoms develop
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Unable to swallow fluids
  • Very unwell or deteriorating
  • Signs of dehydration
  • Severe skin changes or spreading redness
üë• Advice for Household Contacts
  • Routine testing NOT recommended for asymptomatic contacts
  • Monitor for symptoms of GAS infection
  • High-risk contacts should seek immediate medical advice if symptoms develop
  • Good hygiene practices for all household members
  • Contact HPT if unsure about prophylaxis in high-risk situations
