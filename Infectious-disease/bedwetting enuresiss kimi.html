NICE Clinical Knowledge Summaries - Children & Young People (24 months to 19 years)
Last Revised: November 2024
ü©∫
Assessment
‚ñº
üí°
Key Principle: Determine type of bedwetting and identify red flags. Bedwetting is normal in children <5 years.
Determine Bedwetting Type
‚ñ∂
  • Ask about daytime symptoms: urgency, frequency (>7/day), daytime wetting, abdominal straining, poor stream, pain, infrequent voiding (<4/day)
  • Inquire about previous dryness: Have they been dry at night for 6 months? Secondary Bedwetting
  • For <5 years: Ask if daytime toilet training has been attempted
  • Include child in assessment: ask about their concerns and goals
Pattern Assessment
‚ñ∂
  • Frequency: How many nights/week and times/night?
  • Quantity of urine passed
  • Timing: When during night does wetting occur?
  • Wakefulness: Does child wake after wetting?
  • Sleep quality: Snoring or breathing pauses (possible OSA)
  • Consider 2-week diary of fluid intake, wetting, and toileting patterns
Investigations
‚ñ∂

Urinalysis NOT routine unless:

  • Bedwetting started in past few days/weeks
  • Daytime symptoms present
  • Signs of ill health
  • Suspected UTI or diabetes
‚ö†Ô∏è
Important: Dipstick tests can give false results due to contamination, storage issues, or technique.
Red Flags High Priority
‚ñ∂
  • Child Maltreatment Concerns:
    • Reported deliberate bedwetting
    • Punishment for bedwetting despite professional advice
    • Secondary daytime wetting persisting despite management
  • Suspected Type 1 Diabetes Same-Day Referral
  • Suspected Urinary Tract Infection
  • Signs of neurological problems (examine back for dimples, hairy patches)
  • Female Genital Mutilation associated with continence issues
Home & Family Assessment
‚ñ∂
  • Easy access to toilet at night?
  • Does child share bedroom? (affects alarm use)
  • Reason for consultation: reassurance, short-term, or long-term treatment?
  • Family's ability to participate in behavioral interventions
  • Parental coping and support needs
  • Assess for constipation (common cause in <5 years)
  • Fluid intake patterns (80% before 4pm recommended)

Diagnosis & Classification
‚ñº
üìã
DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria: Occurs ‚â•2x/week for 3 consecutive months OR causes significant distress/impairment
Primary Bedwetting Most Common
‚ñ∂

Definition: Child has never achieved sustained nighttime continence

1. WITHOUT Daytime Symptoms

  • No urgency, frequency, or daytime wetting
  • Most common type
  • Caused by: sleep arousal difficulties, nocturnal polyuria, bladder dysfunction

2. WITH Daytime Symptoms Referral Needed

  • Urgency, frequency, daytime wetting present
  • May indicate: overactive bladder, structural abnormalities, neurological disorders
  • Requires specialist assessment
Secondary Bedwetting Investigate Cause
‚ñ∂

Definition: Bedwetting occurs after ‚â•6 months of nighttime dryness

  • Underlying causes to investigate:
  • Constipation and/or soiling
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Urinary tract infection
  • Psychological/behavioral problems
  • Family stressors
  • Child maltreatment
Prevalence & Prognosis
‚ñ∂
  • Prevalence: 5-10% at age 7, 1-2% in adolescents (2:1 male predominance)
  • Spontaneous resolution: 5-10% per year
  • Prognosis: Most resolve by adolescence; 7% continue into adulthood if untreated
  • Impact: Emotional distress, social avoidance, behavioral problems, family stress

Management & Treatment
‚ñº
Scenario 1: Primary Bedwetting WITHOUT Daytime Symptoms
‚ñ∂

Children <5 Years Reassurance

  • Explain: NOT child's fault; due to urine volume > bladder capacity
  • Reassure: Common and usually resolves spontaneously
  • Advice:
    • Healthy diet, adequate fluids (don't restrict)
    • Avoid caffeine before sleep
    • Empty bladder regularly (4-7 times/day) and before bed
    • Use waterproof bedding protection
    • Easy toilet access at night
    • Consider 2-night trial without nappies if day-trained >6 months
  • Lifting/waking: Does NOT promote long-term dryness; only short-term practical measure
  • Positive reward systems: For dry nights, fluid intake, toileting (NOT for bedwetting)
  • NEVER punish for bedwetting

Children ‚â•5 Years

First-line: Enuresis Alarm Preferred

  • High long-term success rate (requires family commitment)
  • Assess response at 4 weeks:
    • No response ‚Üí stop treatment
    • Response ‚Üí continue until 2 weeks dry nights achieved
    • No dryness after 3 months ‚Üí reassess motivation/response
  • If no response: Consider alarm + desmopressin combination OR desmopressin alone

Alternative/Short-term: Desmopressin Rapid Action

  • Use when: rapid improvement needed (sleepovers), alarm inappropriate, or family preference
  • Start 1 week before event to test effectiveness
  • Initial dose: 200mcg (Desmotabs¬Æ) or 120mcg (DesmoMelt¬Æ) or 180mcg (Demovo¬Æ) at bedtime
  • If no dryness after 1-2 weeks ‚Üí increase to 400mcg/240mcg/360mcg
  • Assess at 4 weeks ‚Üí continue 3 months if response
  • Stop for 1 week after 3 months to check if dryness sustained
  • Repeated courses can be used
üíß
Fluid Restriction Required: Only sips from 1 hour before dose until 8 hours after (max 1 regular glass). Avoid swimming water ingestion and NSAIDs. Stop if vomiting/diarrhea.

Infrequent Bedwetting (<2x/week)

Wait-and-See Approach

  • May resolve without treatment
  • Offer reassurance and lifestyle advice
  • Review if frequency increases
Scenario 2: Primary Bedwetting WITH Daytime Symptoms Referral Required
‚ñ∂
  • Treat underlying cause first: constipation, UTI
  • Refer to secondary care/enuresis clinic if:
    • Daytime symptoms severe
    • Recurrent UTI history
    • Known/suspected physical/neurological problems
    • Developmental, attention, or learning difficulties
    • Behavioral/emotional problems
    • No identified underlying cause
  • Consider psychological expertise involvement
  • While <5 years is "normal", children >2 years with awareness but struggling may need assessment
Scenario 3: Secondary Bedwetting Investigate Cause
‚ñ∂
  • Assess for triggers: constipation, diabetes, UTI, psychological/family problems, maltreatment
  • Key questions:
    • When did bedwetting start? (recent onset = systemic illness or environment change)
    • Frequency and pattern of wetting
    • Associated symptoms or stressors
  • Same-day referral for suspected diabetes
  • Refer if: recurrent UTI, neurological problems, developmental issues, behavioral problems, no cause identified
  • If maltreatment suspected ‚Üí contact children's social care
Enuresis Alarm Details
‚ñ∂
  • Types:
    • Beside alarms: sensor pad under sheet, alarm box near bed
    • Body-worn alarms: sensor in pants, alarm on pyjama top
    • Vibrating alarms: for hearing impairment
  • How it works: Trains child to recognize bladder fullness, wake to toilet, eventually hold/spontaneously wake
  • Success requires: family commitment, training, sleep disruption initially, parental help waking
  • Response signs: smaller wet patches, fewer episodes/night, alarm triggers later, fewer wet nights
  • Positive reward system: for waking to alarm, going to toilet, resetting alarm (NOT for dry nights initially)
  • Relapse: Restart alarm; if multiple recurrences ‚Üí combination therapy
  • Availability: Cannot be prescribed on NHS; borrow from enuresis advisor or buy from ERIC (0845 370 8008)

Follow-up & Referral
‚ñº
Assessing Treatment Response
‚ñ∂

Enuresis Alarm

  • At 4 weeks: Assess for any response
    • Smaller wet patches
    • Fewer wetting episodes per night
    • Alarm triggers later
    • Fewer wet nights
  • No response: Stop treatment, consider alternatives
  • Response: Continue until 14 consecutive dry nights achieved
  • At 3 months: If not dry but improving and family motivated ‚Üí continue
  • If no improvement: Consider combination therapy or switch to desmopressin

Desmopressin

  • Assess at 4 weeks: Signs of response?
  • If response: Continue for 3 months, then 1-week withdrawal test
  • If partial response: Increase dose OR take 1-2 hours before bedtime OR continue for 6 months total
  • If no response: Stop OR try taking 1-2 hours before bedtime with strict fluid restriction
  • Long-term use: Regular reviews, no routine blood tests needed
Managing Recurrence Common
‚ñ∂

After Alarm Success

  • First recurrence: Restart alarm treatment
  • Multiple recurrences: Offer combination alarm + desmopressin

After Desmopressin Success

  • Prescribe another course (repeated courses allowed)
  • Consider gradual withdrawal over 8 weeks (increase "no-medication" days)
  • Test dryness with 1-week withdrawal every 3 months
  • Alternative: Switch to enuresis alarm if now appropriate
Referral Criteria Key Thresholds
‚ñ∂

Refer to secondary care/enuresis clinic if:

  • Not responded to ‚â•2 complete courses of treatment (alarm, desmopressin, or combination)
  • Primary bedwetting WITH daytime symptoms (from presentation)
  • Suspected underlying disease (neurological, structural, diabetes)
  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Developmental, attention, or learning difficulties
  • Behavioral or emotional problems requiring psychological expertise
  • Family/social factors complicating management
  • Parents/carers unable to manage in primary care

Same-Day Referral: Suspected Type 1 Diabetes - arrange immediate referral to multidisciplinary pediatric diabetes team
Special Considerations
‚ñ∂
  • Cystic Fibrosis: Desmopressin requires caution (electrolyte alterations)
  • Sickle Cell Disease: Desmopressin requires specialist advice (reduced kidney concentrating ability)
  • Behavioral Disorders: May not comply with fluid restrictions
  • Learning Difficulties: Consider maturity and understanding when choosing treatment
  • Family Stress: Address psychosocial factors concurrently
