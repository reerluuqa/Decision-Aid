Management Pathways

Scenario: Uncomplicated SSTI or UTI

No systemic features. Confirmed or suspected MRSA.

Microbiology Local Guidelines
  • Discuss: Treatment with microbiologist/local team.
  • Do NOT: Routinely treat with oral/topical antibiotics unless directed.
  • Follow-up: Review after 48 hours.
Scenario: Asymptomatic Carrier

Found to be carrier, no infection signs.

  • General Rule: Decolonization NOT routinely required in community.
  • Exception: May be indicated if risk factors (wound, device, care home resident).
  • Action: Discuss suppression therapy with infection control team.
Patient Advice:

Colonization does not present risk to healthy people. Keep wounds covered. Avoid skin-to-skin contact sports until healed.

Definitions & Background

What is MRSA?

Gram-positive bacterium resistant to beta-lactams (e.g., flucloxacillin). Colonization means carrying the bacteria with no symptoms (common sites: nose, skin, gut). Infection requires treatment (e.g., sepsis, boils, pneumonia).

Transmission

Direct contact with infected skin or contaminated objects (towels, razors). MRSA enters tissue via cuts or wounds.

Complications

  • Skin/Soft Tissue (Boils, cellulitis)
  • Urinary Tract Infections
  • Pneumonia
  • Sepsis / Toxic Shock Syndrome
  • Infective Endocarditis

Prognosis

Depends on severity, patient age, and comorbidities. MRSA bacteraemia 30-day mortality has decreased (approx 22.5% in 2022/23) but remains significant.

Infection Prevention Checklist

  • Decontaminate hands immediately before & after contact
  • Patient educated on safe device management
  • MRSA status documented in transfer notes
  • UKHSA mandatory reporting completed
