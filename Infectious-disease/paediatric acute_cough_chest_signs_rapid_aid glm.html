Acute Cough with Chest Signs in Children

Clinical guidance for primary healthcare professionals

Age: 1 month to 5 years

Red Flags - Immediate Referral Required

Any child with life-threatening features: PEFR <33% best/predicted, SpO₂ <92%, altered consciousness, exhaustion, cyanosis, silent chest
Severe respiratory distress: marked chest recession, use of accessory muscles, grunting
Apnoea or pauses in breathing
Signs of severe dehydration (sunken eyes, no tears, poor skin turgor)
High-risk child: premature birth (<32 weeks), chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease, neuromuscular disorders, immunodeficiencyThese conditions increase risk of severe disease and complications

Viral-Induced Wheeze

Affects children 6 months to 5 years. Wheeze associated with respiratory tract infection. Usually resolves by age 6.

Key Features
  • Wheeze associated with infection only
  • No history of wheeze between infections
  • Usually responds to bronchodilators

Bronchiolitis

Predominantly affects infants <12 months, peak 3-6 months. Usually self-limiting.

Key Features
  • Coryzal prodrome 1-3 days
  • Persistent cough
  • Tachypnoea or chest recession
  • Wheeze or crackles on auscultation

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Acute infection of lung parenchyma. Can be bacterial or viral.

Key Features
  • High fever (>39°C) and/or cyanosis
  • Age-specific increased respiratory rate
  • Signs of increased work of breathing
  • Focal crackles

Asthma Exacerbation

Wheezing due to bronchoconstriction in response to respiratory infection.

Key Features
  • Previous asthma diagnosis
  • History of wheeze without infection
  • Triggers other than infection (exercise, allergens)
  • Often associated with atopy or family history

Red Flags - Immediate Referral Required

Life-threatening features: PEFR <33% best/predicted, SpO₂ <92%, altered consciousness, exhaustion, cyanosis, silent chest
Severe features persisting after initial bronchodilator treatment
Previous near-fatal asthma attack

Management Approach

Assess severity - Use PEFR, respiratory rate, pulse rate, ability to speak, oxygen saturation
For severe or life-threatening attacks: Admit to hospital
For moderate attacks: Consider home management if improving after initial treatment
For mild attacks: Home management with short-acting beta-2 agonist

Severity Assessment

Moderate
  • PEFR ≥50% best/predicted
  • Normal speech (if applicable)
  • No acute severe or life-threatening features
Acute Severe
  • PEFR <50% best/predicted
  • Respiratory rate: ≥25/min (>12y), ≥30/min (5-12y), ≥40/min (2-5y)
  • Pulse rate: ≥110/min (>12y), ≥125/min (5-12y), ≥140/min (2-5y)
  • Inability to complete sentences in one breath
  • Accessory muscle use
  • Inability to feed
  • SpO₂ ≥92%
Life-Threatening
  • PEFR <33% best/predicted
  • SpO₂ <92%
  • Altered consciousness
  • Exhaustion
  • Cardiac arrhythmia
  • Hypotension
  • Cyanosis
  • Poor respiratory effort
  • Silent chest
  • Confusion

Treatment Options

For Severe/Life-Threatening
  • Controlled supplementary oxygen to maintain SpO₂ 94-98%
  • Nebulized salbutamol: 5mg (>5y) or 2.5mg (2-5y)
  • Repeat every 20-30 minutes or use continuous nebulization
  • Consider oral steroids (prednisolone 1-2mg/kg)
  • Admit to hospital
For Moderate
  • Short-acting beta-2 agonist via spacer (4-10 puffs)
  • Consider oral steroids (prednisolone 1-2mg/kg)
  • Reassess after initial treatment
  • Admit if no improvement or worsening
For Mild
  • Short-acting beta-2 agonist via spacer (2-4 puffs)
  • Consider oral steroids if symptoms persist
  • Safety-net advice

Safety Net Advice

Return if breathing becomes more difficult
Return if reliever inhaler needed more frequently than every 4 hours
Return if symptoms worsen or don't improve after 2-3 days

Red Flags - Immediate Referral Required

SpO₂ <90% (changed from 92% in 2021 update)
Apnoea or pauses in breathing
Severe respiratory distress: marked chest recession, grunting, nasal flaring
Signs of severe dehydration
High-risk infant: premature (<32 weeks), chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease, neuromuscular disorders, immunodeficiencyThese conditions increase risk of severe disease and complications

Management Approach

Supportive care is the mainstay of treatment
Antibiotics are not recommended unless there is clear evidence of secondary bacterial infection
Most cases are self-limiting and can be managed at home with appropriate safety-net advice

Diagnosis

Typical Features
  • Age <2 years (peak 3-6 months)
  • Coryzal prodrome 1-3 days
  • Persistent cough
  • Tachypnoea or chest recession
  • Wheeze or crackles on auscultation
  • Fever in ~30% of cases (usually <39°C)
  • Poor feeding (typically after 3-5 days)

Home Management

Advice for Parents
  • Continue feeding (small frequent feeds)
  • Ensure adequate fluid intake
  • Monitor breathing and alertness
  • Use saline nasal drops before feeds if needed
  • Keep child comfortable
  • Avoid exposure to tobacco smoke

Treatments Not Recommended

What to Avoid
  • Antibiotics: Not effective for viral bronchiolitis
  • Bronchodilators: Not routinely recommended (may help in some cases but evidence is limited)
  • Corticosteroids: Not effective
  • Chest physiotherapy: Not recommended
  • Antihistamines/decongestants: Not effective and may cause adverse effects

Safety Net Advice

Return if breathing becomes more difficult or fast
Return if child is unable to take adequate fluids
Return if child becomes unusually sleepy or difficult to rouse
Return if symptoms worsen or don't improve after 5-7 days

Red Flags - Immediate Referral Required

SpO₂ <95% in air
Cyanosis
Severe respiratory distress
Signs of sepsis or shock
Complications: pleural effusion, empyema, lung abscess

Management Approach

Assess severity to determine if hospital admission is required
Consider antibiotics - bacterial pneumonia requires antibiotic treatment
Provide supportive care - antipyretics, fluids, rest

Diagnosis

Typical Features
  • High fever (>39°C) and/or cyanosis
  • Age-specific increased respiratory rate:
    • >60/min (0-5 months)
    • >50/min (6-12 months)
    • >40/min (>12 months)
  • Signs of increased work of breathing
  • Persistently focal crackles
  • Absent breath sounds with dull percussion (possible effusion)

Admission Criteria

When to Admit
  • SpO₂ <95% in air
  • Signs of severe respiratory distress
  • Inability to maintain hydration orally
  • Signs of sepsis or shock
  • Complications (pleural effusion, etc.)
  • Social concerns or inability to monitor at home
  • Failure to respond to oral antibiotics
  • Age <3 months

Antibiotic Treatment

First-line Options
  • Low severity (managed at home):
    • Amoxicillin 40-90mg/kg/day in 2 divided doses for 5 days
    • Consider higher dose (90mg/kg/day) if concern about resistance
  • If penicillin allergy:
    • Clarithromycin 7.5mg/kg twice daily for 5 days
    • Or doxycycline (for children >8 years) 2mg/kg twice daily on day 1, then 2mg/kg once daily for 4 days
  • High severity (hospitalized):
    • Intravenous amoxicillin
    • Consider adding macrolide if atypical pneumonia suspected

Safety Net Advice

Return if breathing becomes more difficult
Return if fever persists for >48 hours after starting antibiotics
Return if child becomes increasingly unwell
Return if unable to complete antibiotic course due to vomiting
