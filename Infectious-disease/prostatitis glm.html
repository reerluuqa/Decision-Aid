Prostatitis - Acute

Management of acute bacterial prostatitis in primary care.

Revised: June 2024

Have I got the right topic? Age 18+

✓ Covered

  • Acute bacterial prostatitis
  • Men aged 18+ years
  • Severe systemic infection management

✗ Not Covered

  • Chronic prostatitis
  • Prostatitis caused by STIs
  • Uncomplicated UTIs
  • Pyelonephritis (see separate topic)

Immediate Admission Required

RED FLAG Admit to hospital immediately if the patient presents with any of the following:

  • Signs of Sepsis: Rigors, tachycardia, hypotension.
  • Severe Symptoms: Systemically very unwell.
  • Acute Urinary Retention: Inability to void.
  • Unable to take oral antibiotics: Vomiting, dysphagia.

Management Pathway

Consider Urgent Referral Immunocompromised, Diabetes Mellitus, or Pre-existing Urological condition (e.g., BPH, indwelling catheter).
First Line Antibiotics (14 days)
Ciprofloxacin: 500 mg twice daily
OR Ofloxacin: 200 mg twice daily

Alternative (if Fluoroquinolone inappropriate): Trimethoprim 200 mg twice daily for 14 days.

Second Line (Seek Specialist Advice): Levofloxacin 500 mg once daily OR Co-trimoxazole 960 mg twice daily (only if bacteriological evidence of sensitivity).

Patient Advice & Safety Netting

  • Analgesia: Paracetamol (+/- codeine) or Ibuprofen.
  • Hydration: Drink plenty of fluids.
  • Follow-up: Review within 48 hours. Check MSU results.
  • Safety Alert (Fluoroquinolones): Advise to stop at first signs of tendon pain, muscle weakness, joint pain, or neuropathy. Risk of disabling side effects (1 in 10,000).

Diagnosis

Key Symptoms

  • UTI Symptoms: Dysuria, frequency, urgency.
  • Prostatic Pain: Perineal, penile, or rectal pain.
  • Systemic: Fever, rigors, malaise.
  • Obstructive: Hesitancy, weak stream, acute retention.

Investigations

  • MSU: For dipstick, culture, and sensitivity.
  • Bloods: FBC, Blood Cultures (if systemic signs).
  • DRE: Tender, swollen, boggy prostate. Perform gently.
  • STI Screen: If risk factors present.

Differential Diagnosis

BPH
Gradual reduction in flow, nocturia.
Epididymo-orchitis
Scrotal pain/swelling, dysuria.
Prostate Cancer
Similar to BPH, hard irregular prostate.

Prescribing Information

MHRA Safety Update: Systemic fluoroquinolones only when other antibiotics inappropriate. Risk of tendonitis, tendon rupture, and aortic aneurysm.

Cautions & Contra-indications

  • DO NOT prescribe: History of tendon disorders with quinolone use, previous serious adverse reactions, taking corticosteroids.
  • Caution: Epilepsy (lower seizure threshold), Diabetes (affect BG), G6PD deficiency, Myasthenia gravis, Aortic aneurysm history.

Adverse Effects

  • Common: Nausea, diarrhoea.
  • Rare/Severe: Tendon rupture (stop immediately), tendonitis, CNS effects (convulsions), peripheral neuropathy.

Background

Definition

Severe, potentially life-threatening bacterial infection of the prostate accompanied by UTI. Caused by urinary pathogens (E. coli, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella).

Prognosis & Complications

  • Response: Usually responds well to antibiotics.
  • Risk: 10% develop chronic prostatitis.
  • Complications: Sepsis, Prostatic abscess, Acute urinary retention, Epididymitis.
