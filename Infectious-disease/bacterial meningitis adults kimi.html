Red Flag Recognition

Strongly suspect bacterial meningitis in adults with ALL of the following:
  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Neck stiffness
  • Altered level of consciousness or cognition (confusion/delirium)
‚ö° Red Flag Combination

Classic triad (fever, neck stiffness, altered mental status) occurs in only 41-51% of patients, but the red flag combination indicates strong suspicion.

Sepsis and Life-Threatening Priorities

Think "Could this be sepsis?" FIRST

Cardiorespiratory arrest, suspected sepsis, meningococcal disease, and shock take priority over 'uncomplicated' bacterial meningitis.

‚è±Ô∏è Immediate Action Required

Antibiotics must start within 1 hour of hospital arrival. Do not delay antibiotics for investigations.

ABCDE Assessment

Detailed ABCDE Approach
  • Airway: Signs of obstruction
  • Breathing: Respiratory rate, oxygen saturation
  • Circulation: Pulse, capillary refill time, blood pressure
  • Disability: Glasgow Coma Scale (<9 = urgent), focal signs, seizures, papilloedema
  • Exposure: Petechial/purpuric rash ( meningococcal)

Investigations & Diagnosis

üß™

Blood Cultures

Take within 1 hour; don't delay antibiotics

Take blood for culture ideally before antibiotics but do not delay treatment. Even if antibiotics started in community, still take cultures.

Critical Investigation
üíâ

Lumbar Puncture

Perform before antibiotics when possible, but do not delay antibiotics >1 hour for LP.

Minimum CSF volume: 15 mL

LP Contraindications - DO NOT PERFORM
Absolute Contraindications
  • Shock or sepsis
  • Severe respiratory/cardiac compromise
  • Confusion and signs of raised ICP
  • Known coagulopathy
  • Infection at LP site
  • Extensive or rapidly spreading purpura
‚ö†Ô∏è Check for papilloedema, focal deficits, GCS <9

Differential Diagnosis

Key Differentials to Consider
  • Viral meningitis: CSF lymphocytic, normal glucose, normal lactate
  • TB meningitis: Low grade fever, weight loss, cranial nerve palsies, low CSF glucose
  • Fungal meningitis: Subacute onset, immunocompromise
  • Encephalitis: Altered behavior, speech, motor function
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Sudden severe headache, often no fever

Empirical Antibiotics

START WITHIN 1 HOUR - Do not delay for investigations

Typical regimen: IV ceftriaxone 2g q12h OR cefotaxime 2g q6h

  • Alcohol misuse

Add IV vancomycin (15-20 mg/kg q8-12h) if:

  • Recent travel from area with resistant pneumococci
  • Known colonization with resistant pneumococcus

Dexamethasone

Start with or just before antibiotics: IV dexamethasone 10mg q6h for 4 days

Continue only if organism identified as:
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Haemophilus influenzae

Airway & Respiratory Support

Intubation Indications (GCS <12)
  • Inability to maintain airway patency
  • Inability to protect against aspiration
  • Failure to oxygenate (SpO2 <92% on O2)
  • Persistent seizures

Oxygen target: SpO2 94-96% (88-92% if CO2 retainer)

Contact Management & Prevention

Prophylaxis Guidelines

Haemophilus influenzae type b:

  • Report cases to public health
  • Rifampicin prophylaxis for household contacts (initiated by health protection team)
  • Hib vaccination for unvaccinated household contacts <10 years

Pneumococcus:

  • Contact management not usually indicated
  • Discuss clusters in care homes with health protection
üíâ Vaccinate all at-risk groups

Prognosis Factors

Poor Prognosis Indicators
  • Advanced age
  • Low Glasgow Coma Scale score
  • Tachycardia
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Positive blood culture
  • Delay in antibiotic administration

Case fatality rates:

  • Overall: 18% (modern era)
  • Pneumococcal: 24%
  • Neisseria meningitidis: 9%
  • Haemophilus influenzae: 11%
  • Listeria monocytogenes: 27%
