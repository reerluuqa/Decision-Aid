Acute Prostatitis

CKS Topic Reference
Last Revised: June 2024

Immediate Action Required

Admit to hospital immediately if: Sepsis, Acute Urinary Retention, Prostatic Abscess, or unable to take oral antibiotics.

Quick Decision Helper

Assess the patient to generate an instant management plan.

Diagnosis & Suspecting Clinical Assessment

Key Symptoms

  • UTI Signs: Dysuria, frequency, urgency.
  • Prostate Pain: Perineal, penile, or rectal pain.
  • Systemic: Fever, rigors, malaise.
  • Obstruction: Acute retention, weak stream.

Required Investigations

  • MSU: Dipstick, culture & sensitivity.
  • Bloods: FBC, Blood Cultures (if septic).
  • DRE: Gentle exam only (Tender/Boggy prostate).
    Do NOT perform vigorous massage.

Fluoroquinolone Safety Warnings

Must only be prescribed when other antibiotics are inappropriate.

Contraindications & Cautions
  • Do NOT prescribe: History of tendon disorders related to quinolones, previous serious adverse reactions, taking corticosteroids.
  • Caution: Epilepsy, Diabetes (glucose monitoring), Myasthenia Gravis, over 60s (tendon risk), history of aneurysm (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, etc.).
Patient Advice: Stop & Contact Doctor Immediately

Instruct patient to stop treatment at the first signs of:

  • Tendonitis or tendon rupture
  • Muscle pain, weakness, or joint swelling
  • Peripheral neuropathy (tingling/numbness)
  • CNS effects (confusion, hallucinations, depression)
Drug Interactions
  • Antacids/Iron/Zinc: Separate administration by 2 hours.
  • Theophylline: Increased levels (monitor).
  • NSAIDs: Increased seizure risk.
  • Warfarin: Enhanced anticoagulant effect (monitor INR).
