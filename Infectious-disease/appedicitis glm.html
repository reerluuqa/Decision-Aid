Differential Diagnosis

Condition Distinguishing Feature
Gastroenteritis Diarrhoea prominent, pain usually diffuse/cramping, vomiting often precedes pain.
UTI / Pyelonephritis Dysuria, frequency, loin pain. (Note: Appendicitis can cause pyuria).
Ectopic Pregnancy Amenorrhoea, vaginal bleeding, cervical motion tenderness. Rule out with pregnancy test.
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease Vaginal discharge, cervical excitation, bilateral pain often.
Mesenteric Adenitis Often preceded by viral URTI, pain diffuse, generalized lymphadenopathy.
Renal Colic Sudden onset, severe colic, radiating to groin, haematuria.

High Risk / Atypical

Pregnant Women Appendix displaced by uterus. May present with RUQ or flank pain. Nausea/vomiting easily mistaken for pregnancy symptoms. High risk of fetal loss.
Elderly (>65 yrs) Minimal pain/fever common. May present with confusion or shock. High mortality/morbidity if delayed. Sclerosis/fibrosis increases perforation risk.
Infants & Young Children Rare < 2 years. Non-specific pain, anorexia, withdrawn. Unable to articulate symptoms clearly. High perforation rate.
Signs of Sepsis/Shock Tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental state. Immediate admission required.

Primary Care Tests

  • Pregnancy Test
    Mandatory in females of childbearing age to exclude ectopic pregnancy.
  • Urine Dipstick
    Exclude UTI.
    Note: 40% of appendicitis cases have leucocytes in urine due to ureteric irritation.
  • Bloods (FBC / CRP)
    Neutrophil-predominant leucocytosis (80-90%). High CRP supports diagnosis but normal CRP does not exclude it.

Background & Definition (Expand for details)

Definition

Acute inflammation of the appendix. Usually caused by luminal obstruction (faecolith, lymphoid hyperplasia) leading to distension, ischemia, and potential perforation.

Risk Factors

  • Age: 10-30 years (peak 15-19).
  • Genetics: Family history (3x risk).
  • Sex: Slightly more common in males.
  • Low fiber diet / Smoking.

Complications

  • Perforation: Occurs in 16-40% of cases. Risk increases with delay.
  • Appendix Mass/Abscess: Walled off infection.
  • Generalized Peritonitis: Life-threatening.
  • Bowel Obstruction: Due to adhesions.

Note: Recurrence after non-surgical management is approx. 23%. Risk of bowel cancer may be elevated if managed non-operatively.
