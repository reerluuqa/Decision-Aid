Whitlow Management Algorithm

Clinical Decision Support Tool for Staphylococcal & Herpetic Whitlow

Assessment

Initial evaluation and differentiation

‚ñº

History

  • Preceding injury or trauma
  • Onset and progression of symptoms
  • Previous episodes
  • Occupation (healthcare worker?)
  • Immunocompromised status
  • Contact with oral/genital herpes lesions

Key Features - Staphylococcal

  • History: Penetrating injury or untreated paronychia
  • Pain: Rapid onset, severe, throbbing
  • Exam: Redness, tense swelling of distal pulp
  • Signs: Fluctuance may be present, no vesicles
  • Systemic: Usually absent
Urgent drainage if tense/fluctuant

Key Features - Herpetic

  • History: No injury, recent herpetic lesions
  • Prodrome: Pain and paraesthesia for days
  • Exam: Oedema, redness, severe pain but soft pulp
  • Signs: Vesicles with clear/cloudy fluid
  • Systemic: Fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy possible
DO NOT INCISE

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Immunosuppression (HIV, steroids)
  • Healthcare workers (dental, GPs)
  • IV drug use
  • Farm workers
  • Thumb-sucking children

Diagnosis

Confirm diagnosis & rule out differentials

Diagnostic Differentiation

Feature Staphylococcal Herpetic
Common fingers Thumb & index Thumb & index (multiple possible)
Injury Usually present Absent
Pulp feel Tense, boggy Soft, oedematous
Vesicles Absent Present (clear/cloudy)
Systemic signs Uncommon Fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy

Differential Diagnosis

  • Cellulitis - more diffuse swelling
  • Acute paronychia - nail fold involvement
  • Subungual haematoma - history of trauma
  • Gout - tophi, crystal analysis
  • Osteomyelitis - X-ray changes
  • Orf virus - farm animal contact
  • Skin cancer - atypical lesions
Consider X-ray if history of trauma Swab if uncertain diagnosis

Treatment

Scenario-based management

‚ñº

Staphylococcal Whitlow

Admit if systemic features (fever, tachycardia) Same-day incision & drainage if tense/fluctuant

Urgent Criteria:

  • Fever, tachycardia, signs of shock
  • Ascending lymphangitis
  • Rapid spread
  • Immunocompromised patient

Antibiotic Therapy:

  • First line: Flucloxacillin 250-500mg QDS (5-7 days)
  • Penicillin allergy: Erythromycin 250-500mg QDS or Clarithromycin 250-500mg BD
  • Pregnancy: Erythromycin
  • MRSA: Seek specialist advice
Swab if recurrent, immunosuppressed, or treatment failure

Supportive Care:

  • Elevate finger
  • Moist heat 3-4 times daily
  • Paracetamol/ibuprofen for pain
  • Tetanus prophylaxis if needed

Supportive Care:

  • Paracetamol/ibuprofen for pain
  • Clean, dry dressing to prevent transmission
  • Avoid touching eyes, remove contact lenses
  • Discourage thumb-sucking in children

Patient Education:

  • Infection is self-limiting (~3 weeks)
  • Recurrence is common (50%)
  • Offer HIV testing if extensive
  • Manage underlying oral/genital herpes
Swab for virology if diagnosis uncertain

Follow-up

Monitoring & recurrence prevention

‚ñº

When to Seek Urgent Review

Worsening pain or swelling Becoming systemically unwell No improvement after 48-72 hours Signs of necrosis or tendon involvement

Complications to Monitor

  • Staphylococcal: Flexor tenosynovitis, osteomyelitis, tissue necrosis, septic arthritis
  • Herpetic: Ocular spread, scarring, lymphoedema (rare), recurrent episodes

Prevention Strategies

  • Avoid fingertip injuries
  • Proper hand hygiene
  • Wear gloves when in contact with oral secretions
  • Manage underlying herpes infections
  • Control diabetes
Good prognosis if treated early

Recurrent Infections

  • Take swabs for culture
  • Consider underlying immunosuppression
  • Review risk factors
  • For herpetic: antivirals effective if started early
