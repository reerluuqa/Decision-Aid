Whitlow Management

Staphylococcal & Herpetic • NICE CKS Feb 2025

URGENT ADMISSION REQUIRED

If patient has systemic features (fever, tachycardia, shock), lymphadenopathy, or ascending lymphangitis.

Staphylococcal Whitlow (Felon)

Bacterial
Key Diagnosis Signs
  • History of penetrating injury or paronychia.
  • Rapid onset of severe, throbbing pain.
  • Tense swelling/redness of entire distal pulp.
  • Fluctuance (feels 'boggy').
  • No vesicles.
Management
Incision & Drainage (I&D)

Arrange same-day I&D if tense or fluctuant. Refer if primary care lacks expertise.

Antibiotics

If no I&D indicated:

1st Line:

Flucloxacillin

Alternative (Penicillin Allergy):

Erythromycin or Clarithromycin

Duration: 5–7 days (Update Feb 2025)

Advise Patient

Elevation, moist heat 3-4x/day. Return if worsening or fluctuant.

Herpetic Whitlow

Viral (HSV)
Key Diagnosis Signs
  • No history of injury often.
  • Prodrome of pain/tingling/paraesthesia.
  • Vesicles present (fluid-filled, may be cloudy/bloody).
  • Pulp space is soft, not tensely swollen.
  • Associated with oral/genital herpes lesions.
Management
CRITICAL WARNING

DO NOT perform Incision & Drainage.

I&D is contraindicated and may cause secondary infection/systemic spread.

Antivirals

Consider if presented within 48 hours of onset:

1st Line:

Aciclovir

(Can be used for recurrent episodes if early).

Advise Patient
  • Cover with clean dry dressing (prevent transmission).
  • Avoid touching eyes/contact lenses.
  • Avoid sucking thumb (children).
  • Pain relief (Paracetamol/Ibuprofen).

Quick Differential Comparison

Feature Staphylococcal (Felon) Herpetic Whitlow
Onset Rapid, very severe throbbing pain Prodrome (tingling), then abrupt edema
Appearance Tense swelling, fluctuant (boggy), NO vesicles Soft pulp, clear/cloudy vesicles present
Cause Bacteria (S. aureus), trauma Virus (HSV-1/2), autoinoculation
Primary Treatment Incision & Drainage + Antibiotics Antivirals (if early) + NO Incision
