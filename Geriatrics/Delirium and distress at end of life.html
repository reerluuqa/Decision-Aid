Agitation, Restlessness & Delirium

⚠️ Why This Matters

Agitation is distressing to relatives and is often misinterpreted as severe pain.

If relatives do not understand this is a common preterminal event, it leads to the "Destructive Triangle":

  • Worsening clinical care
  • Lasting distress after death
  • Inappropriate interventions
Patient
Agitation
Family
Distress
Staff
Distress
Action: Explain clearly that this is NOT unbearable pain, but a symptom of the dying process. This reduces relatives' distress significantly.
🔍

Investigation & Diagnosis

Step 1 Identify the Cause

Think Delirium First: Agitation and restlessness are often seen in delirium (hyperactive or mixed).

Consider Agitation WITHOUT Delirium:

  • Pain: Including urinary retention or faecal impaction.
  • Emotional Distress: Anxiety, fear, or existential suffering.
  • Withdrawal: Alcohol or drug withdrawal (incl. nicotine).

Mnemonic PINCH ME

Common causes of delirium in terminal care:

P - Pain
I - Infection
N - Nutrition (Dehydration/Electrolytes)
C - Constipation
H - Hypoxia
M - Medications
E - Environment (Emotional)
Red Whale Note: Don't forget normal changes like Cheyne-Stokes breathing or secretions—ensure families know these are normal parts of dying.
🛠️

Management Goals

When death is imminent, a balance is needed between correcting underlying issues and managing symptoms. Invasive investigations are usually not indicated.

Immediate Checklist

Non-Pharmacological

  • Control Pain: Ensure analgesia is optimized.
  • Sensory Aids: Glasses & hearing aids.
  • Orientation: Familiar faces, photos, explain time/place.
  • Minimize Interventions: Remove unnecessary IV lines/catheters.
  • Environment: Quiet, low light at night. Minimize noise.
  • Withdrawal: Nicotine patches if tobacco withdrawal likely.
💊

Pharmacological Management

Crucial Step: Has the patient expressed wishes regarding sedation at the end of life? If unavailable, act in best interests and communicate clearly. Purpose is to relieve distress, not hasten death.
Midazolam Anxiety

Useful if anxiety/restlessness is the main presentation.

SC / IM (PRN) 2.5 – 5 mg
24h SC Driver 5 – 30 mg
Oral Not avail

Oral Alt (Scottish): Lorazepam 0.5–1mg SL/PO OR Diazepam 5mg PR/PO.

⚠️ Risk: Can cause disinhibition & paradoxical agitation (esp. higher doses).
Haloperidol 1st Line

Use FIRST LINE if delirium present. Useful for paranoia/psychosis. Also antiemetic.

Oral / SC (PRN) 1.5 – 2.5 mg
24h SC Driver 1.5 – 5 mg
Elderly/Frail Start 0.5 mg
Levomepromazine Sedating

Very sedating. Useful for paranoia/psychosis. Antiemetic.

Oral (PRN) 6.25 – 25 mg
SC (PRN) 5 – 25 mg
24h SC Driver 5 – 75 mg
⚠️ Caution: Reduces seizure threshold. Start LOW in frail.
🚩

Critical Warnings

QT Interval & Polypharmacy

The Risk: Many palliative drugs (Haloperidol, Levomepromazine, others) carry a risk of QT prolongation.

⚡
However: In the last days of life, the benefit of symptom control almost always outweighs the risk.

Action: Avoid unnecessary polypharmacy. ECG monitoring is usually not required unless specific risk factors exist.
