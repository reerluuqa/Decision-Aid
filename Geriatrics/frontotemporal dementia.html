Assessment
History & Examination
  • Progressive personality/social behavior changes Core Feature
  • Early language impairment (fluency/comprehension) Core Feature
  • Gradual onset with steady progression
  • Self-neglect and abandonment of activities
  • Altered eating habits and hyperorality
  • Disinhibition, apathy, loss of empathy
  • Presence of motor neuron disease features Consider ALS-FTD
  • Parkinsonian symptoms FTD-Parkinsonism
Cognitive Testing Essential
MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment)
Detects frontal lobe dysfunction over time
Detects executive deficits; tracks progression
ACE-III (Addenbrooke's)
Multi-domain test validated for FTD
Identifies executive/language predominance
Frontal Assessment Battery (FAB)
10-minute bedside screening
Identifies frontal lobe dysfunction
EXIT-25 / Quick EXIT
Executive function screening
Quantifies executive impairment
Risk Factors
Strong Factors:
• MAPT gene mutations
• GRN gene mutations
• C9orf72 expansions
• Family history of FTD
Weaker Factors:
• Traumatic brain injury
• Post-traumatic stress disorder
• Age 45-65 peak onset
Urgent Referral Indications:
• Rapid progression with severe behavioral symptoms
• Suspected motor neuron disease features
• Acute psychosis with dangerous behavior
• Significant safety concerns for patient or others
Diagnosis & Investigations
Initial Laboratory Tests Routine
FBC & CRP
Rule out anemia/infection
Usually normal
TSH & Free T4
Exclude hyperthyroidism
Usually normal
Metabolic Panel
Electrolytes, renal/liver function
Usually normal
Vitamin B12 & Folate
Exclude deficiency
Usually normal
Syphilis, HIV, Lyme serology
Rule out infectious causes
Usually normal
Brain Imaging Essential
  • MRI (Preferred): Focal frontal/anterior temporal atrophy, often asymmetrical
  • CT Alternative: If MRI unavailable, shows focal atrophy
  • FDG-PET/CT: Hypometabolism in frontal/temporal lobes - differentiates from AD
  • Amyloid PET: Usually normal (positive in small proportion with mixed pathology)
  • Specific Patterns:
    • bvFTD: Bilateral frontal lobe atrophy
    • Semantic Dementia: Anterior temporal lobe atrophy
    • nfvPPA: Left posterior frontal lobe atrophy
    • PSP: Midbrain atrophy (hummingbird sign)
Genetic Testing Consider
  • Indicated for: probable bvFTD, strong family history, early onset psychiatric features
  • Target genes: MAPT, GRN, C9orf72 (most common)
  • C9orf72 screening: prominent psychiatric symptoms or family history of late-onset psychiatric disorder
  • Requires pre/post-test genetic counseling
  • Important for family planning decisions
Diagnostic Criteria
Behavioural Variant FTD
Possible: 3/6 features
Probable: + imaging
• Behavioral disinhibition
• Apathy/inertia
• Loss of empathy
• Perseverative behaviors
• Hyperorality/dietary changes
• Executive deficits with spared memory/visuospatial
Primary Progressive Aphasia
• Nonfluent/agrammatic variant
• Semantic variant
• Logopenic variant (often AD pathology)
Differential Diagnosis Priority:
• Alzheimer's Disease (memory-predominant)
• Dementia with Lewy Bodies (visual hallucinations, fluctuating cognition)
• Vascular Dementia (stepwise progression, vascular imaging)
• Primary Psychiatric Disorders (onset pattern, family history)
Treatment & Management
Supportive Care Foundation
  • Patient & caregiver education about FTD symptoms and progression
  • Home safety evaluation by occupational therapist
  • Financial & legal planning (early capacity may be preserved)
  • End-of-life preferences discussion (early in course)
  • Driving assessment Impaired early
  • Behavioral log to identify triggers and patterns
  • Respite care and daycare services
Non-Pharmacological Interventions First Line
Behavioral Strategies
• Environmental modifications
• Routines and structured activities
• Alternative strategies for compulsions
• Safe roaming areas
• Communication techniques (short sentences, eye contact)
Therapeutic Approaches
• Cognitive stimulation therapy
• Music therapy (receptive preferred)
• Massage/touch therapy
• Physical activity/exercise
• Animal-assisted interventions
Pharmacological Management Symptom-Specific
Acute Agitation/Aggression
Benzodiazepine or antipsychotic Short-term only
Lorazepam 2mg PRN (max 10mg/day)
OR Risperidone 0.25mg BID (max 1mg/day)
OR Quetiapine 25mg QHS (max 150mg/day)
Psychosis
Atypical antipsychotics preferred
(Risperidone, Quetiapine, Olanzapine)
Caution: Increased mortality risk
Sleep Disturbances
Mirtazapine 7.5-15mg QHS
OR Trazodone 25-150mg QHS
OR Melatonin/Ramelteon
Impulsivity/Disinhibition
Valproate semisodium (consult specialist)
OR Topiramate (appetite suppression)
SSRI evidence variable
Pseudobulbar Affect
Dextromethorphan/Quinidine 20mg/10mg
Start daily x7 days, then BID
IMPORTANT: Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors and memantine are NOT effective in FTD and may worsen behavioral symptoms
Management of Concurrent Illness Address Promptly
  • UTI, pneumonia can worsen behavioral symptoms significantly
  • Treat underlying infection → prompt resolution of acute behavioral changes
  • Antibiotic selection per standard guidelines for older adults
  • Decisions to withhold treatment must be made when patient is well
End-of-Life Care:
• Focus on comfort: feeding assistance, pain control, skin care
• Behavioral complications uncommon at this stage
• Gastrostomy feeding does NOT improve long-term outcomes
• Palliative care involvement early
Follow-up & Monitoring
Monitoring Schedule Comprehensive
  • Cognitive/Behavioral evaluation: Every 6-12 months Track progression
  • Carer burden assessment: Independent review at each visit
  • Driving status: Reassess every 6 months Safety critical
  • Functional status: Frontotemporal Rating Scale (FRS) more accurate than standard scales
  • Care needs: Anticipate increasing support requirements
  • Dependent children: Enquire about their coping and well-being
Common Complications Anticipate
Financial Crisis
Irrepressible spending, compulsive gambling
Medium likelihood
Legal Crisis
Shoplifting, reckless driving, aggression
Low likelihood
Dangerous Driving
Disinhibition, impulsivity, poor judgment
High likelihood
Malnutrition
Altered eating habits, hyperorality
High likelihood
Falls
Parkinsonism, poor balance
PSP: 90-100% fall rate, bvFTD: 16%
Medium likelihood
Pain Issues
Impaired pain/temperature perception
Especially in bvFTD and semantic dementia
High likelihood
Prognosis Variable
  • Median survival: 7-13 years from diagnosis (clinic cohorts)
  • Shortest survival: FTD-ALS variant (2.5 years)
  • Longest survival: Semantic dementia (median 12 years)
  • FTD-ALS: Considerably shorter (3 years median)
  • Progression rate: Males slower than females
  • Predictors of rapid decline:
    • Reduced baseline grey-matter volume
    • Increased microglial activation in frontal regions
    • Negative behaviors (apathy, aspontaneity, rigidity)
Caregiver Support Critical
  • Behavior logs to identify triggers and warning signs
  • Coping techniques and stress management
  • Local/national support organizations (e.g., Association for FTD)
  • Respite care services
  • Carer counseling and psychological support
  • Financial assistance and legal resources
  • Peer support groups
Driving Assessment:
• Fitness impaired even at early stages
• Early behavioral symptoms (disinhibition, impulsivity) are high risk
• Patients often in denial about driving ability
• Follow local reporting requirements
• American Academy of Neurology and UK Royal College guidelines available
Suicide Risk:
• Increased risk in first 3 months after diagnosis
• Higher in younger patients (<65 years)
• Assess especially in those with known mental illness history
