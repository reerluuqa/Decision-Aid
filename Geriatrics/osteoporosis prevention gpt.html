Primary Care Quick Reference

Osteoporosis – Fragility Fracture Prevention
One‑Page Decision Algorithm

Adults (≥18y) CKS‑aligned Updated Apr 2025

Sections

1. Who to Assess 2. Exclude Secondary Causes 3. Risk Assessment 4. Interpret & Stratify 5. Management 6. Follow‑Up Red Flags
Step 1

Identify Who to Assess

  • Assess all: Women ≥65y; Men ≥75y.
  • Assess ≥50y with risk factors: previous fragility fracture; current/frequent oral corticosteroids; history of falls; family history hip fracture; BMI <18.5; smoking; alcohol >14 units/week; secondary causes (endocrine, malabsorption, RA/other inflammatory arthropathies, CKD, COPD, liver disease, immobility).
  • <50y: assess only if major RFs (steroids; untreated premature menopause; multiple/major fractures). For <40y, assess if high‑dose steroids (≥7.5 mg pred daily for ≥3 mo), previous major osteoporotic fracture, or multiple fragility fractures.
  • Consider meds increasing risk: SSRIs, enzyme‑inducing antiepileptics (e.g., carbamazepine), aromatase inhibitors, GnRH agonists, PPIs, thiazolidinediones.
Step 2

Exclude Non‑Osteoporotic & Secondary Causes

Rule out: metastatic bone disease, multiple myeloma, osteomalacia, Paget’s disease. Evaluate endocrine (premature menopause, hypogonadism, diabetes, hyperthyroidism / Cushing’s / hyperparathyroidism), inflammatory arthropathies, malabsorption (IBD, coeliac, chronic pancreatitis), liver disease, COPD.
Step 3

Risk Assessment

  • Use QFracture® (preferred) or FRAX® for 10‑yr risk of hip & major osteoporotic fracture.
  • ≥10% (10‑yr risk) → arrange DXA (spine & hip).
  • Offer DXA without calculator for: age ≥50 with fragility fracture; <40y with a major RF. Consider treatment with vertebral fracture even before DXA.
  • Use DXA if risk near threshold or FRAX® likely underestimates risk (e.g., chronic steroids, high alcohol, heavy smoking, multiple fractures; age >80y may underestimate short‑term risk).
Step 4

Interpret & Stratify

  • High risk DXA T‑score ≤ −2.5 or FRAX/QFracture above threshold → start treatment.
  • Intermediate Risk close to threshold → DXA; treat if T ≤ −2.5.
  • Low risk Below threshold → no drugs; lifestyle advice; re‑assess in ~5 years.
  • Very high risk Recent vertebral # (<2y), ≥2 vertebral #, T ≤ −3.5, high‑dose steroids (≥7.5 mg pred ≥3 mo), multiple RFs or high imminent risk → refer to osteoporosis specialist (consider parenteral therapy).
Step 5

Management

First‑line Bone‑Sparing Treatment

  • Oral bisphosphonates (if no contraindications):
    • Alendronate 70 mg once weekly or
    • Risedronate 35 mg once weekly
    • Alternative (if unsuitable): Ibandronate 150 mg monthly
  • If oral not tolerated/contraindicated: refer to specialist – options include Zoledronic acid, Denosumab, Teriparatide, Strontium ranelate, Raloxifene.
Risedronate weekly is licensed in men; all oral bisphosphonates licensed in postmenopausal women.

Supplementation & Lifestyle

  • Vitamin D: 10 µg (400 IU) daily if low sunlight; if calcium intake inadequate, add ≥1000 mg/day calcium.
  • Elderly housebound / nursing home: Vit D 20 µg (800 IU) + calcium ≥1000 mg/day.
  • HRT: consider in younger postmenopausal women (also for menopausal symptoms).
  • Lifestyle: regular weight‑bearing & strength exercise; balanced diet; smoking cessation; alcohol within limits; assess & mitigate falls risk.

Bisphosphonate Administration – Key Advice

  • Take on an empty stomach with a full glass of water; avoid food/other meds for ≥30 min (see specific risedronate timing options).
  • Swallow whole; remain upright ≥30 min; avoid bedtime dosing.
  • Common early GI effects often improve; separate from calcium/antacids by ≥30 min.
  • Dental check ideally before starting; maintain oral hygiene; report jaw pain/swelling (possible osteonecrosis).
  • Report new hip/groin/thigh pain (possible atypical femoral fracture).
Step 6

Follow‑Up & Review

  • 12–16 weeks: check tolerance (GI, atypical fracture symptoms).
  • 12 months: check adherence.
  • 5 years on oral bisphosphonate:
    • Continue (≥10 years) if: age ≥70 at start; prior hip/vertebral fracture; fracture during first 5 years.
    • Others: repeat DXA → if T ≤ −2.5 continue 5 more years; if T > −2.5 consider drug holiday 1.5–3 years.
  • On oral corticosteroids: maintain bone protection until steroids stopped; then reassess. If medium/low steroid dose and risk near threshold, reassess at 12–18 months.
  • Reassess at any time if new fracture or risk factors change.
  • If fracture occurs on therapy: check adherence; exclude secondary causes; consider specialist referral to change treatment.
  • Low‑risk pathway: lifestyle advice; re‑assess in ~5 years.
Referral

Red Flags / Specialist Referral

  • Very high imminent risk (e.g., recent vertebral fracture <2 years, ≥2 vertebral fractures).
  • T‑score ≤ −3.5.
  • High‑dose glucocorticoids (≥7.5 mg prednisolone daily for ≥3 months).
  • Multiple clinical risk factors or fracture while adherent to therapy.
