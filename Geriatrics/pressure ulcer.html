Pressure Ulcer

Clinical Quick Reference • Last Updated: Aug 2021
NPUAP 2016 Staging

Critical Red Flags & Actions

  • Signs of Sepsis: Fever, advancing cellulitis (initiate systemic antibiotics).
  • Osteomyelitis Suspected: Exposed bone, elevated ESR (>100) or WBC (>15). Order MRI.
  • Deep Tissue Infection: Immediate sharp debridement required.
  • Stable Eschar (Heel/Ischemic Limb): Do NOT debride. It acts as natural cover.

Rapid Staging Guide

Hover/Tap cards for details.

Stage 1 Intact Skin
Non-blanchable erythema. Darker skin may appear purple/blue.

Key: Persistent redness. Temperature/texture change may precede visual change.

Stage 2 Partial Loss
Dermis exposed. Pink/Red moist bed. Serum-filled blister. Fat NOT visible.

Note: Do not confuse with moisture-associated skin damage or skin tears.

Stage 3 Full Thickness
Adipose (fat) visible. Slough/eschar may be present. No bone/tendon exposure.

Risk: Undermining and tunnelling common. Depth varies by anatomical location.

Stage 4 Critical
Exposed muscle, tendon, ligament, cartilage, or bone. Slough/eschar common.

Action: High risk of osteomyelitis. Requires surgical consult often.

DTI Deep Tissue
Maroon/Purple localized area. Blood-filled blister. Intact or non-intact skin.

Etiology: Pressure/shear at bone-muscle interface. May rapidly evolve to Stage 3/4.

Unstageable Obscured
Full-thickness loss obscured by slough (yellow, tan, gray, green) or eschar (tan, brown, black).

Action: Remove slough/eschar to stage (unless stable eschar on heel/ischemic limb).

Management Algorithm

For ALL Patients
  • Pressure Relief: Reposition every 2 hours (standard). Use appropriate support surface (mattress/cushion).
  • Hygiene: Cleanse and dry incontinent skin regularly. Apply protective creams.
  • Nutrition: Refer to dietitian. Aim for 30-35 kcal/kg/day and 1.25-1.5 g protein/kg/day.
  • Pain Mgmt: Assess and treat. Topical lidocaine before debridement/dressing changes.
Deep Ulcers (Stage 3/4) & Infection
  • Debridement: Required if necrotic tissue/slough present. Methods: Sharp, Autolytic (hydrogels), Enzymatic.
  • Surgery: Consider if not healing or rapid closure needed. Options: Excision, flap, graft.
  • Osteomyelitis Rule-out: MRI if bone exposed. Systemic antibiotics for sepsis/cellulitis/bone infection.

Clinical Decision Tree

Q: Is there eschar?

If on heel/ischemic limb → DO NOT REMOVE.
Elsewhere → Debride to determine stage.

Q: Signs of Infection?

Foul odor, excess exudate, warmth, erythema? → Swab NOT recommended routinely. Treat based on clinical signs. Systemic ABx only for sepsis/spreading infection.

Q: Wound Swab?

Generally useless as they reflect colonization, not infection.
Use deep tissue biopsy for definitive diagnosis.

Key Risk Factors

Immobility Age > 70 Malnutrition Sensory Impairment ICU Stay Peripheral Vascular Disease Diabetes Incontinence

Differentials: Key Distinctions

Condition Key Signs (Differentiating) Differentiating Tests
Moisture-Associated Dermatitis Erythematous, macerated skin in moisture-exposed area. No defined wound bed usually. Clinical diagnosis. No specific test.
Venous Ulcers Lower legs/near ankles. Skin staining (hemosiderin). Lipodermatosclerosis. Duplex ultrasound (retrograde flow).
Arterial Ulcers Feet/heels/toes. Skin white/shiny. "Punched out" appearance. Pain relieved by dependency. Ankle Brachial Index ≤ 0.90.
Diabetic Neuropathy Ulcer Occurs over pressure points from ill-fitting shoes. Callus often present. HbA1c, Monofilament test.
Pyoderma Gangrenosum Purple, undermined edges. Rapid enlargement. Often associated with IBD. Biopsy (often to exclude other causes).
