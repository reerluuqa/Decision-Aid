Orthostatic Hypotension

Quick Reference

Diagnostic Criteria

Fall in SBP ≥20 mmHg (≥30 mmHg if hypertensive) and/or DBP ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing. Heart rate increase <15 bpm suggests neurogenic cause.

Red Flags — Act Immediately

  • Syncope / Falls — High mortality risk; secure patient safety
  • Acute/Subacute Onset — Consider autoimmune or paraneoplastic cause
  • Profound BP Drop — SBP falls >50 mmHg; risk of cerebral hypoperfusion
  • Unexplained Weight Loss — Screen for malignancy/paraneoplastic

Immediate Actions

  • Sit/Lie Patient Down — Restore cerebral perfusion immediately
  • Rapid Water Bolus — 500 mL tap water in 3-4 min; effect in 5-10 min
  • Review Medications — Stop/reduce alpha-blockers, beta-blockers, TCAs
  • Exclude Volume Depletion — Check hydration, diuretic use, bleeding

First-Line: Non-Pharmacological

  • Salt liberalization + NaCl tablets
  • ≥2L water/day
  • Elevate head of bed 15-22 cm
  • Compression stockings
  • Abdominal binder
  • Small, frequent meals
  • Leg-crossing, toe raises
  • Avoid hot environments

Common Underlying Causes

  • Diabetes Mellitus — Autonomic neuropathy
  • Parkinson's Disease — Alpha-synuclein deposits
  • Multiple System Atrophy — Severe, progressive
  • Volume Depletion — Dehydration, diuretics, bleeding
  • Hypertension — Paradoxically linked; arterial stiffness

Posture Test Protocol

  1. Measure BP supine (or seated) after 5 min rest
  2. Patient stands; measure BP at 1 min and 3 min
  3. Record heart rate simultaneously
  4. Positive: SBP drop ≥20 mmHg or DBP drop ≥10 mmHg
Interpreting Heart Rate Response

HR increase <15 bpm with significant BP drop → Neurogenic cause (autonomic failure)

HR increase >15 bpm → Volume depletion or drug-induced (intact baroreflex)

Clinical Presentation

  • Light-headedness
  • Dizziness / vertigo
  • Visual dimming / tunnel vision
  • Fatigue on exertion
  • Syncope / falls
  • Neck/shoulder pain ("coat-hanger")

Worse: morning, after meals, in heat, prolonged standing

Aggravating Factors

  • Early morning
  • Post-prandial
  • Hot environments
  • Prolonged standing
  • Valsalva / straining
  • Exercise

Monitoring & Complications

Follow-Up Every 3-6 Months

  • Supine & standing BP
  • 24-hr ambulatory BP if supine HTN suspected
  • Annual creatinine (renal function)

Complications

  • Falls & fractures (high short-term risk)
  • Supine hypertension (50% of patients)
  • Cognitive decline / dementia
  • CV events (MI, stroke, HF)
