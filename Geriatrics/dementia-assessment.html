üî¥ DELIRIUM
MEDICAL EMERGENCY
  • Onset: Hours to days (vs. months/years in dementia)
  • Fluctuating: Waxing/waning consciousness
  • Inattention: Core feature
  • Often reversible if cause identified
üîç
First Line: FBC, metabolic panel, glucose, urinalysis, urine culture
üü† DEPRESSION (Pseudodementia)
URGENT DIFFERENTIAL
  • Acute onset of cognitive complaints
  • Relatively spared recognition memory
  • Emphasis on concentration problems
  • May improve with antidepressant treatment
üìã
Assess: Geriatric Depression Scale, Cornell Scale for Depression in Dementia
üî¥ RAPID DETERIORATION
RED FLAG
  • Hours to days = Acute confusional state
  • Consider: Infection, metabolic, toxic, stroke, hydrocephalus
  • Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease: Rapid progression, myoclonus

MOST COMMON
  • Onset: Insidious, slow progression
  • Memory: Recent memory affected first
  • Disorientation: Time and place
  • Language: Naming difficulties (proper names)

üü¶ Vascular Dementia (5-20%)
SECOND MOST COMMON
  • Onset: Abrupt or stepwise decline
  • History: Stroke, TIA, hypertension, AF
  • Cognitive: Variable, patchy deficits
  • Emotional: Depression, emotional incontinence

üü¶ Lewy Body Dementia
IMPORTANT

  • Parkinsonism: Rigidity, postural instability

Diagnostic Approach

üìñ HISTORY - Key Questions

  • Onset: Abrupt (vascular) vs Insidious (degenerative)
  • Progression: Stepwise (vascular) vs Gradual (Alzheimer's)
  • Functional decline: ADLs vs IADLs
  • Family history: Dementia, PD, ALS, psychiatric disorders
  • Medications: Review for anticholinergics, sedatives
  • Alcohol use: Important in older patients with MCI
  • Risk factors: Stroke, hypertension, diabetes, head injury

ü©∫ PHYSICAL EXAMINATION

  • Cranial nerves: Visual fields, ataxia, nystagmus
  • Motor: Hemiparesis, rigidity, tremor
  • Sensory: Neuropathy (B12 deficiency)
  • Coordination/Gait: NPH has prominent gait disturbance
  • Reflexes: Asymmetric (vascular), primitive (Alzheimer's)
  • Cardiovascular: BP, murmurs, bruits, rhythm
  • Psychiatric: Mood, affect, thought content

üü¶ Neuroimaging
RECOMMENDED
  • CT/MRI Head: Detect tumors, hematoma, hydrocephalus
  • MRI preferred: Better for vascular lesions, atrophy
  • FDG-PET: Hypometabolism patterns, frontotemporal
  • Amyloid PET: Increases diagnostic confidence

  • HIV test (if at risk)
  • Urine toxicology (drug effects)
  • CSF analysis (if infection suspected)
  • EEG (rapid progression ‚Üí CJD)
  • Heavy metals (if exposure risk)

Treatment & Management

üü¢ Disease-Modifying Medications
SYMPTOMATIC BENEFIT
  • Donepezil, Rivastigmine, Galantamine
  • Memantine (NMDA antagonist)
  • Temporarily improve cognition
  • Slow progression in some patients
üí°
Consider early in diagnosis for maximum benefit
üü¢ Non-Pharmacological
FIRST LINE
  • Cognitive stimulation
  • Regular exercise
  • Social engagement
  • Environmental modifications
  • Caregiver support and education
üü† Agitation Management
USE CAUTION
  • First: Environmental/psychosocial interventions
  • Only if severe/dangerous: Consider antipsychotics
  • WARNING: Increased mortality risk
  • Time-limited: Regular review required
‚ö†Ô∏è
Black Box Warning: Antipsychotics increase mortality in dementia patients
üü¢ Modifiable Risk Factors
  • Smoking cessation
  • Midlife obesity management
  • Diet modification (reduce saturated fat)
  • Limit alcohol (<14 units/week)
  • Control cholesterol
  • Address hearing/vision loss
  • Prevent head injuries

Genetic Testing: Consider if documented family history + patient requests testing

Specialist Referral: If diagnosis unclear, atypical features, or progressive MCI
