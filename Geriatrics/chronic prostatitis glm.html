Prostatitis - Chronic: Quick Reference

Management of chronic prostatitis and chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CPPS) in primary care.

⚠️

Stop & Check: Is it Acute?

Do NOT use this guideline if: Sudden onset fever + rigors + irritative urinary symptoms.

Action: Suspect Acute Bacterial Prostatitis. This requires urgent admission/IV antibiotics. See separate CKS topic.

✅

Have I got the right topic?

Definition: Urogenital pain for at least 3 months (perineal, suprapubic, penile, testicular).

Exclusions: Not cancer (usually), not STI (usually), not acute infection. Men are generally systemically well.

Diagnosis & Assessment

1 Clinical Features

Suspect if pain persists 3+ months in:

  • Perineum (most common)
  • Suprapubic region, Inguinal area
  • Penis (especially tip), Testicles, Rectum

Associated Symptoms:

  • LUTS Frequency, urgency, weak stream, hesitancy.
  • Sexual Painful ejaculation, ED, haematospermia.
  • Psychosocial Anxiety, depression, impact on QoL.

2 Investigations

First Line:

  • Urine Dipstick: Blood, Leucocytes, Nitrite.
  • MSU (Mid-stream urine): Culture & Sensitivity.
  • STI Screen: NAAT for Chlamydia/Gonorrhoea (First pass urine).

Consider (if indicated):

  • PSA: Only after discussion of implications. Not strictly diagnostic for prostatitis.
  • Renal Function (eGFR): If recurrent UTI or retention history.
Note: Do NOT perform prostatic massage in primary care.

Management Scenarios

Suspected CP/CPPS (Non-bacterial)

Context: No proven bacterial infection. 90% of cases. Multifactorial cause.

Explanation & Reassurance

  • Explain cause is often unknown/multifactorial.
  • Reassure: Not cancer, rarely STI.
  • Focus on symptom control rather than "cure".
  • Improvement often takes 6 months.

Symptom Management (UPOINT)

  • Pain: Paracetamol +/- NSAID. Avoid Opioids.
  • Neuropathic Pain: Consider pain specialist review.
  • LUTS: Alpha-blocker (e.g., Tamsulosin, Doxazosin) trial for 4-6 weeks.
  • Constipation: Stool softener (Lactulose/Docusate) if defecation painful.

When to Refer

  • Diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Severe symptoms.
  • Symptoms persist after initial management.

Antibiotics?

Controversial. A single course may be tried if symptoms < 6 months, but evidence is weak.

  • Trimethoprim 200mg BD (4-6 weeks)
  • OR Doxycycline 100mg BD (4-6 weeks)

Suspected Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis (CBP)

Context: History of recurrent UTIs or episode of acute prostatitis within last 12 months. < 10% of cases.

Primary Action: Refer to Urologist for specialist assessment.

While Awaiting Referral

1. Pain Management:

  • Paracetamol and/or NSAID.
  • Seek pain specialist advice if neuropathic features.

2. Antibiotics (Single Course):

  • Trimethoprim 200mg twice a day (4-6 weeks)
  • OR Doxycycline 100mg twice a day (4-6 weeks)

Supportive Care

Constipation: Offer stool softener (Lactulose/Docusate) if defecation is painful.

Safety Alert

Fluoroquinolones Restricted

Do not use Ciprofloxacin/Ofloxacin for non-severe infections or chronic pelvic pain without confirmed bacterial infection due to risk of disabling side effects (EMA/MHRA guidance).

Prescribing Safety & Interactions

Trimethoprim Interactions

Drug Risk/Interaction
Warfarin May potentiate anticoagulant effect. Monitor INR.
Methotrexate Risk of bone marrow suppression.
Phenytoin Risk of phenytoin toxicity.
Renal Impairment Dose adjust if eGFR < 30. Avoid if severe deficiency.
Also warn patient to report signs of blood disorders (sore throat, bruising, bleeding).

Azithromycin & Others

Azithromycin:

  • QT Prolongation: Caution in cardiac history or with other QT-prolonging drugs.
  • Warfarin: Monitor INR (unpredictable increase).
  • Statins: Rare risk of rhabdomyolysis.

Alpha-blockers (e.g. Tamsulosin):

  • First-dose hypotension risk.
  • Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome (IFIS) - inform if cataract surgery planned.

Differential Diagnosis

Consider alternative diagnoses if presentation is atypical:

Urological

  • Urethritis (STI)
  • Epididymo-orchitis
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)
  • Prostate / Bladder Cancer
  • Urethral Stricture

Gastrointestinal

  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
  • Anal/Rectal pathology
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Neuro/Musculoskeletal

  • Pudendal Neuralgia
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction
  • Disc prolapse (Lower back pain)
