🦴 Osteoporosis Quick Guide

🔍
⚠️
Key safety alert: Do NOT stop denosumab without specialist review — risk of multiple vertebral fractures within 18 months (MHRA 2020). Choose one guideline per patient journey — don't switch halfway.

Key Definitions

Osteoporosis

T-score ≤ −2.5 at femoral neck

Severe Osteoporosis

T-score ≤ −2.5 AND fragility fracture

Osteopenia

T-score between −1 and −2.5

Fragility Fracture

Fracture from fall ≤ standing height or spontaneous

Primary Prevention

No prior fracture but at risk

Secondary Prevention

Has had a fragility fracture

🧠 Think of it like stroke & hypertension: The bad outcome is fragility fracture. Osteoporosis is just one risk factor (like hypertension for stroke). Many who fracture do NOT have osteoporotic T-scores. Focus on fracture risk, not just T-scores.

Step-by-Step Clinical Pathway (NOGG-based)

Step 1: Identify Who Is at Risk ▼

Case-finding approach — actively seek out postmenopausal women or men ≥50y with any risk factor.

NOGG risk factor list (extensive!):

  • Low BMI <18.5
  • Previous fragility fracture
  • Parental hip fracture
  • Smoking
  • Alcohol ≥3U/day
  • HIV
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Secondary osteoporosis
  • Height loss ≥4cm
  • Thoracic kyphosis
  • Learning disability (e.g. Down's)
  • Inflammatory diseases
  • Endocrine: DM, thyroid, Cushing's, POI, hypogonadism
  • Haem: myeloma, thalassaemia
  • Muscle disease, sarcopenia
  • Lung: asthma, COPD, CF
  • Neuro: PD, epilepsy, MS, stroke, depression, dementia
  • Nutritional deficiency, bariatric surgery, IBD, coeliac
  • Medications: immunosuppressants, glitazones, AEDs, hormone suppressants, glucocorticoids
Step 2: Quantify Risk Using FRAX ▼
  1. Go to FRAX website
  2. Use tool for country of birth (UK flag for UK-born)
  3. Enter data — leave BMD blank if no DXA yet
  4. T2 diabetes or Parkinson's → tick "rheumatoid arthritis" box
  5. Click Calculate → get 10-year risk of major osteoporotic fracture AND hip fracture
  6. Click "View NOGG guidance"
NOGG says: Use the highest risk score to decide treatment.
NICE says: Use major osteoporotic fracture risk.

FRAX Adjustments (NOGG):

  • Recurrent falls (≥2 in 12m): ↑ fracture risk by ⅓
  • Glucocorticoids >7.5mg/d: ↑ major fracture by 15%, hip by 20% (even after ticking steroid box)
  • Lumbar spine BMD lower than hip: ↑ probability by 1/10 per T-score difference
  • Pick the single biggest adjustment factor if multiple apply

Step 4: Treat & Monitor ▼

Pre-prescribing checks:

  • Check calcium and vitamin D → correct deficiency first
  • Check creatinine/eGFR (thresholds vary by drug — check BNF)
  • Advise dental check → complete invasive procedures first
  • Assess GI history (dysphagia, oesophageal disease, Barrett's)
  • Can patient sit/stand upright ≥30 minutes?

Drug choice summary:

  • Hip fracture: IV zoledronate (↓ mortality at 3y, ↓ recurrence)
  • Other fractures: Oral bisphosphonates or IV zoledronate
  • Postmenopausal ≤60y: Consider HRT first line if safe
  • Very high risk: Treat + refer → may be offered anabolic drugs
Very high risk criteria: FRAX in deep red, vertebral # within 2y, ≥2 vertebral #s ever, T-score ≤−3.5, high-dose steroids, multiple risk factors.

📗 NOGG 2024 Guidance

Most recently updated. Uses FRAX → NOGG graphs. Recommended by Red Whale as most practical.

When to Look for It ▼

Risk assess anyone with a clinical risk factor. Case-finding in any postmenopausal woman or man ≥50y with risk factors.

See pathway tab for full risk factor list.

How to Quantify Risk ▼

Use FRAX → click "View NOGG guidance". Use highest risk score.

Key FRAX adjustments:

  • Country of birth (even if lived in UK most of life)
  • T2 diabetes / Parkinson's → tick RA box
  • Recurrent falls (≥2/12m) → ↑ risk by ⅓
  • On >7.5mg prednisolone/d → ↑ major # by 15%, hip by 20%
  • Lumbar spine BMD < femoral neck → ↑ probability by 1/10 per T-score difference
When & How to Treat ▼

Treat if NOGG graph indicates treatment.

  • Hip fracture: IV zoledronate
  • Other fractures: Oral bisphosphonates or IV zoledronate
  • Women ≤60y high risk: Consider HRT first line if safe
  • Very high risk: Treat + consider referral for anabolic drugs
⏱️ Duration of Treatment (NOGG) ▼

Higher-risk group (ANY of these):

  • Age >70y
  • Previous hip or vertebral fracture
  • ≥2 vertebral fractures
  • Current glucocorticoids ≥7.5mg/d ≥3m
Oral: 10 years | IV: 6 years
(or until steroids stopped if steroid-induced)
Once complete → usually stop (lack of evidence). Individual discussion.

Lower-risk group (NONE of the above):

Oral: 5 years | IV: 3 years
Then re-do FRAX + BMD:
• Below NOGG threshold → consider pausing, review after 1.5y (ibandronate/risedronate) or 3y (alendronate/zoledronate)
• Above NOGG threshold → continue for further 5y oral / 3y IV. Review at 10y/6y total.
When to DXA (NOGG) ▼

DXA for anyone with intermediate, high, or very high risk on NOGG graph.

The FRAX → NOGG graph tells you when to do a DXA!

Repeat DXA: At end of treatment course. Use alongside FRAX to determine future management.

Minimum 2 years between DXA scans. A fall in BMD of up to 4% on treatment may still show benefit. Check compliance.

📙 SIGN 142 (2021) Guidance

Scottish guidance. Uses QFracture. Includes complex flowchart for treatment choice.

Who to Risk Assess ▼

Anyone with major clinical risk factor

  • Known low BMD
  • Oral steroid use
  • HIV
  • Untreated premature menopause

>50y with clinical risk factors

  • Parental osteoporosis
  • Alcohol >3.5U/day
  • Low BMI (<20)
  • Smokers
  • RA/SLE, IBD
  • Moderate/severe CKD
  • Neuro: MS, PD, Alzheimer's, CVA
  • Meds: SSRIs, PPIs, glitazones, AEDs, hormone blockers
  • Asthma, diabetes, endocrine, chronic liver disease
Risk Quantification & Treatment ▼

Use QFracture (preferred — more comprehensive risk factors).

Primary prevention (no prior fracture):

  • QFracture ≥10% → arrange DXA → treat per flowchart
  • QFracture <10% → lifestyle advice

Secondary prevention (prior fragility fracture):

  • Arrange DXA
  • Start treatment while waiting for results

⚠️ Pre-Prescribing Safety Checklist ▼
  • Check & correct vitamin D and calcium
  • Check creatinine/eGFR — threshold varies by drug (check BNF)
  • Dental check — complete invasive procedures first
  • Assess GI history (dysphagia, oesophageal disease, Barrett's, recent GI surgery)
  • Confirm patient can sit/stand upright ≥30 min (oral bisphosphonates)
  • No oesophageal abnormalities (stricture, achalasia)
⚠️ Denosumab Safety ▼
🚨 MHRA 2020: Do NOT stop denosumab without specialist review. Risk of multiple vertebral fractures within 18 months of stopping/delaying.
  • Associated with severe hypocalcaemia — monitor Ca²⁺ before each dose
  • Patients must step down to alternative bone-modifying drug
  • Consider indication prescribing or alerts in patient record
Bisphosphonate Side Effects & Dilemmas ▼

GI Risks

  • Dyspepsia ~7% — due to oesophageal irritation, NOT acid reflux
  • Do NOT use PPI for bisphosphonate dyspepsia (blunts effectiveness, ↓ Ca absorption, ↑ fracture risk in smokers)
  • Oesophageal cancer: mixed evidence (one study 2× risk, NNH 1000/5y; another no risk)

Atypical Femoral Fractures

  • Subtrochanteric/shaft. Presents with thigh pain (bilateral in 50%)
  • Risk ↑ with duration, especially >5y
  • 3–5y: 2.5/10,000 person-years | >8y: 13.1/10,000 person-years
  • Risk drops rapidly after stopping bisphosphonates
  • Higher risk: Asian ethnicity, shorter height, ↑ weight, steroids ≥1y
  • Benefits still far outweigh risks

Osteonecrosis of Jaw

  • Oral mucosa ulceration exposing bone — REFER!
  • Full dental assessment before starting bisphosphonate or denosumab

IV Bisphosphonate Acute Phase Reaction

  • Within first 72h: fever, myalgia, arthralgia (lasts 2–3 days)
  • Affects ~⅓ after first infusion; less with subsequent doses
  • Transient hypocalcaemia possible — check vitamin D first
Romosozumab, Abaloparatide & Newer Drugs ▼

Romosozumab

  • Bone anabolic (formation). Women only. Max 12 months.
  • Contraindicated: Any history of CVD or stroke, hypocalcaemia
  • NICE TA791: for major osteoporotic fracture in past 24m at imminent risk
  • Step down to antiresorptive therapy after to prevent rebound

Abaloparatide (NICE TA991, 2024)

  • PTH analogue → stimulates new bone formation
  • Daily SC injection from pen, max 18 months
  • Alternative to romosozumab/teriparatide for very high risk
  • Potentially safer than romosozumab in CVD/stroke history
  • No refrigeration needed after first dose from pen
  • Step down to antiresorptive therapy after

HRT for Osteoporosis

  • Reduces vertebral fracture risk by 34%, non-vertebral by 13%
  • Consider as first line for women ≤60y at high risk if HRT-related adverse event risk is low

When to X-ray Spine for Vertebral Fractures ▼

Lateral lumbar & thoracic spine X-rays in postmenopausal women or men ≥50y if:

  • Acute-onset back pain with osteoporosis risk factors
  • Height loss ≥4cm
  • Thoracic kyphosis
  • Recent/current long-term glucocorticoid use
  • T-score ≤−2.5 at spine or hip
Note: Most vertebral fractures do not come to medical attention. Moderate/severe asymptomatic vertebral fractures are a strong risk factor for subsequent fracture at other sites.

💉 Corticosteroid-Induced Osteoporosis

Key facts: Steroids cause & exacerbate osteoporosis. Risk of fracture (typically vertebral) rises immediately and returns to normal ~12m after stopping.
🚨 Start Bone Protection IMMEDIATELY (regardless of FRAX) if: ▼
  • Man or woman with previous fragility fracture
  • Any woman ≥70y on ANY glucocorticoid dose
  • Postmenopausal women or men ≥50y:
    • On high doses (≥7.5mg/d prednisolone over 3m) — consider referral for parenteral treatment
    • If FRAX score in yellow section but above intervention line
Still calculate FRAX to see if DXA indicated — but don't delay treatment waiting for DXA.
Everyone Else on Steroids ▼

Calculate FRAX (tick the 'glucocorticoids' box) and follow NOGG guidance.

FRAX accuracy warning:
• Low dose (<2.5mg/d) → FRAX may overestimate risk by 20%
• High dose (≥7.5mg/d) → FRAX may underestimate risk by 15%

Younger people: Bone protection may be indicated on high-dose steroids (≥7.5mg/d) or with previous fracture (caution in women of childbearing age).

Intermittent & Inhaled Steroids ▼

Intermittent oral steroids

  • NOGG: significant steroid load = 30mg/d for ≥4 weeks within any 3-month period
  • BTS/SIGN asthma: frequent courses (3–4/year) → monitor BMD, offer bisphosphonate if significant reduction

Inhaled corticosteroids

SIGN: insufficient evidence — some trials show bone impact, others don't. No specific recommendations.

Licensed Drugs for Steroid-Induced Osteoporosis ▼

All now licensed for bone protection with glucocorticoids:

Alendronate Risedronate Zoledronic acid Denosumab Teriparatide

Oral bisphosphonates likely first line in primary care (ease + cost).

If steroids stopped: Reassess FRAX to decide if bone treatment can also be stopped.

👥 Special Populations

👨 Men with Osteoporosis ▼
  • 40% of all fragility fractures occur in men; higher mortality risk than women
  • Underlying cause in >50%: hypogonadism, prostate cancer Rx, steroids, alcohol
  • On anti-androgen therapy: baseline FRAX + BMD; treat if high/very high risk
  • Repeat FRAX/BMD after 12–18 months if near threshold
SIGN (only guideline with specific drug recs for men):
Secondary prevention: risedronate or zoledronic acid (not alendronate — lack of evidence)
Primary prevention on steroids: alendronate, risedronate or zoledronic acid
(Note: alendronate 10mg daily licensed in men; 70mg weekly is NOT)
👵 Oldest Old (>80y) ▼
  • Under-represented in trials; higher treatment risks but also higher falls risk
  • FRAX may underestimate risk in healthy older patients (competing mortality)
  • QFracture may be more appropriate for healthy >80y
  • Bisphosphonates benefit BMD and decrease hip/non-vertebral fractures at least as much as younger controls
  • Time to benefit: ~12m for non-vertebral fracture (oral); may be quicker with parenteral/anabolic
  • Likely beneficial if life expectancy >6–12 months
NICE multimorbidity guidance: Discuss stopping bisphosphonate after 3 years in this group. Include patient choice, fracture risk and life expectancy.
🎗️ Aromatase Inhibitors & Anti-Androgens ▼
  • Assess baseline fracture risk when starting
  • High risk (red zone on NOGG): Start bisphosphonates or denosumab
  • Men at high risk: consider referral to secondary care
  • Near threshold: repeat FRAX + DXA after 12–18 months
💉 Depot Medroxyprogesterone (Depo Contraceptive) ▼
  • Long-term (>2y) can reduce BMD but reverses when stopped
  • Only small increase in absolute fracture risk in older women
  • No fracture risk assessment needed unless other risk factors present
  • MHRA: re-evaluate risks/benefits every 2y
  • FSRH: UKMEC2 in women >45y. Generally switch at age 50 but can continue if counselled
Osteopenia Management ▼
  • Global prevalence twice that of osteoporosis
  • Lower individual risk but higher absolute number of fractures
  • Recalculate FRAX after BMD — don't be reassured by non-osteoporotic T-score

BMJ 10-Minute Consultation approach:

  • Identify clinical risk factors
  • Measure height & weight (low BMI? vertebral height loss?)
  • Check for thoracic kyphosis
  • Assess fracture risk → follow NOGG pathway
SIGN: T-score −1 to −2.5 AND ≥65y → may be considered for 18-monthly zoledronic acid for 6 years.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What lifestyle advice should I give? ▼
  • Stop smoking
  • Alcohol ≤2U/day (NOGG is stricter)
  • Weight-bearing activity (slows bone density decline)
  • Balance/flexibility/stretching exercises (reduce falls)
  • Falls risk assessment → refer to falls exercise programme if at risk
  • Balanced diet, BMI 20–25
  • Dietary calcium aiming for 700mg/day
  • Vitamin D supplements if deficient (800IU/d recommended by NOGG)
Do NOT recommend: Phyto-oestrogens, vitamin K supplements, acid-balancing diets (SIGN).
Calcium supplementation — who needs it? ▼
SituationCalcium advice
Low dietary intakeConsider supplements
On bisphosphonatesNOT routine — only if low dietary intake. Not within 2h of drug.
On denosumab + renal impairmentUse calcium alongside
Primary prevention of osteoporosisNOT recommended

CV risk: NOGG concludes calcium ± vitamin D does NOT increase cardiovascular risk.

Should we use drug holidays? ▼

Both NOGG and SIGN say drug holidays are NOT supported by evidence and do NOT reduce adverse skeletal effects.

At end of treatment, the options are:

  1. Stop ("drug retirement") — low risk or frailty
  2. Reassess and continue — high-risk people
  3. Pause then reassess — NOGG only, for some patients (sounds like a drug holiday!)
Never stop denosumab without specialist review.
New fracture while on treatment — what to do? ▼

Fracture rates 1–11% within 2–5y in treatment groups. Doesn't necessarily mean failure, but review:

  • Check for secondary causes (hyperparathyroidism, myeloma, vitamin D deficiency, hyperthyroidism)
  • How long on treatment? <1y = likely not at time to benefit yet
  • Check adherence
  • Consider repeat DXA if on treatment ≥3y
  • Consider bone turnover markers (wait 3–6m post-fracture)
  • Consider switching: oral → parenteral; parenteral → anabolic (specialist decision)
  • Bisphosphonates do NOT need to be withdrawn for normal fracture healing unless atypical fracture
Monitoring: DXA or bone turnover markers? ▼

DXA for monitoring

  • Minimum 2 years between scans
  • NOGG: repeat DXA at end of treatment course
  • NICE: repeat DXA after 3y zoledronate / 5y oral (except high-risk → continue without DXA)
  • SIGN: DXA at 3y may identify higher-risk subgroup
  • BMD fall up to 4% on treatment may still show benefit — check compliance

Bone Turnover Markers (P1NP, CTX)

  • SIGN: role remains unclear, may be considered
  • NOGG: may guide treatment intervals post-zoledronate (3m & 6m)
  • Currently a specialist decision, not primary care

Vertebral fracture management ▼

NOGG does NOT recommend:

  • Spinal bracing (soft or rigid orthoses)
  • Vertebroplasty or balloon kyphoplasty

Teriparatide: Better than risedronate for preventing vertebral fractures in postmenopausal women with ≥2 moderate or 1 severe vertebral fracture AND T-score ≤−1.5.

Opportunistically diagnose vertebral fractures found on routine imaging done for other reasons.
