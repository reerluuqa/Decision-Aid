Definition: Vascular Dementia

Chronic progressive disease causing cognitive impairment, particularly affecting executive functions (planning, decision-making) more than memory. Motor and mood changes appear early. Caused by brain damage from infarction, leukoaraiosis, haemorrhage, and small-vessel changes. Second most common cause of dementia after Alzheimer's disease. Mixed dementia (vascular + AD) is common.

Epidemiology

  • ~17% of all dementia cases
  • Incidence: ~3.8 per 1000/year
  • Higher incidence in men than women
  • US prevalence: 332,000-856,000

Key Clinical Features

  • Stepwise deterioration common
  • Early gait disturbance and falls
  • Early urinary incontinence
  • Mood/personality changes early

Red Flags & Emergency Actions

⚠️ IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED - Recent Stroke/TIA

Onset of cognitive symptoms within 3 months after stroke strongly indicates vascular dementia. Aggressive risk factor control is essential to prevent further cerebrovascular events.

⚠️ Suicide Risk Assessment Required

Risk is increased in first 3 months post-diagnosis, particularly in patients under 65 and those with psychiatric comorbidities. Always assess.

Critical Warnings

  • Avoid antipsychotics - increased stroke and mortality risk in vascular dementia
  • Avoid TCAs - cardiovascular adverse effects and overdose risk
  • Monitor for hypotension with mirtazapine - may worsen cerebral perfusion

Contraindications

  • All antipsychotics contraindicated in vascular dementia
  • Tricyclic antidepressants - avoid due to cardiovascular risk
  • SSRIs - use with caution if patient on anticoagulants (bleeding risk)

Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR & NINDS-CSN)

DSM-5-TR Criteria

  • A. Evidence of significant cognitive decline in ≥1 domain
  • B. Clinical features consistent with vascular etiology (temporal relation to stroke OR prominent executive dysfunction)
  • C. Evidence of cerebrovascular disease on neuroimaging
  • D. Not better explained by another disorder

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • History of stroke(s) with cognitive decline
  • Stepwise deterioration in cognition
  • Frontal cognitive syndrome (executive dysfunction, apathy)
  • Focal neurological signs (hemiparesis, dysarthria)

Risk Factors

Strong Risk Factors

  • STRONG Age >60 years - most important risk factor
  • STRONG Hypertension - strong correlation with vascular damage
  • STRONG Diabetes mellitus - dose-dependent risk increase
  • STRONG Smoking - direct vessel wall damage
  • STRONG Obesity - 5x risk with midlife BMI >30
  • STRONG Prior stroke/TIA - up to 1/3 develop dementia within 5 years

Weak/Moderate Risk Factors

  • WEAK Hypercholesterolaemia - predicts stroke but not necessarily dementia
  • WEAK Alcohol misuse - high intake associated; moderate may protect
  • WEAK Chronic kidney disease
  • WEAK Air pollution - associated with increased risk
  • WEAK History of infection - general inflammation link

Neuroimaging

  • MRI brain - gold standard; detects infarcts, white matter lesions
  • CT brain - if MRI contraindicated
  • Absence of cerebrovascular lesions argues against vascular etiology
  • Also excludes treatable causes (hydrocephalus, tumour)

Cardiac Workup

  • ECG - check for atrial fibrillation
  • Carotid duplex ultrasound - if cortical infarction suspected, check for >70% stenosis
  • Echocardiogram - if multiple embolic infarcts or AF suspected

Treatment Algorithm Overview

✅ Primary Goal: Prevention of Further Cerebrovascular Disease

Early aggressive treatment of vascular risk factors is the cornerstone of management. Cognitive-enhancing medications have limited benefit for pure vascular dementia but may help if comorbid Alzheimer's disease.

1

Identify Etiology

Atherosclerotic disease: no cardiac source, suspected artery-to-artery embolism
Cardioembolic disease: cardiac source present (AF, valvular disease, LV thrombus)

2

Initiate Secondary Prevention

Atherosclerotic: Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin ± clopidogrel/ticagrelor)
Cardioembolic: Anticoagulation (warfarin or DOAC)
Carotid stenosis ≥70%: Consider endarterectomy or stenting

3

Risk Factor Modification

BP control (target <140/90 mmHg, consider <130 for lacunar stroke)
Statin therapy for LDL control
Glycemic optimization (HbA1c ≤53 mmol/mol)
Lifestyle: 40min exercise 3-4x/week, smoking cessation, alcohol reduction

4

Assess for Comorbid AD

If mixed dementia suspected: Consider cholinesterase inhibitor and/or memantine
Donepezil and galantamine show greatest effect on cognition in vascular cognitive impairment

Prevention & Risk Factor Control

Blood Pressure Control

  • Target: SBP <140 mmHg, DBP <90 mmHg
  • Consider SBP <130 mmHg for lacunar stroke
  • ACE inhibitors (e.g., perindopril) and calcium-channel blockers show benefit

Statin Therapy

  • Atorvastatin 40-80 mg - high intensity
  • Atorvastatin 10-20 mg - moderate intensity
  • Good evidence for secondary stroke prevention

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Exercise: 40 min moderate-vigorous 3-4x/week
  • Smoking: Complete cessation advised
  • Alcohol: Reduce intake or stop completely
  • Dietary modification for BP and glucose control

Diabetes Control

  • Target HbA1c: ≤53 mmol/mol (≤7%)
  • Screen for diabetes post-TIA/stroke
  • SGLT2 inhibitors may reduce dementia risk vs DPP-4 inhibitors

Behavioral & Psychological Symptoms

⚠️ Non-pharmacological approaches are FIRST LINE for BPSD

Always try environmental and behavioral interventions before medication. Pharmacological treatment has limited evidence and significant risks in vascular dementia.

Non-Pharmacological Strategies

  • Promote independent functioning as long as possible
  • Encourage social activities and routine
  • Explain actions in advance; use written instructions
  • Use calendars, clocks, charts for orientation
  • Scheduled toileting for incontinence
  • Personally-tailored activities reduce agitation
  • Music therapy, massage, animal-assisted intervention

Pharmacological (when needed)

  • Depression: SSRIs first-line (sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram)
  • Agitation: SSRIs (citalopram, sertraline) - avoid antipsychotics
  • AVOID all antipsychotics - stroke and mortality risk
  • AVOID TCAs - cardiovascular risk

Supportive Care

Patient & Family Education

  • Discuss expected symptom progression
  • Future care needs and early care planning
  • Advance directives and power of attorney
  • Carer support groups and resources

Home Safety Assessment

  • OT assessment for home safety
  • Driving assessment requirements
  • Falls risk assessment and interventions
  • Transport and self-care needs

Prognosis & Complications

Prognostic Factors

  • Post-stroke: up to 33% develop dementia within 5 years
  • Stepwise progression characteristic but gradual possible
  • Aggressive risk factor control may slow progression
  • Comorbid AD worsens prognosis

Complications to Monitor

  • Further cerebrovascular events (stroke/TIA)
  • Falls and injuries (gait disturbance)
  • Pressure injuries (reduced mobility)
  • Malnutrition and dehydration

Key Clinical Pearls

  • Prevention is paramount: Aggressive control of hypertension, diabetes, and smoking cessation can prevent further cerebrovascular damage
  • Executive dysfunction is key: Look for problems with planning, decision-making, and attention rather than just memory loss
  • Stepwise deterioration: Classic presentation but gradual decline also occurs; temporal relation to stroke events is diagnostic clue
  • Avoid antipsychotics: All antipsychotics are contraindicated due to increased stroke and mortality risk in vascular dementia
  • MRI is essential: Required for diagnosis; absence of cerebrovascular lesions argues against vascular etiology
  • Mixed dementia is common: Many patients have both vascular and Alzheimer's pathology; consider cholinesterase inhibitors if AD suspected
