Is it a TIA?

Definite TIA Possible TIA Usually NOT TIA
Definite TIA Characteristics (Click to expand)

Focal cerebral/retinal symptoms lasting seconds/minutes (typically <1h). Motor weakness in 2 limbs or 1 limb + face. Sensory deficit in 2 limbs or 1 limb + face. Homonymous hemianopia or monocular blindness. Aphasia/dysarthria. Unsteady gait. Diplopia.

Possible TIA Characteristics (Click to expand)

Vertigo/dizziness. Dysphagia. Isolated sensory deficit in one limb or face only. Unusual cortical visual symptoms (e.g., bilateral blindness).

Note: Patients with atypical TIA often present late and have higher recurrent event risk. If unsure, treat as TIA.

Usually NOT TIA (Click to expand)

Amnesia. Confusion. Transient loss of consciousness. Headache. Incoordination of limbs. Flashes and floaters. Complex visual hallucinations. Palinopsia. Isolated acute confusion.

Detailed Reference

Why TIAs Matter (Statistics)
  • 20% who have a TIA have a stroke within 3 months.
  • Highest risk: First 10 days (especially first 2 days).
  • 20-25% of ischaemic strokes are heralded by a TIA.
  • Strokes are the single biggest cause of disability in adults.
Rehabilitation & Post-Stroke

Rehabilitation: Should be specialist-led. Screen for malnutrition (MUST), swallowing issues, mood (depression), and fatigue.

Activity: Offer cardio/mixed training 30-40min, 3-5x/week for 10-20 weeks.

Returning to Work: Fatigue and subtle cognitive deficits are the main barriers. Early fluoxetine is not recommended for prophylaxis against depression.

Definitions & Updates

TIA Definition: Transient neurological symptoms without evidence of infarct on scan. However, in primary care without imaging, clinical diagnosis relies on symptoms resolving completely (usually within 24h).

Evolution: "Mini-strokes" (TIAs) likely represent minor ischaemic strokes on sensitive MRI. Management remains urgent regardless of the label.
