📌 Key Definitions

TermDefinition
OsteoporosisT-score ≤ −2.5 at femoral neck
Severe osteoporosisT-score ≤ −2.5 AND fragility fracture history
OsteopeniaT-score between −1 and −2.5
Fragility fractureFracture from fall ≤ standing height or spontaneous (e.g. vertebral)
Primary preventionNo fracture yet – identifying those at risk
Secondary preventionAlready had a fragility fracture

💡 Key Concept: Think Fracture, Not Just T-Score

Most people who fracture do NOT have a T-score ≤ −2.5.

Think of it like stroke & hypertension: the bad outcome is the fragility fracture (stroke). Osteoporosis (hypertension) is just one risk factor. Falls risk, age, medications & comorbidities all matter too.

A fragility fracture in a postmenopausal woman or man >50y = presumptive clinical diagnosis of osteoporosis, regardless of T-score.

Clinical Pathway (NOGG-focused)

1 Identify Risk

Consider risk assessment in postmenopausal women & men ≥50y with clinical risk factors (see Assess tab). Also anyone with a fragility fracture.

2 Calculate FRAX Score

Use FRAX (free online). Enter clinical risk factors. Can calculate with or without BMD. Consider adjustments for factors not captured (see Assess tab).

3 Click "View NOGG Guidance"

The FRAX website links to the NOGG treatment graph. This plots your patient's risk and tells you the next step:

LOW Reassure. Lifestyle advice. Reassess if risk factors change.
INTERMEDIATE → DXA scan. Recalculate FRAX with BMD. Re-check graph.
HIGH → DXA + start treatment (oral bisphosphonate likely first line).
VERY HIGH → DXA + consider anabolic therapy (teriparatide/romosozumab/abaloparatide) or specialist referral.

4 Baseline Investigations

For anyone with fragility fracture or starting treatment:
FBC, creatinine, LFTs, bone profile, TFTs, ESR/CRP, vitamin D, phosphate.
Consider spine X-ray to exclude silent vertebral fractures.

5 Start Treatment

Run safety checks (see Safety tab). Usually oral alendronate 70mg weekly first line. See Treat & Drugs tabs for full options.

6 Monitor & Review

Follow guidance-specific duration. Repeat DXA at end of treatment course. Re-assess FRAX. Decide: stop, continue, or pause & reassess.

⚠️ Fragility fracture in postmenopausal ♀ or ♂ >50y = presumptive osteoporosis. Do not be reassured by a 'non-osteoporotic' T-score. Treatment may still be indicated.

Risk Assessment

🔍 FRAX Risk Factors (captured in tool)

  • Age, sex, BMI, height, weight
  • Previous fragility fracture
  • Parental hip fracture
  • Current smoking
  • Alcohol ≥3 units/day
  • Glucocorticoids (≥5mg pred ≥3m current or past)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Secondary osteoporosis causes
  • Femoral neck BMD (optional)

⚡ FRAX Adjustments (NOGG) – factors NOT in the tool

Apply the single greatest adjustment. No evidence for stacking multiple adjustments.

Glucocorticoid dose ≥7.5mg/d↑ fracture probability by 15%

When to Request DXA

NOGG

DXA for anyone with intermediate, high or very-high risk on NOGG graph.

The FRAX → NOGG graph tells you when to scan!

NICE

"In the region of the intervention threshold" on treatment pathways.

Also DXA if age <40y with:

  • Steroids ≥7.5mg/d ≥3m
  • Fragility fracture
  • Untreated premature menopause
  • Starting drugs with rapid bone density effects (e.g. anti-androgens)

SIGN

DXA for everyone with 10y fracture risk ≥10% on initial assessment.

⚡ After DXA – Don't Stop There!

  • Re-enter BMD into FRAX and recalculate risk.
  • Re-check where the new score sits on the NOGG treatment graph.
  • Do not be quickly reassured by T-score > −2.5 → treatment may still be needed based on overall risk.

📋 DXA Reporting Notes

  • Results may be reported as T-score, Hologic, GE Lunar, or Select BMD.
  • Ensure you select the correct option in the FRAX drop-down.
  • Minimum 2 years between repeat DXA scans.

🚫 Other Imaging

NOGG does not recommend ultrasound, radiofrequency echographic multi-spectrometry, or IBEX bone health scans for diagnosis.

Treatment Decisions

🎯 Treatment by NOGG Risk Level

Risk LevelAction
LOW Lifestyle advice. Reassess if risk factors change.
INTERMEDIATE DXA scan → recalculate FRAX with BMD → re-check NOGG graph.
HIGH DXA + start oral bisphosphonate (usually alendronate). Consider HRT if ♀ ≤60y with low HRT risk.
VERY HIGH DXA + consider anabolic therapy first (teriparatide, romosozumab, or abaloparatide) OR specialist referral. Step down to antiresorptive after.

⏱️ Time to Benefit of Bisphosphonates

100 postmenopausal women with osteoporosis on bisphosphonate:

Outcome preventedTime to benefit
1 non-vertebral fracture12 months
1 vertebral fracture12 months (NNT 200 × 12m)
1 hip fracture20 months (NNT 200 × 20m)
1 any clinical fracture8 months (NNT 200 × 8m)

→ Benefit likely for those with life expectancy >6–12 months. Parenteral/anabolic therapies may work faster.

🔄 Treatment Duration (NOGG)

  • Alendronate / ibandronate / risedronate: Review after 5 years.
  • Zoledronate: Review after 3 years.
  • Teriparatide: Maximum 24 months, then step down to antiresorptive.
  • Romosozumab: Maximum 12 months, then step down.
  • Abaloparatide: Maximum 18 months, then step down.
  • Denosumab: Do NOT stop without specialist review → risk of rebound vertebral fractures within 18 months.

At review: repeat DXA + FRAX → decide: stop, continue, or pause & reassess.

♀ HRT for Osteoporosis

NOGG Consider HRT as first-line for women ≤60y at high risk where HRT-related adverse event risk is low.

HRT reduces vertebral # risk by 34%, non-vertebral # risk by 13%.

🚨 Fracture on Treatment

  • Does not necessarily = treatment failure, but review:
  • Check secondary causes (hyperparathyroidism, myeloma, vit D, thyroid)
  • Duration on treatment? (<1y = may not have reached benefit yet)
  • Adherence?
  • Consider repeat DXA if ≥3y on treatment
  • Consider bone turnover markers (wait ≥3–6m post-fracture)
  • Consider switching: oral → parenteral; parenteral → anabolic (specialist decision)
  • Bisphosphonates do NOT need to be stopped for normal fracture healing (unless atypical fracture)

📊 Efficacy Comparison (BMJ 2023 meta-analysis)

  • All treatments reduce symptomatic & vertebral fractures.
  • Anabolic drugs (denosumab, romosozumab, PTH receptor agonists) are more effective than oral bisphosphonates at preventing clinical & vertebral fractures.
  • This was irrespective of baseline risk → authors argue anabolic treatments should not be restricted only to highest-risk cases.
  • Limited head-to-head trial data overall.

Drug Safety & Pre-Prescribing Checks

✅ Pre-Prescribing Checklist – Oral Bisphosphonates

  • Check & correct calcium – treat hypocalcaemia first
  • Check & correct vitamin D deficiency first
  • Check creatinine/eGFR – threshold varies by drug (check BNF)
  • Advise dental check – complete invasive dental work before starting
  • Assess GI history – oesophageal disease, Barrett's, stricture, achalasia, recent major GI surgery
  • Can patient sit/stand upright for ≥30 minutes?
  • Counsel on how to take: empty stomach, plain water, stay upright 30min, ≥30min before food/other meds
  • If calcium supplements needed: take ≥2 hours apart from bisphosphonate
  • Subtrochanteric or diaphyseal (shaft) fracture. Presents with thigh pain (bilateral in 50%).
  • Risk increases with duration, especially >5y.
  • 3–5y: 2.5 per 10,000 person-years
  • 5–8y: 5 per 10,000 person-years
  • >8y: 13.1 per 10,000 person-years
  • Risk drops rapidly to baseline after stopping bisphosphonates.
  • Higher risk: Asian ethnicity, shorter height, increased weight, steroid use ≥1y.
  • Benefits still outweigh risks even in higher-risk groups.
  • Oral mucosal ulceration exposing bone. May be painless initially. REFER!
  • All patients: dental assessment before starting bisphosphonate or denosumab.
  • Complete necessary dental treatment prior to starting if possible.
  • Rarely: osteonecrosis of external ear canal (report ear pain/discharge – MHRA 2015).

Dyspepsia: ~7% affected. Due to oesophageal irritation, NOT acid reflux.

Do NOT use a PPI for bisphosphonate-related dyspepsia because:
  • Acid reflux is not the cause
  • PPIs may blunt alendronic acid effectiveness
  • PPIs reduce calcium absorption
  • PPIs may increase fracture risk in smokers/ex-smokers

Oesophageal cancer: Evidence mixed. Reinforce correct dosing technique. Tell patients to report dysphagia, chest/throat discomfort.

⚠️ MHRA 2020: Do NOT stop denosumab without specialist review. Increased risk of multiple vertebral fractures within 18 months of stopping or delaying treatment.
  • Must step down to alternative bone-modifying drug.
  • Associated with severe hypocalcaemia – monitor calcium before each dose.
  • Consider indication prescribing or adding alerts to patient record.
  • Contraindicated if ANY history of CVD or stroke.
  • Contraindicated in hypocalcaemia.
  • Licensed in women only. Maximum 12 months.
  • Step down to antiresorptive after to prevent rebound bone loss.
  • Cytokine-mediated reaction within 72h of infusion.
  • Fever, myalgia, arthralgia – lasts 2–3 days.
  • ~⅓ patients affected after first infusion; less with subsequent doses.
  • Transient mild hypocalcaemia possible – can be severe if vitamin D deficient.
  • Check renal function carefully – especially in elderly/frail.

Special Populations

  • 40% of fragility fractures occur in men; higher mortality than women.
  • >50% have an underlying cause (hypogonadism, prostate Rx, steroids, alcohol).
  • SIGN recommendations for men:
    • Secondary prevention: risedronate or zoledronic acid (not alendronate – insufficient evidence).
    • Primary prevention on high-dose steroids: alendronate (10mg daily – licensed for men; 70mg weekly is NOT), risedronate, or zoledronic acid.
  • NOGG: Men starting anti-androgens → baseline FRAX + BMD. High/very-high risk → refer for denosumab/bisphosphonates. Re-assess at 12–18m.
  • Men <50y with osteoporosis → specialist referral.
  • Under-represented in trials. Higher treatment risks (polypharmacy, renal impairment).
  • BUT: highest falls risk; fracture devastating for QoL/independence.
  • FRAX may underestimate risk in healthy older patients (competing mortality).
  • QFracture may be more appropriate in this group.
  • Bisphosphonates benefit BMD and reduce hip/non-vertebral # at least as much as in younger patients.
  • Time to benefit: ~12m for non-vertebral #. Parenteral/anabolic may be faster.
  • Shared decision: Likely beneficial if life expectancy >6–12 months.
  • ⚠️ Check renal function carefully with IV bisphosphonates or denosumab.
  • NICE multimorbidity guidance: discuss stopping bisphosphonate after 3y (consider patient choice, fracture risk, life expectancy).
  • Prevalence: 2× that of osteoporosis. More absolute fractures come from this group.
  • Do NOT be reassured by "non-osteoporotic" T-score.
  • Action:
    • Identify clinical risk factors.
    • Measure height/weight (low BMI? height loss?).
    • Check for thoracic kyphosis.
    • Assess fracture risk → follow NOGG pathway.
  • SIGN: Consider zoledronic acid 18-monthly for 6y in those with T-score −1 to −2.5 AND ≥65y.
  • If not treating: reassess if risk profile changes.

Vertebral fractures (even asymptomatic) are a strong risk factor for future fracture.

Consider spine X-ray (lateral lumbar + thoracic) if postmenopausal ♀ or ♂ ≥50y with:

  • Acute-onset back pain + osteoporosis risk factors
  • Height loss ≥4cm
  • Thoracic kyphosis
  • Recent or current long-term glucocorticoid use
  • T-score ≤ −2.5 at spine or hip

Management:

  • NOGG does NOT recommend spinal bracing or vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty.
  • Teriparatide superior to risedronate for preventing vertebral # in postmenopausal women with ≥2 moderate or 1 severe vertebral # AND T-score ≤ −1.5.
  • Opportunistic diagnosis: if vertebral # seen on imaging done for other reasons → trigger secondary prevention.
  • Assess baseline fracture risk when starting these drugs.
  • High risk (NOGG red zone) → start bone protection (bisphosphonates or denosumab).
  • Men at high risk → consider referral to secondary care.
  • Near threshold → repeat FRAX + DXA after 12–18 months.
  • Long-term use (>2y) can reduce bone density – reverses on stopping.
  • Only small increase in absolute fracture risk, even in older women.
  • No fracture risk assessment needed unless other risk factors present.
  • MHRA: re-evaluate risks/benefits every 2y. In <18y, can use first line if others unsuitable.
  • FSRH: use in women >45y is UKMEC2. Generally switch at age 50y.
  • For significant osteoporosis risk factors → consider alternative contraception.
  • FRAX identified only 45% of women <65y with known osteoporosis as "at risk".
  • WHI data: FRAX identified only ~⅓ of women aged 50–64y at increased risk.
  • Consider clinical judgement and QFracture as alternative in this group.

Corticosteroid-Induced Osteoporosis

⚡ NOGG: Start Bone Protection IMMEDIATELY (regardless of FRAX) if:

  • Any man or woman with previous fragility fracture
  • Any woman ≥70y on ANY glucocorticoid dose
  • Postmenopausal women or men ≥50y:
    • On high dose (≥7.5mg/d prednisolone equivalent ≥3m)
    • If FRAX score is in yellow section but above the intervention line

→ Still do FRAX to decide if DXA needed, but don't delay treatment waiting for DXA.

📋 Everyone Else on Steroids

Calculate FRAX (tick "glucocorticoids" box) and follow your chosen guidance.

FRAX accuracy with steroids:
  • Low dose (<2.5mg/d): FRAX may overestimate risk by 20%
  • High dose (≥7.5mg/d): FRAX may underestimate risk by 15%

In younger people: bone protection may be indicated on high-dose steroids (≥7.5mg/d) or with previous fracture. Caution in women of childbearing age.

💊 Drug Options for Steroid-Induced Osteoporosis

All now licensed for glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis:

  • Alendronate – likely first line (cost, convenience)
  • Risedronate
  • Zoledronic acid – consider for high-dose steroids
  • Denosumab
  • Teriparatide

Everyone needs: lifestyle advice + adequate calcium + vitamin D.

🔄 Key Steroid Points

  • Fracture risk rises immediately on starting steroids.
  • Risk returns to normal ~12 months after stopping.
  • Affects bone quality as well as density.
  • If steroids stopped → reassess FRAX → may be able to stop bone-modifying treatment.
  • NOGG: Significant steroid load = 30mg/day for 4 weeks within any 3-month period.
  • BTS/SIGN asthma guidance: Those on frequent oral courses (3–4/year) → monitor BMD; if significant reduction → offer bisphosphonate.
  • NOGG: Significant steroid load = ≥30mg/day for 4 weeks within any 3-month period.

SIGN: Insufficient evidence to make recommendations. Some trials showed impact on bone density, others did not.

Monitoring Treatment

NOGG

Repeat DXA at end of treatment course. Use alongside FRAX to determine future management.

NICE

After 3y zoledronate or 5y oral bisphosphonate → DXA → continue if T-score <−2.5.

Exception – continue WITHOUT DXA if:

  • Age ≥75y
  • Previous hip or vertebral #
  • # while on bone treatment
  • On steroids ≥7.5mg/d

SIGN

DXA at 3y may identify those at greater fracture risk.

📏 DXA Monitoring Notes

  • Minimum 2 years between DXA scans.
  • Even a fall in BMD of up to 4% on treatment may still be associated with reduced fractures – don't despair, but check compliance!
  • If BMD falls >4% → no fracture reduction seen in trials.

🧪 Bone Turnover Markers (P1NP, CTX)

  • SIGN: Role remains unclear, but "may be considered".
  • NOGG: Consider markers at 3m and 6m post-zoledronate to guide treatment intervals.
  • Future: likely more important with "treat-to-target" approach.
  • Currently a specialist decision, not for primary care.

⏸️ Drug Holidays vs Drug Retirement

  • Drug holidays (pause then restart) are NOT supported by evidence. Do not reduce adverse skeletal effects.
  • At end of treatment, options are:
    1. Stop ("drug retirement") – low-risk, multimorbidity/frailty
    2. Continue – high-risk individuals
    3. Pause & reassess – NOGG only, for some patients
  • ⚠️ NEVER stop denosumab without specialist review.

Lifestyle Advice & Calcium/Vitamin D

🏃 Lifestyle Modification (SIGN + NOGG)

  • 🚭 Stop smoking
  • 🍺 Reduce alcohol (NOGG: ≤2 units/day)
  • 🏋️ Weight-bearing activity – may slow bone density decline
  • 🧘 Balance, flexibility & stretching – reduce falls risk
  • ⚡ Falls risk assessment – refer at-risk patients to falls exercise programme
  • 🥗 Balanced diet – maintain BMI 20–25. No specific diet proven superior.
  • 🚫 Do NOT recommend dietary phyto-oestrogens, vitamin K supplements, or acid-balancing diets (SIGN).

🥛 Calcium

  • Aim for 700mg/day through diet (use CGEM calculator).
  • Consider supplements only if dietary intake inadequate and cannot be increased.
  • Calcium alone is NOT adequate to treat osteoporosis.
  • Take calcium supplements ≥2h apart from bisphosphonates.
  • Calcium NOT routinely required with bisphosphonates (unless dietary intake low).
  • USE calcium alongside denosumab in renal impairment.
  • Calcium supplements are NOT recommended for primary prevention of osteoporosis.
Cardiovascular risk: NOGG concludes calcium (± vitamin D) does NOT increase CV risk (JAMA 2018 data).

☀️ Vitamin D

  • Vitamin D alone is NOT adequate to treat osteoporosis.
  • Consider supplements (800 IU/day – NOGG) if:
    • Vitamin D deficiency confirmed
    • Risk factors for deficiency (housebound, covered skin, low sunlight)
    • Living in institutions
  • Rule out vitamin D deficiency before starting potent antiresorptives (zoledronate, denosumab).

🍖 Other Dietary Measures (BMJ 2025)

  • Vitamin K: Insufficient evidence.
  • Protein: Effect on BMD uncertain. Expert opinion: >1g/kg/day, increasing to 1.5g/kg/day if at risk of malnutrition (sarcopenia = fracture risk factor).
  • Vibration therapy: Some evidence for preventing lumbar spine bone loss, but very high cumulative dose needed (~7000 minutes!) and measured on BMD not fractures. Not recommended routinely.
