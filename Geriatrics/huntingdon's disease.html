Huntington's Disease Management

Assessment

Key Diagnostic Factors

CAG Repeat Expansion
≥40 CAG repeats - definite disease development
36-39 repeats - reduced penetrance
29-35 repeats - normal but may expand in offspring
1
Family History
May be absent in 5-8% of cases due to false paternity, early death, or misdiagnosis
2
Cognitive Impairment
Concentration problems, task apathy, increased errors, impaired work performance
3
Behavioral Changes
Irritability, impulsivity, personality changes, disinhibition
4
Motor Features
Chorea, dystonia, twitching, bradykinesia, loss of coordination

Examination Findings

Motor Impersistence
Inability to maintain tongue protrusion or eye closure for 10 seconds. "Milkmaid's grip" in advanced disease.
Saccadic Eye Movements
Slowed rapid eye movements. Prominent in juvenile Huntington's disease.
Tandem Walking
Difficulty with heel-to-toe walking reveals early balance problems.
Finger Tapping
Slowed, irregular rhythm compared to examiner. Should achieve 25 taps in 5 seconds.

High-Risk Features

Suicide Risk

Proactively assess suicidality at every visit. Depression is the most serious treatable problem. Risk factors include family history of suicide, social isolation, access to lethal means.

Juvenile Onset

More likely inherited from father due to CAG repeat expansion during spermatogenesis. Presents with rigidity, bradykinesia, and cognitive decline rather than chorea.

Diagnosis

Genetic Testing

Pre-test Genetic Counseling Required
Discuss implications for insurance, family planning, and psychological impact before testing.
1
CAG Repeat Analysis
Takes several weeks. Arrange post-test counseling for result delivery.
Interpretation of Results
≥40 repeats: Huntington's disease certain

36-39 repeats: May or may not develop symptoms

29-35 repeats: Normal, but may expand in offspring

<28 repeats: Normal result

Differential Diagnosis

Tardive Dyskinesia
History of antipsychotic use. Oral/buccal/lingual predominance. No motor impersistence. CAG repeat testing normal.
DRPLA
Dentatorubropallidoluysian atrophy. More common in Asian populations. Myoclonic epilepsy in juvenile cases. Test atrophin-1 gene.
Neuroacanthocytosis
Autosomal recessive. Areflexia, amyotrophy, tongue biting. Acanthocytes on blood smear. Elevated CK.
McLeod Syndrome
X-linked. May have cardiomyopathy, neuropathy, hemolytic anemia. Abnormality in XK gene.
Wilson's Disease
Decreased ceruloplasmin, copper abnormalities, Kayser-Fleischer rings. Resembles juvenile Huntington's.
Benign Hereditary Chorea
Early onset, very slow progression, without cognitive dysfunction or disabling features.

Imaging Studies

Role of MRI/CT

Not helpful in early diagnosis. Caudate/striatal atrophy appears consistently in later stages but is not specific to Huntington's disease.

Treatment

Multidisciplinary Approach

All patients benefit from multidisciplinary care combining pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions. The goal is symptom reduction, quality of life improvement, and psychosocial support.

Physical Therapy
Improves fitness, motor function, and gait. Home exercise programs beneficial in early-mid stages.
Speech Therapy
For speech, language, and swallowing difficulties. May provide transient benefit.
Nutritional Support
High-calorie density foods, avoid foods exacerbating dysphagia. Dietitian consultation recommended.
Driving Assessment
Independent assessment helpful to determine if driving remains safe.

Depression Management

Most Serious Treatable Problem

Treatment vital - reduces suicide risk. Proactively assess suicidality at every visit.

First-Line: SSRIs
Citalopram: 20-40 mg once daily
Sertraline: 50-200 mg once daily (preferred if irritability/anxiety present)
Escitalopram: 10-20 mg once daily
Preferred if patient may be prescribed tetrabenazine (less CYP2D6 inhibition)
Second-Line/Alternatives
Mirtazapine: 15-45 mg at bedtime
Rapid onset, helpful for insomnia
Adjunctive Therapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and counseling extremely helpful. Evidence from other neurodegenerative diseases supports psychotherapy.

Refractory Depression
Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT): Can provide significant benefit without aggravating other Huntington's features

Chorea Treatment

Important: Establish if chorea significantly impacts daily activities before treatment. Mild chorea may not require pharmacotherapy.
Tetrabenazine (VMAT2 Inhibitor)
Start: 12.5 mg morning for 1 week
Week 2: 12.5 mg twice daily
Titrate: Increase by 12.5 mg weekly
Maximum: 50 mg/day (up to 100 mg/day in CYP2D6 extensive metabolizers)
Advantages: No tardive dyskinesia risk, shorter onset/offset
Disadvantages: More depression and sedation
Contraindicated in active suicidality or untreated depression
Atypical Antipsychotics
Olanzapine: 2.5-10 mg daily (max 30 mg)
Risperidone: 0.5-2 mg daily (max 10 mg)
Quetiapine: 25-750 mg daily
Preferred for patients with co-existent behavioral problems, weight loss, or need for acute management

Behavioral & Psychiatric Symptoms

Irritability & Aggression
SSRIs: Citalopram 20-40 mg (upper end of range)
Atypical Antipsychotics: Olanzapine, quetiapine
Mood Stabilizers: Valproic acid 250-500 mg TID
Benzodiazepines: Clonazepam (if anxiety/insomnia prominent)
Obsessive-Compulsive Behaviors
SSRIs: High-dose, long-duration therapy (citalopram up to 60 mg, fluoxetine up to 80 mg)
Alternative: Clomipramine 25-250 mg daily
Antipsychotics: Olanzapine may be effective
Anxiety
SSRIs: Citalopram 20-40 mg
Benzodiazepines: Clonazepam 0.25-4 mg/day
Adjunct: CBT and psychotherapy

Bradykinesia & Rigidity

Treatment Options
Levodopa/Carbidopa: 10/100 mg 3-4 times daily, titrate to max 200/2000 mg
Response should be apparent by 1 month, otherwise discontinue

Amantadine: 100 mg twice daily, max 300 mg
Evaluate at 3 weeks, discontinue if no response

Emerging Therapies

AMT-130 Gene Therapy
Phase 1/2 - Positive Results

RNA interference therapy targeting mutant huntingtin. September 2025 data showed 75% slowing of disease progression at 36 months. FDA breakthrough therapy designation. Potential BLA submission in 2026.

Deutetrabenazine & Valbenazine
FDA Approved (US)

VMAT2 inhibitors with longer-acting properties. Not yet approved in Europe. May have fewer adverse effects than tetrabenazine.

Antisense Oligonucleotides
Phase 3 Completed

GENERATION-HD1 showed no clinical improvement. GENERATION-HD2 ongoing in younger patients with lower disease burden.

Follow-up

Monitoring Schedule

6
Visit Frequency: Every 6-12 months or more often as problems arise
Even brief visits useful for identifying carer fatigue or critical function loss
Assessment Domains
Early Stage: Employment loss, complex home management tasks
Mid Stage: Driving cessation, adult responsibilities
Late Stage: Placement, feeding issues, end-of-life management

Common Complications

Weight Loss
High likelihood, long-term
Dysphagia-related and metabolic defect. Manage with high-calorie density foods, dietitian consultation.
Dysphagia
High likelihood, late manifestation
Reduce portion size, select easy-to-swallow foods, use thickeners. Vaccinate against pneumonia and influenza.
Falls
High likelihood, late manifestation
Indicates need for assist devices (frame, wheelchair). May precipitate nursing home placement.
Incontinence
Medium likelihood, end-stage
Common precipitating cause for nursing home placement. Anticholinergics may help partially.
Suicide
High risk throughout disease
Restrict access to means, maintain social connections, treat depression aggressively.

Prognosis & Disease Course

Disease Duration

Approximately 20 years from diagnosis to death. Sequential evolution varies among individuals and families.

UHDRS Staging
Stage 1: Minor work performance changes
Stage 2: Marginal work capacity, financial problems
Stage 3: Unable to manage work/finances/domestic tasks
Stage 4: Requires skilled care
Clinical Course

Chorea may lessen as disease progresses and bradykinesia/rigidity increase. Behavioral problems may fluctuate with cognitive changes. Dose reductions should be periodically attempted.

Palliative & End-of-Life Care

A
Advance Care Planning
Discuss end-of-life wishes, power of attorney, treatment refusals when patient still has decision-making capacity
B
Palliative Approach
Integrated throughout disease course to address difficult symptoms
Family Planning

Discuss pre-implantation genetic testing with at-risk offspring before serious relationships. Highlight emotional/financial implications of caring for affected spouse while raising children.
