Assessment & Screening

Initial evaluation and risk stratification for suspected sarcopenia

SARC-F Questionnaire Screening

First-line screening tool recommended by EWGSOP2 and ICSFR. Score ≥4 indicates risk of sarcopenia.

Strength
How much difficulty do you have lifting/carrying 10 lbs?
Ambulation
How much difficulty walking across a room?
Rising from Chair
How much difficulty transferring from chair/bed?
Climbing Stairs
How much difficulty climbing 10 steps?
Falls
How many falls in the past year?

Positive Screen

Total Score ≥4 → Further assessment warranted

OR

Calf Circumference: <34cm (men) / <33cm (women)

Physical Performance Tests Assessment

  • Hand Grip Strength (HGS) <27kg (men) / <16kg (women)
  • Sit-Stand Test (5 reps) >15 seconds
  • Gait Speed (usual) <1.0 m/s
  • Short Physical Performance Battery ≤8 points
  • Timed Up-and-Go ≥20 seconds
    • Low muscle strength alone is sufficient to diagnose probable sarcopenia

    Risk Factors Caution

    Age
    ≥65 years primary risk factor
    BMI
    Low (<18.5) or High (≥30)
    Lifestyle
    Sedentary, physical inactivity
    Protein Intake
    <0.8g/kg/day (low)
    Comorbidities
    COPD, CHF, CKD, cancer
    Medications
    Corticosteroids, GnRH agonists

    Initial Investigations Routine

    Rule out secondary causes and assess baseline status:

    FBC & Electrolytes
    Liver & Renal Function
    Vitamin D Level
    Thyroid Function
    Blood Glucose
    Testosterone (if indicated)

    Diagnosis & Confirmation

    Confirm diagnosis and determine severity using validated criteria

    EWGSOP2 Criteria Primary

    1
    Probable Sarcopenia
    Low muscle strength: HGS <27kg (men) or <16kg (women)
    2
    Confirmed Sarcopenia
    Low strength + Low muscle quantity/quality (DXA/CT/MRI)
    DXA ASMI: <7.0kg/m² (men) / <5.5kg/m² (women)
    3
    Severe Sarcopenia
    Confirmed + Low physical performance (gait speed ≤0.8m/s)

    AWGS Criteria Asia-Pacific

    • Low Grip Strength <28kg (men) / <18kg (women)
    • Low Physical Performance Gait <1.0m/s or SPPB ≤9
    • DXA ASMI <7.0kg/m² (men) / <5.4kg/m² (women)
    • BIA ASMI <7.0kg/m² (men) / <5.7kg/m² (women)

    Muscle Mass Assessment Imaging

    DXA
    Gold standard - low radiation, accessible
    CT
    High radiation - measures muscle quality
    MRI
    No radiation - detailed muscle volume/quality
    BIA
    Portable, inexpensive - less accurate
    If imaging unavailable, treatment may be initiated based on low muscle strength alone

    Differential Diagnosis Rule Out

    Parkinson's Disease

    Bradykinesia, rigidity, resting tremor
    Clinical diagnosis, neuroimaging if needed

    ALS

    Muscle spasms, stiffness, speech/dyspnea
    EMG, nerve conduction studies

    Hyperthyroidism

    Heat intolerance, sweating, palpitations
    Thyroid profile, antibodies

    Cachexia

    Rapid muscle/fat loss with underlying disease
    Blood tests, imaging for malignancy

    Treatment & Management

    Evidence-based interventions to prevent and treat sarcopenia

    Resistance Training First-Line

    First-line treatment for all patients with confirmed sarcopenia.

    Frequency: 2-3 times/week
    Intensity: ~80% 1RM (high intensity superior)
    Sets/Reps: 1-3 sets of 8-12 reps
    Exercises: Free weights, resistance bands, body weight
    • Increases muscle protein synthesis and fiber size
    • Targets fast-twitch (type II) fibers specifically
    • Combine with balance training initially if needed
    • Consider supervised program for frail patients

    Expected Improvements

    After 18 weeks high-intensity training:

    • Leg press: +29% (+31.6kg)
    • Knee extension: +33% (+12.1kg)
    • Chest press: +24% (+9.83kg)

    Protein Intake Essential

    Combine with resistance exercise for optimal outcomes.

    Target: 1.2g/kg body weight/day
    Per meal: 25-30g protein
    Sources: Lean meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes
    Distribution: Evenly across all meals
    Protein supplementation without exercise has not proven effective

    Note: Older adults have anabolic resistance—require higher protein intake than younger individuals.

    Aerobic Exercise Adjunct

    Important for overall health but not a substitute for resistance training.

    WHO Guidelines: 150 min moderate or 75 min vigorous/week
    Frequency: 20-60 min sessions, 3-7 times/week
    Types: Walking, cycling, swimming
    Benefits: Cardiovascular, mitochondrial function

    Walking is highly accessible and beneficial—do not overlook as primary activity.

    Emerging Therapies Investigational

    Currently no effective drug therapy available. Following are under investigation:

    • Myostatin inhibitors: ACE-083, bimagrumab - limited functional improvement
    • Omega-3 fatty acids: May enhance anabolism
    • HMB (β-hydroxy β-methylbutyrate): Mixed evidence
    • Leucine: May overcome anabolic resistance
    ICSFR states evidence base is very limited for these interventions in older adults with sarcopenia

    Patient Education Essential

    1
    Dietitian Referral
    Review dietary habits, increase energy/protein intake
    2
    Exercise Program
    Structured supervised program increases success odds
    3
    Active Walking
    Complement training sessions between workouts

    Follow-up & Monitoring

    Track progress and manage complications

    Monitoring Protocol Standard

    Reassess after 8-12 weeks of intervention

    Physical Performance: Gait speed, chair stand, grip strength
    Anthropometrics: Calf circumference (<34cm men / <33cm women)
    Imaging: DXA superior but limited by accessibility
    Frequency: Annual review reasonable after initial phase
    If no improvement identified → check adherence, consider secondary causes, intolerance to supplements, or poor nutrition

    Complications to Monitor Important

    Hospitalization Medium Risk

    Sarcopenia is a significant predictor of hospitalization. Exercise may prevent.

    Falls & Fractures Medium Risk

    Associated with osteosarcopenia. Manage alongside osteoporosis treatment.

    Care Home Admission Medium Risk

    Consequence of progression to frailty. Requires physiotherapy.

    Chemotherapy Toxicity Medium Risk

    Increased sensitivity in cancer patients

    Mortality Low Risk

    Independent risk factor - highlights need for early intervention

    Post-op Infections Medium Risk

    Independent risk factor in surgical patients

    Prognosis Information

    • Progression: Muscle weakening can progress to loss of function and disability
    • Interventions: Nutrition and exercise training can mitigate progression
    • Chronic sarcopenia: ≥6 months duration associated with chronic/progressive conditions
    • Key message: Early screening and intervention reduce morbidity/mortality burden
    Improvement: Weeks to months with adherence
    Timeline: Building muscle mass takes weeks
