Delirium & Hypoactive Delirium

Quick-Scan Clinical Reference • Focus on Investigation & Management

THINK DELIRIUM (NICE)

Immediate Recognition

Recent Changes (Hours-Days)

  • Cognitive: Worsened concentration, slow responses, confusion.
  • Physical: Reduced mobility, restlessness, agitation, sleep disturbance.
  • Social/Behaviour: Withdrawal, difficulty engaging, altered mood.
  • Perception: Visual or auditory hallucinations.

High Risk of "Missed" Diagnosis

Be extra vigilant if:

  • Hypoactive subtype: Quiet, withdrawn, "spaced out". (Don't mistake for depression!)
  • Pre-existing dementia.
  • Age 80y+.
  • Impaired vision.
Do Not Miss

Hypoactive Delirium

Why it's missed: Patients are quiet. They do not make a fuss. It is easily confused with depression or dementia.

Key Distinguishing Feature: An abrupt change in mental state is the hallmark of delirium.

Depression: Activity reduction is NOT abrupt, no fluctuation, consciousness intact.
Dementia: Usually slow progression. Abrupt change = Delirium superimposed on Dementia.

Outcome: Hypoactive delirium has poorer outcomes (increased mortality) than hyperactive, despite looking less "urgent".

Investigation

Finding the Cause (PINCH ME)

Rule: Look for reversible causes. No standard battery—target based on history/exam. Caution: UTI is rarely the sole cause.

PINCH ME

P - Pain

I - Infection

N - Nutrition

C - Constipation

H - Hydration

M - Medication

E - Environment

(+) - Plus: Falls, Head Injury, Anticoagulation

View Predisposing Factors (CccASE+F)

Confusion (Dementia), cognitive impairment, comorbidity, Age >65, Sensory impairment, Environmental factors, Frailty (Biggest risk).

View High-Risk Drugs
  • Benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, Opioids
  • Anticholinergics (e.g., Oxybutynin)
  • Antihistamines, H2-blockers
  • Antipsychotics, Antiparkinsonians
  • Tricyclic antidepressants
  • Alcohol withdrawal
Management Plan

NICE Priorities (Updated 2023)

  1. Manage acute life-threatening causes immediately.
  2. Detect & Treat Underlying Causes (See PINCH ME above).
  3. Manage Distress/Agitation:
    • Address pain.
    • De-escalation & reduce stimulation.
    • No evidence for antipsychotics/benzos for routine treatment.
  4. Prevent Complications: Falls, aspiration, dehydration.
  5. Communicate: Explain to patient & relatives. Debrief after recovery.
Decision Support

When to Admit?

Most patients need admission.
If refuses, consider Mental Capacity Act.

Consider treating at home ONLY if:

  • Clear cause identified & treatable.
  • Patient is safe & calm.
  • Family/carers can manage symptoms.
  • Close clinical follow-up (inc. continuous nursing if needed) arranged.
  • Palliative context (sometimes appropriate).

Review at 24–48h: Admit if no response to initial treatment.

Prognosis & Dementia

Delirium is linked to increased risk of death (1.5x) and diagnosis of dementia within 5 years (3x risk). It may unmask pre-existing dementia or accelerate decline.

Audit Points

  • Review last 10 delirium codes: Was debriefing offered?
  • Check care home residents diagnosed with depression for possible hypoactive delirium.
