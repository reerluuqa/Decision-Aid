Delirium and hypoactive delirium

Last reviewed: August 2025

Susan was 86 years old and a new resident at the local care home. The staff were concerned that she had been withdrawn at times, appearing spaced out and distant. At other times, she seemed more like her usual self. In hospital, Susan was diagnosed with hypoactive delirium, secondary to constipation. Hypoactive delirium is a subtype of delirium that I had not previously appreciated properly; her case made me realise how many times I had probably missed this over the years.

Delirium is covered in a 2019 SIGN guideline (SIGN 157) and several BMJ articles considering different subtypes; we have amalgamated the salient points below.

Headlines

Prevalence is higher than we might perhaps anticipate. A systematic review of older adults living in the community concluded that:

  • In older adults living at home, prevalence was 0.2–8%.
  • In those who were frail and receiving care at home, prevalence was 1.4–44%.
  • For over-65s in long-term care, earlier research suggests that prevalence can be up to 34%.

Types of delirium

There are three subtypes of delirium: Hypoactive, Hyperactive, and Mixed.

Hypoactive delirium

Hypoactive delirium may be missed because patients are quiet and withdrawn; they do not make a fuss (very different from hyperactive delirium). Key features to help distinguish:

  • Depression: reduction in activity levels should not have an abrupt onset, fluctuations or any reduction in consciousness.
  • Dementia: can cause withdrawal and apathy, but an abrupt change in mental state is more indicative of delirium.

While easier to miss than its hyperactive twin, it is at least as clinically significant and actually has poorer outcomes, including increased mortality.

Diagnosis of delirium

THINK DELIRIUM!

One of the key challenges is that we need to consider delirium as a possibility far more often than we do. NICE reminds us of the following changes that can indicate delirium (NICE CG103, updated 2023):

  • Cognitive function: worsened concentration, slow responses, confusion.
  • Physical function: reduced mobility, restlessness, sleep disturbance.
  • Social functioning: withdrawal, altered communication.
  • Perception: visual or auditory hallucinations.

Which tool should we use?

NICE recommends use of 4AT by a health or social care practitioner competent to do the assessment. It is accurate, evidence-based, and does not require special training.

Score interpretation

  • ≥4: Possible delirium or cognitive impairment.
  • 1–3: Possible cognitive impairment.
  • 0: Delirium and cognitive impairment unlikely.
