Delirium Assessment Protocol

Quick Reference Clinical Tool

LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCIES - Rule Out First

Sepsis / Severe Infection

EMERGENCY

  • Urgent lactate, blood cultures, antibiotics within 1 hour
  • Look for: tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension, fever/hypothermia
  • Common sources: UTI, pneumonia, intra-abdominal

Myocardial Infarction

EMERGENCY

  • Delirium may be ONLY sign in elderly
  • ECG + Troponin for ALL delirium patients
  • Atypical presentation common in older adults

Hypoglycaemia

EMERGENCY

  • Glucose < 3.9 mmol/L (<70 mg/dL)
  • Symptoms: confusion, sweating, tremor, seizures, coma
  • Check glucose IMMEDIATELY in all delirium

Wernicke's Encephalopathy

EMERGENCY

  • Thiamine deficiency - give thiamine IV immediately
  • Classic triad: confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia (often incomplete)
  • High risk in alcohol misuse, bariatric surgery, malnutrition

Stroke / TIA

URGENT

  • Focal neurological signs - consider CT/MRI
  • 15.6% of elderly delirium patients have abnormal CT
  • Look for: unilateral weakness, speech disturbance, visual changes

Hypoxia / Hypercapnia

EMERGENCY

  • SpO₂ < 95% or PaCO₂ > 6.5 kPa
  • Causes: PE, pneumonia, COPD exacerbation, cardiac failure
  • Check ABG and pulse oximetry

Urgent Considerations

  • Drug toxicity / withdrawal
    Review: sedatives, anticholinergics, opioids, benzodiazepines, alcohol
  • Metabolic abnormalities
    Na, K, Ca, renal failure, hepatic encephalopathy
  • Hyperglycaemia / DKA / HHS
    Check glucose; may present with confusion
  • Hip fracture / acute pain
    Occult fracture in frail elderly - check hips
  • Endocrine: Adrenal crisis / Myxoedema
    TSH, cortisol if suspected
  • Acute urinary obstruction
    Palpable bladder, suprapubic tenderness
  • Meningitis / Encephalitis
    Neck stiffness, headache, photophobia, fever

Screening Tools

1. Alertness

Assess baseline alertness. Any abnormality suggests delirium or other pathology.

2. AMT4 (Abbreviated Mental Test)

Age, date of birth, place (building/floor), year. Score <4 suggests cognitive impairment.

3. Attention (Months Backwards)

Ask patient to recite months in reverse. Inability suggests attention deficit.

4. Acute Change / Fluctuation

Evidence of acute change from baseline, or fluctuating course.

Feature 1: Acute Onset & Fluctuating Course

Is there evidence of acute change? Does behaviour fluctuate during the day?

Feature 2: Inattention

Difficulty focusing attention. Easily distracted or cannot follow conversation.

Feature 3: Disorganised Thinking

Rambling conversation, irrelevant conversation, illogical flow of ideas.

Feature 4: Altered Level of Consciousness

Any level other than alert (e.g., vigilant, lethargic, stupor, coma).

Diagnosis: CAM Positive = Feature 1 + 2 AND either 3 or 4

1. Acute Onset / Fluctuating Course

Assess using ICU sedation chart or talk to family/staff.

2. Inattention

SAVEAHAART or letters attention test. Eyes open, sustained attention.

3. Altered Level of Consciousness

RASS score other than 0 indicates possible delirium.

4. Disorganised Thinking

Yes/no questions and command following.

Initial Workup

  • Point-of-care glucose
    First test - rule out hypoglycaemia
  • FBC, U&E, LFT
    Infection, anaemia, electrolytes, renal/hepatic
  • Urinalysis + culture
    UTI common cause in elderly
  • 12-lead ECG
    MI, arrhythmia - delirium may be only sign
  • Chest X-ray
    Pneumonia, heart failure, hypoxia causes
  • Arterial / Venous Blood Gas
    Hypoxia, hypercapnia, acidosis, lactate
  • Drug levels / Toxicology
    Digoxin, lithium, paracetamol, alcohol

If No Cause Found → Further Tests

Imaging

  • CT Head
  • MRI Brain

Other

  • Lumbar Puncture
  • EEG

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria

  1. Disturbance in attention

    Reduced ability to focus, sustain, or shift attention. May present as lethargy or distractibility.

  2. Change in cognition

    Memory deficit, disorientation, language disturbance, or perceptual disturbance not better explained by dementia.

  3. Acute onset & fluctuation

    Develops over hours to days, represents acute change from baseline, fluctuates during the day.

  4. Evidence of causation

    Disturbance caused by medical condition, substance intoxication/withdrawal, or exposure to toxin.

Clinical Subtypes

Hyperactive

  • Restlessness
  • Agitation
  • Hallucinations
  • Inappropriate behaviour

Hypoactive

  • Lethargy
  • Reduced motor activity
  • Incoherent speech
  • Lack of interest

Mixed

  • Combination
  • Fluctuating between
  • hyperactive and
  • hypoactive features

Key Statistics

  • • Occurs in ≥20% of hospitalisations annually
  • • 10-40% prevalence in older hospitalised patients
  • • Mortality 2x higher than patients without delirium
  • • Frequently under-recognised
