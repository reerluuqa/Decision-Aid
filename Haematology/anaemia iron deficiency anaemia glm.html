Iron Deficiency Anemia - Quick Reference

For people aged 12+ and pregnant women

Last revised: Oct 2025
RED FLAGS - IMMEDIATE ATTENTION REQUIRED

Hb < 70 g/L

Worsening angina in pre-existing cardiac disease

Dyspnoea at rest

Ankle swelling (heart failure)

Definition

Iron deficiency anaemia is defined as diminished red blood cell production due to low iron stores in the body — it is the most common cause of microcytic, hypochromic anaemia.

Population Haemoglobin Threshold
Men (15+ years) < 130 g/L
Non-pregnant women (15+ years) < 120 g/L
Children (12-14 years) < 120 g/L
Pregnant women < 110 g/L throughout pregnancy
Postpartum < 100 g/L
Diagnostic Confirmation: A serum ferritin level of less than 30 micrograms/L confirms iron deficiency.
Common Causes
GI Blood Loss (most common in men/postmenopausal women)
Aspirin/NSAID use, colonic/gastric carcinoma, ulceration
Menstruation (most common in premenopausal women)
Increased Requirement
Pregnancy (3x higher than menstruating women)
Malabsorption
Coeliac disease, gastrectomy, H. pylori infection
Prevalence
Global: Affects ~1 billion people worldwide
UK Prevalence:
3% of men, 8% of women
Premenopausal Women: Up to 12% in UK
Pregnancy: 23% in UK
Hospital Admissions: 57,000/year in UK
Common Symptoms
Fatigue Very Common
Dyspnoea Very Common
Headache Very Common
Cognitive dysfunction Common
Restless leg syndrome Common
Dizziness/Lightheadedness
Weakness
Palpitations
Pica (abnormal dietary cravings)
Common Signs
Pallor Very Common
Atrophic glossitis Very Common
Dry/rough skin Very Common
Dry/damaged hair Common
Diffuse alopecia Common
Angular cheilosis (ulceration at mouth corners)
Nail changes (ridging, koilonychia)
Red Flag Symptoms
Worsening angina in pre-existing coronary artery disease
Ankle swelling (heart failure)
Dyspnoea at rest
Symptoms of GI bleeding (melena, hematemesis)
Initial Investigations
Full Blood Count (FBC)

Check for low haemoglobin and low mean cell volume (MCV)

Ferritin Level

Check in all people with MCV < 95 femtolitres

Diagnostic confirmation: Serum ferritin < 30 micrograms/L confirms iron deficiency
Consider Additional Tests

Vitamin B12 and folate levels if:

  • Anaemia is normocytic with low/normal ferritin
  • Inadequate response to iron supplements
  • Suspected B12/folate deficiency
  • Older age group (risk of pernicious anaemia)
Interpreting FBC Results
Parameter Iron Deficiency Indication
MCV < 80 femtolitres (microcytosis)
MCH Reduced (hypochromia)
Hypochromic RBC % Increased
Blood Film Microcytic, hypochromic RBCs
Note: An MCV < 95 femtolitres has a sensitivity of 97.6% for iron deficiency anaemia
Special Considerations
Pregnancy

Serum ferritin is reliable in first trimester but limited in 2nd/3rd trimesters

Consider treatment when ferritin < 30 micrograms/L

Inflammatory Conditions

Ferritin may be elevated despite iron deficiency

Consider cutoff of 100 micrograms/L in chronic kidney disease/inflammation

Diagnostic Trial of Iron

Consider for 2-4 weeks in:

  • Premenopausal women with menorrhagia
  • Pregnant women (if no coeliac disease suspicion)
Treatment Options
First-line Treatment

One tablet once daily of oral ferrous sulfate, ferrous fumarate or ferrous gluconate

For Significant Intolerance
  • Alternate day dosing
  • Oral ferric maltol
  • Parenteral iron preparations
Note: Switching to an alternate oral iron salt is not supported by evidence
Monitoring

Recheck FBC after 2-3 months to ensure adequate response

Treatment Goals
Normalize haemoglobin
Replenish iron stores
Resolve symptoms
Identify and treat underlying cause
Managing Adverse Effects
Gastrointestinal upset
Take with food, consider alternate day dosing
Constipation
Increase fluid and fiber intake
Black stools
Reassure - this is normal and harmless
Severe intolerance
Consider parenteral iron therapy
Special Populations
Pregnancy

Iron requirements are 3x higher than in menstruating women, increasing as pregnancy advances. Monitor Hb at booking and 28 weeks.

Pre-existing Cardiac Disease

More aggressive treatment may be needed as even moderate anaemia can worsen angina, tachycardia, or heart failure.

Endurance Athletes

Iron deficiency anaemia is common due to increased iron loss through sweat, hemolysis, and GI bleeding.

Immediate Referral Required
Hb < 70 g/L or severe symptoms
Worsening angina or other cardiac symptoms
Suspected GI malignancy
FIT result ≥ 10 micrograms Hb/g (use suspected cancer pathway for colorectal cancer)
Consider Referral
Men and postmenopausal women with iron deficiency anaemia (to investigate GI bleeding)
Inadequate response to iron therapy after 3 months
Recurrent iron deficiency anaemia after adequate treatment
Severe symptoms despite mild anaemia
Specialist Referral Considerations
Gastroenterology

For investigation of GI bleeding, especially in men and postmenopausal women

Haematology

For complex cases, diagnostic uncertainty, or parenteral iron therapy

Gynaecology

For women with heavy menstrual bleeding not responding to treatment

Pregnancy

Refer to obstetric team for pregnant women with severe anaemia (Hb < 100 g/L)

IV Iron Therapy Consideration
Menstruating pre-menopausal women with normal haemoglobin but low ferritin who are symptomatic
Intolerance to oral iron despite trying different formulations
Malabsorption (e.g., coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease)
Chronic kidney disease or inflammatory conditions where oral iron is ineffective
