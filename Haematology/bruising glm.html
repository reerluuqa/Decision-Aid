Bruising Assessment - Clinical Reference

Quick reference for assessment and management of bruising in children and adults

Critical Alert: Always consider non-accidental injury in vulnerable populations (children, elderly, learning difficulties).

Definition

A bruise is a haematoma that forms due to bleeding under intact skin into subcutaneous tissue, caused by vascular damage or injury.

Typically appears as red discolouration that doesn't blanch on pressure, gradually darkens to purple, fades to brownish yellow, and usually disappears within 2-3 weeks.

Note: The colour of a bruise is not a reliable indicator of its age.

Epidemiology

  • Up to 55% of otherwise healthy people report easy bruising
  • Common in elderly due to skin fragility (senile purpura)
  • Most common injury sustained by children who have been subject to physical abuse

Scope

This CKS topic covers:

  • Assessment and management of bruising (normal and abnormal)
  • Children and adults (from age 1 month onwards)

This CKS topic does NOT cover:

  • Other types of purpura
  • Management of underlying causes of abnormal bruising

Common Causes

Category Examples
Trauma Accidental injury, non-accidental injury
Vascular disorders Senile purpura, HHT, Ehlers-Danlos
Platelet disorders ITP, aplastic anaemia, malignancy
Coagulation disorders Haemophilia, VWD, liver disease
Drugs Anticoagulants, antiplatelets, steroids

Drug-Induced Bruising

  • Corticosteroids - cause skin atrophy and purpura
  • Antiplatelet drugs - aspirin, NSAIDs, clopidogrel
  • Anticoagulants - warfarin, DOACs, heparins
  • Thrombocytopenia-inducing - alcohol, certain antibiotics

Assessment Approach

  • Ask about symptoms suggesting bleeding disorder
  • Assess general health for underlying causes
  • Ask about alcohol and medication use
  • Take family history of bleeding disorders
  • Document location and pattern of bruising
  • Examine skin, hair, nails, joints, abdomen

Initial Investigations

For all patients with unexplained bruising:

  • Urine dipstick (check for haematuria)

Adults:

  • Urgent full blood count with platelet count (within 48 hours)
  • Clotting screen (PT, aPTT, INR)
  • Liver, renal, thyroid function tests (as indicated)

Children:

  • Urgent full blood count with platelet count (within 48 hours)

Assessment of Bruising Pattern

Consider:

  • Distribution, number, site, shape, size
  • Dependent areas (thrombocytopenia)
  • Atypical areas (trunk - suggests bleeding disorder)
  • Patterned bruising (non-accidental injury)
  • Periorbital (neuroblastoma in children)
  • Dorsum of hands/forearms (senile purpura)

Physical Examination:

  • Skin for age-related changes
  • Evidence of delayed healing
  • Skin laxity (Ehlers-Danlos)
  • Pallor (anaemia)
  • Jaundice (liver disease)
  • Petechiae (platelet disorder)
  • Joint swelling (haemarthrosis)
  • Splenomegaly/hepatomegaly

Non-Accidental Injury Red Flags

High-Risk Groups:

  • Infants and children
  • Elderly
  • People with learning difficulties
  • Other vulnerable populations

Immediate Actions:

  • Refer immediately to appropriate agency
  • Local child/adult social care services
  • Police or NSPCC
  • If admission needed, inform hospital of concerns

Red Flags in Children

Red Flag Significance
Bruises on non-mobile child Highly suspicious for abuse
Patterned bruises (handprint, ligature) Indicative of non-accidental injury
Bruises on face, neck, torso, genitals Unusual locations for accidental injury
Periorbital bruising Potential neuroblastoma
Implausible explanation Inconsistent with presentation

Red Flags in Adults

Red Flag Significance
Unexplained petechiae Possible leukaemia
Hepatosplenomegaly Possible leukaemia
Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) Possible malignancy
Bruising with minimal trauma Possible bleeding disorder
Patterned bruises Possible abuse

When to Admit

  • Significant active bleeding not responding to simple measures
  • Suspected non-accidental injury (requires safeguarding)
Immediate Referral Required

When to Refer Urgently

Children:

  • Unexplained petechiae
  • Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Full blood count suggestive of leukaemia
  • Periorbital bruising with palpable abdominal mass

Adults:

  • Full blood count suggestive of leukaemia
  • Low platelet count
  • Abnormal clotting screen
Refer Within 48 Hours

Management in Primary Care

For simple bruising:

  • Prescribe simple analgesia (paracetamol)
  • Reassure about normal healing process

For warfarin patients with abnormal clotting screen:

  • Alter warfarin dosage according to local protocol
  • Consider vitamin K if significantly elevated INR
Safe to Manage in Primary Care

Referral Pathways

Condition Referral Urgency
Suspected non-accidental injury Social services/police/NSPCC Immediate
Suspected leukaemia Haematology/Oncology Immediate
Periorbital bruising with abdominal mass Paediatrics (for neuroblastoma) Urgent (48h)
Abnormal bleeding tests Haematology Urgent
Suspected bleeding disorder Haematology Routine
