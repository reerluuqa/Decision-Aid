Immediate HIV Testing Required

Test immediately for HIV if any of the following conditions are present:

  • Category 1 AIDS-defining conditions:
    • Kaposi's sarcoma
    • Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma
    • Cervical cancer
    • Disseminated Mycobacterium infection
    • Recurrent pneumonia (2+ episodes in 12 months)
    • Pneumocystis pneumonia
    • Cerebral toxoplasmosis
    • Cryptosporidiosis (>1 month)
    • CMV retinitis
  • Category 2a High prevalence (>0.1%) conditions:
    • Sexually transmitted infections
    • Anal/cervical dysplasia
    • Herpes zoster
    • Hepatitis B or C
    • Unexplained leukocytopenia/thrombocytopenia (>4 weeks)
    • Seborrheic dermatitis/exanthema
    • Invasive pneumococcal disease
    • Unexplained fever
Order HIV Test Now

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

Initiate PEP immediately if:

  • Recent (72 hours) potential exposure to HIV
  • Source is known to be HIV-positive or high-risk
  • Exposure through:
    • Unprotected vaginal/anal sex
    • Occupational exposure (needlestick)
    • Shared needles

PEP Protocol:

  • Start within 72 hours (ideally <24 hours)
  • 28-day course of antiretroviral therapy
  • Baseline HIV test (must be negative)
  • Follow-up testing at 6 weeks and 3 months
Refer for Emergency PEP

HIV Testing

When to Test

  • People requesting testing
  • Men who have sex with men (MSM)
  • Black Africans
  • People who inject drugs
  • Sex workers
  • People from high prevalence countries (>1%)
  • Partners of people with HIV
  • Pregnant women (as part of antenatal screening)
  • People with any HIV indicator condition

Testing Methods

Test Type Window Period
4th generation antigen/antibody test 4 weeks
Rapid point-of-care test 6-12 weeks
HIV RNA PCR 10-14 days

Testing Frequency:

  • High-risk groups: At least annually
  • MSM: Every 3-6 months
  • After potential exposure: Repeat at 6 weeks and 3 months

Clinical Features of HIV

Primary HIV Infection (2-4 weeks after exposure)

  • Fever
  • Sore throat
  • Rash
  • Swollen lymph nodes
  • Fatigue
  • Headache
  • Diarrhea
  • Weight loss
  • May be asymptomatic

Advanced HIV Disease

  • CD4 count <200 cells/μL
  • Opportunistic infections
  • AIDS-defining illnesses
  • Weight loss
  • Chronic diarrhea
  • Persistent fever
  • Night sweats
  • Multiple dermatological conditions

Risk Factors for HIV Acquisition

Behavioral Risk Factors

  • Unprotected sex
  • Multiple sexual partners
  • High-risk sexual practices (chemsex)
  • History of other STIs
  • Injecting drug use
  • Sharing needles

Population Risk Factors

  • Men who have sex with men (MSM)
  • Trans women
  • Trans men with additional risk factors
  • Sex workers
  • Black Africans
  • People from high prevalence countries (>1%)
  • People in prisons or closed settings

Prevalence Statistics (2024)

Global

  • 40.8 million people living with HIV
  • 1.3 million new infections in 2024
  • 630,000 HIV-related deaths in 2024
  • 65% of cases in WHO African Region

UK

  • 113,500 people living with HIV
  • 5,200+ undiagnosed cases
  • 6,512 new diagnoses in 2023
  • 46% increase from 2022
  • London has highest rates but increases concentrated outside London

Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

When to Start

  • All people with HIV regardless of CD4 count
  • Start as soon as possible after diagnosis
  • Same-day initiation if patient ready

Goals of Therapy

  • Suppress viral load to <50 copies/mL
  • Restore and preserve immune function
  • Reduce HIV-related morbidity and mortality
  • Prevent HIV transmission (U=U)

Monitoring

  • Viral load at baseline, 2-8 weeks after starting, then every 3-6 months
  • CD4 count at baseline and every 3-6 months
  • Renal function and liver function tests
  • Lipid profile
  • Screen for comorbidities (CVD, diabetes, etc.)

Special Considerations

  • Statin therapy for all people with HIV aged ≥40 years for CVD prevention
  • Screen for mental health issues
  • Address lifestyle factors (smoking, diet, exercise)
Refer to HIV Specialist

Patient Education

Key Messages

  • U=U (Undetectable = Untransmittable)
  • Adherence to ART is critical
  • Regular follow-up appointments
  • Healthy lifestyle choices
  • Mental health support

Transmission Prevention

  • Consistent condom use
  • PrEP for HIV-negative partners
  • Treatment as prevention
  • Avoid sharing needles
  • Safe pregnancy planning

Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)

Who Should Be Offered PrEP

  • Men who have sex with men (MSM)
  • HIV-negative partners in serodiscordant relationships
  • People who inject drugs
  • Sex workers
  • Transgender individuals
  • Heterosexual individuals at high risk

PrEP Regimens

  • Daily oral TDF/FTC (tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/emtricitabine)
  • Daily oral TAF/FTC (tenofovir alafenamide/emtricitabine)
  • Cabotegravir injection (every 2 months)
  • Event-driven (2-1-1) dosing for MSM

Monitoring

  • HIV test every 3 months
  • Renal function monitoring
  • STI screening
  • Adherence counseling
Refer for PrEP Assessment

Prevention of Mother-to-Child Transmission

Key Interventions

  • Universal antenatal HIV screening
  • Maternal ART during pregnancy
  • Intravenous zidovudine during labor (if viral load >1000 copies/mL)
  • Infant prophylaxis for 4-6 weeks
  • Advising against breastfeeding (if safe alternatives available)

Outcomes

  • With effective interventions, transmission risk <1%
  • Without interventions, transmission risk 15-45%
  • Maternal viral suppression is most important factor

HIV-Related Complications

Opportunistic Infections

  • Pneumocystis pneumonia
  • Tuberculosis
  • Candidiasis (oral, esophageal)
  • Cryptococcal meningitis
  • Toxoplasmosis
  • CMV retinitis
  • MAC (Mycobacterium avium complex)

Non-AIDS Defining Conditions

  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Renal disease (HIVAN)
  • Liver disease (co-infection with hepatitis B/C)
  • Neurocognitive disorders
  • Osteoporosis
  • Metabolic complications
  • Non-AIDS malignancies

ART-Related Toxicities

Common Side Effects

  • Gastrointestinal intolerance
  • Rash
  • Headache
  • Fatigue
  • Insomnia
  • Dizziness

Potentially Serious Toxicities

  • Renal impairment (tenofovir)
  • Hepatotoxicity (nevirapine, protease inhibitors)
  • Bone demineralization (tenofovir)
  • Cardiovascular risk (protease inhibitors)
  • Lipodystrophy
  • Lactic acidosis (NRTIs)
