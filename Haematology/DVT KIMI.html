üö® When to Suspect DVT Urgent

Consider DVT if typical symptoms/signs are present, especially with risk factors:

  • Pain or tenderness (throbbing/cramping, occurs with walking/weight-bearing)
  • Swelling (often noticeable in calf)
  • Skin changes: pitting edema, redness, warmth
  • Prominent dilated superficial veins

Note: Symptoms are usually unilateral and range from severe to subtle. Patient may be asymptomatic.

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Risk Factors

Continuing/Intrinsic:

  • History of DVT
  • Cancer (known/undiagnosed)
  • Age >60 years
  • Overweight/obesity
  • Male sex
  • Heart failure, acute infection
  • Thrombophilia
  • Inflammatory disorders
  • Varicose veins, smoking

Temporary:

  • Recent surgery/hospitalization/trauma
  • Significant immobility
  • Prolonged travel (>4 hours)
  • Hormone treatment (OCP/HRT)
  • Pregnancy/postpartum
  • Dehydration

üìè Physical Examination

  • Measure leg circumference 10 cm below tibial tuberosity
  • Compare with asymptomatic leg
  • Difference >3 cm increases probability
  • Assess for edema and dilated collateral veins

üéØ Diagnostic Pathway

‚ö†Ô∏è
Pregnancy/Postpartum: If suspected DVT in pregnant woman or within 6 weeks postpartum ‚Üí IMMEDIATE SAME-DAY REFERRAL

For all others: Use Wells score to guide management:

If DVT Likely (Wells ‚â•2 points):

  • Offer proximal leg vein ultrasound within 4 hours
  • If scan cannot be done within 4 hours:
    • Offer D-dimer test
    • Start interim therapeutic anticoagulation
    • Offer ultrasound within 24 hours

If DVT Unlikely (Wells ‚â§1 point):

  • Offer D-dimer test within 4 hours
  • If D-dimer unavailable within 4 hours ‚Üí start interim anticoagulation
  • If D-dimer positive:
    • Offer ultrasound within 4 hours
    • If scan unavailable ‚Üí interim anticoagulation + scan within 24 hours
  • If D-dimer negative:
    • Stop interim anticoagulation
    • Consider alternative diagnosis
    • Advise: DVT unlikely but seek help if symptoms worsen or chest pain/breathing difficulties

‚ùì Differential Diagnoses

  • Physical trauma: calf muscle tear, hematoma, fracture
  • Cardiovascular: superficial thrombophlebitis, post-thrombotic syndrome, venous obstruction, acute arterial ischemia, vasculitis, heart failure
  • Other: ruptured Baker's cyst, cellulitis, dependent edema, lymphatic obstruction, septic arthritis, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, compartment syndrome

When indicated (while awaiting tests):

  • LMWH for ‚â•5 days, then Dabigatran (Pradaxa) or Edoxaban (Lixiana)

üìã Parenteral Anticoagulants (LMWH)

Dalteparin (Fragmin)

  • Contraindications: HIT type II, bacterial endocarditis, hemophilia, mechanical prosthetic valve, major trauma/bleeding
  • Weight-based dosing:
    • <46 kg: 7500 units OD
    • 46-56 kg: 10,000 units OD
    • 57-68 kg: 12,500 units OD
    • 69-82 kg: 15,000 units OD
    • ‚â•83 kg: 18,000 units OD

Enoxaparin (Clexane)

  • Contraindications: HIT within 100 days, end-stage renal disease (CrCl <15 mL/min)
  • Dosing:
    • Low risk: 150 IU/kg (1.5 mg/kg) OD
    • High risk (obesity, cancer, recurrent VTE): 100 IU/kg (1 mg/kg) BD
    • Severe renal impairment (CrCl 15-30): 100 IU/kg OD

Tinzaparin (Innohep)

  • Dose: 175 units/kg once daily
  • Caution: Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30) - monitor anti-Factor Xa

Fondaparinux (Arixtra)

  • Weight-based dosing:
    • <50 kg: 5 mg daily
    • 50-100 kg: 7.5 mg daily
    • >100 kg: 10 mg daily

üìà Follow-up
‚ñº

üè• After Hospital Treatment

Patients with confirmed DVT will be discharged with maintenance anticoagulation:

  • Continue for at least 3 months
  • Longer if unprovoked DVT and risk of recurrence outweighs bleeding risk
  • Regular follow-up: at 1 month, then at least annually
  • Check compliance, adverse effects, ongoing suitability
  • INR monitoring for warfarin; no routine INR needed for DOACs
üì±
Patient Education: Provide verbal and written information on dose, duration, adverse effects, drug interactions, dental treatment, sports, travel, seeking help, and anticoagulant alert card.

üîç Investigations for Unprovoked DVT

Cancer Screening

  • Review medical history and baseline bloods
  • Offer physical examination
  • Do NOT offer further cancer investigations unless clinical symptoms/signs present

Thrombophilia Testing

Consider if planning to stop anticoagulation:

  • Antiphospholipid antibodies in unprovoked DVT
  • Hereditary thrombophilia if first-degree relative had DVT/PE
  • Be aware: tests affected by anticoagulants - seek specialist advice

Do NOT offer:

  • Hereditary thrombophilia if continuing anticoagulation
  • Thrombophilia testing for provoked DVT
  • Routine testing to first-degree relatives

üß¶ Compression Stockings

‚ö†Ô∏è
Do NOT offer graduated elastic compression stockings to prevent post-thrombotic syndrome or VTE recurrence after proximal DVT.

May be used for symptom management after DVT (not for prevention):

  • Explain proper application and use
  • Advise on duration and replacement

üéØ Modifiable Risk Factors

Address and manage:

  • Obesity
  • Smoking cessation
  • Mobility and exercise
  • Hydration
