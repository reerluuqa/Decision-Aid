🔬 What Does Vitamin B12 Do? ▼

B12 (cobalamin) is a co-factor for two enzymes in the body. It is necessary for:

  • Normal bone marrow function and erythropoiesis (red blood cell production).
  • Development, myelination and maintenance of the nervous system.
🚨
B12 deficiency is a potentially reversible cause of bone marrow failure and demyelination. Early identification and treatment can avoid permanent neurological disability.
🥩 Dietary Sources of B12 ▼

B12 is found in animal products:

  • Meat (beef, poultry, lamb)
  • Fish and shellfish
  • Eggs
  • Milk and dairy products
  • B12-fortified foods (e.g. breakfast cereals)

Individuals eating no animal products (vegans, some vegetarians) are at higher risk of dietary B12 deficiency.

📋 NICE NG239 Key Messages (2024) ▼
  • Symptoms and signs are numerous, varied and not specific to B12 deficiency.
  • Who to test depends on: compatible symptoms/signs AND underlying risk factors.
  • B12 results should be classified as Low (<180 ng/L), Indeterminate (180–350 ng/L), or Normal (>350 ng/L).
  • In some cases, immediate B12 replacement is required without waiting for test results.
  • B12 deficiency is very likely if total gastrectomy / complete terminal ileum resection and NOT taking B12.
  • Causes include: autoimmune gastritis, malabsorption, medications, recreational nitrous oxide use, and diet.
  • If the cause is irreversible, lifelong IM B12 replacement is needed.
  • B12 replacement is an effective treatment for most people.
  • B12 deficiency often co-exists with folate deficiency.
  • Symptoms can affect family, social life, work and education.

🔍 Identifying B12 Deficiency

💡
NICE recommends considering risk factors AND symptoms/signs together when deciding who to test. This is based on expert consensus rather than strong evidence.
🩺 Symptoms & Signs of B12 Deficiency ▼
  • Paraesthesia (pins and needles) — often symmetrical, distal limbs
  • Numbness
  • Impaired proprioception & vibration sense
  • Ataxia (unsteady gait)
  • Muscle weakness
  • Hyperreflexia
  • Cognitive changes (memory problems, confusion)
  • Visual disturbances
  • Mood changes (depression, irritability)
🚨
Subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord — see Urgent Scenarios tab. Potentially reversible if treated early; irreversible if delayed.
  • Fatigue / lethargy
  • Breathlessness
  • Pallor
  • Palpitations
  • Dizziness / light-headedness
  • Macrocytosis (raised MCV on FBC)
  • Megaloblastic anaemia on blood film (megaloblasts, poikilocytosis, anisocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils)
  • Glossitis (sore, red tongue)
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Anorexia / weight loss
  • Diarrhoea
  • Infertility

Remember: symptoms are varied, overlap with many other conditions, and are NOT specific to B12 deficiency.

⚡ Risk Factors for B12 Deficiency ▼
  • Autoimmune gastritis (previously: pernicious anaemia)
  • Total gastrectomy or complete terminal ileum resection B12 deficiency very likely
  • Malabsorption conditions (e.g. coeliac disease, IBD, Crohn's affecting terminal ileum)
  • Bariatric / upper GI surgery (including gastric bypass)
  • Dietary deficiency — vegan/vegetarian, no animal products
  • Medications:
    • Metformin
    • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
    • H2-receptor antagonists
    • Colchicine
  • Recreational nitrous oxide use (N₂O inactivates B12)
  • Older age (reduced absorption; 5% at 65–74y, >10% at ≥75y)
  • Genetic conditions (e.g. Imerslund-Gräsbeck syndrome, transcobalamin II deficiency)
  • Other autoimmune conditions (thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes, vitiligo)

🧪 Testing & Interpreting Results

⚠️
No gold-standard test exists. No test for B12 deficiency can completely rule it in or out. Total B12 is most commonly used and more cost-effective. Active B12 (serum holotranscobalamin) is thought to be more accurate.
📊 Available Tests ▼
Test Notes Limitations
Total serum B12 Most commonly used; more cost-effective Cannot completely rule deficiency in/out
Active B12 (serum holotranscobalamin) Thought to be more accurate than total B12 Less widely available; higher cost
Methylmalonic acid (MMA) Useful for indeterminate results; recommended by NICE in certain scenarios Recent antibiotics → false normal; elevated in renal impairment
Homocysteine Alternative confirmatory test Not specific to B12 (also raised in folate deficiency, renal impairment)

Clinicians must use the test their lab offers. Over time, labs may offer MMA in line with NICE guidance.

📐 NICE Result Classification (Expert Consensus) ▼
Classification Total B12 Level Interpretation Action
✅ Normal >350 ng/L B12 deficiency unlikely Consider other causes for symptoms
⚠️ Indeterminate 180–350 ng/L B12 deficiency may or may not be present Consider MMA/homocysteine, clinical picture, risk of not treating (see below)
🔴 Low <180 ng/L B12 deficiency likely Treat — determine cause and commence replacement
🚨
Lab Reference Range Risk: The NICE ‘normal’ threshold (>350 ng/L) is considerably higher than many lab reference ranges. A lab-reported ‘normal’ result may fall in the NICE ‘indeterminate’ zone. Always check the numerical value.

Managing Indeterminate Results (180–350 ng/L)

The decision on whether to treat depends on the risks of NOT treating:

  • Pregnancy — treat (risk to foetus if untreated)
  • Significant neurological symptoms — treat (e.g. ataxia; risk of irreversible damage)
  • Symptoms of subacute combined degeneration — treat immediately
  • Consider MMA or homocysteine testing to clarify
  • Consider full clinical picture and risk factors

💊 Treatment

💉 IM vs Oral B12 Replacement — Decision Guide ▼

Who Needs Lifelong IM B12?

💉
Lifelong intramuscular B12 is recommended for:
  • Autoimmune gastritis — inability to absorb B12 via GI tract; oral replacement may be ineffective
  • Previous total gastrectomy — permanent malabsorption
  • Complete terminal ileum resection — permanent malabsorption

IM replacement is also more cost-effective than oral replacement if used for ≥6 months.

If Lifelong IM Not Required

NICE says either oral or IM replacement can be considered.

Route Details Evidence
Oral Cyanocobalamin tablets (typical doses 1000–2000 mcg/d); licensed dose 50–150 mcg/d for general use Low-quality evidence: both routes improve serum B12. Higher oral doses (2000 mcg/d) achieved higher B12 levels than IM
Intramuscular Hydroxocobalamin injection; standard loading & maintenance schedules No RCT data on clinical improvement or quality of life for either route (Cochrane 2018, 3 RCTs, n=153)

Self-Administration of B12 Injections

  • Self-administration occurred during COVID; potential cost benefit for NHS
  • No evidence on effectiveness/safety vs usual care
  • Subcutaneous administration is an option but off-label
  • NICE recommends further research
  • BJGP 2022 survey: 39% of respondents self-medicated via injection (self-sourced)
  • Safety considerations: legitimacy of purchased product, safe needle use
💬
Clinical Tip: Do you routinely ask about self-purchasing of additional B12 injections? 4 in 10 people in the BJGP study were self-medicating beyond their primary care prescription.
📋 Treatment Regimen Quick Reference ▼
Scenario Route Regimen Duration
Autoimmune gastritis / Total gastrectomy / Complete ileal resection IM Hydroxocobalamin IM loading then maintenance Lifelong
Neurological involvement IM IM loading — more frequent initially to prevent irreversible damage Until resolution / lifelong if cause irreversible
Dietary deficiency Oral or IM Oral cyanocobalamin 1000–2000 mcg/d OR IM Until corrected + dietary advice
Dietary deficiency in pregnancy Oral Oral cyanocobalamin 1 mg/d (NOT the licensed 50–150 mcg/d) Throughout pregnancy & breastfeeding
Suspected autoimmune gastritis in pregnancy IM Hydroxocobalamin IM Throughout pregnancy & breastfeeding — do NOT stop
Metformin-associated deficiency Oral or IM Treat as per standard approach; try to continue metformin As clinically indicated

🚨 Urgent Scenarios — Treat Immediately

⛑️
NICE NG239: Replace B12 immediately, without waiting for test results, if suspected megaloblastic anaemia AND neurological symptoms (especially any symptoms of subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord).
🩸 What Is Megaloblastic Anaemia? ▼

Megaloblastic anaemia is a type of macrocytic anaemia:

  • Macrocytic anaemias can be megaloblastic or non-megaloblastic
  • B12 deficiency causes ineffective / abnormal erythropoiesis
  • Erythroblasts become large and abnormal (megaloblastic)
  • Blood film may show: poikilocytosis (abnormal shape), anisocytosis (abnormal size), hypersegmented neutrophils
  • Can occur with B12 AND/OR folate deficiency

What Is “Suspected” Megaloblastic Anaemia?

Per NICE (confirmed in correspondence, Dec 2024), this means ANY suspicion:

  • Symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency + FBC showing raised MCV / anaemia without a blood film to confirm megaloblasts
  • Symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency + clinical suspicion (without any tests) that the cause may be B12 deficiency
🧠 Subacute Combined Degeneration of the Spinal Cord ▼
🚨
Potentially reversible if treated early. Irreversible if treatment is delayed.

A neurological complication of B12 deficiency: demyelination of the spinal cord, classically affecting the posterior (dorsal) columns.

Key Features

  • Symmetrical distal limb paraesthesia — often the initial presentation
  • Impaired proprioception
  • Loss of vibration sense
  • Sensory changes
  • Hyperreflexia
  • Muscle weakness
  • Ataxia (unsteady gait)

URGENCY: HIGHEST — Treat immediately without waiting for results

🛡️ Autoimmune Gastritis

📝
NICE uses “autoimmune gastritis” rather than “pernicious anaemia”. NICE defines pernicious anaemia as “life-threatening anaemia as a consequence of chronic, severe B12 deficiency” — such severe presentations are rarely seen now.
🔬 Pathophysiology ▼

Auto-antibodies against parietal cells and intrinsic factor → inflammation of the stomach → destruction of parietal cells.

Two Main Consequences

EffectConsequence
Reduced gastric acid secretion Prevents B12 release from food; impairs iron absorption
Reduced intrinsic factor production Impairs B12 absorption in terminal ileum

Treatment: Lifelong IM B12 — oral replacement may be ineffective due to inability to absorb B12 via GI tract.

🎗️ Cancer Monitoring ▼
⚠️
People with autoimmune gastritis are at increased risk of gastric neuroendocrine tumours and may be at increased risk of gastric adenocarcinoma.

If New/Worsening Upper GI Symptoms

(e.g. dyspepsia, nausea, vomiting) in someone with known/suspected autoimmune gastritis:

  • Follow NICE suspected cancer referral guidance
  • If not meeting cancer referral criteria, consider referral for OGD

BSG Recommendation

Consider a baseline OGD for ALL patients ≥50y with pernicious anaemia.

British Society of Gastroenterology (Gut 2019;68:1545)

🤰 Special Populations

🤰 Pregnancy & Breastfeeding ▼
❗
Do NOT stop B12 replacement during pregnancy or breastfeeding. No known harm of treatment; stopping risks harm.

NICE Recommendations

  • If suspected autoimmune gastritis: offer IM replacement
  • If dietary deficiency: offer oral cyanocobalamin 1 mg/d (higher than the licensed dose of 50–150 mcg/d)
  • Offer initial follow-up at 1 month (not 3 months as for other patients)
  • Do not stop B12 replacement during pregnancy/breastfeeding

Risks of Untreated B12 Deficiency in Pregnancy

  • Neural tube defects
  • Early pregnancy loss
  • Pre-eclampsia
  • Preterm birth
  • Low birth weight

Neonatal Risks

  • Hypotonia
  • Failure to thrive
  • Apnoeas
  • Seizures

BMJ 2023;383:e071725

👴 Older Adults (≥65y) ▼
  • B12 deficiency affects 5% of those aged 65–74y
  • Affects >10% of those aged ≥75y
  • Reduced absorption is common with ageing
  • Symptoms may be attributed to “normal ageing” — maintain high clinical suspicion
  • Cognitive changes, mood disturbance and peripheral neuropathy may be misdiagnosed
  • Often on multiple medications that affect B12 (metformin, PPIs)

💊 Metformin & B12

1 in 10
People on metformin affected by B12 reduction
Slow onset
Body stores B12 in liver; deficiency develops over time, not suddenly
⚙️ Mechanism & Risk ▼

Metformin can reduce B12 levels and lead to B12 deficiency. The mechanism is multifactorial:

  • Altered intestinal motility
  • Reduced B12 absorption in ileum
  • Bacterial overgrowth

Risk increases with: higher metformin doses, longer duration of treatment, and additional risk factors for B12 deficiency.

MHRA, June 2022

📋 Clinical Management ▼

When to Test

  • If patient has ≥1 symptoms/signs of B12 deficiency → check B12
  • Consider ‘periodic’ monitoring if on metformin WITH other risk factors (no defined interval)

Additional Risk Factors to Consider

  • B12 at the lower end of normal range
  • No animal products in diet
  • Older age (reduced absorption)
  • Malabsorption conditions (IBD, previous surgery)
  • Other drugs impairing B12 absorption (PPI, colchicine)
  • Genetic conditions (Imerslund-Gräsbeck syndrome, transcobalamin II deficiency)

Treatment

✅
If B12 deficiency found:
  • Treat in the usual way
  • Try to CONTINUE metformin (unless not tolerated or contraindicated)
💡
Practical Tip: Stratifying everyone on metformin by risk factors is impractical. Most clinicians will pragmatically add B12 to annual diabetes bloods.
⏳
B12 stores in the liver are substantial. Deficiency develops slowly over time. Checking B12 a few weeks after starting metformin will NOT identify metformin-induced deficiency.

📌 Other Considerations

🔶 Beware: Copper Deficiency ▼
⚠️
Copper deficiency is much less common than B12 deficiency but can present with similar symptoms and signs. Diagnosis is often delayed.
  • Copper is absorbed in the stomach and duodenum
  • Can co-exist with B12 deficiency
  • Likely to become more common as bariatric surgery increases
  • Consider testing copper in:
    • Persistent or unexplained cytopenias
    • Peripheral neuropathy syndromes
    • Especially with history of upper GI surgery (e.g. gastric bypass) or malabsorption syndromes

BMJ 2014;348:g3691

📈 Incidental Raised Vitamin B12 ▼

Very little published guidance on managing high B12 in primary care. Supplementation is a possible cause.

Associations of Raised B12

  • Liver disease
  • Chronic renal failure (CKD)
  • Autoimmune disorders
  • Inflammatory diseases
  • Myeloid blood malignancies
  • Solid tumours

Pragmatic Approach

  • Acute conditions may transiently raise B12 → repeat sample as first step
  • Consider: FBC, LFT, U&E depending on clinical picture
  • The absolute risk of solid cancer from raised B12 is thought to be insufficient to warrant systematic investigation in all cases

Scientific Reports 2021;11:13361

🥬 Folate & B12 Co-deficiency ▼
🔗
B12 deficiency often co-exists with folate deficiency. Both can cause megaloblastic anaemia. Always consider checking folate alongside B12.
