Assessment of Platelet Abnormalities
History Taking

Recent Infections

  • Bacterial infections (most common in adults)
  • Viral infections (EBV, varicella, HIV)
  • Systemic infections (pericarditis, endocarditis)
  • Recent travel to malaria-endemic areas

Medication Review

  • Prescribed medicines (quinine, heparin, NSAIDs)
  • Over-the-counter medications
  • Recent vaccinations
  • Recreational drug use

Medical History

  • Chronic inflammatory conditions
  • Rheumatological conditions
  • Malignancy (personal/family)
  • Prior splenectomy
  • Iron deficiency anemia

Pregnancy Status

  • Current pregnancy status
  • Gestational age
  • History of preeclampsia
  • Previous pregnancy complications
Suspicious Cancer Symptoms: Change in bowel habit, dysphagia, unexplained weight loss, persistent dyspepsia >55y, post-menopausal bleeding, unexplained cough/hemoptysis
Physical Examination

Inspection

  • Bruising or rashes
  • Pallor
  • Jaundice
  • Petechiae/ecchymoses
  • Spider telangiectasis

Hands & Extremities

  • Digital ischemia/swelling
  • Livedo reticularis
  • Palmar erythema
  • Pedal edema

Systemic Signs

  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Splenomegaly
  • Hepatomegaly
  • Joint deformity
  • Skeletal abnormalities
Initial Investigations

Essential Blood Tests

  • Full Blood Count: Review platelet count, Hb, WBC, reticulocytes
  • Blood Film: Review platelet morphology, rule out pseudothrombocytopenia
  • Iron Studies: Check for iron deficiency (common cause of reactive thrombocytosis)
  • Inflammatory Markers: CRP and ESR (usually elevated in reactive thrombocytosis)
  • Coagulation: PT/INR if bleeding suspected

Organ Function

  • Renal function tests
  • Liver function tests
  • Thyroid function (TSH)

Autoimmune Screening

  • Antinuclear antibodies (ANA)
  • Rheumatoid factor
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies

Infectious Screening

  • HIV test (if >4 weeks duration)
  • Hepatitis C
  • EBV, varicella if indicated
JAK-2 Testing: Consider in patients with persistent thrombocytosis to evaluate for myeloproliferative neoplasms
Diagnostic Interpretation
Platelet Count Interpretation

Thrombocytosis

>450 × 10⁹/L

Secondary in >80% cases
  • Mild elevation: 450-600 × 10⁹/L
  • Moderate elevation: 600-1000 × 10⁹/L
  • Severe elevation: >1000 × 10⁹/L

Thrombocytopenia

<150 × 10⁹/L

Risk of bleeding <50 × 10⁹/L
  • Mild: 100-150 × 10⁹/L
  • Moderate: 50-100 × 10⁹/L
  • Severe: <50 × 10⁹/L
  • Critical: <20 × 10⁹/L
Pseudothrombocytopenia: 1-2% of population have EDTA-dependent antibodies. Confirm by reviewing blood film or using citrate tube.

Key Diagnostic Points

  • Platelet size: Small platelets suggest reactive thrombocytosis
  • Trend analysis: Rising or falling trends are significant
  • Clinical context: Always interpret with clinical picture
  • Pregnancy: Normal platelet count decreases with gestation
Cancer-Related Causes

Primary Thrombocytosis

  • Essential thrombocythaemia
  • Polycythaemia vera
  • Primary myelofibrosis
  • Chronic myeloid leukaemia
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes

Cancer-Associated Thrombocytosis

  • Lung cancer (most common)
  • Colorectal cancer
  • Gastric cancer
  • Oesophageal cancer
  • Endometrial cancer

Treatment-Related Thrombocytopenia

  • Systemic chemotherapy
  • Radiation therapy
  • Targeted therapies
  • Immunotherapy

Marrow Infiltration

  • Acute leukaemias
  • Myeloma
  • Lymphoma
  • Metastatic solid tumors
Cancer Marker: Thrombocytosis has a positive predictive value for cancer (11.6% men, 6.2% women) that exceeds NICE referral thresholds.

Investigation Strategy

  • Chest X-ray: Age ≥40 with thrombocytosis
  • Pelvic ultrasound: Women ≥55 with symptoms
  • FIT test: For colorectal cancer screening
  • JAK-2 testing: Persistent unexplained thrombocytosis
Non-Cancer Causes

Reactive Thrombocytosis

  • Chronic inflammation (RA, SLE)
  • Iron deficiency anemia
  • Chronic infections
  • Post-surgical/trauma
  • Hyposplenism

Drug-Induced

  • Quinine, quinidine
  • Heparin-induced
  • NSAIDs
  • Penicillins
  • Anticonvulsants

Nutritional

  • Vitamin B12 deficiency
  • Folate deficiency
  • Alcohol misuse
  • Severe malnutrition

Other Conditions

  • Chronic liver disease
  • Autoimmune disorders
  • Thrombotic microangiopathies
  • Gestational thrombocytopenia
Iron Deficiency: Most common cause of reactive thrombocytosis. Platelet count usually normalizes after iron supplementation.
Management Pathways
Thrombocytosis Management

Immediate Actions

  • Urgent Referral Platelets >1000 × 10⁹/L - Urgent hematology referral
  • Cancer Pathway Suspected cancer symptoms - 2-week wait referral
  • Urgent Referral 600-1000 × 10⁹/L with:
    • Recent thrombosis
    • Neurological symptoms
    • Abnormal bleeding
    • Age >60 years
    • Other abnormal FBC indices

Investigation Thresholds

  • >450 × 10⁹/L for >3 months → Hematology referral
  • >600 × 10⁹/L on 2 occasions (4-6 weeks apart) → Refer
  • 450-600 × 10⁹/L with other abnormalities → Refer
Investigate cause

Primary Care Management

  • Investigate and treat underlying cause
  • No specific treatment needed for reactive thrombocytosis
  • Repeat FBC in 4-6 weeks if asymptomatic
  • Monitor for thrombotic symptoms
Usually resolves with treatment
Key Point: Reactive thrombocytosis does not usually require specific therapy and resolves once the underlying cause is treated.
Thrombocytopenia Management

Emergency Referrals

  • Immediate Referral <20 × 10⁹/L or Active bleeding
  • Urgent Referral 20-50 × 10⁹/L - Same day assessment
  • Urgent Referral 50-100 × 10⁹/L with:
    • Pancytopenia
    • Splenomegaly/lymphadenopathy
    • Pregnancy complications
    • Upcoming procedures

Monitoring Thresholds

  • 100-150 × 10⁹/L → Repeat FBC in 4-6 weeks
  • 50-100 × 10⁹/L → Repeat FBC in 1-2 weeks
  • Look for falling trends
  • Assess for bleeding symptoms

Drug-Induced Thrombocytopenia

  • Stop suspected drug with specialist consultation
  • Recovery in 4-5 drug half-lives
  • Usually within days to 2 weeks
  • Review and repeat count

Pregnancy Specific

  • Gestational thrombocytopenia: 100-150 × 10⁹/L
  • HELLP syndrome: <100 × 10⁹/L
  • Monitor for preeclampsia features
  • Liaise with obstetrics

Oncology Patients

  • Liaise with oncologist
  • Follow shared care arrangements
  • Anticipated myelosuppression
  • Supportive care measures
Malignancy Suspicion: Any unexplained thrombocytopenia with suspicious symptoms warrants urgent cancer pathway referral.
Safety Considerations

Bleeding Risk

  • Avoid NSAIDs if platelets <50 × 10⁹/L
  • Stool softeners to prevent straining
  • Soft toothbrush/electric razor
  • Fall prevention measures

Procedure Thresholds

  • Dental: >50 × 10⁹/L
  • Minor surgery: >50 × 10⁹/L
  • Major surgery: >80 × 10⁹/L
  • Neuraxial anesthesia: >80 × 10⁹/L

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Severe headache/neurological signs
  • Active bleeding (GI, GU, epistaxis)
  • Shortness of breath/chest pain
  • Fever with thrombocytopenia
Follow-up and Monitoring
Monitoring Schedule

Thrombocytosis Follow-up

  • No underlying cause: Repeat FBC in 4-6 weeks
  • Known cause: Monitor until resolution
  • Persistent elevation: Refer to hematology
  • Monitoring: Document trend over time

Thrombocytopenia Follow-up

  • 100-150 × 10⁹/L: Repeat FBC in 4-6 weeks
  • 50-100 × 10⁹/L: Repeat FBC in 1-2 weeks
  • Stable/improving: Continue monitoring
  • Falling trend: Urgent referral

Pregnancy Monitoring

  • Monthly FBC: After 20 weeks gestation
  • BP monitoring: For preeclampsia
  • Symptom review: Headache, visual changes
  • Delivery planning: Coordinate with OB team

Oncological Follow-up

  • Chemotherapy cycles: Pre-treatment FBC
  • Nadir monitoring: Day 7-14 post-chemo
  • Recovery tracking: Before next cycle
  • Supportive care: Transfusion thresholds

When to Escalate Care

  • Falling platelet trend on serial measurements
  • New symptoms develop (bleeding, bruising, fatigue)
  • Hemodynamic instability or active bleeding
  • Pregnancy complications (HELLP syndrome features)
  • Before procedures: Platelet count inadequate
Long-term Management

Primary Care Role

  • Coordinate with specialists
  • Medication review and monitoring
  • Patient education and support
  • Lifestyle advice (alcohol, diet)
  • Vaccination status (influenza, pneumococcal)

Preventive Measures

  • Osteoporosis prevention (if steroids used)
  • Cardiovascular risk management
  • Thromboprophylaxis assessment
  • Infection prevention strategies

Patient Education

  • Signs of bleeding to report
  • Medication adherence importance
  • When to seek urgent care
  • Procedure planning and notification
  • Travel considerations
Prognosis: Reactive thrombocytosis typically resolves within 2-4 weeks of treating the underlying cause. Chronic conditions may require ongoing monitoring.
Quality Assurance

Documentation Requirements

  • Platelet count and trend documented
  • Underlying cause identified and treated
  • Referral decisions and urgency recorded
  • Patient safety advice provided
  • Follow-up plan established
  • Shared decision making documented

Key Performance Indicators

  • Time to referral for suspected cancer
  • Appropriate use of urgent vs routine pathways
  • Patient safety information provision
  • Appropriate follow-up intervals
  • Reduction in unnecessary repeat testing
