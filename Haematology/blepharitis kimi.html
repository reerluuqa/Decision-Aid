Assessment
History Taking
  • Symptoms: Soreness, burning, itching, crusting of eyelids (typically bilateral, worse in morning)
  • Pattern: Periods of exacerbation and remission
  • Associated conditions: Dry eye disease, seborrhoeic dermatitis, rosacea
  • Exacerbating factors: Wind, makeup, contact lenses
  • Previous episodes: History of blepharitis, eye trauma, or surgery
Examination
  • Staphylococcal: Erythema, oedema, telangiectasia, matted hard scales/collarettes
  • Seborrhoeic: Less inflammation, oily/greasy deposits on lashes/lid margins
  • Posterior (MGD): Thick/opaque meibomian gland secretion, capped/dilated/obstructed glands
  • Tear film: Instability and rapid evaporation

Diagnosis
Diagnostic Basis: Diagnosis is based on history and examination findings. Investigations are not routinely required unless needed to exclude complications or differential diagnoses.
Diagnostic Features by Type
  • Anterior Blepharitis:
    • Staphylococcal: Erythema, oedema, telangiectasia, matted hard scales/collarettes
    • Seborrhoeic: Less inflammation, oily/greasy deposits
  • Posterior Blepharitis: Thick/opaque meibomian gland secretion, capped/dilated/obstructed glands, telangiectasias
  • Mixed: Elements of both anterior and posterior blepharitis
Differential Diagnoses
  • Dermatological: Psoriasis, contact dermatitis, eczema
  • Infections/Infestations: Herpes simplex eye infection, pubic lice, impetigo
  • Cellulitis:
    • Periorbital cellulitis (in front of orbital septum)
    • Orbital cellulitis (behind orbital septum) - MEDICAL EMERGENCY
  • Malignant Eyelid Tumours: Basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma, sebaceous gland carcinoma

  • Atypical, unilateral, inflammatory eyelid disease unresponsive to treatment
  • Features: eyelid distortion, nodular mass, ulceration, bleeding, extensive scarring, lash loss
  • Yellow conjunctival nodules surrounded by intense inflammation
  • Unresponsive, chronic, unilateral blepharitis in older people ‚Üí consider sebaceous gland carcinoma

Treatment
üö® Immediate Actions First
  • Emergency admission if signs of periorbital/orbital cellulitis
  • Same-day ophthalmology if corneal disease symptoms, rapid vision loss, painful/red eye
  • Suspected cancer pathway if malignant eyelid cancer suspected
  • Ophthalmology referral if gradual vision deterioration or uncertain diagnosis
Conservative Treatment (First-line)
  • Eyelid Hygiene:
    • Wet cloth/cotton bud with cleanser (baby shampoo 1:10, sodium bicarbonate solution, or commercial lid cleanser)
    • Gently wipe lid margins to clear debris
    • Twice daily initially, then once daily as symptoms improve
  • Warm Compress:
    • Apply warm (not hot) compress to closed eyelids for 5-10 minutes
    • Once or twice daily
  • Eyelid Massage (Posterior):
    • After warm compress, gently massage in direction of eyelashes
    • Downward on upper lid, upward on lower lid
    • To express meibomian gland content
Patient Education
  • Explain chronic nature and need for ongoing treatment
  • Continue eyelid hygiene even when well-controlled to minimize relapses
  • Avoid eye makeup (especially eyeliner/mascara) during exacerbations
  • Seek medical advice if symptoms persist/worsen or new features develop
Associated Conditions

Treat concurrently if present:

  • Dry eye disease
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis
  • Acne rosacea
Pharmacological Treatment
  • Anterior Blepharitis: Consider topical antibiotic (chloramphenicol) rubbed into lid margin if eyelid measures ineffective
  • Posterior Blepharitis: Consider oral antibiotic if associated with meibomian gland dysfunction and rosacea
    • Doxycycline (off-label use)
    • Tetracycline (contraindicated in pregnancy, lactation, and children under 12 years)

Follow-up
Chronic Condition Management
  • Prognosis: Chronic condition with periods of exacerbation and remission
  • Cure: Generally not possible, but symptoms can be controlled
  • Conservative treatment may facilitate symptom resolution
  • Maintenance treatment required to minimize number and severity of exacerbations
  • Serious complications are rare with proper management
Ongoing Care
  • Continue eyelid hygiene even when symptoms are well controlled
  • Regular follow-up to monitor disease control and complications
  • Adjust treatment based on disease activity and patient response
  • Reassess if new symptoms develop or existing symptoms worsen
When to Seek Medical Advice
  • Symptoms persist or worsen despite appropriate treatment
  • New features develop:
    • Visual changes or loss
    • Severe eye pain
    • Marked redness or swelling
    • Discharge or bleeding
    • Eyelid distortion or lash loss
  • Recurrent chalazia or styes
  • Contact lens intolerance develops
Referral Pathways
  • Primary care treatment ineffective: Refer to ophthalmology (urgency depends on clinical situation)
  • Low threshold for referral in children
  • Specialist management needed for:
    • Severe or refractory cases
    • Complications requiring specialist intervention
    • Surgical management of chalazia or lid malposition
    • Phototherapy or advanced treatments
Potential Complications to Monitor
  • Eyelid complications: Chalazion, stye, lash loss/misdirection, lid thickening/scarring, entropion/ectropion
  • Eye complications: Contact lens intolerance, dry eye disease, chronic conjunctivitis
  • Vision-threatening: Corneal inflammation, neovascularization, scarring, ulceration, perforation (rare, more common in children)
