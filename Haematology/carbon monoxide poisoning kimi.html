Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

NICE CKS Guidelines (June 2023) | Primary Care Management Algorithm

Assessment
Identify & Evaluate Risk

When to Suspect CO Poisoning

Ask about C-O-M-A:

  • C - Co-occupants: Is anyone else affected? (including pets)
  • O - Outdoors: Do symptoms improve when leaving the building?
  • M - Maintenance: Are fuel-burning appliances properly maintained?
  • A - Alarm: Is there a carbon monoxide alarm installed?
Key Clues: Symptoms develop following exposure and improve slowly when exposure stops. Multiple people/pets with similar symptoms strongly suggests CO poisoning.

High-Risk Groups

Age >80 years Pregnant women Children Respiratory disease Cardiovascular disease Anaemia Adult males Socioeconomically deprived
Important: Maternal CO levels may not reflect fetal levels. Fetal elimination half-life is 4-5x longer than mother's.

Common Sources of CO

  • Poorly installed, faulty, or unmaintained fuel-burning appliances
  • Blocked chimneys or flues
  • Inadequate ventilation
  • Using gas ovens for heating
  • Indoor BBQs (homes, caravans, tents)
  • Oversized pots on gas stoves
  • Shisha/hookah pipes in poorly ventilated rooms
  • Petrol/diesel engines in enclosed spaces
  • Paint removers containing methylene chloride
  • Neighboring properties with faulty appliances
Visual Warning Signs: Yellow/orange flames (instead of blue), soot stains, frequently blown-out pilot lights, increased window condensation

Clinical Features

Acute Low-Level Exposure:

  • Headache (90% of cases)
  • Dizziness, vertigo
  • Nausea & vomiting
  • Muscle pain
  • Flushing
  • Personality changes

Acute High-Level Exposure:

  • Confusion
  • Hypotension
  • Loss of consciousness & death
  • Movement problems
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Respiratory failure
  • Seizures, cerebral oedema, metabolic acidosis

Chronic Low-Level Exposure:

  • Flu-like symptoms with tiredness
  • Lethargy, dizziness
  • Headache
  • Memory problems
  • Neuropsychological problems
  • Vision problems
  • Cardiovascular problems
Severe: Seizures, coma, respiratory failure, MI Moderate: Confusion, hypotension, movement disorders Mild: Headache, nausea, dizziness
Diagnosis
Confirm & Quantify Exposure

Carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) Interpretation

Timing is Critical: Measure exhaled CO levels immediately - levels decline rapidly once away from the source. Sample during or soon after exposure ends.

Normal Levels:

  • Non-smokers: < 1-2%
  • Pregnant women: up to 5%
  • Smokers: up to 10%
  • Heavy smokers: up to 13%

Abnormal Levels:

  • Toxic effects: 15-20%
  • Severe exposure: ≥30%
  • Important: Levels <15% do not exclude significant poisoning
Critical: COHb ≥30% Moderate: COHb 15-30% Mild: COHb <15%
Note: Some conditions (e.g., haemolytic anaemia, sickle cell disease) cause physiologically high COHb due to endogenous CO production.

Investigations

  • Breath test: Measure exhaled CO levels immediately if available
  • Blood sample: Heparinised venous blood for COHb analysis
  • Pulse oximetry: Standard pulse oximeters are unreliable with COHb
  • CO-oximeter: Use if available to measure COHb accurately
  • Neurological exam: For chronic exposure or neurological signs
For interpretation help: Contact UK National Poisons Information Service (NPIS): 0344 892 0111

Differential Diagnosis

Many features are non-specific. Consider these alternatives:

  • Viral infection - flu-like symptoms
  • Migraine - headache with photophobia/phonophobia
  • Tension headache - common non-specific symptom
  • Depression - lethargy, mood changes
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - persistent tiredness
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis - metabolic acidosis, hyperglycaemia
Critical Point: Do not exclude CO poisoning based on non-specific symptoms alone, especially if exposure history is suggestive.
Treatment
Immediate Management

Oxygen Therapy

First-line: Give 100% oxygen via tight-fitting mask with inflated seal
  • Continue until person is asymptomatic AND:
    • COHb ≤2% in non-smokers
    • COHb ≤10% in smokers
  • Typical duration: about 6 hours
  • Use nasal high flow cannula if available (up to 60 L/min)
Mechanism: Reduces COHb half-life from 4-6 hours (room air) to ~80 minutes (100% O₂)

Referral Criteria

URGENT ED Referral

Immediate ED referral required for:

  • Pregnant women (any exposure)
  • Children with suspected exposure
  • COHb >15%
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Seizures
  • Respiratory failure
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Arrhythmias
  • Severe metabolic acidosis
  • Impaired consciousness
  • Any high-risk group with significant symptoms
Clinical Judgment: Consider referral for other cases based on overall clinical picture.

Prevent Further Exposure

  • Immediate: Warn people not to use suspected appliances
  • Ventilation: Ensure fresh air and proper ventilation
  • Property: Check neighboring properties if in attached dwellings
  • Investigation: Contact local Health Protection Team for coordination

Initial Primary Care Actions

If managing in primary care:
  • Ensure 100% oxygen therapy
  • Obtain COHb measurement
  • Perform neurological examination if indicated
  • Advise to seek medical attention if symptoms develop
  • Arrange follow-up (see Follow-up section)
Follow-up & Prevention
Long-term Management

Follow-up Timeline

1-2 months post-treatment: Assess for delayed, persistent, additional, or intensified symptoms

Assess for complications:

  • Apathy and inability to concentrate
  • Memory impairment
  • Personality changes, emotional lability, irritability
  • Dementia and parkinsonism
  • Disorientation
  • Lethargy, insomnia
  • Vision problems, cortical blindness
  • Neuropathy, chorea, apraxia
  • Mutism, incontinence
  • Psychosis, parosmia
Delayed Onset: Neurological complications can appear up to 40 days post-exposure and may persist in up to 50% of symptomatic acute exposures.

Prevention Advice

DO:
  • Fit audible CO alarm meeting EN 50291 with Kitemark/LPCB logo
  • Have chimneys swept at least annually (twice if burning coal/wood)
  • Use qualified chimney sweep
  • Have gas appliances installed/serviced by Gas Safe registered engineers
DON'T:
  • Use faulty gas appliances (yellow/orange flames, soot, pilot issues)
  • Sleep in room with flueless gas fire or paraffin heater
  • Burn charcoal indoors for cooking/heating
  • Use oversized pots on gas stove or foil around burners
  • Block ventilation grilles, air bricks, or outside flues
Landlord Duty: Annual gas appliance checks and provide safety certificate to tenants.
Install CO Alarm Annual Servicing Ensure Ventilation Gas Safe Engineer

Self-Harm Assessment

Critical Alert: Intentional self-harm accounts for ~40% of CO poisoning admissions. Carefully assess exposure scenario details.

Increased suicide risk in:

  • Males
  • Repeated self-harm history or expressed suicidal intent
  • People with physical health problems
  • Concomitant alcohol or sedative use
Assess Suicide Risk Mental Health Referral

Prognosis

  • Majority recover completely with appropriate treatment
  • Risk factors for complications: Severe poisoning, prolonged exposure, unconsciousness during episode
  • Neurological sequelae: May persist in up to 50% of symptomatic acute exposures
  • Delayed onset: Up to 40 days post-exposure
  • Mortality: ~40 deaths/year in England & Wales from accidental poisoning
