Critical Distinction

What is a 'normal' result which needs action?

These are situations where the lab will mark the result as normal, but clinically, the result still needs action. These are tricky to pick up in practice.

Thyroid Cancer Surveillance

A normal TSH in someone with a history of thyroid cancer, where the TSH should be suppressed.

Gout Management

A normal urate, but it is above target for that person with gout (target <360 vs lab normal <450).

Post-Prostatectomy

A normal PSA when it should be undetectable in someone who has had treatment for prostate cancer.

Deteriorating Trend

Renal function dropping through normal range, but eGFR still >60. The trend matters more than the absolute number.

System Check

Is there a system where you work to catch these subtle abnormalities?

Case Study: The Fishing Trip

The Dangers of Over-investigation

A "fishing trip" is the medical equivalent of bottom trawling: dragging a weighted net across the seabed to catch everything. The problem? You might find a red herring.

Jill (45): I'm feeling tired all the time. It's been going on for a month. It's probably nothing (long pause)... The thing is, I'm not sleeping much because my brother recently got diagnosed with cancer, and I just want a blood test to make sure everything's OK.

Patient

GP: Book in. I'll do some blood tests (ticks ALL the boxes!).

Clinician
2 weeks later...

GP: Your ESR is raised a bit and your CA125 is borderline. You need a scan.

Clinician

CA125...that's a cancer test, right? How long is the scan going to take?

Patient

Now I'm really worried...

2 weeks later...
During the ultrasound scan

Sonographer: Your ovaries are normal, but there's a cyst on your kidney. I'll write to your doctor to get you referred to the urologist.

Result: Anxiety, unnecessary referrals, and incidental findings

Scientific Principles

Why am I ordering this test?

For each tick we place in the box to request a test, we need to be clear about WHY we want the test, and be prepared to take ownership of the result (BJGP 2024;74:e133).

  • 1 Clinical investigation: "My patient is tired and pale – are they anaemic?"
  • 2 Safety monitoring: "My patient is on a diuretic and I need to ensure the potassium is OK."
  • 3 Target measurement: "HbA1c for QoF or clinical targets."
  • 4 External request: Preoperative or secondary care requests (see below: follow-up!)

How will the result change my management?

Related to the first question, we need to have a clear plan for what will happen when we get the results – like a mental flowchart.

IF the haemoglobin is low THEN we will do the following….

We should avoid 'fishing trips': the patient looks unwell so I'm going to tick all the boxes to see what I can find.

Do I know how to interpret?

A good rule of thumb is not to request a test unless you are prepared to interpret and own the result. How will you relay this information to the patient? What will be the implications?

Who will follow-up?

This will often be the person requesting the result. If another clinician (e.g., hospital team) requests it, they should make arrangements to follow-up or provide specific instructions: "Check PSA annually and refer back if >6.5"

What is Normal?

The lab will provide a result, usually along with an upper and lower limit of normal (or 'reference range'). How are these limits set?

How Normal Limits Are Set

Normal limits are often based on a set of values from a healthy population. These values usually make a bell curve (normal distribution). Statistical methods determine the boundary between normal and abnormal.

Example: FIT Test Thresholds

When a FIT is done on an asymptomatic person, it can allow for more blood compared with a test performed on someone with symptoms. This is because individuals with symptoms are more likely to have an underlying issue so we need to examine them more carefully.

'Normal' and 'Target' Levels May Differ

Gout

A lab reference range may be <450 µmol/l, but we need to get the urate <360 to have a clinical impact. Thus, the result may be inadvertently filed as 'normal' if it is 400, but we need to up-titrate therapy.

Secondary Prevention (Len's Story)

Len had an MI last year. He is reluctant to take atorvastatin because he can see his cholesterol is normal. Of course, in secondary prevention, there is no 'normal'. We have a clinical target, but the aim is to get LDL as low as possible.

Primary Prevention

A patient may have a QRISK of 30% despite a healthy lipid profile. The risk reflects their age, sex, smoking and diabetes, but the answer may still involve a statin as part of the plan.

Trends

Declining trends may signal emerging issues, and may be obscured within the normal range.

Anna's Story

Anna has heavy periods and has been tired. She thinks she might be anaemic.

3 Months Ago
150 g/L
Wonderful
Today
122 g/L
Still "Normal"
The Risk: She rings and is told her results are normal. She is not anaemic. Will we spot this 28g/L drop? It's important to study trends as well as today's result.

A Sudden Change for the Worse

Occasionally, we see a sudden, very significant change in the blood results. Why might this happen?

Recent hip fracture and replacement?
Acute illness or hospital admission?
Recent blood transfusion?
New drugs started (e.g., DMARD)?
Sample issue (e.g., high potassium in sample that sat overnight)

If in doubt, assume the worst case

Do not assume a potassium of 6.5 is due to sample degradation! Plan accordingly while investigating the cause.
