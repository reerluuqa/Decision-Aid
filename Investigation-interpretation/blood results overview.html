🧬 Core Concepts

Scientific Principles

For each test we order, we need clarity on purpose, plan, interpretation, and follow-up.

1
Why am I ordering this test?

There are several reasons we might order a test. For each tick in the box, be clear about WHY and be prepared to take ownership of the result.

Clinical investigation: My patient is tired and pale — are they anaemic?
Safety monitoring: My patient is on a diuretic and I need to ensure the potassium is OK.
Clinical/QoF target: HbA1c needs measuring.
Requested by others: Preoperative bloods requested by hospital team.
2
How will the result change my management?

Have a clear plan — like a mental flowchart — for what will happen when you get the results.

✅ Good Practice

IF the haemoglobin is low THEN we will investigate further with iron studies, B12, and folate.

🚫 Avoid

"Fishing trips" — ticking all boxes to see what you find. This risks red herrings and unnecessary anxiety.

3
Do I know how to interpret the result?

A good rule of thumb: don't request a test unless you are prepared to interpret and own the result.

How will you relay this information to the patient? What will be the implications?

Some blood tests will be part of a protocol with measures in place for other clinicians to manage the result, e.g. a healthcare assistant requests bloods in preparation for a cardiovascular disease review with the nurse.

4
Is it clear WHO will follow-up the result?

This will often be the person requesting the result, but not always.

When a secondary care clinician requests tests via primary care, it is good practice that they:

  • Make arrangements to follow-up their test
  • Provide specific instructions on how to act on the result
Example: "Check PSA annually and refer back to clinic if it is higher than 6.5."
📏 Reference Ranges

What is Normal?

The lab provides a result with upper and lower limits. But what does 'normal' really mean?

📊 The Bell Curve

Normal limits are based on values from a healthy population, which usually form a normal distribution (bell curve). Statistical methods determine the boundary between normal and abnormal.

🔧 Adjustments & Context

Reference ranges may be adjusted based on:

👤 Age or biological sex
🤰 Pregnancy status
🎯 Test purpose & clinical context
⚖️ Symptomatic vs. asymptomatic (e.g. FIT test)

'Normal' vs 'Target' — They Can Be Different!

A lab-normal result may not be clinically acceptable.

⚗️ Gout: When Normal Isn't Good Enough

A lab reference range for urate may be <450 µmol/L, but the clinical target for gout management is <360 µmol/L.

Lab limit: 450
Target: 360
0 µmol/L 560 µmol/L
⚠️

A result of 400 µmol/L may be filed as "normal" but the patient still needs urate-lowering therapy up-titrated!

🫀 Case Study: Len's Cholesterol

Len had an MI last year. He is reluctant to take atorvastatin to lower his cholesterol because he can clearly see on his results that his cholesterol is "normal."

🚩

In secondary prevention, there is no "normal." The clinical target is to get LDL as low as possible. A lab-normal cholesterol does not mean treatment isn't needed.

💚 Primary CVD Prevention

A patient may have a QRISK of 30% despite a healthy lipid profile. The risk reflects their age, sex, smoking, and diabetes.

💡

The answer may still involve a statin as part of the plan, even with "normal" lipid results. Risk is about more than a single blood number.

🚩

'Normal' Results That Need Action

Critical Awareness

These are situations where the lab marks the result as normal, but clinically, the result still needs action.

🚩

A normal TSH in someone with a history of thyroid cancer, where the TSH should be suppressed.

🚩

A normal urate, but it is above target for that person with gout.

🚩

A normal PSA when it should be undetectable in someone who has had treatment for prostate cancer.

🚩

A deteriorating trend: renal function dropping through normal range, but eGFR still >60.

🤔

These are tricky to pick up in practice. Is there a system where you work?

📈 Spotting Patterns

Trends Matter

Declining trends may signal emerging issues, and may be obscured within the normal range.

A Sudden Change for the Worse

❓ Why might this happen?

  • ⚠️ Has anything happened since the last sample — e.g. hip fracture and replacement?
  • ⚠️ Has there been an acute illness or hospital admission?
  • ⚠️ Has the patient had a blood transfusion?
  • ⚠️ Have new drugs been started, e.g. a DMARD?

✅ What should you do?

  • ✓ Check the hospital results for alignment
  • ✓ Check the interval since the last result
  • ✓ How is the patient clinically?
  • ✓ Consider a sample issue (e.g. high K⁺ in an overnight sample)
🛑

If in doubt, assume the worst case and plan accordingly. Do not assume a potassium of 6.5 is due to sample degradation!

🎣 Caution

Fishing Trips & Over-Investigation

The dangers of ticking every box "just to see what comes up."

🎣 What is a 'Fishing Trip'?

A fishing trip is the medical equivalent of bottom trawling: dragging a weighted net across the seabed to catch everything, then seeing what you found.

The problem: You might find a red herring — irrelevant, incidental positive findings which lead to anxiety and a merry-go-round of unnecessary and costly onward tests and referrals.

📖 Jill's Story

A cautionary tale of over-investigation

🗓️ Initial Appointment
Jill (45 years old)
I'm feeling tired all the time. It's been going on for a month. It's probably nothing… The thing is, I'm not sleeping much because my brother recently got diagnosed with cancer, and I just want a blood test to make sure everything's OK.
Doctor
Book in. I'll do some blood tests (ticks ALL the boxes!)
📅 2 Weeks Later
Doctor (phone call)
Your ESR is raised a bit and your CA125 is borderline. You need a scan.
Jill
CA125… that's a cancer test, right? How long is the scan going to take?
Jill's thoughts
Now I'm really worried…
📅 2 More Weeks Later — Ultrasound
Ultrasound Results
Your ovaries are normal, but there's a cyst on your kidney. I'll write to your doctor to get you referred to the urologist.
Jill's thoughts
A cyst? On my kidney? What does that mean? Is it cancer too?

🔴 The Cascade of Consequences

Jill came in tired and stressed about her brother's cancer diagnosis. The "fishing trip" blood tests found incidental findings that were almost certainly unrelated — but triggered anxiety, further tests, scans, and referrals. The original problem (sleep deprivation due to worry) was never addressed.

✅ Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Always know WHY you are requesting a test before you tick the box
  • ✓ Have a clear mental flowchart for how you'll act on the result
  • ✓ Don't request a test unless you can interpret and own the result
  • ✓ Ensure it's clear WHO will follow up the result
  • ✓ "Normal" on the lab report doesn't always mean clinically acceptable
  • ✓ Trends matter — a dropping value within range can be significant
  • ✓ Avoid fishing trips — they risk red herrings and patient anxiety
  • ✓ If in doubt about a sudden change, assume the worst and plan accordingly
