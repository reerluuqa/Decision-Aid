Key Point: Male factor infertility alone contributes up to 30% of cases of infertile opposite-sex couples and to an additional 30% to 40% in combination with other factors.

Definition

The clinical definition of male factor infertility is the presence of abnormal semen parameters or sperm functional assays, or the functional inability to adequately deliver semen into the vaginal canal, in the male partner of an opposite-sex couple unable to achieve conception after 1 year of unprotected intercourse.

Epidemiology

  • 10% to 15% of opposite-sex couples will have difficulty conceiving
  • Male factor alone is responsible for 20% to 30% of cases
  • In an additional 30% to 40% of opposite-sex couples, male factor is present in combination with other factors
  • The incidence of male factor infertility is believed to be increasing in the developed world

Aetiology

  • Abnormal spermatogenesis (most common, often unknown aetiology)
  • Reproductive tract anomalies or obstruction
  • Sexual and ejaculatory dysfunction
  • Impaired sperm motility

Pathophysiology

Male fertility requires normal sperm production and transport and adequate sexual performance. These functions require normal levels of testosterone, which is produced by testicular Leydig cells under the influence of luteinising hormone (LH).

Spermatogenesis is controlled directly by intratesticular testosterone and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH). FSH acts on the Sertoli cells, which support spermatogenesis in the seminiferous tubules.

Diagnostic Approach: Male factor infertility should be suspected in the presence of normal ovulation and patent tubes in the female partner of an infertile opposite-sex couple. The main goal of evaluating male factor infertility is to find a correctable cause.

Key Diagnostic Factors

  • Presence of risk factors (common)
  • Inability of an opposite-sex couple to conceive (common)
  • Vasectomy (common)
  • Palpable and dilated testicular veins (common)
  • Erectile dysfunction and decreased libido (uncommon)
  • Testis atrophy (uncommon)

Strong Risk Factors

  • Varicocele - Dilation of the veins of the pampiniform plexus of the scrotum
  • Cryptorchidism - Undescended testis
  • Prior chemotherapy or radiotherapy
  • Current medications - Hormone therapy, certain antifungal agents, etc.
  • Cystic fibrosis and congenital bilateral absence of vas deferens
  • Y chromosome abnormalities
  • Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY)
  • Endocrinopathy

Other Tests to Consider

  • Sperm viability - Histological test using eosin and nigrosin
  • Sperm membrane function - Determines cell viability using hypo-osmotic solution
  • Hormonal assays - FSH, LH, testosterone, estradiol, prolactin
  • MRI of the pituitary and hypothalamus - For hypogonadotrophic hypogonadism
  • Genetic analysis - Karyotype analysis and Y chromosome microdeletions
  • Testicular biopsy - For non-obstructive azoospermia

Recommended Investigations Based on Semen Analysis

Oligozoospermia (<16 million sperm/mL)

May indicate a disruption of spermatogenesis at many different levels. Morning endocrine evaluation is indicated, particularly in men with sperm counts <10 million sperm/mL. Physical examination or Doppler imaging may show the presence of a varicocele.

Azoospermia

Defined by the absence of sperm in the semen after centrifugation of the sample and analysis of the pellet. Indicative of primary or secondary testicular failure, presence of cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) mutation, or blockage of the reproductive tract.

Abnormal sperm motility (<42% total motility, <30% progressive motility)

If the sperm motility is less than the normal value, sperm viability and membrane function may be tested. Anti-sperm antibody testing may also be considered, but only if the results will affect management.

Abnormal sperm morphology (<4% normal forms - Kruger strict morphology)

Determines whether the sperm has successfully completed spermiogenesis, and is a measure of sperm fitness for fertilisation and conception.

Aspermia (absence of ejaculate)

Low volume, decreased pH, or aspermia indicates reproductive tract disorders and should be followed up with prostate examination, transrectal ultrasound, and/or post-ejaculatory urine analysis.

Treatment Goal: The treatment goal for male infertility is to achieve pregnancy and restore normal reproductive function for the man. Assisted reproductive techniques are often the fastest and most effective method to achieve pregnancy regardless of the aetiology.

Medical and Surgical Therapy

Obstructive Azoospermia
  • Transurethral resection of the ejaculatory ducts
  • Microsurgical vasovasostomy or epididymovasostomy
  • Sperm retrieval with IVF/ICSI
Hormonal Deficiencies
  • Gonadotrophins or pulsatile GnRH for secondary hypogonadism
  • hCG for hypogonadotrophic hypogonadism
  • Clomifene for low testosterone levels
  • Aromatase inhibitors for high estrogen/low testosterone
Varicocele Treatment
  • Controversial benefit in subfertile men
  • May improve semen parameters
  • May reduce need for IVF/IUI
  • Microsurgical repair has better outcomes

Assisted Reproductive Techniques (ART)

These techniques are recommended when medical/surgical intervention is ineffective, contraindicated, or unlikely to succeed.

Intrauterine Insemination (IUI)
  • Minimally invasive ART
  • Considered for men with low post-wash total motile sperm counts
  • Pregnancy rates optimized with post-wash TMSC of at least 9 million
IVF with ICSI
  • Recommended for severe male factor infertility
  • Often the fastest and most effective method to achieve pregnancy
  • Bypasses many male factor infertility issues
Sperm Retrieval Techniques
  • Microdissection testicular sperm extraction (micro-TESE) for non-obstructive azoospermia
  • Percutaneous and open techniques from epididymis and testicle
  • Followed by IVF/ICSI

Patient Discussion Points

  • Discuss all available treatment options with the couple
  • Consider female partner's age in treatment decisions
  • Explain success rates and potential complications of each option
  • Discuss financial implications of ART
  • Address psychological impact of infertility
  • Discuss lifestyle modifications that may improve fertility

Treatment Algorithm Overview

Initial Evaluation

History, physical examination, and at least two semen analyses (1 month apart)

Identify Correctable Causes

Endocrine evaluation, genetic testing, and imaging to identify specific aetiological factors

Targeted Treatment

Medical or surgical interventions based on identified causes (e.g., varicocelectomy, hormonal therapy)

Assisted Reproductive Techniques

If targeted treatment is ineffective, contraindicated, or unlikely to succeed, consider ART (IUI, IVF/ICSI)

Follow-up and Monitoring

Regular assessment of treatment effectiveness and adjustment of management plan as needed

Emergency Situations: These red flags require immediate attention and may indicate serious underlying conditions that need urgent evaluation and management.

Urgent Referral Required

  • Testicular torsion - Sudden onset of severe testicular pain and swelling
  • Testicular cancer - Hard, painless testicular mass or lump
  • Pituitary tumor - Headaches, visual disturbances, galactorrhoea
  • Severe hypogonadism - Markedly decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, loss of secondary sexual characteristics

Prompt Evaluation Needed

  • Azoospermia - Absence of sperm in semen after centrifugation
  • Severe oligozoospermia - Sperm concentration <5 million/mL
  • History of chemotherapy or radiotherapy - May cause permanent loss of spermatogenesis
  • Genetic abnormalities - Y chromosome microdeletions, Klinefelter syndrome

Important Considerations

  • Female partner age - Natural decline in female fertility accelerates after 35
  • Previous pregnancies - Previous successful pregnancies suggest acquired cause
  • Duration of infertility - Longer duration may indicate more severe issues
  • Psychological impact - Address stress and relationship strain

Contraindications & Precautions

  • Testosterone supplementation - Can inhibit spermatogenesis
  • Anabolic steroids - Negative feedback on FSH production
  • 5-alpha reductase inhibitors - May impair semen parameters
  • Long-term opiate use - Can cause hormonal dysregulation
  • Post-chemotherapy/radiotherapy - Prevent pregnancy for at least 12 months

Positive Actions & Preventive Measures

  • Lifestyle modifications
    • Smoking cessation
    • Reduced alcohol consumption
    • Weight management
    • Avoid excessive heat exposure
  • Medication review - Identify and discontinue gonadotoxic medications
  • Fertility preservation - Sperm banking before chemotherapy/radiotherapy
  • Early evaluation - Prompt assessment of infertile couples
  • Psychological support - Address emotional aspects of infertility
