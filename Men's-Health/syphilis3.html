Syphilis — Primary Care Fast‑Reference

Syphilis — Primary Care Fast‑Reference Algorithm

Designed for rapid recognition and decision‑making in consultation (adults ≥16 y).

Based on NICE CKS (last revised Mar 2025) & BASHH 2024/25 First‑contact / primary care use Excludes pregnancy, children & congenital syphilis

Bottom line: Suspect → Refer · If referral declined → Discuss with GUM and arrange testing as advised → Any +ve → Re‑refer.

When to suspect syphilis

Consider in non‑specific or absent symptoms; diagnosis is commonly delayed.

Key clinical cues
  • Primary: solitary, painless, indurated ano‑genital/oral/perianal ulcer (chancre) ± regional lymphadenopathy; can be multiple, painful or purulent; may be extra‑genital.
  • Secondary: non‑pruritic generalized rash (often palms/soles), condylomata lata, mucous patches, patchy alopecia, generalized lymphadenopathy.
  • Unexplained neuro‑ophthalmic features: meningitis, cranial nerve palsies, uveitis, optic neuropathy, retinitis, hearing loss.
Risk factors
  • Unprotected sex; multiple/anonymous partners; transactional sex.
  • MSM; HIV infection; prior STI; injecting drug use.
  • Social vulnerability (poverty, homelessness, migrant/refugee status).

Immediate primary‑care actions

  • Refer all suspected cases to GUM/sexual‑health specialist for testing, staging, management & partner notification.
  • Advise no sexual contact until diagnosis excluded or treatment completed/confirmed.
  • Screen for other STIs (HIV, Hep B/C, gonorrhoea, chlamydia) via specialist service.
Brief counselling & prevention
  • Condom use; avoid drugs/alcohol with sex; risk‑reduction and regular screening for high‑risk groups.
  • Provide patient information (NHS/BASHH resources).

If referral is declined

Discuss with GUM. If advised to test in primary care, proceed as follows and re‑refer promptly if any result is positive.

Direct tests (from active lesions)
  • Dark‑field microscopy — immediate result; high specificity/low sensitivity; not suitable for oral lesions; less reliable for rectal/non‑penile lesions.
  • PCR — higher sensitivity; usable on oral/other lesions; may detect primary infection before serology.
Serology
  • Treponemal (EIA/CLIA, TPHA, immunoblot): high sensitivity after early primary; usually remain positive for life → not for monitoring.
  • Non‑treponemal (RPR): typically positive ~6 wks post‑infection; titres peak by 1–2 y; fall post‑treatment → useful for monitoring.
False results & repeats
  • False‑negatives: treponemal tests pre‑chancre/early; RPR in secondary/early latent (more likely with HIV); very late disease.
  • False‑positives: non‑treponemal tests with viral infections, autoimmune disease, malignancy, pregnancy, age, IDU.
  • Repeat serology: after isolated high‑risk exposure at 3 months; or 2 weeks after possible chancre if DFM/PCR negative.
  • Neurosyphilis: requires CSF examination in addition to serology.
Also
  • Take a virology swab of lesions to exclude HSV.
  • Screen for other STIs as above.
  • Legal note — intentional/reckless transmission may be prosecutable; document advice given.

Key differentials

Genital/oral/anal ulcer
  • HSV, chancroid, LGV, Behçet’s, secondary infection/trauma, Crohn’s (peri‑anal), malignancy.
Secondary‑like features
  • Rash: HIV seroconversion, rubella, scabies, measles; psoriasis, pityriasis rosea, eczema; drug eruption.
  • Condylomata‑like: HPV, molluscum, haemorrhoids.
  • Patchy alopecia: alopecia areata, scarring alopecia, tinea capitis.
  • Oral mucosal lesions: aphthous, oral cancer; tonsillar ulcers vs infectious mononucleosis (esp. post‑ampicillin/amoxicillin rash).

Management principles (specialist‑led)

  • Penicillin (benzathine penicillin G) is first‑line; alternatives (e.g., doxycycline) per specialist advice and stage.
  • Partner notification & testing via trained staff; prevent re‑infection.
  • Follow‑up with RPR titres to assess response and detect relapse/re‑infection; consider CSF where indicated.

Complications to remember

Neurosyphilis
  • Early: meningitis, CN palsies, hearing loss, uveitis/retinitis, ischaemic events.
  • Late: tabes dorsalis; general paresis → cognitive decline, seizures, hemiparesis.
Other
  • Cardiovascular: aortic aneurysm/regurgitation, angina, heart failure.
  • Gummatous disease of skin/bone.
  • Pregnancy: adverse outcomes; congenital syphilis (managed separately).

Bottom line

Suspect → Refer → (If testing in primary care is unavoidable) test per GUM advice → Any positive → Re‑refer for full staging and treatment.

Prepared for rapid in‑consult use. This aide excludes screening, pregnancy, children and congenital syphilis. Follow your local pathways and lab repertoire.
