Syphilis Management in Primary Care

A Quick Reference Algorithm for Physicians

1

Initial Suspicion & Assessment

When to Suspect Syphilis

  • Risk Factors: Unprotected sex, multiple partners, MSM, history of other STIs, HIV infection, or illicit drug use.
  • Primary Syphilis: A solitary, painless, firm ulcer (chancre) on genitals, anus, or mouth. Appears 9-90 days post-exposure.
  • Secondary Syphilis: Non-itchy rash (often on palms/soles), moist wart-like lesions (condylomata lata), patchy hair loss, oral lesions, fever, and generalized swollen lymph nodes.
  • Latent/Tertiary Syphilis: Asymptomatic (latent) or late-stage severe cardiovascular/neurological symptoms (tertiary).

Immediate Assessment

  • Sexual History: Cover partners from the last 3 months (primary) or 2 years (secondary).
  • Physical Exam: Check genitals, all skin (including palms/soles), mouth, and perform basic neuro/eye exams.
2

Primary Management: Refer to Specialist

Refer ALL patients with suspected syphilis to a GUM clinic or local sexual health service.

Rationale for Urgent Referral

  • Specialized Testing: Access to tests like dark-field microscopy.
  • Expert Interpretation: Complex serology requires expertise for staging and monitoring.
  • Comprehensive Screening: Full screening for other STIs, including HIV.
  • Contact Tracing: Trained staff manage partner notification to prevent further spread.
  • Crucial Follow-Up: Essential to detect treatment failure, relapse, or reinfection.

Advice While Awaiting Referral

  • Abstain from sexual contact until diagnosis is ruled out or treatment confirmed.
  • Provide patient information leaflets from NHS or BASHH.
  • Discuss prevention (e.g., condom use).
3

Alternative Pathway: Patient Declines Referral

Action Steps

  • Reiterate Importance: Strongly advise specialist care again.
  • Consult a GUM Specialist: Seek immediate phone guidance on next steps.
  • HIV Positive Patients: Offer referral to their infectious diseases/HIV clinic.

If Testing in Primary Care (per specialist advice)

  • Virology Swab: Take from active lesions to rule out herpes simplex.
  • Syphilis Serology: Be aware of potential false negatives in early infection. Repeat testing may be necessary.
  • Screen for other STIs as appropriate.

Important Considerations

Positive Results: If any test is positive, strongly re-urge GUM referral. Appropriate treatment is not easily accessible in primary care.

Legal Responsibility: Advise patients they may be liable for prosecution if they decline treatment and knowingly infect others.
