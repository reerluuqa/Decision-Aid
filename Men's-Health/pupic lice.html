Pubic Lice Management Algorithm

A Quick Reference Guide for Primary Care Professionals

☴1. Initial Assessment

Patient History

  • Symptoms: Ask about onset and nature (e.g., genital itching, often worse at night).
  • Sexual History: Essential for determining STI screening needs and partner notification.
  • Previous Treatments: Note any self-treatments or previously failed therapies.

Examination

  • Visual Inspection: Look for live lice (crab-shaped, grey-brown, ~2mm) or eggs/nits (yellow-white) on pubic and perianal hair. A magnifying lens is helpful.
  • Check Other Areas: Examine other coarse body hair (chest, armpits, legs, beard, eyebrows, eyelashes).
  • Associated Signs: Note rust-coloured specks (lice faeces), small blue macules at feeding sites, or skin excoriation from scratching.

🔍2. Diagnosis

Diagnosis is confirmed by the visual identification of live lice or eggs (nits) on hair shafts.

Differential Diagnosis to Consider

  • Scabies, Body Lice, Head Lice
  • Folliculitis or Seborrhoeic Dermatitis
  • Dermatophyte (Fungal) Infection
  • Conjunctivitis/Blepharitis (if eyelashes are involved)

ďA;3. Management Plan

First-Line Treatment (Excluding Eyelashes)

  • Permethrin 5% cream: Apply to whole body. Leave on for 8-12 hours (overnight), then wash off. Repeat application after 7 days.
  • OR Malathion 0.5% aqueous solution: Apply to whole body. Leave on for 12 hours (overnight), then wash off. Repeat application after 7 days.

Special Consideration: Eyelash Infestation
Treat with an inert occlusive ophthalmic ointment (e.g., simple eye ointment BP) applied twice daily for 8-10 days. Regular petroleum jelly is not recommended due to potential eye irritation.

Crucial Patient Education & Advice

  • Application: Provide clear, written instructions. Stress the importance of treating the entire body.
  • Decontamination: Machine wash all bedding, clothing, and towels from the last 3 days in hot water (≥50°C). Seal non-washable items in a plastic bag for 2 weeks.
  • Transmission: Advise no close body or sexual contact until the patient and their partner(s) have successfully completed treatment.
  • Persistent Itching: Reassure the patient that itching may continue for several days after successful treatment.
  • Nit Removal: Dead nits can be removed with a fine-toothed comb for cosmetic reasons.

📅4. Sexual Health & Follow-up

Sexual Health Management

  • GUM Clinic Referral: If acquired via sexual contact, refer to a GUM clinic for contact tracing (partners from last 3 months) and full STI screening (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, HIV, Syphilis).

Safeguarding Alert: Presence in a Child
While non-sexual transmission is possible, the presence of pubic lice in a child requires consideration of sexual abuse. Manage according to local safeguarding procedures.

Follow-up Appointment (1 week after 2nd treatment)

  • Live lice present? Confirm correct technique. If incorrect, repeat treatment. If correct, switch to the alternative insecticide.
  • No live lice? Treatment is successful. Reassure patient (dead nits may remain).
  • Persistent Failure? Refer to or discuss with a GUM specialist.

This algorithm is a condensed summary based on NICE CKS guidance on Pubic Lice for rapid clinical decision-making.
