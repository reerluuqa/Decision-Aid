Haematospermia Management

A Quick-Reference Clinical Algorithm for Primary Care

Patient Presents with Haematospermia (Blood in Semen)

STEP 1: Initial Assessment (History & Examination)

  • History: Age, symptom duration/frequency, associated urinary/STI symptoms, recent procedures, trauma, medication (anticoagulants), sexual/travel history, family history of cancer/bleeding disorders.
  • Examination: Measure BP. Palpate abdomen. Examine urethral meatus & testes. Consider Digital Rectal Exam (DRE) based on age (≥40) and clinical picture.

STEP 2: Initial Investigations (For All Patients)

  • Urinalysis: Send mid-stream urine (MSU) for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.

STEP 3: Risk Stratification & Management Pathway

PATHWAY A: Patient < 40 Years Old

  • Single episode & no red flags: Reassure patient (likely benign & self-limiting). Advise return if recurrent.
  • Suspected cause:
    • UTI → Treat with antibiotics.
    • STI → Refer to GUM clinic.
  • When to Refer to Urology: Recurrent/persistent haematospermia with no cause, or if symptoms continue despite treatment.

PATHWAY B: Patient ≥ 40 Years Old

  • Additional Investigation: Measure Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) level.
  • URGENT 2-WEEK WAIT REFERRAL (Urology): If signs of prostate/testicular cancer (elevated PSA, suspicious DRE, testicular mass).
  • ROUTINE REFERRAL to Urology: All men in this age group with haematospermia where no cause is identified in primary care.
  • While awaiting specialist review, manage any identifiable causes (e.g., UTI, hypertension).

Red Flags Requiring Urgent Action / Referral (Any Age)

  • Suspicion of Urological Malignancy: Abnormal DRE, elevated PSA, testicular mass/swelling → Urology (2-week wait).
  • Suspicion of Haematological Malignancy: Unexplained bruising, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy → Haematology (2-week wait).
  • Recurrent or Persistent Haematospermia: Warrants specialist referral if no cause is identified and treated in primary care.

This is a memory aide based on the NICE CKS topic on Haematospermia (last revised March 2022). Always use clinical judgment and refer to the full guidance when necessary.
