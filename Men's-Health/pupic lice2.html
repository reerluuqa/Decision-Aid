Pubic Lice — Quick Reference (Point of Care)

Pubic Lice (Pediculosis pubis)

One‑page algorithm for fast diagnosis & management in primary care. From age ≥6 months.

Algorithm (at a glance) Re‑examine at 1 week

Confirm diagnosis. Genital pruritus (often nocturnal) + see live lice/nits; look for blue macules/red papules and rust‑coloured specks. Check other coarse hair (eyelashes/eyebrows/axillae/beard/chest/trunk/thighs).
Assess acquisition. If likely sexual: GUM referral for partner notification (last 3 months) and STI screen (chlamydia, gonorrhoea, HIV, syphilis).
Treat (non‑eyelash areas). Choose one:
  • Permethrin 5% cream — whole body application; leave 8–12 h; repeat day 7.
  • Malathion 0.5% aqueous — whole body application; leave 12 h; repeat day 7.
Cover pubic, perianal, inner thighs to knees, trunk hair contiguous with pubic area; treat facial hair if infested (not eyelashes/eyebrows). Remove nits with a fine‑tooth comb.
Eyelashes involved? Use inert/paraffin ophthalmic ointment BID × 8–10 days (preferred in <18 yrs, pregnancy, breastfeeding). Alternative: permethrin 1% lotion to lashes (eyes closed) then wash off after 10 min. Avoid Vaseline.
Give safety & prevention advice. Itch may persist days–weeks post‑treatment. Launder clothes/bedding ≥50°C or dry‑clean; items that can’t be laundered → seal in plastic bag for 2 weeks. Avoid close body contact and sharing clothing/bedding until all partners treated.
Follow‑up at 1 week. If live lice: verify technique.
  • Incorrect technique → repeat same agent correctly.
  • Correct technique → switch to the alternative agent.
Dead nits may remain and do not indicate failure.
Refer / discuss with GUM for treatment failure after alternative regimen, atypical/uncertain diagnosis, or safeguarding concerns in children.

Diagnosis — Key cues

  • Genital itching (often worse at night).
  • See live lice (~2 mm, grey‑brown, crab‑shaped) and/or nits on hair shafts.
  • Feeding signs: small blue macules (maculae ceruleae), red papules; rust specks on underwear.
  • Examine other coarse hair: eyelashes, eyebrows, beard, axillae, scalp margins, trunk/limbs.

Initial actions

  • Ascertain sexual vs non‑sexual acquisition.
  • If sexual: refer to GUM for partner notification (last 3 months) & full STI screen.
  • Consider sexual abuse in children; follow safeguarding pathways.

First‑line treatment (non‑eyelash areas)

  • Permethrin 5% cream — apply to whole body; allow to dry naturally; wash off after 8–12 h; repeat after 7 days.
  • Malathion 0.5% aqueous solution — apply to whole body; allow to dry naturally; wash off after 12 h/overnight; repeat after 7 days.
  • Cover pubic/perianal region, inner thighs to knees, any trunk hair contiguous with pubic area; treat facial hair if infested (excluding eyelashes/eyebrows).
  • Remove nits with a fine‑tooth comb.

Eyelash involvement

  • Preferred: inert/paraffin ophthalmic ointment BID × 8–10 days (use in <18 yrs, pregnancy, breastfeeding).
  • Alternative: permethrin 1% lotion to eyelashes (eyes closed) → wash off after 10 min.
  • Avoid petroleum jelly (can irritate eyes).

Patient information & prevention

  • Itching may persist for several days–weeks after effective treatment (especially if excoriated).
  • Decontaminate clothing & bedding: wash ≥50°C; or dry‑clean; or seal in a plastic bag for 2 weeks.
  • Avoid close body contact and sharing of clothing/bedding until all partners have successfully completed treatment.
  • Provide clear written instructions to maximise adherence.

Follow‑up (1 week)

  • Re‑examine to confirm eradication.
  • If live lice present:
    • Incorrect technique → repeat same agent correctly.
    • Correct technique → switch to the alternative insecticide.
  • Dead nits may remain attached — does not indicate failure.

When to refer / discuss

  • Failure despite alternative regimen.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty or atypical clinical picture.
  • Safeguarding concerns in children.

Prescribing at a glance

Permethrin 5% cream

  • Indication: crab louse infestation; generally used from ≥2 months.
  • Apply whole body; avoid eyes; not on broken/secondarily infected skin.
  • Wash off after 8–12 h; reapply day 7.
  • Caution in pregnancy/breastfeeding; common AEs: paraesthesia, burning, itch, erythema, dry skin.

Malathion 0.5% aqueous solution

  • Licensed from ≥6 months; avoid eyes; flammable — avoid heat/hair dryers.
  • Whole body; wash off after 12 h/overnight; reapply day 7.
  • Do not use more than once a week for 3 consecutive weeks.
  • Caution in pregnancy/breastfeeding; possible AEs: angioedema, hypersensitivity, skin reactions.

Use local formulary/BNF for full contraindications, interactions and adverse effects.

Differentials

  • Scabies; head lice; body lice; folliculitis.
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis (scales brush off; nits don’t).
  • Dermatophyte infection.
  • Conjunctivitis/blepharitis if eyelashes involved.

Notes

This page condenses user‑supplied guidance into a single‑page clinical aide. It is for trained clinicians; apply clinical judgment and local policy. If infestation thought sexually acquired, use GUM pathways for partner tracing and STI screening. Children: presence of pubic lice can occur via close non‑genital contact, but consider sexual abuse and manage per safeguarding guidance.
