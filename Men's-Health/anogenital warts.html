Anogenital Warts Management

Clinical Decision Support Algorithm

Last Updated: May 2024

Assessment Management Treatment Special Cases Patient Advice Referral

Assessment & Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • Visual diagnosis (biopsy only for atypical lesions)
  • History: Sexual activity, symptoms (discharge, bleeding, dysuria)
  • Examination: External genitalia, perianal area, adjacent sites
  • Consider: Speculum exam, proctoscopy, meatoscopy if indicated

Differential Diagnosis

  • Pearly penile papules
  • Molluscum contagiosum
  • Condylomata lata (secondary syphilis)
  • Carcinoma in situ
  • Skin tags, seborrhoeic keratoses

Management Approach

Primary Care Management Criteria

  • Refer to sexual health specialist if possible
  • Primary care management if:
    • Referral declined/unavailable
    • Clear diagnosis of external warts
    • Expertise/resources available
    • STI screening performed (especially <25 years)

Special Considerations

Pregnancy

  • Warts may enlarge during pregnancy
  • Delay treatment until postpartum if possible
  • Avoid topical treatments
  • Consider cryotherapy/TCA if necessary
  • Low risk of vertical transmission

Immunocompromised

  • Refer to specialist
  • May have larger/more numerous lesions
  • Higher risk of treatment failure and recurrence
  • Higher risk of malignant transformation

Patient Advice

Key Counseling Points

  • Multiple treatments often needed (1-6 months)
  • High relapse rates (treatments don't eliminate HPV)
  • Local skin reactions common
  • No changes to cervical cytology screening
  • Recommend condom use and smoking cessation
  • Provide written information (NHS patient guide)

Referral Criteria

When to Refer

  • All cases (preferred)
  • Atypical lesions
  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Pregnant women
  • Treatment failure
  • Internal warts (urethral, vaginal, anal canal)
