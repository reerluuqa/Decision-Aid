Male Urethritis — One‑Page Reference

NICE CKS • May 2024

Fast decision‑support for consultations

Concise, clinic‑ready algorithm covering assessment, empiric therapy, follow‑up and treatment failure for men ≥16 years.

Primary feature: urethral discharge ± dysuria Test broadly (NAATs + BBV screen) Refer to GUM when possible Empiric doxycycline first‑line
One‑page layout Fluoroquinolone caution (MHRA) Print‑friendly

Bottom line

  • Refer to GUM where possible for confirmation and management.
  • If managed in primary care, doxycycline 100 mg PO BD × 7 days is first‑line for NGU.
  • Advise no sex until both patient and partners complete treatment and symptoms resolve.
  • Review at 1–2 weeks: symptom resolution, results, and partner notification.

1) Clinical suspicion

  • Urethral discharge (mucoid / mucopurulent / purulent) — may be minimal; may need urethral milking.
  • Dysuria; ± penile irritation/erythema, itch, balanoposthitis.

2) Assessment

History

  • Symptoms, onset, last sexual contact (and previous if ≤3 months), partners and numbers.
  • Type of sex/sites exposed; condom use; risk behaviours; prior STIs; BBV risks; trauma/irritants; PMH; drugs; allergies.

Examination

  • Underwear for discharge; genital exam; retract foreskin; assess meatus for discharge/crusting/redness.
  • If no discharge, gently milk urethra. Palpate for adenopathy, masses, tenderness. ± DRE if prostatitis suspected.

Investigations

  • First‑void urine NAAT: Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae (same sample if local lab supports).
  • Consider: Mycoplasma genitalium NAAT (local availability); Trichomonas (urethral swab and/or first‑void urine) if suspected/partner positive.
  • Screen: HIV, syphilis ± hepatitis (risk‑based). If UTI suspected → MSU for C&S.

Working diagnosis in primary care if any:

  • Mucopurulent/purulent discharge; or
  • First‑void urine leukocyte esterase ≥1+ (collect ≥1 h after last void); or
  • Urinary threads (mucus/pus strands) in first‑void urine (note: may be physiological).

3) Management

Refer

Offer GUM referral to all for confirmation and treatment. If declined or not possible → manage in primary care.

Empiric treatment for suspected NGU
RegimenDose & DurationNotes
Doxycycline (first‑line) 100 mg PO BD × 7 days Check contraindications (pregnancy, breastfeeding, <12 y), photosensitivity advice.
Azithromycin (alternative) 1 g stat → 500 mg PO OD × 2 days Abstain from sex until 14 days after start and symptoms resolve; QT‑risk cautions.
Ofloxacin (alternative if others unsuitable) 200 mg BD or 400 mg OD × 7 days Fluoroquinolone safety: use only if necessary (MHRA). Tendon/CNS/QT/aorta/valve cautions.
If specific organism suspected
  • Gonorrhoea suspected (e.g., local outbreak) → treat per gonorrhoea guideline.
  • Trichomonas suspected/partner positive → treat per trichomonas guideline.
Advice & partner management
  • Likely STI cause; discuss complications for patient/partners.
  • No sexual contact (incl. oral) until patient and partners complete treatment and symptoms resolve.
  • Adherence and adverse‑effect counselling; safer‑sex/condom use; provide BASHH/FPA leaflets.
  • Partner notification: all partners in previous 4 weeks (follow specific STI guidance if positive).

4) Follow‑up (1–2 weeks)

  • Check symptom resolution; review lab results.
  • Ensure partner notification completed.
  • Address adherence/adverse effects; reinforce safer sex.

5) Treatment failure (persistent/recurrent symptoms)

First steps

  • Strongly re‑offer GUM attendance.
  • Check adherence and exclude re‑infection (partners treated simultaneously?).
  • Reconsider diagnosis (UTI, prostatitis, HSV, balanitis, stones, reactive arthritis, non‑infective causes).

Quick safety notes

Doxycycline

  • Avoid in pregnancy, breastfeeding, and children <12 y.
  • Photosensitivity; oesophagitis risk (take with water, sit upright).
  • Interactions: antacids/iron/zinc (separate 2–3 h), warfarin (↑INR), retinoids (avoid), lithium/carbamazepine/phenytoin (monitor).

Azithromycin

  • QT‑prolongation risk; caution with other QT‑drugs/electrolyte issues.
  • Interactions: digoxin/warfarin/statins/rifabutin/DOACs (monitor).

Fluoroquinolones (e.g., Ofloxacin)

  • Use only if alternatives unsuitable (MHRA). Risks: tendonitis/rupture, neuropathy, CNS, dysglycaemia, QT, aortic aneurysm/dissection, valve regurgitation.
  • Avoid with corticosteroids; caution in >60 y and seizure risk.
Metronidazole notes
  • Warn possible disulfiram‑like reaction with alcohol (avoid during treatment and for ≥48 h after).
  • Interactions: warfarin (↑INR), lithium (toxicity risk), ciclosporin (↑levels), 5‑FU (↑toxicity).
