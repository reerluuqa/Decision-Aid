Genital Herpes Clinical Algorithm

A concise reference guide for healthcare professionals managing genital herpes in primary care

Initial Assessment

Suspect genital herpes if patient presents with:

  • Multiple painful genital blisters that burst to leave erosions/ulcers
  • Lesions typically develop 4-7 days after exposure
  • Prodromal tingling/burning (48hrs before lesions in recurrences)
  • Dysuria, vaginal/urethral discharge
  • Systemic symptoms (more common with first episode): headache, malaise, fever

Examination findings:

  • First episode: usually bilateral lesions, redness, vesicles, ulcers
  • Recurrent episodes: usually less severe, unilateral, localized to same area
  • May have tender bilateral inguinal lymphadenopathy
Diagnostic Approach

Refer to specialist sexual health service for confirmation (ideal)

If unable to refer:

  • Take viral swab from base of lesion for PCR testing
  • Consider rectal swab if suspected proctitis
  • Screen for other STIs
Differential Diagnosis

Infectious:

  • Syphilis, chancroid, lymphogranuloma venereum
  • Granuloma inguinale, trichomoniasis, herpes zoster

Non-infectious:

  • Genital malignancy, skin conditions (eczema, psoriasis)
  • Sexual trauma, Behçet's syndrome, fixed drug eruptions
Management
First Episode

Refer to specialist sexual health service (preferred)

If managing in primary care:

  • Antiviral therapy: aciclovir 400mg three times daily for 5-7 days
  • Self-care measures:
    • Saline bathing (1 tsp salt in 560ml warm water)
    • Analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen)
    • Topical petroleum jelly or lidocaine 5% for dysuria
    • Increased fluid intake to dilute urine
  • Patient education and support resources
Recurrent Episodes

Self-care measures (as above)

Consider antiviral therapy if clinically indicated:

  • Episodic treatment: aciclovir 400mg three times daily for 5 days
  • Suppressive therapy: aciclovir 400mg twice daily
    • Consider if ≥6 episodes/year
    • Or if significant psychological impact
Patient Advice
  • Avoid sexual contact during symptomatic episodes
  • Use barrier contraception to reduce transmission risk
  • Be aware that asymptomatic viral shedding can occur
  • Provide resources:
    • Family Planning Association
    • Herpes Viruses Association
    • BASHH patient leaflets
Referral Criteria
All suspected first episodes
Severe or complicated disease
Immunocompromised patients
Pregnancy (requires specialist management)
Suspected sexual abuse
Treatment failure or atypical presentation
Complications to Monitor
Secondary bacterial infection
Autoinoculation to fingers/eyes
Urinary retention
Psychosocial impact
Increased risk of HIV acquisition/transmission
