Gonorrhoea Clinical Reference

One-page algorithm for fast decision making in primary care settings

Based on NICE CKS Gonorrhoea Guideline (June 2025)

Definition & Epidemiology

Definition
  • STI caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae
  • Affects mucous membranes: urethra, endocervix, rectum, pharynx, conjunctiva
Epidemiology
  • 2nd most common bacterial STI worldwide
  • Most common in ages 15-24
  • High risk groups: MSM, people of black Caribbean ethnicity
  • Associated with deprivation and urban areas

Clinical Features

Men
  • Urethral discharge (>80%)
  • Dysuria (>50%)
  • Onset 2-8 days after exposure
  • Asymptomatic in <10%
Women
  • Asymptomatic in ~50%
  • Increased/altered vaginal discharge (up to 50%)
  • Lower abdominal pain (up to 25%)
  • Intermenstrual bleeding (rare)
Extra-genital
  • Rectal Usually asymptomatic, may cause discharge/pain
  • Pharyngeal Usually asymptomatic, occasionally sore throat

Assessment

History
  • Sexual history: partners, practices, previous STIs
  • Protection use, contraceptive needs
  • Symptom onset and characteristics
Examination
  • Men: Inspect penis, palpate testes/epididymis
  • Women: Speculum exam, bimanual pelvic exam
  • Assess for complications (epididymitis, PID)
Screening
  • Test for chlamydia, HIV, syphilis (minimum)
  • Consider hepatitis B/C based on risk

Diagnosis

Key Recommendation

Refer to GUM clinic/specialist sexual health service (preferred). If unable to attend, manage in primary care with appropriate expertise.

Testing
  • NAAT (primary diagnostic method)
  • Culture for susceptibility testing
Sampling Sites
  • Women: Vulvovaginal swab (self-taken)
  • Men: First pass urine
  • Rectal: Routine in MSM, consider in women contacts
  • Pharyngeal: Recommended for ALL with urogenital gonorrhoea

Management

Referral & Admission
  • Refer: All patients to GUM/specialist service
  • Admit: Suspected disseminated gonorrhoea, severe/complicated PID
Treatment
  • Antibiotic therapy per BASHH guidelines
  • Ceftriaxone 1g IM first-line
  • Test of cure not necessary for anogenital infections treated with ceftriaxone 1g if susceptible
Partner Management
  • Treat sexual partners
  • Encourage patient-led partner notification
Follow-up
  • Arrange follow-up after treatment
  • Reinforce safe sex practices

Special Considerations

Child Protection

In children/young people: consider possibility of sexual abuse

  • Age <13: Always consider abuse unless clear evidence of perinatal transmission
  • Age 13-15: Consider unless clear evidence of consensual peer activity
  • Age 16-17: Consider if power imbalance or exploitation suspected
Safeguarding
  • Refer to children's social care if child maltreatment suspected
  • Follow Local Safeguarding Children Board procedures
