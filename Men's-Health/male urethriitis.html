Male Urethritis Clinical Algorithm

A comprehensive guide for the diagnosis and management of male urethritis in primary care settings

What is Urethritis?

Urethritis is inflammation of the urethra, usually caused by a sexually transmitted infection (STI).

  • Gonococcal urethritis - caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae
  • Non-gonococcal urethritis (NGU) - no identifiable cause in >50% of cases
  • Persistent/recurrent urethritis - occurs 30–90 days after treatment for acute NGU

Prevalence & Prognosis

  • Incidence: ~200 cases per 100,000 men annually
  • NGU is more common than gonococcal urethritis
  • Of STIs diagnosed in England (2022):
    • 50.8% chlamydia
    • 21.1% gonorrhoea
  • Persistent urethritis occurs in 15–25% of treated NGU cases
  • Recurrent urethritis occurs in 10-20% of cases

Infective Causes

  • Most common:
    • Chlamydia trachomatis
    • Mycoplasma genitalium
    • Neisseria gonorrhoeae
  • Less common:
    • Ureaplasma urealyticum
    • Trichomonas vaginalis
    • Urinary tract infection
    • Adenoviruses
    • Herpes simplex virus

Non-Infective Causes

  • Trauma (such as catheterization)
  • Irritation (soaps, lotions, spermicide creams, deodorants)
  • Urethral stricture
  • Persistent/recurrent urethritis usually has no identifiable cause

Clinical Features

  • Urethral discharge - mucoid, mucopurulent, or purulent (primary feature)
  • Dysuria - present in 73-88% of gonococcal urethritis, 53-75% of NGU
  • Balanoposthitis
  • Penile erythema
  • Penile irritation
  • Urethral discomfort and/or itch

Potential Complications

If untreated or inadequately treated:

  • Chlamydia-related:
    • Epididymo-orchitis
    • Sexually-acquired reactive arthritis (Reiter's syndrome)
  • Gonococcal-related:
    • Acute prostatitis
    • Disseminated gonococcal infection
    • Epididymitis
    • Periurethral abscess

History & Examination

History:

  • Signs and symptoms
  • Sexual history (partners, practices, condom use)
  • Previous STIs
  • Recent trauma/instrumentation
  • Drug history and allergies

Examination:

  • Check underwear for discharge
  • Examine entire genital area
  • Retract foreskin to evaluate meatus
  • Milk urethra if no visible discharge
  • Palpate for lymphadenopathy, masses, tenderness
  • Consider digital rectal exam if prostatitis suspected

Investigations

  • First-void urine for NAAT (chlamydia & gonorrhoea)
  • Urine dipstick (leukocyte esterase 1+ or greater)
  • Consider Mycoplasma genitalium testing if available
  • Urethral swab for trichomoniasis if suspected
  • Screen for other STIs (syphilis, HIV)
  • Consider hepatitis screening if high risk
  • Mid-stream urine for culture if UTI suspected

Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnose urethritis if:

  • Mucopurulent or purulent urethral discharge is present, OR
  • First-void urine tests positive for leukocyte esterase (1+ or greater), OR
  • Urinary threads are present in first-void urine

Note: Urine should be collected at least 1 hour after previous void

Differential Diagnosis

  • Physiological discharge
  • Balanitis
  • Epididymitis
  • Genital herpes
  • Prostatitis (acute or chronic)
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Nephrolithiasis
  • Non-infective urethritis
  • Urinary tract infection

Management Algorithm

Suspected Urethritis
REFER to GUM clinic (preferred)
Unable/unwilling to attend GUM?
MANAGE IN PRIMARY CARE

Treatment Options

First-line

Doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7 days

Alternative 1

Azithromycin 1g single dose, then 500mg once daily for 2 days

Alternative 2

Ofloxacin 200mg twice daily or 400mg once daily for 7 days

Note: Ofloxacin only if other antibiotics inappropriate

Patient Advice

  • Urethritis usually caused by STI (but not always)
  • Complete full course of antibiotics
  • Abstain from sex until treatment completed and symptoms resolved
  • Safer sex practices (condom use) to prevent future STIs
  • All recent sexual partners need notification and treatment

Partner Management

  • Advise patient to notify all recent sexual partners
  • Partners should attend GUM clinic for assessment and treatment
  • Contact tracing duration:
    • Chlamydia: See CKS Chlamydia topic
    • Gonorrhoea: See CKS Gonorrhoea topic
    • All others: All partners within previous 4 weeks

Treatment Failure

If symptoms persist or recur after treatment:

  • Refer to GUM clinic (strongly advised)
  • If declined/not possible:
    • Check adherence to treatment
    • Exclude reinfection
    • Reconsider diagnosis

Second-line Treatment

If first treated with doxycycline:

Azithromycin 1g single dose, then 500mg once daily for 2 days
PLUS
Metronidazole 400mg twice daily for 5 days

If first treated with azithromycin:

Moxifloxacin 400mg once daily for 10 days
PLUS
Metronidazole 400mg twice daily for 5 days

If symptoms persist after second course: Seek specialist advice
