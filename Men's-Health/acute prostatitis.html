Acute Prostatitis

Quick Reference Algorithm for Clinical Management

Definition & Presentation

Severe bacterial infection of prostate with accompanying UTI

Suspect if patient presents with:

  • UTI symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency
  • Prostate symptoms: perineal/penile/rectal pain, urinary retention, obstructive voiding, low back pain, pain on ejaculation
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, tachycardia, rigors
  • Examination: tender, swollen, warm prostate on gentle DRE

Diagnostic Steps

1
MSU for dipstick, culture & sensitivity
2
Blood cultures & FBC
3
Physical exam: abdomen, genitalia, gentle DRE
4
Consider STI screening if at risk

Management Decisions

Admission Criteria (Hospital)

  • Unable to take oral antibiotics
  • Severe symptoms
  • Signs of serious condition (sepsis, acute urinary retention, prostatic abscess)

Urgent Referral

  • Immunocompromised or diabetes
  • Pre-existing urological condition (BPH, indwelling catheter)

Outpatient Management

First-line antibiotics (14 days):
Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily OR Ofloxacin 200 mg twice daily
Alternative: Trimethoprim 200 mg twice daily
Second-line antibiotics (after specialist advice):
Levofloxacin 500 mg once daily OR Co-trimoxazole 960 mg twice daily (only with bacteriological evidence)

Supportive Care:

  • Paracetamol ± codeine for pain (or ibuprofen if suitable)
  • Adequate fluids
  • Patient education about course and adverse effects
Fluoroquinolone Safety Advice
  • Explain risk of rare but serious adverse effects (tendonitis, tendon rupture, muscle pain, neuropathy)
  • Provide patient information sheet
  • Advise to stop at first sign of serious reaction and seek help

Follow-up

48-hour review:

  • Check response to treatment
  • Review urine culture results
  • Adjust antibiotic if needed based on sensitivity
  • Admit if no improvement

14-day review:

  • Stop antibiotics or continue for additional 14 days (up to 6 weeks total)
  • Assess based on symptoms, examination, and test results

After recovery:

Refer to exclude structural urinary tract abnormality

Special Considerations

  • If STI identified: urgent referral to GUM clinic
  • If symptoms worsen: reassess for complications or alternative diagnoses

Fluoroquinolone Cautions

DO NOT PRESCRIBE:

  • History of tendon disorders related to quinolones
  • Previous serious adverse reactions to quinolones
  • Taking corticosteroids

USE WITH CAUTION:

  • Family history of aneurysm or heart valve disease
  • Pre-existing aortic aneurysm/dissection or heart valve disease
  • Epilepsy or seizure risk
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • G6PD deficiency
  • History of tendonitis
  • QT prolongation risk factors
  • History of psychotic disorders
  • Myasthenia gravis
  • Age >60 years, renal impairment, or solid-organ transplants
