Balanitis Clinical Reference

One-page algorithm for fast decision making

NICE CKS Balanitis Guidance (May 2024) • Fast reference for busy clinicians

Definition

Inflammation of glans penis (balanitis) and/or foreskin (posthitis). Term "balanitis" used for both conditions in this guide.

Initial Assessment

History

  • Onset/duration of symptoms
  • Hygiene practices
  • Potential irritants/allergens (soaps, lubricants, condoms)
  • Sexual history (if appropriate)
  • Underlying conditions (diabetes, skin conditions)
  • Previous episodes

Examination

  • Redness/swelling of glans/foreskin
  • Discharge, fissuring, ulceration
  • Foreskin retraction (phimosis?)
  • Inguinal lymphadenopathy

Differential Diagnosis

Non-specific dermatitis (common)

  • Redness extending to shaft
  • Bacterial/fungal overgrowth possible

Candidal infection

  • Blotchy redness, sparing urethral meatus
  • Small papules, glazed appearance

Bacterial infection

  • Painful redness, edema, erosions
  • Purulent exudate

Contact dermatitis

  • Irritant: pain/burning with red, scaly rash
  • Allergic: itch with similar rash

Other causes

  • STIs (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, Herpes)
  • Lichen sclerosus (white patches, scarring)
  • Psoriasis, Lichen planus, Zoon's balanitis

Investigations

  • Sub-preputial swab if severe/recurrent
  • STI screen if high risk/ulcers/blisters
  • HbA1c if severe/recurrent (especially candidal)
  • HIV test if clinically appropriate

Management

Children (1 month - 16 years)

All cases:

  • Good hygiene: lukewarm water daily
  • Keep foreskin retracted until dry (if possible)
  • Avoid irritants (soaps, bubble bath)
  • Emollient as soap substitute
  • Frequent nappy changes (infants)

By cause:

  • Non-specific: Hydrocortisone 1% + imidazole cream
  • Contact: Avoid triggers + hydrocortisone + emollient
  • Candidal: Imidazole cream ± hydrocortisone
  • Bacterial: Topical/oral antibiotics ± hydrocortisone

Adults (16+ years)

All cases:

  • Good hygiene as above
  • Identify/avoid potential triggers

By cause:

  • Non-specific: Hydrocortisone 1% + imidazole cream
  • Contact: Avoid triggers + hydrocortisone + emollient
  • Candidal: Imidazole cream ± hydrocortisone
  • Bacterial: Topical/oral antibiotics ± hydrocortisone

When to Refer

Children

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Persistent/recurrent cases not responding to treatment
  • Suspected lichen sclerosus
  • Phimosis causing urinary problems

Adults

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Persistent/recurrent cases not responding to treatment
  • Suspected lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, or Zoon's balanitis
  • Phimosis/paraphimosis
  • Suspected penile malignancy
  • Suspected STI requiring specialist management

Follow-up

  • Review if symptoms severe or not improved with initial treatment
  • Consider further investigations if persistent/recurrent
  • Reassess diagnosis if poor response to treatment
