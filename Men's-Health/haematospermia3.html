Step 1 Initial Assessment

Aim: stratify risk & look for red flags
  • Age → <40y usually benign/infective; ≥40y ↑ risk of malignancy.
  • History: duration & frequency; colour (bright red vs brown); associated symptoms:
    • Dysuria, frequency, urgency, pelvic/perineal pain → consider UTI/prostatitis.
    • Painless haematuria → possible urological malignancy.
    • Urethral discharge → STI risk.
    • Urinary hesitancy/frequency/dribbling → BPH/prostate CA risk.
    • Unilateral testicular swelling / change → testicular tumour.
  • Recent urological procedure? (biopsy, cystoscopy, vasectomy, radiotherapy)
  • PMH/meds: HTN, bleeding disorder, anticoagulants/antiplatelets; FHx prostate CA; sexual & travel history (TB/schisto risk).
  • Exam: BP, temperature; abdomen & nodes; external genitalia; testes/scrotum; consider DRE (size, nodules, tenderness); look for bruising/petechiae.

Step 2 First‑Line Investigations

  • Urinalysis & culture – all patients.
  • PSA – all men ≥40y; consider in younger if FHx/risk or suspicious DRE.
  • Targeted tests (as indicated):
    • FBC, coag screen, renal & LFTs.
    • STI screen (GUM clinic if risk) – enables Dx + contact tracing.
    • Scrotal ultrasound if swelling/discomfort.
    • Semen M/C/S if TB or schistosomiasis suspected.

Step 3 Management in Primary Care

  • Single episode, <40y, no cause → reassure; safety‑net if persistent/recurrent.
  • UTI suspected → treat per culture & local guidance.
  • STI suspected → refer to GUM clinic for Dx & partner notification.
  • Hypertension → treat per guideline.
  • Prostatitis (acute/chronic) → manage per CKS; review response.
  • Post‑procedure (e.g., prostate biopsy) → reassure: typically resolves in 3–4 weeks.

Step 4 Referral Criteria

Suspected cancer (2‑week‑wait): ↑PSA or abnormal DRE; clinical suspicion of testicular cancer; painless haematuria or other urological CA features.
Urology (routine/urgent): all men ≥40y with unexplained haematospermia; any age with persistent/recurrent episodes; ongoing symptoms despite treatment; features suggesting cysts/calculi of prostate/seminal vesicles.
  • Other referrals:
    • Haematology if lymphoma/leukaemia suspected.
    • Infectious diseases if TB/schistosomiasis suspected.

Step 5 Key Reassurance Points

Most cases are benign & self‑limiting; typically no impact on fertility (exception: untreated STIs). Provide safety‑netting advice.

At‑a‑glance

  • <40y: benign/infective most likely.
  • ≥40y: PSA for all; consider DRE; lower threshold for referral.
  • Always: urinalysis + MSU.
  • GUM for STI risk (Dx + contact tracing).
  • 2WW for prostate/testicular cancer suspicion or painless haematuria.
