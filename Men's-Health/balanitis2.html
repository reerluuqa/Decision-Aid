Overview

Inflammation of the glans (± foreskin). Causes include non‑specific dermatitis with secondary infection, Candida, bacterial infection (incl. anaerobes), STIs, and inflammatory dermatoses (e.g., lichen sclerosus, psoriasis). Episodes can be short‑lived, persistent, or recurrent.

Age: boys ≥1 month & men Avoid irritants (soaps, wipes, bubble bath) Use emollient as soap substitute Keep foreskin retracted until dry (where possible) Do not forcibly retract a fixed foreskin in children

Assessment

History

  • Pain/itch, erythema, swelling, discharge, fissuring/ulcers, bleeding; urinary symptoms, dyspareunia.
  • Onset/duration; hygiene (nappy change frequency; washing routines).
  • Irritants/allergens (soaps, detergents, creams, latex condoms, lubricants).
  • Mechanical irritation (over‑washing, friction; children playing with foreskin).
  • Skin history (eczema/psoriasis); sexual history/STI risk.
  • Diabetes/HIV or other immunosuppression; new meds (fixed drug eruption).

Examination & Tests

  • Inspect penile & surrounding skin for erythema, oedema, discharge, erosions; check circumcision status.
  • Look for tight foreskin/meatal stenosis (lichen sclerosus), phimosis; inguinal nodes.
  • Adults: exclude penile mass or persistent lesion.
  • Swabs (sub‑preputial) for Candida & bacterial C&S if severe or infection suspected.
  • STI screen if high risk (ulcers, blisters, nodes) per judgement.
  • Bloods: HbA1c ± HIV if severe/persistent/recurrent, esp. candidal.
Pragmatic classification
  • Likely infective: Candida, streptococci/staphylococci, anaerobes (Gardnerella), STIs
  • Likely non‑infective: irritant/allergic dermatitis, psoriasis, lichen sclerosus, Zoon’s

Management — Children (1 month–16 years)

General

  • Daily lukewarm water cleanse; dry gently. Emollient as soap substitute.
  • Do not forcibly retract a fixed foreskin; frequent nappy changes.

Non‑specific / irritant dermatitis

  • Avoid triggers.
  • Topical hydrocortisone 1% once daily up to 14 days.
  • + Topical imidazole (e.g., clotrimazole/miconazole) until settled (≤14 days).

Infective

  • Candidal: Topical imidazole; consider hydrocortisone 1% up to 14 days if inflamed.
  • Bacterial:
    • Mild: topical mupirocin 2% 2–3×/day for 7–10 days.
    • Severe: oral phenoxymethylpenicillin for 10 days; if true penicillin allergy, clarithromycin 7 days.

Review if severe or not improving. Consider swabs & HbA1c if severe/persistent/recurrent.

Management — Adults (≥16 years)

General

  • Daily hygiene; avoid irritants; emollient as soap substitute.

Likely candidal

  • Topical imidazole until settled (≤14 days).
  • Severe symptoms: fluconazole 150 mg single oral dose.
  • Marked inflammation: add hydrocortisone 1% up to 14 days.

Likely bacterial

  • Mild: topical mupirocin 2% 2–3×/day for 7–10 days.
  • Severe: phenoxymethylpenicillin 500 mg QID for 10 days.
    • Penicillin allergy: clarithromycin 250 mg BD for 7 days.
  • Recurrent streptococcal: consider testing/treating partner(s).

Likely anaerobic (Gardnerella)

  • Metronidazole 400 mg BD × 1 week; alternative co‑amoxiclav 375 mg TID × 1 week.

Consider dermatoses

  • Manage seborrhoeic dermatitis/psoriasis per respective guidance.

STI suspected → manage or refer to sexual health. If severe or not improving, arrange review, swabs, and consider HbA1c/HIV.

Referral Criteria

Children

  • Diagnostic uncertainty (biopsy may be needed).
  • Persistent/recurrent not responding to primary care.
  • Suspected allergic contact dermatitis (patch testing).
  • Suspected lichen sclerosus and/or persistent phimosis → consider circumcision.

Adults

  • Diagnostic uncertainty or persistent/recurrent cases.
  • Recurrent with phimosis → urology (circumcision).
  • Suspected allergic contact dermatitis (patch testing).
  • Suspected Zoon’s or lichen sclerosus → biopsy to exclude PeIN.
  • Any penile mass or persistent lesion (post‑STI treatment) → Urgent 2‑week cancer pathway.

Prescribing Essentials

Topical imidazoles (examples)

DrugTypical use
Clotrimazole 1%Apply 2–3×/day ≥2 weeks (balanitis licensed). May damage latex condoms — advise alternative contraception for 5 days after use.
Miconazole 2%Apply 2×/day; continue 10 days after healing. Monitor interactions (e.g., warfarin).
Econazole 1%Apply 2×/day ≥2 weeks (mycotic balanitis licensed).
Ketoconazole 2%Apply 1–2×/day; continue a few days after lesions disappear.

Key cautions

  • Fluconazole: check QT‑prolonging drugs, hepatic/renal issues; multiple interactions (statins, warfarin, sulfonylureas, etc.).
  • Phenoxymethylpenicillin: avoid in true penicillin allergy; monitor INR if on warfarin.
  • Clarithromycin: avoid with QT prolongation/electrolyte disturbance; interactions (e.g., DOACs slightly ↑ exposure).
  • Metronidazole/co‑amoxiclav: standard cautions; alcohol avoidance with metronidazole.
Practical tips
  • When inflammation is prominent, a short course of hydrocortisone 1% can improve comfort alongside antimicrobials.
  • Group B strep is often a commensal; Candida may be superadded and doesn’t exclude a dermatosis.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Non‑specific urethritis (dysuria, meatal erythema, discharge).
  • Fixed drug eruption; erythema multiforme; autoimmune bullous disorders.
  • Dermatitis artefacta.
  • Extra‑mammary Paget’s disease (older men).
  • Penile squamous cell carcinoma / PeIN (persistent ulcer/erosion/red area/mass).

Safety Netting

  • Review if symptoms are severe or not improving after initial management.
  • Discuss complications: phimosis/paraphimosis; urinary symptoms from meatal stenosis (lichen sclerosus); sexual dysfunction; rare malignant transformation with chronic disease.
  • Advise to return earlier if systemic symptoms, spreading erythema, or urinary retention.

Quick reference for clinicians. Always consider local antimicrobial guidance and patient‑specific factors.
