Acute Abdominal Pain

Quick Reference Algorithm for Primary Care

Based on NICE CKS Guidance (January 2025)

Initial Assessment

Vital Signs

  • Blood pressure
  • Pulse rate
  • Temperature
  • Capillary refill time

Pain Characteristics

  • Location (see Causes by Location tab)
  • Onset: Sudden vs. gradual
  • Duration: Less than 7 days for acute pain
  • Severity: Scale 1-10
  • Progression: Improving, stable, worsening
  • Radiation: To back, groin, etc.
  • Quality: Cramping, sharp, dull, constant

Associated Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Constipation
  • Cough or shortness of breath
  • Urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency, haematuria)
  • Colic

History

  • Previous episodes of similar pain
  • Exacerbating or relieving factors
  • Change in bowel habit
  • History of trauma or bleeding
  • Previous medical and surgical history
  • Medication history (including OTC)
  • Travel history
  • For women: menstrual history, pregnancy status, contraception, sexual history
  • Smoking, alcohol, substance use
  • Family history

Physical Examination

  • Inspection: Scars, contour, skin changes, signs of dehydration
  • Auscultation: Bowel sounds (hyperactive in obstruction, absent in perforation)
  • Percussion: Tenderness suggests peritoneal inflammation
  • Palpation: Tenderness, guarding, rigidity, masses

Specific Signs

  • McBurney's sign: RLQ tenderness (appendicitis)
  • Murphy's sign: RUQ tenderness with arrested inspiration (cholecystitis)
  • Carnett's sign: Increased pain with abdominal wall flexion (abdominal wall pain)
  • Psoas sign: Pain with right thigh extension (appendicitis)
  • Rovsing's sign: RLQ pain after palpating LLQ (appendicitis)
  • Obturator sign: Pain with internal hip rotation (appendicitis)

Red Flags (Requires Urgent Referral)

Medical Red Flags

  • Signs and symptoms of sepsis (fever, tachycardia, lowered temperature)
  • Generalised oedema (suspect nephrotic syndrome)
  • Jaundice
  • Polyuria/polydipsia (suspect diabetic ketoacidosis)
  • Purpuric or petechial rash (suspect meningococcal disease if febrile)
  • Respiratory symptoms (tachypnoea, respiratory distress, cough)
  • Significant dehydration (clinically or over 5% weight loss)

Surgical Red Flags

  • History of recent significant abdominal trauma or recent surgery
  • Irreducible hernia
  • Peritonitis (guarding, rebound tenderness, constant dull pain exacerbated by movement)
  • 'Red currant jelly' stool (indicating possible intussusception)
  • Signs and symptoms of bowel obstruction (colicky abdominal pain, absolute constipation, bilious vomiting, hyperactive or tinkling bowel sounds)
  • Testicular or labial pain

Other Red Flags

  • Abdominal distension
  • Bilious (green) or blood-stained vomit
  • Blood in stool
  • Palpable abdominal mass
  • Severe or increasing abdominal pain
  • Unintentional weight loss

Red Flags in Children

  • Non-mobile or change in gait pattern due to pain
  • Unresponsive or excessively drowsy

Investigations

Initial Tests (Primary Care)

  • Full blood count
  • Urea and electrolytes
  • Liver function test
  • Blood glucose
  • C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • Urinalysis
  • Pregnancy test - for all women of childbearing age

Additional Tests (Based on Clinical Suspicion)

  • Amylase and lipase - if pancreatitis suspected
  • Chlamydia and gonorrhoea testing - for women at risk of STIs
  • Coagulation screen - if vascular cause suspected
  • Markers for specific diseases - e.g., coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease
  • Quantitative faecal immunochemical testing (FIT) - if malignancy suspected
  • Serum lactic acid levels - if acute mesenteric ischaemia suspected
  • Stool culture - if infectious cause suspected

Secondary Care Investigations

  • Abdominal CT scan - first choice for RLQ, LLQ, LUQ or generalized pain
  • Abdominal MRI
  • Abdominal X-ray - if intestinal obstruction, fulminant colitis, or perforation suspected
  • Abdominal ultrasound - first choice for RUQ and suprapubic pain
  • Colonoscopy/sigmoidoscopy
  • ECG - in people aged over 50 years, or younger if appropriate
  • Endoscopy - if oesophageal or gastric pathology suspected
  • Laparoscopy
  • Transvaginal ultrasound - if ectopic pregnancy or endometriosis suspected

Referred Pain Patterns

  • Right scapular: Gallbladder disease, liver disease
  • Left scapular: Cardiac, gastric, pancreatic disease
  • Scrotal/labial: Renal stones, ureteric disease
  • To back: Pancreatitis, AAA dissection/rupture

Age-Specific Considerations

General Patterns

  • Under 60 years: Renal colic and appendicitis more common
  • Older adults: Gallbladder disease, diverticulitis, mesenteric ischaemia more common

  • Malrotation
  • Hernias

  • Intussusception

School-Aged Children

  • Constipation

  • Urinary tract infections

Female Adolescents

Management Decision

Presence of Red Flags?

Admit

If red flags present, uncertain diagnosis, or significant comorbidities

Refer

If suspicion of specific condition requiring specialist input (e.g., appendicitis, cholecystitis)

Manage in Primary Care

If no red flags and clear benign cause (e.g., gastroenteritis, constipation)

Common Conditions in Primary Care

  • Gastroenteritis: Supportive care, hydration, antiemetics if needed
  • Constipation: Laxatives, dietary advice, fluid intake
  • Urinary tract infection: Antibiotics, analgesia
  • Dyspepsia: Lifestyle advice, PPI or H2 antagonist
  • Abdominal wall pain: Reassurance, analgesia, physiotherapy

When to Refer

  • Suspected appendicitis: Surgical referral
  • Suspected cholecystitis/gallstones: Surgical referral
  • Suspected diverticulitis: Medical referral
  • Suspected gynaecological cause: Gynaecology referral
  • Suspected renal/ureteric colic: Urology referral
  • Suspected malignancy: Urgent referral (2-week wait)

Follow-up

  • Reassess within 24-48 hours if symptoms persist or worsen
  • Consider safety netting advice for all patients
  • Document differential diagnoses and rationale for management plan
